# Eugène Atget

## 1. Basic facts
- French photographer, born in 1857 and died in 1927; worked mainly in Paris and began photographing around 1897, after earlier ambitions in acting and painting did not lead to a stable career.[S1][S2]
- He marketed his work as `Documents pour artistes` and supplied images of streets, courtyards, shopfronts, parks, trees, and old buildings to painters, decorators, and craftspeople.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: old Paris before and during modernization, vernacular architecture, streets, courtyards, shop windows, urban fixtures, and marginal urban sites such as small trades or entertainment districts.[S1][S2]
- Representative work example: *Coin, Boulevard de la Chapelle et Rue Fleury 76* (1921) and later storefront / street images such as *Avenue des Gobelins* or *Boutique automobile, avenue de la Grande Armée* show the way Atget moved from straightforward urban record toward photographs later prized for their strange modern stillness.[S3][S4]
- Technique / formal traits: large-format wooden camera, tripod, slow working process, albumen contact prints, and frontal or quietly observational views that preserve surface detail and tonal gradation.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: the work began as documentary supply for artists and artisans who needed clear visual records; the descriptive clarity of the images was therefore practical before it became historically or aesthetically significant.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: Atget worked during a period when Haussmannized Paris and later urban development were erasing older streetscapes. His photographs became records of a city in transition, even though he did not initially present them as avant-garde art.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Atget does not fit neatly inside pictorialism. His photographs were not made through painterly manipulation, and later reception positioned him closer to documentary, street photography, and modernist seeing than to salon-style art photography.[S1][S3]
- Historical significance: after his death, Berenice Abbott preserved and promoted the archive, helping move Atget from an obscure supplier of documentary photographs into the history of modern photography.[S2]
- Where and how the work was used: during his lifetime the photographs circulated as source material for artists and artisans; after his death the same archive was reused in exhibitions, publications, and museum collecting to narrate the emergence of modern documentary and modernist photographic vision.[S1][S2][S4]
- Critical meaning: later critics, especially John Szarkowski, argued that Atget’s descriptive records unintentionally discovered a specifically photographic way of seeing, in which light, surfaces, emptiness, and fragmentary urban time matter as much as subject matter itself.[S3]
- Well-supported facts and later interpretation should be kept distinct in final writing: it is well supported that Atget described the work as documents and sold it as such; the framing of him as “the first modern photographer” belongs to later critical reception rather than to his own stated program.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Atget was not broadly canonized as an art photographer during his lifetime; his later reputation depended heavily on posthumous preservation and interpretation.[S2]
- French critics and the Parisian avant-garde of the 1920s noticed aspects of the work, but the strongest institutional shift in reception came later through Abbott and then MoMA’s exhibitions and publications, which stabilized Atget as a key predecessor for modern photography.[S3][S4]
- Man Ray’s publication of Atget’s work in a surrealist context and Abbott’s later advocacy were crucial in shifting the reception of the photographs from utilitarian documents toward artistic and historical objects.[S1][S2]
- Szarkowski’s multi-volume *The Work of Atget* became especially influential in positioning him within the genealogy of modern photography.[S3]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Atget’s own self-description most often appears through the phrase `Documents pour artistes`, used on his business materials; reviewed sources support this as the clearest available statement of purpose.[S1][S2]

## 5. Quote candidates
- `Documents pour artistes.` [S1]
- `These are simply documents I make.` — reported in later museum framing of Atget’s response to surrealist appropriation; useful, but should be checked against the exact publication context before final website use.[S1]
