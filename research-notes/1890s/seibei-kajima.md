# Seibei Kajima

## 1. Basic facts
- Japanese photographer, entrepreneur, and photography patron, 1866–1924.[S1][S2]
- Known as a `shashin daijin` (“photographic grandee”) in Meiji Japan; involved in dry-plate production, photographic institutions, studio culture, and technical experimentation.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: Kajima is important less as a single-image auteur than as a figure linking studio practice, photographic technology, exhibition culture, and the social prestige of photography in Meiji Japan.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: he operated the studio `Genrokkan` in Ginza and is associated with advanced technical demonstrations, including X-ray imaging and magnesium flash night photography, alongside portraiture and stage-related images.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: the sources suggest that Kajima pursued photography as both spectacle and technical frontier. His role was to expand what photography in Japan could do materially, socially, and institutionally.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: he worked in the period when photography in Japan was moving from imported novelty and commercial portrait practice toward a more organized field with associations, competitions, and domestic production of materials such as dry plates.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Kajima should be placed alongside figures such as Kazumasa Ogawa and other Meiji photographic entrepreneurs and technicians, though reviewed sources emphasize Kajima’s flamboyant patronage and experimental zeal rather than a clearly defined formal school.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: he helped support the formation of photographic institutions and the technological environment in which later Japanese photography developed. This includes participation in dry-plate manufacture and photographic societies.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: Kajima complicates a narrow artist-centered history. He shows that the history of photography is also built by patrons, experimenters, studio owners, and people who make a medium socially visible.[S1][S2]
- Research gap: detailed scholarly analysis in English remains scarce. Final writing should rely on verified institutional histories and avoid overstating influence where only anecdotal or exhibition-driven material is available.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Recent reception often presents Kajima through rediscovery exhibitions that emphasize his role in modernizing Japanese photographic culture and his eccentric public persona.[S1][S2]
- The label `shashin daijin` itself indicates that his reception mixes technical contribution, wealth, patronage, and theatrical personal history.[S1][S2]
- More sustained critical writing beyond exhibition journalism appears limited in the reviewed sources.[S1][S2]
