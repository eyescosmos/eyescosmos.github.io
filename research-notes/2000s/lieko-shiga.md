# Lieko Shiga

## 1. Basic facts
- Lieko Shiga, born 1980 in Okazaki, Aichi, is a Japanese photographer based in Miyagi Prefecture [S1][S2].
- Her major project `RASEN KAIGAN` / `Rasen Kaigan` centers on Kitakama, a coastal community near Sendai, before and after the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake and tsunami [S2][S3].
- MoMA included Shiga in `Ocean of Images: New Photography 2015` and holds works from `Rasen Kaigan` [S1].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: community memory, disaster, oral history, local ritual, the instability of photographic time, mourning, myth, and the relation between archive and lived place [S2][S3][S4].
- Technique / formal traits: Shiga's images are staged, collaborative, flash-lit, and often dreamlike. They can look supernatural or theatrical while remaining tied to specific people, places, and events in Kitakama [S2][S3].
- The project developed from Shiga's residence in Kitakama from 2008, where she photographed local residents, festivals, sports days, and oral histories with the help of a local cameraman and the community [S2][S3].
- The 2011 tsunami destroyed Shiga's home and studio and killed many residents in the village. Afterward, she continued to work in temporary housing and cleaned/digitized anonymous photographs found in debris [S2][S3].
- Why this method was chosen: the work does not simply document the disaster. Shiga uses staging, collaboration, and archival recovery to show how photographs mediate between the living, the dead, memory, and communal imagination [S2][S4].
- Historical context: `Rasen Kaigan` belongs to post-3.11 Japanese photography but resists being reduced to disaster reportage. It joins documentary, performance, archive, and mythic image-making in response to a ruptured local history [S2][S3].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Shiga's practice can be placed within contemporary Japanese photography's move beyond street/documentary conventions toward socially embedded, performative, and community-based projects [S1][S2].
- Representative works / examples: `portrait of cultivation` (2009), `rasen kaigan 31` (2010), `garden of tears` (2010), `child's play` (2011), `no one understands this song` (2012), and `rasen kaigan 46` (2011) [S1][S4][S5].
- Historical significance: Shiga's work is significant because it expands photography's role from image production into community archive, ritual, and post-disaster memory work [S2][S3].
- Critical meaning: the pictures collapse past, present, and future, showing photography not as a stable record but as a charged object whose value can shift from debris to mourning object, prayer, and historical testimony [S3].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- SFMOMA describes `Rasen Kaigan` as deeply personal and collaborative, and notes that Shiga is reluctant to let the work be defined only by the disaster [S2].
- Getty Iris emphasizes her role as a community archivist after the tsunami and connects the project to the Sendai Mediatheque recorder311 context [S3].
- MoMA's collection page lists eleven works from `Rasen Kaigan` and shows the project's institutional position in contemporary photography [S1].
- Sendai Mediatheque identifies Shiga's long-term residence and work in Kitakama, her continued activity after the disaster, and her 2012 solo exhibition there [S4].
- Tate's photography publication frames `RASEN KAIGAN` through ecology and environment and notes that Shiga evokes the zone between dream and reality without digital retouching [S6].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Getty Iris cites Shiga's own explanation of `Rasen-Kaigan` as spiral/coast and also as past, present, future; this is a strong first-person source [S3].

## 5. Quote candidates
- "there is no past, present or future" [S3].
- "an elegy to a community that has been dislocated" [S2].
- "spirit and history of the place and its people" [S6].
