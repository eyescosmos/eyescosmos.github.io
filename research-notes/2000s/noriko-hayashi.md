# Noriko Hayashi

## 1. Basic facts
- Noriko Hayashi, born 1983 in Japan, is a documentary photographer based in Tokyo [S1][S2].
- She began photographing for a small local newspaper in The Gambia while studying International Relations at university [S1].
- Her projects include `Unholy Matrimony - Bride Kidnapping in Kyrgyzstan`, `Alaka-chuu`, and `The Prayer of the Yazidis` [S1][S3].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: under-reported social issues, gendered violence, forced marriage, displacement, religious minorities, and everyday life in regions underrepresented in mainstream news [S1][S3].
- Technique / formal traits: Hayashi works in a documentary/photojournalistic mode, using close access, captions, and sustained time in communities to show specific social practices through individual stories [S1][S3].
- In `Unholy Matrimony - Bride Kidnapping in Kyrgyzstan`, she photographed rural Kyrgyz communities in 2012, documenting the practice of `ala kachuu`, including scenes of women being persuaded to accept marriage and the symbolic use of the white scarf [S3][S4].
- Why this method was chosen: Hayashi's background in International Relations and early work in The Gambia shaped a practice focused on issues that are not usually prominent on the international news agenda [S1].
- Historical context: her Kyrgyzstan work was made during a period when documentary photographers were using long-form visual storytelling to connect human-rights questions with intimate social detail. Bride kidnapping had been illegal in Kyrgyzstan since 1994, yet sources describe continuing local tolerance and weak enforcement [S4][S5].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Hayashi belongs to an international documentary/photojournalism network rather than a gallery-based post-internet context. Her selection for World Press Photo's Joop Swart Masterclass places her among emerging global visual storytellers [S2].
- Representative works / examples: `Unholy Matrimony - Bride Kidnapping in Kyrgyzstan` (2012), `Alaka-chuu`, and `The Prayer of the Yazidis` [S1][S3].
- Historical significance: her work is useful in photography history because it shows how Japanese photographers in the 2000s/2010s participated in international documentary networks and focused on transnational social issues outside Japan [S1][S2].
- Critical meaning: Hayashi's photographs depend on access and captioned specificity. Their critical force comes from showing structures of coercion through particular women, rituals, rooms, gestures, and family negotiations rather than through abstract statistics alone [S3][S4].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Panos Pictures states that Hayashi's work has been recognized by NPPA Best of Photojournalism, Visa d'Or feature award at Visa pour l'Image in 2013, DAYS Japan International Photojournalism awards, the Alexia Foundation, and Amnesty Media Awards; it also notes her 2015 Joop Swart Masterclass selection [S1].
- World Press Photo lists Hayashi among the 2015 Joop Swart Masterclass participants, confirming her international recognition in documentary photography [S2].
- Verve Photo published a profile on the Kyrgyzstan project and quotes Hayashi on spending five months visiting villages and witnessing the practice [S4].
- Highlighting Japan notes that she became the first Japanese person to win the Visa d'Or feature award in its 25-year history, for images of kidnapping and forced marriage in Kyrgyzstan [S6].
