# Kelli Connell

## 1. Basic facts
- Kelli Connell, born 1974 in Oklahoma City, is an American photographer based in Chicago and known for staged, digitally composited photographs about identity, gender, sexuality, and relationship roles [S1][S2].
- She received a BFA from the University of North Texas in 1997 and an MFA from Texas Woman's University in 2003 [S2].
- Her long-term project `Double Life` began in the early 2000s and was later presented as `Double Life, 20 Years` [S1][S3].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: duality of self, intimate relationships, sexuality, gender roles, interior conflict, and the difference between photographic document and constructed reality [S1][S2][S4].
- Technique / formal traits: Connell photographs a single model in multiple roles and digitally stitches the negatives or image files into seamless scenes that appear to show two women together [S1][S2][S4].
- The images use controlled lighting, body language, clothing, and domestic or everyday settings to create plausible but impossible encounters. The viewer initially reads them as documentary scenes before recognizing the double performance [S1][S2].
- Why this method was chosen: the doubling allows Connell to externalize interior psychological and social contradictions. Rather than illustrating identity as fixed, the same body becomes a field for competing roles: masculine/feminine, rational/irrational, exterior/interior, motivated/resigned [S1][S2].
- Historical context: `Double Life` emerged at a moment when digital compositing was becoming common in photography. Connell uses that tool not as a spectacle of manipulation but as a quiet way to test documentary belief and queer self-representation [S2][S4].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Lawndale's early exhibition text links Connell to the "Photoshop Generation," Surrealist photographic doubling, and Cindy Sherman's role-playing and gender politics [S4].
- Representative works / examples: `Picnic` (2002), `The Valley` (2005-06), `Carnival` (2006), `Reflection` (2008), `Head to Head` (2008-10), and later `Pictures for Charis`, which reconsiders Edward Weston and Charis Wilson through contemporary collaborative photography [S1][S2][S5].
- Historical significance: Connell's work matters because it uses digital construction to reopen older photographic questions of self-portraiture, performance, documentary credibility, and queer intimacy without relying on spectacle [S1][S4].
- Critical meaning: the photographs "lie" as documents but can still reveal emotional, social, and psychological truths. This tension between fiction and truth is central for a later Japanese website paragraph [S1].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- The Metropolitan Museum of Art describes `Carnival` as part of a decade-long project that appears to document two similar women but is actually composed from multiple images of one model [S1].
- The Museum of Contemporary Photography emphasizes Connell's interest in the viewer's own notions of identity and social constructs [S2].
- Alice Austen House presents `Double Life, 20 Years` as an autobiographical questioning of sexuality and gender roles in intimate relationships [S3].
- Lawndale Art Center's 2004 exhibition text situates Connell's work within digital-era photography, Surrealist doubling, and gendered role-playing [S4].
- The High Museum's `Pictures for Charis` frames her later work as a reassessment of the story of Charis Wilson and Edward Weston, shifting attention to collaboration and the risk of flattening another person's life [S5].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- The Met quotes Connell explaining that her photographs can lie as documents but tell truth as images [S1].
- Catherine Edelman Gallery includes Connell's statement on fluid identity and her resistance to fixed labels [S6].
