# Lucas Blalock

## 1. Basic facts
- Lucas Blalock, born 1978, is an American artist working with photography, still life, digital manipulation, and image circulation [S1][S2].
- MoMA included Blalock in `Ocean of Images: New Photography 2015`, where works such as `Right Shoe` and related manipulated photographs appeared [S1][S3].
- His work has also been included in the 2019 Whitney Biennial, including the large public-facing work `Donkeys Crossing the Desert` [S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: the constructed nature of photographs, software as a visible drawing tool, still life after digital culture, humor, awkwardness, and the circulation and reuse of images [S2][S3][S5].
- Technique / formal traits: Blalock often begins with large-format photographs of ordinary objects - erasers, fabrics, drinking glasses, strawberries, shoes, or bags - and then manipulates them in Photoshop [S2].
- Crucially, the manipulation is not hidden. Sections are duplicated, layered, erased, redrawn, or made visibly clumsy so that the image exposes its own digital facture [S2][S5].
- Why this method was chosen: Blalock uses digital tools not to perfect the photograph but to make the labor of alteration visible. This approach challenges the expectation that software should disappear into seamless realism [S2][S5].
- Historical context: the work belongs to a post-2000 moment in which photography was shaped by smartphones, screens, software, online circulation, and a new visual literacy around swiping, pinching, and retouching [S2][S3].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Aperture places Blalock alongside Michele Abeles and Margaret Lee in a context where artists are steeped in photographic tradition but attentive to image dissemination and the collapsing divide between objects and images [S2].
- Representative works / examples: `xyz` installation (Ramiken Crucible, 2011), `Right Shoe` (2013), `Strawberries (fresh forever)` (2014), `Strawberries (forever fresh)` (2015), and `Donkeys Crossing the Desert` (2019) [S1][S2][S4][S6].
- Historical significance: Blalock's work matters for photography history because it presents digital manipulation as an obvious, comic, and critical part of photographic seeing rather than a hidden defect or post-production secret [S2][S5].
- Critical meaning: the photographs produce an uneasy relation between object, photograph, and software mark. Their humor helps make a serious argument: every photograph is constructed, and in the digital era that construction has become newly visible [S2][S5].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Aperture's `Swiping at Pictures` identifies Blalock's work as a key example of photographic tradition intersecting with image dissemination and obvious digital alteration [S2].
- MoMA's `Ocean of Images` framed Blalock within a broad institutional inquiry into post-Internet photographic experience and the effects of an image-based reality [S3].
- Collector Daily's review of `Ocean of Images` says Blalock received a repeated, connective role in the exhibition, linking software manipulation, versioning, reuse, and recirculation [S7].
- TIME's discussion of the 2019 Whitney Biennial describes `Donkeys Crossing the Desert` as a surreal digital collage with an augmented reality component, emphasizing the relation between photograph and smartphone experience [S4].
- MoMA's `Inside/Out` text notes that Blalock foregrounds intentionally obvious or clumsy editing in order to challenge ideas of photographic purity [S5].
