# Eileen Quinlan

## 1. Basic facts
- Eileen Quinlan, born 1972, is an American photographer associated with still life, abstraction, material experimentation, and feminist histories of photographic form [S1][S2].
- MoMA included Quinlan in `New Photography 2013`; the Art Institute of Chicago holds multiple works, including `Smoke and Mirrors` and `Broken Figure` [S1][S3].
- The Whitney Museum collection includes works from `Smoke & Mirrors`, `Fahrenheit`, and `Failed Portrait` [S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: photographic abstraction, illusion, feminist material culture, motherhood, body, surface, and the instability of photographic evidence [S1][S3].
- Technique / formal traits: Quinlan works with medium- and large-format cameras, still-life setups, mirrors, smoke, Mylar, gels, flash, expired film, Polaroid material, scans, and digital colorization [S1][S2][S3].
- In `Smoke & Mirrors`, reflected smoke and broken mirrors make the image disclose its own construction while still producing atmospheric abstraction [S4].
- In `Sophia` and `Laura`, shown in MoMA's `New Photography 2013`, she used a folded yoga mat, expired black-and-white film, scanning, and digital colorization. MoMA connects these works to feminist history, material culture, Judy Chicago, and David Lynch's `Twin Peaks` [S1].
- In `Broken Figure` (2014), the Art Institute of Chicago notes that Quinlan pressed bodies against shower glass and manipulated the developing fluid of obsolete black-and-white Polaroid film, producing blurred, layered images in which body, glass, and photographic material become difficult to separate [S3].
- Why this method was chosen: Quinlan uses analog and hybrid processes during a period of digital image saturation to show that photographic abstraction is not outside reality but produced through staged material contact, failure, and chemical instability [S1][S2][S3].
- Historical context: her work belongs to a 2000s/2010s resurgence of experimental photography in which artists questioned whether photography was still indexical, sculptural, performative, or painterly [S1][S5].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: her work can be placed beside artists in `New Photography 2013` and post-2000 abstraction such as Barbara Kasten, James Welling, Lisa Oppenheim, and other artists who used photography to test the border between image, object, and process [S1][S5].
- Representative works / examples: `Smoke and Mirrors #40` (2005), `Sophia` (2012), `Laura` (2012), `Failed Portrait` (2013), and `Broken Figure` (2014) [S1][S3][S4].
- Historical significance: Quinlan helps make the studio still life newly critical by turning reflections, accidents, damaged materials, and reproductive technologies into evidence of how pictures are made and gendered [S1][S3].
- Critical meaning: rather than presenting abstraction as pure formalism, her photographs suggest that abstraction carries social, bodily, and feminist histories embedded in objects and photographic materials [S1][S3].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- MoMA framed Quinlan's abstract photography as grounded in feminist history and material culture, a key institutional reading for later interpretation [S1].
- The Art Institute of Chicago's entry for `Broken Figure` emphasizes motherhood, Polaroid manipulation, and the obstruction of the viewer's gaze as part of the work's meaning [S3].
- Collector Daily described her post-`New Photography 2013` work as moving between recognizable imagery and uncontrolled, gestural abstraction through process decay [S5].
- Time Out's review of `New Photography 2013` linked Quinlan's abstract still lifes to the legacy of Barbara Kasten, placing her in a history of constructed studio abstraction [S6].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- MoMA's `New Photography 2013` page includes a video in which Quinlan discusses `Laura`, but the accessible text is curatorial rather than a full statement [S1].
