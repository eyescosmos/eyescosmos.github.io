# Rashid Johnson

## 1. Basic facts
- Rashid Johnson, born 1977 in Chicago, is an American artist whose practice includes photography, painting, sculpture, film, drawing, and installation [S1][S2].
- He received a BA in Photography from Columbia College Chicago and studied at the School of the Art Institute of Chicago [S1][S3].
- Early photographic series such as `Seeing in the Dark` and `The New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club` are central to his photographic position [S2][S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: Black identity, cultural memory, double consciousness, Afrocentric objects, art history, class, masculinity, anxiety, and the construction of public and private selves [S1][S2][S4].
- Technique / formal traits: Johnson's early photographs used historic processes such as Van Dyke brown printing and staged portraiture. Later photographic works include black-and-white fictional portraits of members of the imagined `New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club` [S2][S4][S5].
- In `Seeing in the Dark`, Johnson photographed Black men experiencing homelessness in Chicago using a 19th-century process, linking contemporary subjects to the history of photographic portraiture and racial representation [S5][S6].
- In `The New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club`, he constructed a fictional secret society of Black intellectuals, using period styling, doubled figures, formal poses, and titles that reference Black history and theory [S4][S7].
- Why this method was chosen: Johnson uses photographic historicism and staged fiction to critique the burden of representation. Rather than offering a transparent documentary image of Black life, he shows identity as mediated by history, archives, class aspiration, performance, and coded materials [S1][S4][S7].
- Historical context: Johnson's early career is tied to The Studio Museum in Harlem's `Freestyle` (2001), a key exhibition associated with the term "post-black." This positioned his photography within debates about race, identity, and contemporary art after the 1990s [S5][S6].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Johnson's photography intersects with post-black art, conceptual portraiture, and materially symbolic installation. His later move into sculpture and painting does not erase photography; rather, the photographic question of identity construction remains foundational [S1][S2].
- Representative works / examples: `Jonathan's Hands` (1998), `Michael` (1998), `Calvin` (1999), `Self Portrait as the Professor of Astronomy, Miscegenation and Critical Theory...` (2008), and `The New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club (Emmett)` (2008) [S2][S4][S5][S6][S7].
- Historical significance: Johnson is important for photography history because he used older photographic processes and staged portraiture to complicate the politics of Black visibility at the start of the 21st century, before expanding into a wider multidisciplinary practice [S1][S5].
- Critical meaning: the work challenges the idea that racial identity can be stabilized by documentary evidence. Johnson's portraits are deliberate performances that show how Black subjectivity is formed through history, fiction, and visual codes [S4][S7].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- MCA Chicago's `Message to Our Folks` was Johnson's first major solo museum exhibition and surveyed the first fourteen years of his career [S2].
- MCA describes his method as incorporating everyday materials from childhood in a process Johnson calls "hijacking the domestic" [S2].
- The National Gallery of Art holds `The New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club (Emmett)`, confirming the photographic series' museum collection presence [S7].
- Bowdoin College's object text for `Larry` connects `Seeing in the Dark` to Shelley Rice's reading of the photographs as social arenas and notes their exhibition history in `Freestyle` and MCA contexts [S5].
- TIME's 2025 article quotes Thelma Golden on the Van Dyke prints as both old and new, emphasizing Johnson's knowledge of photographic and portrait histories [S6].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- MCA Chicago quotes Johnson describing his use of materials as "hijacking the domestic" [S2].
- TBA21 includes Johnson's explanation of Du Bois's double consciousness as a conceptual frame for `The New Negro Escapist Social and Athletic Club` [S4].
