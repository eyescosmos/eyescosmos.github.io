# Taiji Matsue

## 1. Basic facts
- Taiji Matsue, born 1963 in Tokyo, is a Japanese photographer known for detailed, frontal, topographical images of land, cities, cemeteries, and built environments [S1][S2].
- He studied geography at the University of Tokyo before becoming a photographer, a background that directly informs his method [S3].
- Major series include `TRANSIT`, `CC`, `JP`, `LIM`, `jp0205`, and `gazetteer` [S1][S3][S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: land surface, geography, mapping, urban texture, anti-picturesque description, scale, flatness, and the optical possibilities specific to photography [S3][S4][S5].
- Technique / formal traits: Matsue often photographs from elevated or frontal viewpoints, suppresses horizon lines and sky, and uses front lighting to reduce cast shadows. The result is a dense, flat field of detail that resists scenic depth [S5].
- Tokyo Art Beat's text on `makietaCC` states that rules such as excluding the horizon and sky and using front lighting create a flatness that questions the essence of photography [S5].
- Why this method was chosen: Matsue's geographical training and interest in land as surface lead him away from expressive landscape and toward systematic visual description. He treats the photograph as a field of information rather than a window onto a scenic view [S3][S5].
- Historical context: his practice belongs to Japanese contemporary photography after the 1980s but connects to older topographic, mapping, and survey traditions. It also resonates with postwar debates about whether landscape photography can be descriptive without becoming sentimental or nationalistic [S3][S4].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Matsue can be read alongside topographic photography and conceptual landscape practices. However, his work is less about industrial critique than about the photographic registration of surfaces, density, and scale [S3][S5].
- Representative works / examples: `Kumamoto, Japan` (1985, from `TRANSIT`), `IRAN 1998 #17`, `UTAH 1995 #7`, `PYRENEES 1992 #12`, `JP-22 08` (2005), `MCT 17551` (2012), `JP-34 02` (2015), and `DENMARK 17939` (2012) [S1][S2][S5][S6].
- Historical significance: Matsue is important because he makes the landscape photograph operate like a map, a data field, and a visual archive while preserving the physical specificity of place [S3][S4][S5].
- Critical meaning: the work's neutrality is not absence of meaning; it is a method for refusing dramatic viewpoint and instead letting accumulated detail, surface, and repetition reveal how place is structured [S3][S5].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Hiroshima City Museum of Contemporary Art's `gazetteer` was Matsue's first retrospective and assembled his major series, confirming the breadth of his practice [S3].
- SFMOMA holds multiple works by Matsue, including `IRAN 1998 #17`, `JP-22 08`, `UTAH 1995 #7`, and `PYRENEES 1992 #12` [S1].
- MFAH holds `Kumamoto, Japan` from `TRANSIT`, documenting early work in museum collection form [S6].
- Tokyo Art Beat's text on `makietaCC` gives a precise account of Matsue's formal rules and their effect on photographic flatness [S5].
- Museum of Contemporary Art Tokyo has presented Matsue in collection displays, connecting his work to the museum's account of contemporary Japanese art [S4].
