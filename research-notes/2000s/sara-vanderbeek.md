# Sara VanDerBeek

## 1. Basic facts
- Sara VanDerBeek, born 1976, is an American artist whose practice joins photography, sculpture, found images, studio constructions, and installations [S1][S2].
- MoMA included her in `New Photography 2009`, and the Whitney Museum presented her first solo museum exhibition, `Sara VanDerBeek: To Think of Time`, in 2010 [S1][S3].
- Her work often uses temporary sculptural forms and photographed fragments to think through memory, scale, time, place, and art-historical reference [S1][S2].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: memory, time, place, scale, sculpture, architectural fragments, art-historical images, collective history, and the photograph as the trace of temporary construction [S1][S2][S3].
- Technique / formal traits: VanDerBeek gathers images from art history books, archives, magazines, newspapers, and other sources; she incorporates them into temporary sculptures or studio arrangements, photographs them, and then dismantles the source structure [S1][S4].
- The photograph is therefore both image and residue. It is often the only remaining evidence of a sculptural object made for the camera [S1].
- In `To Think of Time`, the Whitney notes that she continued the practice while also working outside the studio, meditating on time as both personal and collective [S1].
- Why this method was chosen: VanDerBeek uses photography to alter the reading of scale, time, and place. A photograph can disorient the viewer but also reveal barely perceived aspects of lived experience [S2].
- Historical context: her work participates in post-2000 photography's expansion into sculpture and installation, where artists investigated what it meant to make a photograph in the 21st century rather than simply take one [S3][S4].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: MoMA's `New Photography 2009` grouped VanDerBeek with Walead Beshty, Daniel Gordon, Leslie Hewitt, Carter Mull, and Sterling Ruby, artists who pushed photography into hybrid, constructed, or self-reflexive forms [S3][S4].
- Representative works / examples: `A Composition for Detroit` (2009), `Blue Caryatid at Dusk` (2010), `Foundation, Reynes Street` (2010), `Baltimore Departure` (2010), `Treme` (2010), and `Roman Woman I` (2013) [S1][S2][S5].
- Historical significance: VanDerBeek is important because she places photography between sculpture, archive, and memory, making the photograph a site where personal and collective history are assembled and then destabilized [S1][S3][S5].
- Critical meaning: the work refuses a single documentary viewpoint. Its meaning comes from the way found images, temporary objects, urban sites, and art-historical fragments are recomposed into images that are both evidence and abstraction [S1][S4].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- The Whitney describes her quiet semi-abstract photographs as based on sculptural forms and emphasizes the temporary status of the objects made to be photographed [S1].
- Aperture's interview with Brian Sholis records VanDerBeek's own interest in how photography affects scale, time, and place [S2].
- MoMA's `New Photography 2009` press release describes `A Composition for Detroit` as responding to postindustrial cities and incorporating references from the 1967 Detroit riots to Walker Evans [S4].
- The New Yorker review of `New Photography 2009` describes VanDerBeek and related artists as photographing ephemeral arrangements filled with personal, political, and art-historical references [S3].
- MFAH's entry for `Baltimore Departure` describes her photographs as blurring boundaries between time, space, and light [S5].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Aperture's interview is a strong first-person source; VanDerBeek explains her interest in scale, time, place, direct observation, and the photograph as an object [S2].

## 5. Quote candidates
- "scale, time, and place" [S2].
- "specific but not fixed" [S2].
- "only remaining evidence" [S1].
