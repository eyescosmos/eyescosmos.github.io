# Jessica Eaton

## 1. Basic facts
- Jessica Eaton, born 1977 in Regina, Canada, is a Canadian photographer known for experimental color photography and camera-based abstraction [S1][S2].
- She studied photography at Emily Carr University and is represented by galleries including Higher Pictures Generation, M+B, and Bradley Ertaskiran [S1][S3].
- Her best-known early series is `Cubes for Albers and LeWitt` / `cfaal`, which uses additive color separation and multiple exposures [S2][S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: color as a photographic event, perception, the limits of the visible, abstraction produced in camera, and photography's relation to modernist color theory [S2][S4].
- Technique / formal traits: Eaton photographs simple studio forms with a large-format camera, colored filters, and repeated exposures. In `cfaal`, gray cubes become saturated color structures through additive RGB exposure rather than post-production colorization [S2][S4].
- Her process is highly notated and iterative. A Higher Pictures interview describes her notebooks and pre-planned exposure systems; she follows notes, tests possibilities, and returns to the same perspective in order to focus on generating color [S3].
- Why this method was chosen: Eaton is interested in how photography can make visible phenomena outside ordinary perception, such as ultraviolet patterns on flowers or colors generated by light rather than pigment [S4][S5].
- Historical context: the work responds to a moment when digital manipulation had become common, but Eaton uses analog exposure systems to show that photography's capacity for artificial color and abstraction existed inside the camera's material apparatus [S2][S3].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: her work belongs to the 2010s reconsideration of abstract photography and can be read alongside Josef Albers, Sol LeWitt, Paul Outerbridge, and contemporary artists in `Photography is Magic!`-type discussions of experimental photographic construction [S2][S4][S6].
- Representative works / examples: `cfaal (mb RGB) 18` (2010), `Cubes for Albers and LeWitt`, `cfaal 2220` (2018), `Iterations (III)`, and later floral works made with exacting color processes [S2][S3][S6].
- Historical significance: Eaton's work is important because it repositions abstract photography away from digital effect and toward the camera as an optical laboratory, where light, exposure, film, and perception generate images that cannot be seen directly [S2][S4].
- Critical meaning: her photographs undermine the assumption that the camera is bound to the visible world; they show photographic truth as an interpretation of radiation, perception, and technical procedure [S4].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Eaton won the 2012 Hyeres International Festival of Fashion and Photography prize for her cube works, noted in TIME's coverage [S2].
- Canadian Art described her images as works that ask viewers to think about what they see, connecting technical procedure with perception and philosophical questions about photography [S4].
- Higher Pictures presented `Iterations (III)` as a cumulative body from more than three years of intensive analog exploration [S3].
- The New Yorker contrasted her earlier cube works with later floral still lifes, emphasizing how repeated color processes subvert photographic realism [S6].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- In a FOAM interview republished by Higher Pictures, Eaton says she wanted to use the tricolor process to defy the idea that photography is literally bound to the visible world [S4].
- In the same interview she defines photography broadly as radiation-sensitive material recording radiation-based information [S4].
