# Daisuke Yokota

## 1. Basic facts
- Daisuke Yokota, born 1983 in Saitama Prefecture, Japan, is a Japanese photographer known for experimental, process-based work using analogue and digital manipulation, zines, photobooks, installation, and performance [S1][S2].
- He graduated from the Nippon Photography Institute, received an Honorable Mention at Canon New Cosmos of Photography in 2008, won the 2nd 1_WALL Photography Competition in 2010, received the Foam Paul Huf Award in 2016, and received the 45th Kimura Ihei Photography Award in 2019 [S1][S2].
- Major projects and publications include `Back Yard`, `Site`, `Site / Cloud`, `VERTIGO`, `Tarachine`, `Matter`, `MATTER / BURN OUT`, `Matter / Vomit`, and `Photographs` [S1][S2][S3][S4].

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: the materiality of photography, memory, time, deterioration, repetition, chemical accident, the boundary between image and object, and the overload of images in contemporary information society [S1][S2][S5].
- Technique / formal traits: Yokota repeatedly photographs, scans, prints, re-photographs, damages, develops, burns, waxes, folds, and reprints images. His methods include hot-water development, direct development of unexposed film, darkroom experiments, digital manipulation, cameraless abstraction, installation-scale paper rolls, and photobook sequencing [S1][S2][S5][S6].
- Why this method was chosen: Canon's interview shows Yokota moving from trial-and-error zine production toward making the photographic process itself the subject. He became interested in the substance of film, flawed material, uneven copying, and the tactile act of making images by hand [S1].
- Historical context: Yokota's practice emerged in the 2010s, when digital image circulation, zine culture, photobook festivals, and international photography fairs created new routes for Japanese photographers outside older magazine and gallery systems. He sent images directly to overseas contacts, participated in Unseen, and developed an international career before his recognition fully consolidated in Japan [S1][S2].
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: his work is connected to post-internet photography, contemporary abstraction, and Japanese experimental photobook culture, while also recalling earlier avant-garde cameraless photography. The Met links his cameraless abstractions to Man Ray and Laszlo Moholy-Nagy; Le Bal places his concern with photographic materiality near Daido Moriyama's `Bye Bye Photography` [S5][S7].
- Representative works / examples: `Back Yard` and `Site` zines (2011); `Site / Cloud` exhibition at Foam (2014); `Cloud` (2014); `Matter` / `MATTER / BURN OUT` (2015-16); `Matter / Vomit` at Aichi Triennale 2016; `Inversion` in Tate Modern's `Shape of Light` (2018); `Photographs` at rin art association (2021) [S1][S2][S3][S4][S6][S8].
- Historical significance: Yokota is important because he treats photography less as a stable image of the world than as a chain of physical, chemical, digital, and performative transformations. His work keeps the photographic object materially stubborn at a moment when images are often treated as frictionless data [S2][S5][S6].
- Critical meaning: the repeated damage and recycling of images do not merely produce abstraction. They test whether memory, time, and photographic evidence survive when the image is subjected to excessive reproduction, deterioration, and reprocessing [S2][S4][S6].

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Foam selected Yokota for the 2016 Paul Huf Award and presented `Matter`, describing the exhibition as room-filling installations that gave viewers different experiences of photography in each space [S2].
- The Met's collection text for `Untitled` (2015) describes Yokota's practice as performance-like and cameraless, emphasizing abstract harmonies, static, delay, reverb, and echo [S5].
- Phillips' catalogue essay for `Cloud` reads the work through time and memory, stressing the combination of digital manipulation, re-photographing, repetition, and hot-water film development [S6].
- Collector Daily situates Yokota's early visibility in self-published zines such as `Back Yard` and `Site`, and describes his photobook practice as central to his experimental process [S8].
- Japanese critic Kotaro Iizawa, reviewing `MATTER /`, argued that Yokota's work differs from lighter post-digital image manipulation because of its excessive attention to bodily and material limits in photographic images [S4].

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Canon's New Cosmos interview is the strongest primary source. Yokota explains how failed or damaged film led him toward `MATTER`, how zine production shaped his attention to copying and touch, and why he sees materials and process as part of photography [S1].
- Foam also quotes Yokota on reversing the usual hierarchy between documented image and film materiality [S2].

## 5. Quote candidates
- "I became interested in the actual substance of film" [S1].
- "the production process included, is what makes photography interesting" [S1].
- "I wanted to focus on the emulsion" [S5].
