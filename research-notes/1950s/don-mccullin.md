# Don McCullin

## 1. Basic facts
- British photojournalist, born in 1935.[S1][S2]
- Known for war photography, social documentary in Britain, and later landscape work.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: war, deprivation, urban poverty, aftermath, moral burden, and later the search for stillness after prolonged exposure to violence.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: Cyprus, Biafra, Vietnam, Cambodia, and Northern Ireland photographs define his mid-century and late-century reputation; later landscapes mark a self-conscious turn away from war.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: stark black-and-white, close proximity to danger, emotionally forceful framing, and a documentary rhetoric that aims at immediate ethical confrontation.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: McCullin has repeatedly framed photography as something felt rather than merely seen. His method arises from a belief that photographic witness must carry emotional and moral force.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical context: his work belongs to the maturation of postwar photojournalism and to the period when war photography became central to global visual consciousness through magazines, newspapers, and later museum display.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: McCullin stands alongside Jones Griffiths and Seymour within the history of political photojournalism, but his work is marked by an especially stark moral tone and later self-critique.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: he is significant because he helped define the ethical intensity of late twentieth-century war photography, while also making visible the psychic cost of sustained witness.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the photographs matter because they force the problem of guilt, witness, and spectatorship into the center of photographic history. McCullin’s later turn to landscape is part of that meaning, not an escape from it.[S1][S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: his images circulated through magazines, newspapers, books, major museum shows, and later interviews and films that made his own reflections part of the reception history.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Reception has long treated McCullin as one of the central war photographers of the postwar era, but more recent writing also stresses his discomfort with artistic canonization and his own critique of the medium’s complicity.[S1][S2][S3]
- Tate, ICP, and interview materials together show that the afterlife of the work depends as much on ethical debate as on visual admiration.[S1][S2][S3]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- `Photography for me is not looking, it's feeling. If you can't feel what you're looking at, then you're never going to get others to feel anything when they look at your pictures.` [S3]
