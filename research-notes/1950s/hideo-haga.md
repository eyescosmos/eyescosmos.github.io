# Hideo Haga

## 1. Basic facts
- Japanese photographer, born in 1921 and died in 2022.[S1][S2]
- Known especially for photographing Japanese festivals, folk customs, and ethnographic-cultural subjects.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: festivals, ritual, folk culture, pregnancy, the body in public life, and the survival of local custom within modern Japan.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: *Pregnant Japanese Woman Hurrying on Her Way* (1952), selected by Edward Steichen for *The Family of Man*, is a key example because it combines everyday public space with a culturally unusual and historically charged view of the body.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: close documentary observation, attention to gesture and costume, and a method that treats ritual or custom not as static folklore but as active lived performance.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Haga appears to have used photography to preserve and interpret cultural practices that might otherwise vanish or become simplified. The camera becomes a tool of both record and cultural transmission.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work belongs to the decades in which rapid modernization in Japan intensified concern about folk custom, regional identity, and the representation of everyday life in transition.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Haga is distinct from metropolitan experimental modernists; he is closer to ethnographic and folkloric documentary, though his images also entered international exhibition contexts such as Steichen’s MoMA project.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: Haga is significant because he links Japanese documentary photography to the preservation and visualization of folk culture and ritual life.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: his photographs matter because they broaden photo history beyond urban modernism and show how ritual, gender, and local custom were made photographically visible in the postwar period.[S1][S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: his photographs circulated in books, documentaries, museum and gallery exhibitions, and international exhibition frameworks such as *The Family of Man*.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Reception in Japan emphasizes Haga as a major visual recorder of folk culture, while international reception most often returns to the Steichen selection as an index of his broader photographic power.[S1][S2]
- The strongest critical writing available here is about role and archive rather than sustained formal criticism of individual images.[S1][S2]
