# Larry Clark

## 1. Basic facts
- American photographer and filmmaker, born in 1943.[S1][S2]
- Known for photographing youth, drugs, sex, violence, and self-destructive social worlds from inside rather than from outside.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: adolescence, addiction, masculinity, desire, boredom, violence, and the collapse of social distance between subject and photographer.[S1][S2]
- Representative work example: *Tulsa* (1971) is the foundational body of work, using Clark’s own circle in Oklahoma to build a brutally intimate account of youth and drug culture.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: close-in black-and-white photography, diaristic proximity, rough sequencing, and an inside position that refuses neutral documentary distance.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Clark photographed the milieu he belonged to. The work depends on implication and participation rather than detached observation, which gives it its power and ethical difficulty.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: *Tulsa* belongs to the aftermath of 1960s social upheaval and to the broader redefinition of documentary photography in the United States, where subjectivity, confession, and subculture were becoming central.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Clark stands near Nan Goldin and later intimate documentary practice, but *Tulsa* precedes many of those developments and remains a key antecedent for autobiographical and transgressive documentary work.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Clark is significant because he helped make self-implicated documentary and youth subculture central subjects in late twentieth-century photography.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: the work matters because it collapses the distinction between witness and participant. That collapse remains one of the defining ethical and formal problems of contemporary documentary photography.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: *Tulsa* circulated as a photobook and later through exhibitions and museum collections, with the book format remaining crucial to its reception.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Reception has consistently been divided between recognition of *Tulsa* as a landmark photobook and concern over its intimacy with violence and exploitation.[S1][S2]
- Institutional reception now treats Clark as a key figure in American photography, but the controversy around the work remains part of its meaning rather than a side issue.[S1][S2]
