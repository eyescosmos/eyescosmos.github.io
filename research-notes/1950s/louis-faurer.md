# Louis Faurer

## 1. Basic facts
- American photographer, born in 1916 and died in 2001.[S1][S2]
- Associated with New York street photography in the 1940s and 1950s, especially Times Square and 14th Street.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: urban loneliness, marginal city life, neon culture, shop windows, fashion, and the psychological tension of postwar New York.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: *New York, N.Y.* (1946) and the Broadway / Times Square images are central because they show Faurer’s ability to turn crowds, signage, reflections, and chance pauses into compressed psychological scenes.[S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: blurred light, shallow depth of field, reflections, high-contrast night photography, and framing that often isolates figures inside crowded public space.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Faurer used the camera less to stabilize the city than to show how unstable and emotionally dense it felt. His method suits postwar New York as a place of anonymity, seduction, and estrangement.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work belongs to postwar American photography, when the city, fashion imagery, and European-inflected modernist ideas were reshaping street photography in the United States.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Faurer is often discussed in relation to Robert Frank, Lisette Model, and later New York street photography, but his work is marked by a more fragile, nocturnal, psychologically claustrophobic tone.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: he is significant because he helped shift American street photography away from descriptive clarity toward a more subjective, unstable urban modernism.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: the photographs matter because they make the public city feel inward, fractured, and vulnerable. That mood-based modernism influenced later photographers such as Arbus and Friedlander.[S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: his photographs circulated in fashion and editorial contexts, later museum collections and retrospectives, and now serve as an important link between postwar street photography and more personal urban image-making.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Later reception repeatedly frames Faurer as a major but once underrecognized New York photographer, especially important for the emotional intensity of his street work.[S1][S2][S3]
- Museum writing also stresses his influence on later artists and his connection to fashion/editorial culture, which helped keep the work mobile between art and publication.[S1][S2]
