# Seydou Keïta

## 1. Basic facts
- Malian photographer, born in 1921 or 1923 and died in 2001.[S1][S2]
- Known for studio portraiture in Bamako in the 1940s and 1950s.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: self-fashioning, social aspiration, domestic and familial representation, clothing, gesture, and the emergence of modern identity in Bamako before and around independence.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: the Bamako studio portraits—single sitters, couples, families, and children staged with textiles, motorbikes, radios, or European-style dress—are the core of his historical importance.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: frontal and carefully posed studio compositions, patterned cloth backdrops, soft but exact control of light, and a strong sensitivity to clothing and props as social language.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Keïta worked within a portrait studio economy in which sitters actively collaborated in how they wished to appear. His photographs therefore emerged from a reciprocal structure of performance and representation rather than from unilateral authorship.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work belongs to late-colonial and early-independence West Africa, when photography became a key means for urban subjects to present themselves within changing social and political conditions.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Keïta is often discussed alongside Malick Sidibé and later African studio photography, but his mid-century Bamako portraits hold a foundational place in the international recognition of African photographic modernity.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: he is significant because he made the portrait studio central to world photographic history, not peripheral to it. His work shows that modernity in photography has multiple centers.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the portraits matter because they record agency. Sitters do not merely appear before the camera; they use it to stage identity, belonging, and ambition.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: initially made in a commercial studio setting, the photographs later entered museum, book, and exhibition circuits that helped reframe African studio portraiture as a major modern tradition.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Institutional reception now consistently treats Keïta as a central figure in twentieth-century photography, particularly in relation to African modernity and portraiture.[S1][S2][S3]
- Later criticism emphasizes the collaborative and social nature of the studio portrait, rather than treating the work only through a Western auteur model.[S1][S2]
