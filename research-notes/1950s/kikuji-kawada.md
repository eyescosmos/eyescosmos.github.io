# Kikuji Kawada

## 1. Basic facts
- Japanese photographer, born in 1933.[S1][S2]
- One of the central photographers of postwar Japan, associated with the group *VIVO* and with the landmark photobook *Chizu / The Map*.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: postwar memory, Hiroshima, war traces, imperial residue, the American occupation, and the psychic afterimage of Japan’s defeat and rapid economic regrowth.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: *Chizu / The Map* (1959–1965) is the defining work, especially photographs of the Atomic Bomb Dome, military traces, and stained or damaged surfaces that turn historical ruin into a fragmented visual map.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: dark tonality, fragment, symbolic sequencing, close attention to surfaces and ruins, and the photobook as a primary form rather than a neutral container.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Kawada used photography to address history indirectly—through scars, fragments, and visual residues—rather than through straightforward reportage. This allows the work to register trauma as atmosphere and structure.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work emerged from postwar Japan, where memory of defeat, occupation, and modernization was politically unsettled. The photobook became a major site for thinking through these tensions.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Kawada belongs to the postwar Japanese generation around *VIVO* and is often discussed alongside Tomatsu and Hosoe, though his work is especially concerned with historical residue and symbolic sequencing.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Kawada is significant because *The Map* helped redefine the photobook and because his work made postwar historical memory central to Japanese photographic modernism.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the photographs matter because they refuse direct explanation. History appears as damaged matter, emblem, and sequence, which makes the viewer reconstruct meaning rather than receive it as report.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: his work circulated through books, exhibitions, and major international museum contexts; the photobook and later display reconstructions are central to its reception.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- *The Map* is now one of the most discussed photobooks in postwar Japanese photography and remains central to international exhibitions of photography and conflict.[S1][S2][S3]
- Recent reception stresses both its historical specificity and its formal radicalism, especially the way book structure and image sequence carry meaning.[S1][S2]
