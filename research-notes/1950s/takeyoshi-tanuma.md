# Takeyoshi Tanuma

## 1. Basic facts
- Japanese photographer, born in 1929 and died in 2022.[S1][S2]
- Known for postwar Tokyo photographs, portraits, UNICEF-related work, and a long institutional role in Japanese photography.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: postwar Tokyo, children, urban recovery, everyday vitality, international humanitarian subjects, and the dignity of ordinary life.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: photographs from *Tokyo on My Mind* / *Picturing My Tokyo 1948–1964*—including images such as the rooftop SKD dancers and Tsukuda Island kamishibai scenes—are central to understanding his early contribution.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: clear, human-centered composition, direct but affectionate observation, and a documentary method that turns postwar city life into a shared civic memory rather than private nostalgia.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Tanuma’s work suggests a durable commitment to people as the measure of history. Even when photographing large urban change, he returns to children, faces, neighborhoods, and shared public spaces.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his early photography belongs to postwar Japanese recovery, when Tokyo was being physically rebuilt and socially reimagined. Later international work expands that documentary concern into a broader humanitarian horizon.[S1][S2][S4]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: he belongs to postwar Japanese documentary photography, but his tone is often more humane and civic than antagonistic. That difference helps distinguish him from more overtly critical or formally radical peers.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Tanuma is significant because he created one of the most sustained photographic records of postwar Tokyo while also helping raise photography’s public and institutional status in Japan.[S1][S2][S4]
- Critical meaning: his photographs matter because they show recovery not as abstract history but as lived collective experience. Their emotional clarity is part of their documentary force.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: his work circulated through exhibitions, books, UNICEF-related contexts, museum retrospectives, and major Japanese photographic institutions.[S1][S2][S4]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Museum and exhibition materials consistently present Tanuma as a major witness to postwar Tokyo and as a figure of unusual institutional importance within Japanese photography.[S1][S2][S4]
- Later reception also stresses the longevity of his practice and the range of subjects, from local neighborhoods to international children’s welfare.[S1][S2]
