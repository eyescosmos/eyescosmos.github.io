# OHIO

## 1. Basic facts
- OHIO is a German photography magazine and art project launched in 1995, edited in its early phase by Jörg Paul Janka, Uschi Huber, Stefan Schneider, and Hans-Peter Feldmann.[S1][S2]
- It has been described as an "art project in magazine form" and as a mobile exhibition space for photographic imagery rather than a standard authored photography journal.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: vernacular image culture, reproducibility, editorial sequencing, and the circulation of everyday photographs outside the single-print art object.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: the project works through magazine editing, juxtaposition, inserts, and serial publication. Photography is treated as a reproducible and distributable medium rather than a singular autonomous artwork.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative examples: the available descriptions of early issues emphasize the mixture of black-and-white and color imagery, inserts, and the magazine’s status as an art project. This matters because OHIO uses the periodical itself as a curatorial device.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: the available descriptions suggest that OHIO foregrounded the everyday and reproducible character of photographs, deliberately moving away from the aura of the single framed print. The project format allowed editors to treat publication, editing, and circulation as central artistic operations.[S2][S3]
- Historical context: OHIO belongs to a 1990s moment in which photography, artists’ magazines, and editorial practices became central to post-conceptual image culture. It should be understood alongside other projects that relocated photographic meaning from the wall to the page, archive, and circulation network.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: the project is closely related to the broader turn toward found, vernacular, and editorial photography that would also become important for later projects such as Useful Photography. Its emphasis is not on authorial mastery but on the social life of images.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: OHIO is useful in a photography-history context because it shows how the magazine could function as both artwork and exhibition structure. It helps mark a shift from photography as singular object toward photography as edited sequence and distribution format.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: final prose should emphasize editorial form. OHIO matters because it reframes photography through publishing, selection, and circulation rather than through the unique image alone.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- The surviving references are bibliographic and project-based rather than deeply interpretive, so final writing should remain measured.[S1][S2][S3]
- Even so, the recurring characterization is consistent: OHIO is treated as a photomagazine and art project that foregrounds the reproductive and everyday character of photography.[S1][S2][S3]
