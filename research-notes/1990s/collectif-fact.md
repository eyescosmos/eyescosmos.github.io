# Collectif_fact

## 1. Basic facts
- Swiss artist collective active since the early 2000s, formed by Annelore Schneider and Claude Piguet after an earlier three-person formation that also included Swann Thommen.[S1][S2][S3]
- Their projects are framed through urban space, mediated vision, fiction, and the circulation of images rather than through single-authored documentary photography.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: urban fictions, image circulation, simulation, architecture, speculative futures, and the unstable relation between observed reality and constructed visual worlds.[S1][S2][S3][S4]
- Technique / formal traits: photography is combined with 3D scans, architectural modeling software, archival images, film dialogue, sound excerpts, and moving-image montage. Their work often appears simple and familiar at first, but institutional texts repeatedly stress its polysemy and ambiguity.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative examples: *Worldmaking*, *White Shadow*, and *No Picture, No Glory or the Triumph of Apophenia* are especially useful because they show the group turning urban imagery and cinematic conventions into speculative environments. These projects clarify that the photograph is not treated as a neutral view, but as raw material for recontextualization and narrative drift.[S1][S2][S4]
- Why this method was chosen: the DDA Genève interview and profile sharpen the reason. Collectif_fact uses narration, editing, and cinematic codes to speculate on "near futures" and to test how visual habits condition perception. Their images do not simply represent the world; they probe the formats through which a world becomes thinkable.[S3][S4]
- Historical context: collectif_fact belongs to a post-1990 field in which photography intersects with digital modeling, databases, and installation practice. Their work emerges at a moment when the authority of the photographic image is being rethought through networked and speculative media environments.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: the work can be placed near post-conceptual photography and media-based urban research, but also beside artists using fiction and cinematic grammar to critique contemporary visual culture.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: the collective matters because it shows how photography after the documentary turn could be folded into synthetic and architectural image production without disappearing as a critical reference point. Their practice expands photography into an ecology of moving, edited, and digitally reconstructed images.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: their work treats photography less as a final object than as one layer within mediated seeing. The sharper formulation now available is that collectif_fact plays with the viewer’s desire to be drawn in and deceived, thereby producing critical awareness of the habits that shape perception.[S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Reception is still largely exhibition-based, but it is more precise than before. Geneva materials consistently frame the collective around advertising, urban signage, Hollywood cinema, and the speculative use of digital image tools; DDA Genève adds that their projects explore the massive production of images and digital contents and their alienating, ecological, and social impacts.[S1][S2][S3]
- Final prose should avoid attributing a single manifesto to the group, but it can now safely emphasize fiction, speculation, and perceptual conditioning rather than generic "media hybridity" alone.[S1][S2][S3]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- In interview, the collective describes its moving-image practice with the phrase `our videos stage a near future`, a useful clue to how speculative temporality functions across the work.[S4]
