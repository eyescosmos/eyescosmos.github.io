# G.R.A.M.

## 1. Basic facts
- Austrian artist group founded in Graz in 1987 by Martin Behr, Günther Holler-Schuster, Armin Ranner, and Ronald Walter; later continuing as a duo of Behr and Holler-Schuster.[S1][S2][S3]
- Their collective practice spans photography, performance, installation, print, and video while repeatedly addressing how public images are produced, circulated, remembered, and re-enacted.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: media spectacle, re-enactment, collective memory, public visibility, image consumption, and the politics of attention.[S1][S2][S3][S4]
- Technique / formal traits: photography appears alongside video, performative actions, newspaper editions, installation formats, and press-image appropriation. Rather than presenting single autonomous photographs, G.R.A.M. repeatedly rebuilds or re-stages already-circulated images and exposes their constructedness.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative examples: the series *Nach Motiven von…*, *Global Player* (2006), *Paparazzi* (1997), *Wiener Blut* (2001/02), *Hohes Haus* (2011), and *NixCheckCashing* (2012) are especially useful because they show the range of the method: iconic press images, Viennese Actionism, financial-crisis imagery, and architectural-political scenes are all turned into re-enactments that keep the original image at a critical distance.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: the Camera Austria texts make this clearer than before. G.R.A.M. does not simply quote media images; it reconstructs the image politics through which history and memory are produced. Re-enactment is chosen because it makes the original visible as already ideological and already staged.[S2][S4]
- Historical context: G.R.A.M. emerged in a late-20th-century European context in which mass-media spectacle, financial imagery, and postwar memory had become central artistic subjects. Their work belongs to the expansion of conceptual photography into media criticism, especially in relation to the reuse of iconic press imagery.[S1][S2][S4]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: their practice connects with post-conceptual and media-critical art more than with classical straight photography. Photography remains important, but as a social instrument tied to circulation, repetition, and collective memory rather than as a self-sufficient medium.[S1][S2][S4]
- Historical significance: G.R.A.M. is useful in photographic history because it helps show how post-1980 image practice shifted from making individual pictures toward analyzing and re-performing image systems. Their work marks a strong example of photography moving into reenactment and critique.[S1][S2][S4]
- Critical meaning: final writing can now be sharper. G.R.A.M.’s work oscillates between reenactment, appropriation, reinterpretation, and critical exposure; its importance lies in showing that public images already organize reality before artists touch them.[S2][S4]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Institutional materials consistently present G.R.A.M. as a collective concerned with media structures, public visuality, and reenactment rather than private expression.[S1][S2][S4]
- Camera Austria’s writing is especially useful because it gives a concrete critical frame: the group’s projects are described as reconstructing image politics that determine memory and history, and as holding the original at arm’s length in order to expose its construction.[S2][S4]
- Final prose should therefore emphasize reenactment and image politics, not just generalized media critique.[S1][S2][S4]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Martin Behr has said of one installation that the group did not want it understood as arguing in one political direction or another, even while acknowledging its close context with current events; this is useful for describing the work’s refusal of one-dimensional messaging.[S5]
