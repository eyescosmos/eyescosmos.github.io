# Multiplicity

## 1. Basic facts
- Multiplicity is an interdisciplinary research collective formed in Italy in the 1990s and associated with Stefano Boeri and collaborators across architecture, urbanism, mapping, image research, and documentary practice.[S1][S2]
- Their work combines photography, video, cartography, research dossiers, and exhibition installations to study contemporary space and mobility.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: migration, borders, infrastructure, urban transformation, territorial control, and the politics of mobility.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: photography is rarely autonomous in their work; instead, it appears alongside mapping, field research, testimony, cartography, and moving images. Images are treated as evidence within broader investigative systems.[S1][S2]
- Representative examples: *Solid Sea* is the key project because it shows how the collective used photographs, maps, testimonies, and installation formats to rethink the Mediterranean not as empty geography but as a politically structured field of passage and death.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: the collective’s approach suggests that no single photograph can adequately describe contemporary territorial systems. Their method therefore multiplies viewpoints and evidentiary forms to make invisible structures legible.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: Multiplicity belongs to a post-1990 context in which globalization, migration, and networked territory became central themes in art and urban research. Their work also reflects the expansion of documentary practice beyond single-medium authorship.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: they can be placed near documentary research collectives and post-conceptual practices that treat photography as one investigative instrument among many. Their work is particularly important for showing how photography enters urbanism and forensic-style inquiry.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Multiplicity matters because it demonstrates a major shift in photographic culture: from the single authored image toward distributed documentary research structured by archives, diagrams, routes, and testimonies.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: final writing should stress that photography here is not abandoned, but embedded in a larger epistemic structure. The image becomes one component in the production of political knowledge.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Institutional writing consistently presents Multiplicity as a hybrid collective working between art, research, and political spatial analysis.[S1][S2]
- Final prose should avoid describing the group as a conventional photography collective; the stronger claim is that they expand what documentary image practice can be.[S1][S2]
