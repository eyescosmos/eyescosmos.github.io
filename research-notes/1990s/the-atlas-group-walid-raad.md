# The Atlas Group / Walid Raad

## 1. Basic facts
- The Atlas Group (1989–2004) is a long-term project by Walid Raad, born in Lebanon in 1967, presented as a fictional or quasi-fictional archive of the Lebanese wars.[S1][S2][S3]
- It combines photography, video, notebooks, documents, and performance to question the truth-claims of historical and photographic evidence.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: war, memory, archive, fiction, evidence, historical violence, and the instability of documentary truth.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: fabricated and appropriated archival documents; photographic series presented through invented authors, dossiers, and file structures; artist lectures and walkthroughs that turn interpretation into part of the work; and a sustained blurring of factual, emotional, and aesthetic truth.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work examples: *The Bachar Tapes*, *My neck is thinner than a hair: Engines*, *Section 139: The Atlas Group*, and *We are a fair people. We never speak well of one another* are central because they show how photography is embedded within larger archival and narrative systems rather than presented as transparent evidence.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Raad’s own statements are explicit that he begins from facts, but insists there are different kinds of facts—historical, emotional, and aesthetic. The Atlas Group’s invented archive form is therefore not an escape from history; it is a method for showing how conflict exceeds ordinary documentary certainty.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical context: the project emerges after the Lebanese Civil War and during a broader late twentieth-century reconsideration of archives, testimony, and postwar memory. It is historically important because it enters photography’s documentary tradition at the moment when that tradition is being deeply questioned.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: the work belongs to conceptual art, archival art, and post-documentary photography, but is distinct in the way it uses fictional personae and unstable documents to probe the limits of public memory.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: The Atlas Group matters because it transformed the archive itself into a photographic problem. Instead of asking viewers whether a single image is true, it asks how photographic truth is organized, narrated, and institutionalized.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the project is crucial because it demonstrates that documentary doubt can itself be historically responsible. Its fiction is not decorative; it is a way of registering the fractures, absences, and competing narratives produced by war.[S1][S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: MoMA’s survey, audio materials, and archive-related writing show that The Atlas Group circulated as a foundational reference point for contemporary art’s rethinking of photography, video, and archival authority.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- MoMA’s survey framing is especially useful because it positions Raad not simply as a Lebanese artist of memory, but as a cross-disciplinary artist whose work is decisive for understanding photography, conflict, and the archive in contemporary art.[S1][S2]
- Critical reception consistently turns on the productive confusion between fiction and evidence. Final website copy should stress that this confusion is the method, not an accidental side effect.[S1][S2][S3]
- The project’s place in photographic history is strongest when framed through post-documentary practice and the political afterlife of archives.[S1][S2][S3]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Raad says, `I always proceed from facts. But I think that there are different kinds of facts. Some facts tend to be historical. Some facts are emotional. And some facts are aesthetic.` This is a primary statement of method and should be preserved.[S2]

### 8.1 本文改訂で強める論点
アトラス・グループ／ワリド・ラード（The Atlas Group / Walid Raad）は、1990–2000年代の写真史で archive art, fictional documentary, postwar memory と結びつけて整理したい作家である。活動地域・制度的文脈としては Lebanon / United States を軸に、美術館、写真集、映像・インスタレーション、ギャラリー、批評誌、オンライン・アーカイブを通じて作品が流通した点を確認する必要がある。略歴だけではなく、Lebanese civil war, archive, testimony, historical fiction を、fictional archive, lecture performance, photography and documents によってどのように写真の問題へ変えたのかを中心に読むと、本文に厚みが出る。

表現解説では、archive, historical doubt, documentary fiction を中心語にしつつ、作品を単なる代表作紹介で終わらせないことが重要である。1990–2000年代は、写真が紙のプリントだけでなく、映像、インスタレーション、出版、アーカイブ、ウェブ、ギャラリー制度、グローバル・ビエンナーレの中で再編された時期である。比較対象としては Joachim Koester, Sophie Calle, Ana Torfs を優先し、浅い類似ではなく、写真観、方法、対象の捉え方、受容史の差異を整理する。

評価・批評では、同時代の展覧会、写真集、ギャラリーでの流通、後年の美術館・研究機関による再評価を分けて扱う。架空アーカイブを通じて、レバノン内戦の記憶と写真・文書の証拠性を問い直したプロジェクト。 この一文を起点に、本文では「何を撮ったか」よりも「その方法が何を変えたか」を中心に組み立てる。
