# Manuel Álvarez Bravo

## 1. Basic facts
- Mexican photographer, born in 1902 and died in 2002.[S1][S2]
- Central figure in twentieth-century Mexican photography, with a career spanning street observation, still life, bodies, ruins, and modernist allegory.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: everyday Mexico, the body, death, ritual, labor, irony, and the unstable boundary between vernacular reality and poetic metaphor.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: works such as *Los Agachados* and *The Good Reputation Sleeping* show his ability to make an ordinary or culturally specific scene carry wider symbolic force without losing local texture.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: controlled black-and-white tonality, quiet frontal or lateral compositions, understated surreal charge, and a refusal of spectacle in favor of visual condensation.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Álvarez Bravo developed a photography that could register modern Mexican life without reducing it to folklore or propaganda. His method allowed factual scenes to remain open to ambiguity and metaphor.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: he worked in the decades after the Mexican Revolution, when debates over national identity, modernism, indigenous pasts, and international avant-garde exchange shaped visual culture. His meeting with Tina Modotti and contact with Weston and Breton are part of this context.[S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: his work intersects with Surrealism and international modernism, but it remains distinct in how it roots symbolic charge in everyday Mexican life rather than in overt dream imagery.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: Álvarez Bravo is significant because he built a modern photographic language that was at once local and internationally legible, helping to define the place of Latin American photography within twentieth-century history.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: his photographs matter because they neither document nor symbolize in a simple way. They keep both registers active, which is why later criticism returns to them as models of photographic ambiguity.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: his photographs circulated through magazines, books, exhibitions, and later major international retrospectives; museum reception has been central to the consolidation of his reputation.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Museum writing frequently presents Álvarez Bravo as one of the inventors of a modern photographic vocabulary in Mexico, and as a figure whose work outlived narrower nationalist or surrealist labels.[S1][S2]
- Later reception also emphasizes the durability of his images in museum and photobook contexts, where their formal restraint and symbolic density continue to read strongly.[S1][S2][S3]
