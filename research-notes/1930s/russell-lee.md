# Russell Lee

## 1. Basic facts
- American photographer, born in 1903 and died in 1986.[S1][S2]
- One of the major documentary photographers of the Farm Security Administration.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: labor, migration, agriculture, everyday American life, and the social fabric of rural and small-town communities.[S1][S2]
- Representative work example: bodies of work on coal, agricultural labor, and small-town America, together with his vast FSA production, show the scale and steadiness of Lee’s documentary attention.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: patient observational sequencing, clear descriptive framing, and a tendency to build social understanding through accumulation rather than through a few spectacular images.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Lee’s work suggests a commitment to comprehensive social description. He often looked not for the single emblematic frame but for a sustained record of how communities actually lived and worked.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work belongs squarely to the New Deal documentary project, where photographers were sent to build a visual archive of American life during the Depression and wartime transition.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: among the FSA photographers, Lee is often distinguished by the scale and consistency of his production and by the social density of his bodies of work.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Lee is significant because he expanded documentary photography beyond iconic social types into a more comprehensive record of community life. The size of his archive is itself historically important.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: his work demonstrates that documentary force can arise through breadth and continuity, not only through a few canonical masterpieces.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: the FSA archive, later books, National Archives projects, and Library of Congress research have all been central to Lee’s reception and continued use.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Recent Library of Congress framing emphasizes that Lee made more FSA prints than any other photographer, which has strengthened later recognition of his centrality.[S1]
- Reception increasingly values the archive as a whole rather than only a small set of famous images, which is important for final writing.[S1][S2]
