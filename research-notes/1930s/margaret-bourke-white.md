# Margaret Bourke-White

## 1. Basic facts
- American photographer, born in 1904 and died in 1971.[S1][S2]
- Worked in industry, magazine photojournalism, war coverage, and social documentary; one of the best-known photographers of *Fortune* and *Life*.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: industrial modernity, labor, the American South during the Depression, war, and the relation between monumental public image and social crisis.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: industrial photographs from the early *Fortune* years, Depression-era works such as *At the Time of the Louisville Flood* (1937), and later war images define the range of her method.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: dramatic composition, assertive vantage points, strong contrast, and a magazine-aware sense of image power. Even documentary scenes are often built around emblematic visual structures.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Bourke-White worked where photography had to communicate quickly and publicly. Her style is therefore bound to publication, circulation, and the need to create images that could stand for industrial power, social breakdown, or geopolitical conflict.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: her rise belongs to the expansion of illustrated magazines and the transformation of photography into a mass medium with political and cultural force in the 1930s and 1940s.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: she overlaps with documentary and photojournalism rather than with museum modernism alone, but her work also entered art institutions early, making her a key bridge figure.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: Bourke-White is significant because she helped define the public authority of twentieth-century photojournalism. Her images show how industrial photography, documentary, and magazine circulation converged in modern media culture.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: the work often operates through visual emblem. In images like the Louisville flood photograph, social contradiction is condensed into a sharply readable public icon rather than a neutral report.[S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: her photographs were used in *Fortune*, *Life*, war reportage, and later museum exhibitions and collections; publication context is inseparable from their meaning.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Bourke-White’s reception has often centered on her role as a pioneering woman in photojournalism, but institutional writing also emphasizes her formal command and her role in shaping magazine photography itself.[S1][S2]
- Later reception frequently returns to the tension in her work between industrial spectacle and social critique, especially in Depression and wartime imagery.[S2][S3]
