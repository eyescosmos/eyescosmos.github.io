# George Rodger

## 1. Basic facts
- British photographer, born in 1908 and died in 1995.[S1][S2]
- War correspondent, later African documentary photographer, and founding member of Magnum Photos.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: war, liberation, aftermath, Africa, landscape, and the ethical problem of witnessing violence.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: his photographs of Bergen-Belsen in 1945 and later work in Africa, especially among the Nuba, define the two poles of his career: catastrophic witness and long-form ethnographic/documentary attention.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: clarity, mobility, and an ability to render large historical events through spatially intelligible compositions. In the African work, the emphasis often shifts toward sustained environmental and bodily presence.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Rodger’s career suggests a strong investment in direct witness, but also a postwar refusal of endless war imagery after Bergen-Belsen. That ethical rupture shaped his move toward Africa.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work emerges from World War II photojournalism and from the postwar formation of Magnum as a more independent platform for documentary practice.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Rodger belongs with Magnum founders such as Capa and Seymour, but his postwar trajectory is distinct in the degree to which war trauma redirected his practice.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Rodger is significant because he links the most brutal phase of wartime witnessing to a later documentary engagement with Africa that shaped mid-century visual knowledge outside Europe.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the work matters because it exposes the ethical burden of witness. In Rodger’s case, the history of the image cannot be separated from the photographer’s difficulty in continuing after atrocity.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: his images circulated in magazines, Magnum contexts, later photobooks, and museum displays; both wartime and African work have remained central to his reception.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Magnum’s own framing emphasizes Rodger’s foundational role and the way Bergen-Belsen altered the rest of his career.[S1][S2]
- Later reception has often focused on his African work, but recent writing tends to hold war and postwar Africa together as one ethical and historical trajectory.[S1][S2][S3]
