# Robert Doisneau

## 1. Basic facts
- French photographer, born in 1912 and died in 1994.[S1][S2]
- Best known for Paris street photography, but also active in editorial, industrial, and portrait work.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: everyday Paris, workers, lovers, children, street humor, and the fragile choreography of ordinary urban life.[S1][S2]
- Representative work example: the body of work later summarized through images such as *Le Baiser de l’Hôtel de Ville* stands for Doisneau’s ability to turn fleeting city life into a memorable, accessible image-world, while earlier labor and street pictures show the stronger documentary foundation beneath the charm.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: small-camera mobility, close human observation, understated wit, and compositions that make public life feel lightly theatrical without severing it from reality.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Doisneau’s photography depends on proximity, patience, and an openness to everyday human interaction. He aimed less at shock or abstraction than at making ordinary urban life newly legible.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his career emerges from interwar and postwar French photography, where illustrated magazines, labor imagery, and later “humanist photography” all shaped reception. He became one of the most legible public faces of that tendency.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Doisneau is closely tied to French humanist photography, alongside figures such as Willy Ronis, but his work is often more playful and narratively concise.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: he matters because he helped define an enduring image of postwar Paris and because he demonstrated how accessible street photography could still carry formal intelligence and historical resonance.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: his work is sometimes reduced to nostalgia, but that reading is incomplete. The labor pictures and urban observations show that his method was grounded in the structures of everyday life, not just in sentiment.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: Doisneau’s photographs circulated in books, magazines, and exhibitions, and later entered museum and library contexts that fixed his role within the canon of twentieth-century French photography.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Reception has often centered on his status as a poet of Parisian everyday life, but later criticism also stresses the work’s documentary and labor dimensions.[S1][S2]
- His reputation has been stabilized by photobooks and exhibitions, which often foreground both the charm and the constructedness of the Paris myth his work helped shape.[S1][S2]
