# Ansel Adams

## 1. Basic facts
- American photographer, born in 1902 and died in 1984.[S1][S2]
- Best known for large-format landscape photography of the American West and for technical and pedagogical influence on modern photography.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: the American landscape, wilderness, ecological value, light, and the translation of natural form into highly controlled photographic print structure.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work example: *Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico* (1941) and the Yosemite photographs are the clearest examples of Adams’s effort to render landscape as a field of tonal drama and spiritual intensity.[S1][S2]
- Technique / formal traits: large-format clarity, deep detail, extensive darkroom control, and the Zone System as a way of previsualizing tonal relationships from exposure to print.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Adams sought maximum control over photographic translation, not simply faithful recording. His method was chosen to make the final print carry a precise emotional and tonal structure.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work belongs to the consolidation of straight photography in the United States and to the environmental and conservationist understanding of the western landscape in the twentieth century.[S1][S2]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Adams is inseparable from Group f/64 and from the defense of sharply focused, straight photography against pictorial softness. His position also overlaps with modernist ideas of medium specificity.[S1][S2]
- Historical significance: Adams is historically significant not only because of famous landscapes, but because he helped define photographic craft, pedagogy, and the cultural authority of fine-art photography in the United States.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the photographs matter because they make technical control itself historically meaningful. In Adams, craft is not neutral; it is the vehicle through which landscape becomes monumental and ethically charged.[S1][S2]
- Where and how the work was used: his work circulated in books, exhibitions, museum collections, conservation discourse, and educational writing on technique. The pedagogical afterlife is central to his historical role.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Adams has long occupied a canonical place in American photography, but recent reception also emphasizes his role in environmental imagery and institutional legitimation rather than only in technical mastery.[S1][S2]
- Museum presentations often return to the relation between his prints and his teaching, showing how tightly linked image-making and photographic education were in his career.[S1][S2][S3]
