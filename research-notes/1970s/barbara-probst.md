# Barbara Probst

## 1. Basic facts
- German artist and photographer, born in 1964.[S1][S2]
- Known for photographic works that use several cameras triggered simultaneously to produce multiple views of the same instant.[S1][S2][S3]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: simultaneity, perspective, the instability of a single viewpoint, the constructedness of photographic truth, and the relation between event, staging, and spectatorship.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work examples: the *Exposures* series and the earlier *My Museum* project are central because they show how Probst transforms a single scene into a set of competing but simultaneous descriptions.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: multiple cameras positioned around one scene and triggered at the same instant, often combining color and black-and-white, frontal and oblique views, or apparently documentary and visibly staged framings.[S1][S2][S3]
- Why this method was chosen: Probst’s method directly tests the authority of the single photographic viewpoint. By multiplying perspectives at one moment, she demonstrates that no one photograph can claim total descriptive privilege.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical context: her work emerges from the late twentieth-century conversation between photography and conceptual art, especially the period when artists increasingly examined the photograph as a constructed object rather than a transparent record.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Probst belongs to conceptual photography, but her work also draws on cinema, performance, and staged image-making. She differs from single-frame conceptualism by insisting on simultaneity as a structural test of photographic truth.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: she is significant because she made photographic multiplicity itself into the subject of art. Her work helps define a contemporary understanding of photography in which perspective, authorship, and event are always unstable.[S1][S2][S3]
- Critical meaning: the work matters because it literalizes the relativity of viewpoint. It does not simply claim that all images are partial; it makes that partiality visible and comparable within the work’s own structure.[S1][S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: Probst’s work circulated through museum exhibitions, artist books, catalog essays, and interviews, with institutions repeatedly presenting the simultaneous multi-image format as the key to her contribution to contemporary photography.[S1][S2][S3]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Whitney’s artist page gives a concise but reliable account of the simultaneous multi-camera system, which remains the central axis of Probst’s reception.[S1]
- MoMA’s New Photography press release and later criticism present her work as a critical intervention into how photography constructs event and meaning.[S2][S3]
- Reception therefore consistently treats Probst not as a documentarian of scenes but as an analyst of photographic seeing itself.[S1][S2][S3]
