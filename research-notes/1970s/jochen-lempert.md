# Jochen Lempert

## 1. Basic facts
- German photographer, born in 1958 in Moers and based in Hamburg; originally trained as a biologist.[S1][S2]
- Turned to photography in the late 1980s after earlier work in experimental film, and became known for black-and-white studies of plants, animals, traces, and perceptual phenomena.[S1][S2]

## 2. Expression / method
- Main themes: animal and plant life, observation, perception, classification, ecological proximity, and the unstable border between scientific looking and poetic attention.[S1][S2][S3]
- Representative work examples: bodies of work shown in *Field Guide* and *Honeyguides and Milk Teeth*, as well as series such as *A Field (8 parts)*, are central because they show how Lempert builds meaning across groups of modest-sized photographs rather than through singular iconic prints.[S1][S2][S3]
- Technique / formal traits: hand-printed black-and-white photographs, 35mm camera work, conventional and experimental darkroom processes, unmounted and often unframed installation, and a sequencing method in which images operate relationally. The photographs are small, direct, and materially modest, but conceptually dense.[S1][S2]
- Why this method was chosen: Lempert’s biological training matters because it gives his work scientific precision without turning it into illustration. His photographs test how observation changes once classification, analogy, and visual comparison enter the field of art.[S1][S2]
- Historical context: his work emerges after conceptual photography and after the Düsseldorf school had already expanded photography’s institutional presence, yet Lempert moves away from monumentality and spectacle toward small-scale attentiveness. In that sense, his practice belongs to a late twentieth-century reconsideration of what photographic knowledge can be.[S1][S2][S3]
- Relation to contemporaries or movements: Lempert’s work intersects with conceptual photography, artist-books, and ecological image practices, but it is distinct in the way it joins art and science without fully settling into either documentary natural history or formal conceptualism.[S1][S2][S3]
- Historical significance: he is significant because he redefined nature photography within contemporary art, replacing spectacular wildlife imagery with a photography of minute relations, traces, and taxonomic uncertainty.[S1][S2]
- Critical meaning: the photographs matter because they challenge habits of looking. They ask viewers to notice how naming, grouping, and scale condition what counts as knowledge, while preserving photography’s capacity for surprise and contingency.[S1][S2][S3]
- Where and how the work was used: Lempert’s work circulated through museum surveys, artist conversations, photobooks, and site-responsive installations. Cincinnati’s *Field Guide* and MACS’s *Honeyguides and Milk Teeth* are especially helpful because they frame the work as an interplay of scientific rigor, poetic sensibility, and exhibition sequencing.[S1][S2]

## 3. Criticism and reception
- Museum framing consistently emphasizes Lempert’s unusual position between biology and art, often treating that double background as essential to his historical significance.[S1][S2]
- Cincinnati Art Museum’s presentation is especially useful because it stresses both the scientific rigor and poetic sensibility of the work, while also noting that his images question the very systems of classification through which viewers usually organize visual information.[S1]
- MACS extends this reception by foregrounding ecology, minutiae, and seriality; it presents the work as a practice in which no single image is sufficient and where meaning often emerges through relation and grouping.[S2]

## 4. Artist statement / artist's own words
- Lempert is quoted in the MACS press material: `several pictures are needed for something to emerge. And sometimes, one is enough.` The remark is useful because it explains his commitment to seriality without treating sequence as a rigid rule.[S2]
