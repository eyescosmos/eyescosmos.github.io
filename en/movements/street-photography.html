What Is Street Photography?

Street Photography refers to a photographic practice centered on capturing fleeting human moments in public space. This page places the term within the history of photography through representative photographers, nearby movements, and the periods in which the approach became visible.

Rather than stopping at a dictionary definition, this page places the term beside photographers, periods, and adjacent approaches so that its historical use can be read in context.

Where It Sits in the History of Photography

On this site, photographers connected to Street Photography appear mainly from 1910–1920s to 1970–1980s, often overlapping with Documentary, American Photography, Photojournalism, and FSA Photography. The category is most useful when read alongside the people and contexts that made it visible.

Key Photographers

A practical way into this page is to start with photographers whose work makes the category visible in concrete terms.

André Kertész was a Hungarian-born photographer whose work helped define the lyric, modern possibilities of the medium between the wars.

William Klein brought a painter's aggression to photography, using grain, blur, wide-angle distortion, and invasive closeness as a language of urban energy rather than as technical flaws.

Garry Winogrand made street photography feel fast, unstable, and improvisational.

Lee Friedlander made what he called the social landscape, a photography not of untouched nature but of roads, storefronts, signs, windows, cars, and the built environment of modern America.

Related Movements

Street Photography often overlaps with Documentary, American Photography, Photojournalism, and FSA Photography. These links do not mean the categories are identical; they show where subject matter, method, institutions, or historical context begin to touch.
