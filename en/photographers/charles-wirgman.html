Charles Wirgmanチャールズ・ウィルグマン

Country: GB
Era: 1839 — 1860s
1832–1891

Essay

Charles Wirgman was born in England in 1832. He arrived in Japan in 1861 as a correspondent for the Illustrated London News and remained in Yokohama until his death in 1891*1. In 1862 he founded Japan Punch, a satirical magazine that ran until 1887 — the first Western-style humor publication in Japan. The magazine introduced speech balloons and Western caricature conventions to Japanese readers; the Japanese genre of “ponchi-e” (caricature illustration) derives its name directly from “Punch” and is considered one of the roots of modern Japanese manga culture*1. Wirgman was not himself a photographer. His connection to photographic history lies in his partnership with Felice Beato. Their formal arrangement, “Beato & Wirgman, Artists and Photographers,” operated from 1864 to 1867. The MIT Visualizing Cultures project — a peer-reviewed scholarly resource used by major museums — records that “Wirgman initially painted the photographs with watercolor” and may have been the first to propose hand-coloring albumen prints as a commercial technique in Japan*2. Wirgman was also a significant teacher of Western art. His students included Takahashi Yuichi, Goseda Yoshimatsu, and Kobayashi Kiyochika — key figures in the early development of Western-style painting (yoga) in Japan*1. The Metropolitan Museum holds a Japan Punch lithograph (1873)*3. Wirgman married Ozawa Kane, a Japanese woman, in 1863; their son Ichiro was born in 1864, and Wirgman is buried at the Yokohama Foreign General Cemetery. In the history of Japanese visual culture, Wirgman is understood less as a photographer than as a cultural intermediary who connected Beato's photographs to Western markets, introduced Western pictorial conventions to Japanese artists, and helped shape the visual vocabulary of the Meiji era*2.
