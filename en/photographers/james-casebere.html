    James Casebere

    ジェームズ・ケースベア
    Country1990s Period1990–2000s ChannelIssues in photo history · Conceptual
    Abstract

    James Casebere builds models of prisons, schools, tunnels, suburban houses, and historic buildings from paper, cardboard, and plaster, then lights and photographs them. He selects walls, windows, corridors, and stairways from architectural sources, films, and memory, constructing only what the camera can see. The model lets him control light, viewpoint, and depth in ways unavailable on location, revealing how spaces produce surveillance, isolation, ownership, and security. His method is examined through the Pictures Generation, his interest in Gaston Bachelard, Martin Heidegger, and modern architecture, and his differences from Thomas Demand.

    What this photographer changed

    Constructed photography in the 1970s often used performed figures and preexisting images to question narratives produced by film and advertising. Casebere removed addresses and people from models of prisons and houses, then controlled light, circulation, and scale. He thereby photographed architecture as a system that organizes human behavior, emotion, and social relations. The model was necessary because it allowed him to isolate that system.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    As a child, Casebere studied the blueprints his father brought home from his work on school buildings and drew imaginary structures of his own. In a later interview, he recalled wanting to become an architect and learning to imagine space from plans*15. At the Minneapolis College of Art and Design, he studied with Siah Armajani, whose work addressed public space and politics. At the California Institute of the Arts, he entered John Baldessari’s cross-disciplinary environment of photography, language, and found imagery*16*12*10.

    In the conceptual art of the 1970s, many earthworks and performances disappeared quickly, leaving photography as the only form through which the work survived. Casebere reversed that relationship. He built temporary models so that only the photograph would remain, then dismantled them after shooting. The work came to encompass the process by which a three-dimensional construction became a single image through light and perspective*14.

    A model offers freedoms unavailable when photographing an existing site. Casebere can alter the height of a wall, the width of a window, or the length of a corridor; combine elements from different buildings; and construct only the single direction seen by the camera. During the shoot he can move the lights, add water, and adjust the slope of the floor. These controls allow him to remove information that explains a building’s use and intensify the pressure, isolation, or anticipation a space produces in the body. His decision to leave seams visible reflects the influence of Constructivism and Jean-Luc Godard: the image invites immersion while also revealing how it was made*1. The model was necessary because it let him rebuild walls, windows, and circulation routes that are fixed in an actual site, then compare their effects within the same photographic frame.

    The Pictures Generation refers to a group of artists working in the United States from the late 1970s through the 1980s who reconsidered authorship and the influence of mass culture through images borrowed from film, advertising, magazines, and art history, as well as photographs that performed familiar visual types. Cindy Sherman, Richard Prince, and Sherrie Levine examined how recognizable figures and product images shape ideas about desire, gender, and success*17. Casebere directed the same question toward interior settings. Corridors, cells, and suburban houses repeated across film and advertising provide visual cues through which viewers recognize “a school,” “a prison,” or “a safe home.” A photograph of an existing building ties those cues to a particular address and current use. By removing site-specific information in a model and retaining only walls, windows, lighting, and circulation, he could examine the shared images through which society understands space.

    § 02 / 04 How the Method Developed

    Why Build Models?

    Architectural models are usually made to verify a building’s overall structure or its appearance after construction. Casebere’s models are built for a single camera view and include only the necessary portions of walls, floors, windows, and stairs. Rooms and roofs outside the frame are omitted, while seams in the paper or plaster, slight distortions, and ambiguities of scale remain. He selects the minimum elements required to recognize a space, leaving room for the viewer’s memory to complete it*1.

    Seen through the camera, paper walls acquire the mass of concrete, and a corridor only a few feet long can resemble a vast facility surrounding the body. Casebere adjusts wall heights, window positions, and angles of light to test whether the same space suggests security, isolation, or surveillance. Because the model lets him reconstruct before exposure what remains fixed in an actual building, it becomes an experimental device for comparing the conditions through which architecture affects feeling. By dismantling it after the shoot and retaining only the photograph as the finished work, he also makes the transformation of construction and light into a plausible space part of the image*7.

    Prisons and Surveillance

    In Casebere’s prisons, cells, watchtowers, and underground passages from the 1980s onward, doors, windows, and repeated corridors dominate the frame with no people present. Open sight lines carry the guard’s gaze, while narrow apertures restrict movement and contact with the outside. Casebere studied the prison as an institution of punishment, reform, and isolation that took shape during the Enlightenment *2. A review in Frieze likewise discusses his models of prisons, schools, and hospitals as works that make architectural authority visible*8.

    This work coincided with a period of rapidly expanding incarceration in the United States. The number of people held in state and federal prisons reached approximately 330,000 in 1980 and continued to rise*18. The empty cells do not directly depict how incarceration falls unevenly across race and class. They instead expose the design of a system whose structures can be reproduced indefinitely. The abstraction created by removing people also risks diminishing the specific experiences of those confined within them, and that limitation remains central to the critical reception of the work.

    Domestic Memory and Modern Architecture

    Through Siah Armajani’s classes, Casebere read Edward T. Hall, Gaston Bachelard, and Martin Heidegger*14. Bachelard treated attics, cellars, corners, and drawers as concrete structures that direct childhood memory and imagination. The darkness of a cellar, the brightness of an attic, the confinement of a corner, and the opening or closing of a door give domestic experience its distinctions between high and low, inside and outside, safety and fear. Heidegger described dwelling as the activity through which people establish relations with places, things, and communities. Hall studied how interpersonal distance and the placement of walls alter behavior. From these ideas, Casebere understood architecture as both a repository for memory and an apparatus that shapes emotion and action.

    Casebere extended these ideas beyond the house to prisons, schools, hospitals, and public buildings. If a home can cultivate memory and security, a cell repeats isolation, a school corridor directs movement and orderly lines, and a hospital passage treats the body as an object of administration. In models stripped of furniture and people, the viewer’s own memories enter the space, then meet the institutional demands encoded in doors, windows, and walls. Architectural historian Anthony Vidler’s research on institutional buildings also became an important point of reference for the prison series*14. Modern architecture’s history of designing for light, circulation, hygiene, and efficiency led to his interest in how the same techniques classify education, treatment, correction, and habitation. Casebere approached domestic memory and institutional discipline through the same walls, windows, and corridors. Repeated cellars and passageways, sparse furniture, and emphatic doors and windows draw the viewer’s own domestic memories into the image while making them aware of the movement, stillness, or obedience the space demands from a body.

    Suburban Housing and Ownership

    In “Subdivision with Spotlight,” identical houses receive theatrical illumination in darkness, emphasizing the uniformity of a sales model. Windows, lawns, and roads suggest both “a safe home” and housing as an asset, while the lives of residents remain imagined*6. In “Landscape with Houses,” his interest in housing, credit, and debt after the financial crisis is joined to an artificial development composed like a Dutch landscape painting*19. Prisons and suburban houses serve very different purposes, yet both use parcels, sight lines, and routes of movement to prescribe a desired form of life.

    Light, Color, and Water

    In the early black-and-white works, stark contrasts create a tension associated with surveillance photography and film noir. From the 1990s onward, blue water, yellow light, and green or peach walls introduced anxiety, cleanliness, seduction, and relief alongside the stated uses of the spaces*7. In models filled with water, floors reflect like mirrors and architectural boundaries become unstable. Flooded prisons, cellars, and houses evoke suppressed histories and the climate crisis eroding present-day security from within*3.

    § 03 / 04 Key Works, Methods, and Medium

    Spiral Staircase

    “Spiral Staircase” shows only a spiral stair and a curving wall, without naming the place or depicting a person. The National Gallery of Art records it as a 2003 chromogenic print *5.The lucid geometry of the stair coexists with the uncertainty of an image that reveals neither entrance nor exit. The staircase suggests ascent or escape, yet both ends fall outside the frame, preventing the viewer from assigning it a definite use. The institutions Casebere addresses are first felt through this anticipation of movement, before they are identified through explanatory text.

    Monticello

    In works based on historic buildings, the generic quality of the model acquires a proper name. “Monticello” refers to Thomas Jefferson’s estate as a site where Enlightenment thought, republican government, and slavery coexisted. Its ordered symmetry carries both a national self-image and the history of exploited labor. Sean Kelly’s artist materials describe Casebere’s use of historic architecture as a vessel for political memory as an alternative to the heroic portrait*4.

    Differences from Demand: Source Images

    Casebere and Thomas Demand both construct models, exhibit photographs as the final works, and do not preserve the models after shooting. Demand rebuilds specific rooms, corridors, and gardens found in press or documentary images at full scale in paper and cardboard. By removing lettering, stains, and incidental detail, the model resembles a news image the viewer may remember while withholding the information that would identify the event.The Met describes his process of converting an existing news image into a full-scale paper model and destroying the model after it is photographed *20.

    Casebere seldom uses a single press photograph as a blueprint. For prisons, schools, and suburban houses, he combines architectural sources, films, and personal memories to make spaces that correspond to no particular address. Demand’s photographs raise the question of which event an image refers to; Casebere’s ask why a given wall or corridor feels like a prison, school, or home.

    Differences from Demand: The Models

    Demand’s paper models remove traces of use and lettering from their source images, hollowing out the memory of an event preserved by the media. Casebere adjusts light, depth, water, and eye level to give bodily force to spaces that have never existed. Demand’s work depends on the gap between a source image and the photograph of its model; Casebere’s depends on the gap between a small construction and a space that feels monumental. Together, they show how model photography can address both the provenance of media images and the effects architecture exerts on behavior*11.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism, Reception, and Photographic History

    Constructed Photography and Architectural Critique

    In the late 1970s, artists associated with the Pictures Generation quoted and reenacted figures and familiar images circulating through film, advertising, and magazines in order to examine the visual conventions through which society understands reality*17. Casebere directed the same inquiry toward architecture. Instead of photographing an existing building, he made models suggestive of prisons, schools, and houses. The model allowed him to set aside address, occupants, and event, and compare walls, windows, corridors, light, and circulation. Constructed photography could therefore examine spaces that organize bodily movement and feeling before a narrative begins, alongside staged scenes in which figures perform a story. Casebere’s significance within photographic history lies in using constructed photography to show architecture as a social mechanism that produces surveillance, isolation, ownership, and protection. Architecture becomes an active subject: a set of forms that directs behavior before any narrative unfolds*11.

    Beauty and the Limits of Abstraction

    Balanced compositions, deep shadows, and still surfaces of water give visual appeal even to prisons and post-disaster spaces. In “Credit, Faith & Trust,” the authority of financial, religious, and civic architecture is conveyed through sublime light and expansive interiors*9. That beauty communicates institutional force at the level of bodily sensation, while pushing the people subjected to it beyond the frame. The moment viewers feel drawn into an appealing space, they may also recognize that it organizes surveillance, isolation, credit, and ownership. This structure gives the work both its critical force and its limits.

    Why Only the Photograph Remains

    The model is dismantled after the shoot, leaving the photograph as the only finished form of a space that never existed. Through lighting and the lens, paper, plaster, paint, and water come to resemble stone, concrete, groundwater, and monumental architecture. Viewers begin by reading the photograph as a real building, then return to the model through a seam or an unnatural light. This movement makes clear that photography records spaces while also producing the shared visual ideas through which a place appears “prison-like” or “domestic”*13.
