Julia Margaret Cameron

A Victorian British photographer who picked up a camera at forty-eight and produced around 900 works over twelve years. A pioneer who used intentional soft focus to evoke the inner life of her subjects, establishing photography as a fine art. Virginia Woolf was her great-niece.

Biography

Julia Margaret Cameron (1815–1879) was born in Calcutta, the daughter of a senior British colonial official. She moved within a circle of Victorian intellectuals including the poet Tennyson and the scientist Sir John Herschel, and made her home at Freshwater on the Isle of Wight — a gathering place for the Victorian intelligentsia. In December 1863, when her daughter gave her a camera, Cameron was forty-eight. With the declared aim of "ennobling photography and giving it the character and use of high art," she produced around 900 works over the next twelve years. In 1875 she moved with her husband to Ceylon (present-day Sri Lanka), where she died in 1879*1.

Expression

While the photographic world of the day regarded sharp focus, even exposure, and blank backgrounds as marks of technical mastery, Cameron deliberately chose long exposures, shallow depth of field, and low light, and left visible chemical stains and fingerprints on her glass plates. What critics dismissed as "technical defects" was, for Cameron, a means of drawing out inner spirit rather than precise likeness — an expression of her belief that photography's truth lay not in precision but in evoking the interior*2. She converted a coal shed into a darkroom and a glass-walled conservatory into a studio, keeping her subjects in pose for long periods. Her large-format (approximately 38 × 28 cm) albumen silver prints rivalled paintings in scale, and Cameron presented photography as an autonomous work of art. Alongside portraits of Charles Darwin, Tennyson, and Thomas Carlyle, she produced elaborate staged tableaux drawn from Arthurian legend, the Bible, and Shakespeare — works that invited comparison with Pre-Raphaelite painting*3. Her first self-acknowledged success, Annie, My First Success (1864), is widely known, and The Passing of Arthur (1875), made to illustrate Tennyson's poems, stands as one of the earliest instances in which photography was treated as the equal of literature and painting*2.

Criticism and Reception

During her lifetime Cameron faced repeated criticism from the technically oriented critics centred on the Photographic Society of London. At the same time, Sir John Herschel, Tennyson, and Ruskin valued her work highly for its expression of spirituality — Herschel praised her early prints as "beautiful"*1. Virginia Woolf, her great-niece, published a book of her photographs in 1926, an event that triggered Cameron's twentieth-century rediscovery. Subsequently, her influence on Diane Arbus and Sally Mann has been noted, and she holds a significant place in photography history both as a forerunner of Pictorialism and in the context of establishing photography's status as a fine art*2.
