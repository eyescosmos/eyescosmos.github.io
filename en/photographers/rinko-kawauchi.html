    Rinko Kawauchi

    川内倫子 1972–
    CountryJapan MovementColor Photography Period2000 — 2010s ChannelPhotobook
    Abstract

    Rinko Kawauchi is a key figure in contemporary Japanese photography whose pale 6×6 color photographs and edited photobooks bring everyday details, light, and cycles of life and death into relation. Through Utatane, Hanabi, Illuminance, Ametsuchi, and M/E, this page traces how personal perception moves into a wider sense of the world through photobook culture, exhibitions, and international reception.

    What this photographer changed

    Kawauchi’s contribution to photographic history lies in a method: using the order of a photobook and the relations across a spread to turn small events into a perception of life and death, memory, and time. After Utatane and Hanabi in 2001, she used 6×6 color photographs, soft light, open space, and sequential editing to show photography as a medium whose meaning can shift through the act of turning pages, beyond the single decisive image or the private diary. This approach opened another route through which contemporary Japanese photography could be read internationally after Provoke’s rough urban imagery and the discourse of autobiographical “I-photography.”

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period

    Rinko Kawauchi was born in Shiga Prefecture in 1972. After graduating from Seian Women’s Junior College in 1993, she worked at a photography studio in Tokyo while continuing to make her own work*1. In 1997 she received the Grand Prix at the 9th Photography Hitotsubo Exhibition, and the following year she held the exhibition Utatane at Guardian Garden; these events show that the early method later developed through her photobooks had already begun to take shape*9. In 2001 she published Utatane, Hanabi, and Hanako, and in 2002 she received the 27th Kimura Ihei Award for Utatane and Hanabi*1. At this moment Kawauchi presented everyday fragments, pale light, family, nature, and small living things as images to be read through the flow of a photobook. Her work offered another register within discussions of postwar Japanese photography, which often foreground rough street records and political documentary. Fondation Cartier introduced her first European solo exhibition in 2005 by describing an artist who turns everyday details—a drop of water from a tap, a split watermelon, a spoonful of tapioca—into sequences of images charged with emotion and poetry*11.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    The Distance of the 6×6 Format and Overlooked Details

    What matters in Kawauchi’s photographs is the way they place nearby things at a distance that feels almost touchable yet slightly removed. The Shiga Museum of Art introduces her work as an expression that connects familiar events and small lives found along the roadside with Lake Biwa’s natural environment and the cycle of life and death, through “soft light” and “pale tones”*15. The museum also notes that even after she began making more digitally shot series, the 6×6 square format she had used since her debut has remained a form closely associated with her work*15. The square frame places insects, food, light, water, skin, fire, and clouds as events on the same plane, without forcing them into dramatic perspective. Kawauchi’s photographs can appear calm while holding unease because the distance of the frame keeps the subject from returning to the viewer as an everyday possession; it appears as one part of the world.

    How the Order of the Photobook Creates Meaning

    Kawauchi’s work is difficult to grasp through single representative images alone. The Minneapolis Institute of Art emphasizes that she is known for photobooks and for developing sequences of images page by page*10. In her books, babies, insects, fireworks, blood, light, food, and sky do not follow an explanatory story. They respond to one another through similar shapes, reversed colors, sudden breaks, and lingering echoes. As Fondation Cartier notes, Kawauchi creates unexpected links of form, mood, and atmosphere by arranging images, drawing the viewer toward the world’s “infinite surprise”*11. For that reason, the photobook is not a container for her works. The act of viewing in sequence becomes part of the work’s structure. A photograph does not fix meaning as a single piece of evidence; its meaning shifts as it touches the images before and after it.

    When overseas critics call Kawauchi’s photographs “poetic,” the word does not simply mean a soft or beautiful mood. TIME describes Illuminance as a photobook that creates a space recalling dreams, memory, and temporality, and preserves everyday scenes as poetry*25. The point is that the photograph does not function as an illustration of explanatory prose. Through spreads and page order, it generates movements of feeling and memory that are difficult to put into words. A review of Kawauchi’s Arnolfini exhibition by the Contemporary Art Society also likens her sequences to visual poetry and haiku, and explains that they should be read through the relations between neighboring images rather than as isolated photographs*29.

    Kawauchi’s editing of the spread took shape in the environment in which she learned photography in the 1990s and reorganized accumulated prints into books. In a long interview with IMA, she recalls studying graphic design, video, silkscreen, and other media at Seian Women’s Junior College, learning camera work and lighting at an advertising production company, and continuing to make work in the company darkroom*21. In the same interview, she speaks about the climate of open-call exhibitions such as New Cosmos of Photography, the Hitotsubo Exhibition, and Urbanart in the 1990s; the stimulus she received from the color photography of Kyoji Takahashi and Takashi Homma; and the quiet shutter sound and 120-film texture of the Rolleiflex she began using in 1996*21. The example of Takahashi and Homma related to the material quality of color photography, the visibility of authorship in magazines, and the use of color and tone to give the work a distinct character. That experience joined with the repeated labor of rearranging prints and testing the reactions of shape, color, and atmosphere that arise when two photographs meet on a spread. From that process came the editing sensibility specific to Kawauchi’s photobooks.

    This poetics cannot be explained by small subjects or by a generalized idea of Japanese sentiment. Masatake Shinohara’s essay in Aperture reads Kawauchi’s photographs not as records of everyday appearances, but as images that show a field of perception at the edge of daily life, one that cannot be absorbed into explanation*13. Chiara Masnatta’s essay calls Kawauchi’s work a “poetics of the everyday” and focuses on the process that moves across photobooks, exhibitions, video, and words, as well as on her creation of nonlinear time without page numbers*23. David Campany also writes that in Kawauchi’s photographs, ordinary things—an insect, a hand, reflected light, a face, a plant, a fleeting moment with her grandmother—are returned from familiar daily life as something slightly strange and new*31. A review in Frieze likewise notes that subjects that may seem intimate or sentimental, such as birth, butterflies, dew, and skin, are transformed into another way of seeing instead of falling into the banal*32.

    Light Loosens Boundaries Instead of Explaining the Subject

    In Kawauchi’s photographs, light does not simply illuminate the subject. It blurs contours and loosens the boundaries between things. Masatake Shinohara’s essay in Aperture reads the light of Illuminance as something that reflects, passes through, dematerializes, and sometimes makes things invisible, rather than as a force that presses objects into a single brightness*13. The beauty of Kawauchi’s photographs does not lie only in soft color or white light. More precisely, light places insects, skin, and drops of water in a state where they begin to lose the firmness of fixed objects. Seeing and disappearance occur at the same time, so her images stand before the viewer as perception not yet translated into words, rather than as daily life already explained.

    Life and Death, the Minute and the Cosmic at the Same Distance

    From Utatane onward, the brightness of life and the presence of death appear in Kawauchi’s work within the same time. Nippon.com notes that Utatane draws attention to contrasts between light and darkness, life and death, and points to the way images with the sensation of life, such as a spoonful of tapioca, resonate in the same book with images strongly marked by death*9. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum also explains that Kawauchi’s photographs cut out and join scenes from private daily life, creating images in which light and darkness, life and death, past and present intersect*12. The crucial point is that Kawauchi does not use “life and death” as a large symbolic theme. She places small fragments—eggs, insects, tables, fire, wounds, sky, and shadow—so that the viewer senses a cycle. Her photographs handle the moment when tiny things connect with the scale of the world, without closing into private sentiment.

    Family Photographs Beyond Private Record

    Kawauchi’s works about family can resemble personal albums, yet they do not settle into autobiographical record. Cui Cui includes family time photographed from 1992 to 2005, and Nippon.com writes that the scenes around her grandfather’s death bring a sense of irreversibility to a photobook that may first appear to be an ordinary family record*9. At KYOTOGRAPHIE 2024, Kawauchi presented Cui Cui and as it is in dialogue with Tokuko Ushioda, showing how two photographers from different generations approach family, home, everyday life, death, and birth through their respective light*16. Seen in this light, Kawauchi’s family photographs treat family as a place where time, death, birth, and memory intersect. They do not gather intimacy back into the artist’s interior life. A personal distance remains, yet the feelings produced there do not end with a single family.

    After 2011, Nature and Planetary Time

    After 2011, Kawauchi’s work continued to begin with small details of daily life while bringing disaster, nature, and long durations of the earth and living beings to the foreground. In the 2012 exhibition Illuminance, Ametsuchi, Seeing Shadow, the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum described Illuminance, Ametsuchi, and Seeing Shadow as works in which the artist’s senses and intuition move toward a larger world*12. In Ametsuchi, the field burning of Aso becomes a central subject, and Tokyo Opera City Art Gallery introduces the series as work made with a 4×5 camera and including Aso’s field burning, Jerusalem’s Western Wall, and acts of prayer and sacrifice*14. In M/E, glaciers in Iceland, snow scenes in Hokkaido, and nearby living things and family during the pandemic appear together; Tokyo Opera City situates the series as an inquiry into nature from both micro and macro perspectives*14. The collection text of the Kanazawa 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art also explains M/E as a series in which earth and body, macro nature and micro views such as light reflected in a bottle of water, are treated on equal terms*20. Kawauchi has consistently looked at small things since her early work. In later works, that smallness is understood as inseparable from the scale of the earth.

    § 03 / 04 Representative Works, Method, and Medium

    Utatane, Hanabi, and Hanako, 2001

    Published in 2001, Utatane, Hanabi, and Hanako strongly marked Kawauchi’s debut within photobook culture. The official publications list confirms that all three books were published in 2001*2. Utatane places fragments of daily life between dream and waking, while Hanabi draws the accumulation of light that disappears in an instant toward memory and bodily sensation. The rhythm of editing established in these early photobooks shows that Kawauchi’s photographs take on meaning through the order in which they are seen as much as through what they depict.

    Illuminance and Ametsuchi, 2011–2013

    Illuminance brought Kawauchi’s interest in light into an international field of publication and criticism. The official publication list confirms that it was published in 2011 by Aperture, Kehrer, Editions Xavier Barral, POSTCART, and FOIL*2. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum’s 2012 exhibition presented Illuminance, Ametsuchi, and Seeing Shadow as one exhibition structure, including not only still photographs but also video works*12. In Ametsuchi, her method expands from the intimate distance of the 6×6 format to the large-format 4×5 camera, and the earthly ritual of Aso’s field burning connects to scales of the earth and prayer. Here Kawauchi’s work extends beyond the lyricism of the everyday toward nature, disaster, sacrifice, and layered time.

    M/E and Installation

    M/E, a series begun in 2019, is important for understanding Kawauchi’s work as a spatial experience as well as a photobook-based one. Tokyo Opera City Art Gallery describes Rinko Kawauchi: M/E On this sphere Endlessly interlinking as her first solo exhibition in six years and the largest solo exhibition of her work ever held at a Japanese museum*14. The exhibition included photographs, video, and other media, and its installation, designed by architect Hideyuki Nakayama, shaped a bodily encounter with the works*14. In this exhibition, the photobook-like sequence of Kawauchi’s images is translated from the movement of pages into movement through space. M/E thus marks a stage in which images expand toward body, place, and the scale of the earth while retaining the sense of order and open space present from the early books.

    In the developments after Halo, Kawauchi’s photobooks have also been received as poetic media that order images, not merely as collections of works. Unseen Magazine describes Halo as a visual poem made through the sequencing of photographs shot intermittently in China, Japan, and the United Kingdom, and Kawauchi herself says that bookmaking plays an important role in her working process*33.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Position in Photographic History

    From Photobooks to Museums

    Kawauchi’s reception history can be read as a process in which an expression first developed through photobooks moved into international museum and gallery exhibitions. Fondation Cartier introduced her 2005 exhibition as her first solo exhibition in Europe, organized around AILA, Cui Cui, and the eyes, the ears,*11. ARGOS presented her 2010 exhibition as an installation including slides, photographs, and video, centered on family, nature, cycles of life, and brief moments of everyday experience*17. The Minneapolis Institute of Art positioned the 2014 exhibition New Pictures 9 as Kawauchi’s first solo museum exhibition in the United States, placing ordinary moments and photobook sequencing at the center of its presentation*10. The sense of continuity established in her photobooks was translated in museums into the rhythms of walls, slides, video, and exhibition space, broadening the reception of her work.

    Another Route in Contemporary Japanese Photography

    Kawauchi is one of the artists who offered another entry point for contemporary Japanese photography alongside the strong signs of city, body, and autobiographical “I-photography” associated with Daido Moriyama and Nobuyoshi Araki. Nippon.com notes that Kawauchi’s name is now almost always mentioned among Japanese photographers who draw the attention of photography professionals overseas, and states that her photographs have reached a level of value that is not reducible to “Japaneseness”*9. Contemporary Japanese photography also includes the cool observation of suburbs and urban spaces in the work of Takashi Homma. Kawauchi likewise deals with the everyday, yet her work is marked by the order of the photobook, pale color, and repeated spreads that lead the viewer to read light, time, and the cycle of life. Her position is defined by how she turned everyday fragments into a language of photography that could be shared internationally from a place outside urban record or private confession.

    International Reception and Current Readings

    Recent readings of Kawauchi place less emphasis on the lyricism of the early photobooks alone, and reconsider her work as an engagement with environment, body, family, and the long time of the earth and nature. Her official biography confirms that she received the ICP Infinity Award in the Art category in 2009 and the Outstanding Contribution to Photography Award at the Sony World Photography Awards in 2023*1. Masatake Shinohara’s essay in Aperture reads her photographs as connecting the edge of everyday life to the fragility of the world and to realms of nature that humans cannot control*13. Fotografiska Berlin introduces M/E as an exhibition that links the personal and the universal, and that considers being alive here and now through experiences including Iceland, Hokkaido, the pandemic, and becoming a parent*18. Kawauchi’s position is therefore not limited to the success of early-2000s photobook culture. Her work continues to show how photography can become a medium for thinking about environmental and planetary time through small acts of perception.

    Martin Parr’s early attention to Kawauchi also helps explain how her work entered an international field of reception. In 2004, British Journal of Photography introduced her as still little known outside Japan, while noting Parr’s view that the “refreshing and disturbing innocence” of her work pointed toward wider recognition*35. Her collaboration with Terri Weifenbach offers another concise example of how Kawauchi’s sequencing could operate as an exchange between images. The official publication information for Gift explains that the two photographers began exchanging images by email in 2011, and that the exchange was published as a book in 2014*36. centre Inc. describes the book as two volumes joined at the spine, with Weifenbach’s photographs in a left-opening book and Kawauchi’s in a right-opening book, so that alternating pages embody their photographic correspondence*37.

    The reason Kawauchi is read as “poetic” overseas lies less in the beauty of small everyday things than in the editorial method that connects those things to the time of the world and to cycles of life. In Aperture, Pico Iyer writes that Kawauchi finds a universe in the smallest moments and subjects, and hands that attentive way of looking to the viewer*30. This reading helps explain her international reception through form rather than through a vague idea of Japanese delicacy: her photographs slow down the act of seeing and turn fragments of daily life into another experience of time.

    The comparison with Luigi Ghirri clarifies this point. Aesthetica describes Ghirri’s photographs as a field of reflection on how the world is composed, framed, and reproduced, rather than as images offered for consumption*34. Kawauchi and Ghirri do not need to be joined by a direct line of influence. The comparison is useful because both artists use everyday fragments, quiet color, framing, and the rhythm of books or exhibitions to alter how the world appears. Kawauchi’s poetics lies in this change of temporal perception through the relations that emerge between photographs, rather than in sentimental beauty.
