    Ed van der Elsken

    エド・ファン・デル・エルスケン
    Country1950s Period1950–1960s ChannelEntry to photo history · PHOTO HISTORY
    Abstract

    Ed van der Elsken was a Dutch photographer and filmmaker who shaped his records of young people in postwar Paris into the photo novel Love on the Left Bank, combining intimate relationships with text, fiction, and page design. In Sweet Life, Amsterdam!, and the films De verliefde camera and Bye, he included exchanged glances, the photographer’s voice, and the duration of editing within the work. His photobooks and films continually asked whose experience a photograph narrates.

    What this photographer changed

    Van der Elsken made the photographer’s preferences, physical proximity, writing, and page sequence explicit within documentary photography, giving concrete form to a first-person mode of documentation. Love on the Left Bank organized real people and events as a photo novel, while Sweet Life and his films presented travel, cities, family, and illness as experiences in which the photographer himself was involved.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    Ed van der Elsken lived in Paris from 1950 to 1954, photographing young people, artists, migrants, and inhabitants of the city’s nightlife around Saint-Germain-des-Prés. The official archive summarizes his more than four decades of work in photography, photobooks, and film as an effort to give form to encounters with people, and notes that he walked through cities with his camera trained on distinctive individuals*1. Across the work are people photographed at close range, repeated encounters with the same subjects, and reunions years later. Love on the Left Bank, published in 1956, was the first major work to translate this method into the structure of a photobook*2. His Paris subjects included young people who frequented sites associated with the Lettrists and, later, the Situationist International. Libero Andreotti’s research notes that the book functions as a photographic record of places favored by the Lettrists and of their culture of urban wandering*18. Evidence is limited for identifying Van der Elsken’s work as the direct expression of a particular intellectual movement. His method rested on walking the city, speaking to people, meeting them repeatedly, and editing photographs into stories and pages. Nan Goldin later described his photography as a way of forming and sustaining relationships with his subjects*15. His first-person form depended on making clear, through writing and sequence, who attracted him and which relationships he chose to preserve in the work.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work

    Love on the Left Bank—Shaping Real Relationships into a Photo Novel

    Love on the Left Bank is a photo novel that arranges photographs of real young people around a story of unrequited love narrated by a fictional young man named Manuel. Its central figure, Ann, was modeled on the Australian painter Vali Myers and appears repeatedly in cafés, rooms, streets, mirrors, and nighttime gatherings. The official bibliography describes the book as a fictional love story constructed from documentary photographs made between 1950 and 1954, closely integrating Van der Elsken’s writing with Jurriaan Schrofer’s design*2. Photo Anthology analyzes the repetition of images, narrow photographic sequences, changes across spreads, and returns to earlier moments, showing how the book was designed to lead the reader through its characters and its chronology*13. The people and places were real, while the love story the reader follows was created through the published sequence and narration. The structure distinguishes recorded events from the editorial decision to place them within a particular story. Goldin cited Van der Elsken as a precursor and connected photography with maintaining relationships because she valued this first-person quality*15. People close to him were recast as characters in a photobook, with the photographer and editor directing the narrative. The images convey affection for their subjects, while the photographer and editor still determined the roles through which each person appeared.

    Close-Range Photography in Sweet Life and Amsterdam!—Turning Encounters into First-Person Documents

    Van der Elsken’s close-range photography records his presence within the same space as his subjects, at a distance where conversation and response could unfold. The official archive’s text on Amsterdam! explains that he documented the city where he lived and the residents who interested him from close range, photographing people in Nieuwmarkt, the Jewish Quarter, and De Pijp, as well as workers, rebellious young people, demonstrations, and police intervention*2. This view of the city was assembled from scenes the photographer selected: people he knew, gestures that caught his attention, demonstrations, and encounters with the police. Decisions about whom to choose, how closely to approach, and what words to add became part of the work. Editing assumed even greater weight in Sweet Life, based on his travels around the world in 1959 and 1960. TIME reported that he selected from roughly five thousand photographs, built storyboards with cut-out prints, and spent about four years completing a book that used severe cropping, full bleeds, double-page spreads, and twenty-six pages of captions*14. The official bibliography also records that Van der Elsken handled the photographs, text, and book design himself, while Wim Crouwel’s typography and gravure printing in Japan shaped the book’s final form*2. The captions and changes in image scale convey surprise, attraction, aversion, exhaustion, and excitement encountered during the journey. In Amsterdam!, social class, youth culture, protest, and everyday conflict appear as the photographer’s account of his own city.

    De verliefde camera and Bye—Voice, Body, and the Responsibility of Filming

    Film brought the photographer’s voice and body, which remained outside the pages of the photobooks, directly into the work’s unfolding time. In Een fotograaf filmt Amsterdam, the film shows Van der Elsken approaching people in the street and forming his own image of the city through those encounters*11. De verliefde camera was structured as a self-portrait centered on photographic work and received the Dutch State Film Award in 1971*8. The camera, his voice, and the other person’s response reveal the interaction behind the finished photograph. His films preserve the time in which photographer and subject shared the same situation*3. Welkom in het leven, lieve kleine addresses a birth within his family*12. In Death in the Port Jackson Hotel, he returned some twenty years later to Vali Myers, the model for Ann in his Paris photographs*10. Bye, made as he was dying of cancer, joins the ailing protagonist and the filmmaker in the same person, making death the film’s subject*9. The film records his physical decline, his family’s responses, and the words he directs toward the camera; observer and subject occupy the same body. In his final work, he made his own illness and death the object of first-person documentation.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Love on the Left Bank and Sweet Life—Dummies, Spreads, and Gravure Printing

    Van der Elsken’s major works emerged from prolonged photobook editing as much as from the split-second responsiveness of photographing. The Rijksmuseum exhibition Up Close used contact sheets, darkroom experiments, annotations, and book dummies to show how he searched, narrowed his choices, and remade sequences while looking for forms that matched the sensation of taking the photographs*4. The archive acquired by the Rijksmuseum and the Nederlands Fotomuseum also contains rejected images and unpublished dummies, revealing the extended selection and rearrangement behind the finished works*5. In the unpublished dummy Feest, made around 1960, he combined older and newer photographs, arranging festivity, excitement, intoxication, and fatigue as rhythmic pairs*2. The cinematic progression of Love on the Left Bank and the severe shifts of scale, full bleeds, and long captions in Sweet Life made page design part of the work. Adjacent images, the pace of the pages, margins, dense black ink, and the amount of text all shape how each photograph is read. Production materials discussed by TIME trace the process from storyboards in which cut-out photographs were repeatedly repositioned to the final sequence*14. Selection, enlargement, repetition, and printing density translated the sequence of the photographer’s memory into the reader’s act of turning pages.

    Hallo! and Color Photography—Linking Images Through Associations Across the Spread

    Hallo! Een nieuwe Ed van der Elsken, published in 1978, paired two photographs through a different association on each spread and at times placed a single image across both pages. The official bibliography explains that the method, little understood when the book appeared, later came to be valued as a form that influenced subsequent photographers*2. People, animals, bodies, objects, and gestures sit beside one another across different places and periods, allowing resemblance, contrast, and humor on the page to redirect the meaning of each image. Love on the Left Bank builds narrative continuity; Hallo! uses leaps between photographs as its editorial principle. Van der Elsken also worked in color over many years through magazine assignments and travel. The official archive records that, following the restoration of more than 42,000 color slides, Lust for Life was published as a broad reassessment of his color work*2. Eye Love You, published in 1977, was his first entirely color photobook; its subject moves from love between women and men around the world toward solidarity and isolation in the struggle to survive*2. The New Yorker essay “Full Spectrum” argues that the American-centered account of postwar color photography left European work, including Van der Elsken’s, underrecognized for many years*17. In his color photographs, travel, the material colors of everyday life, and associations across the spread produced an editorial logic distinct from that of his black-and-white work.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History

    In Relation to Robert Frank and William Klein—The “I” in Postwar Photobooks

    A comparison among Van der Elsken, Robert Frank and William Klein reveals several ways in which photobooks of the 1950s incorporated first-person experience. Frank’s The Americans sequences fragments gathered on the road, connecting flags, automobiles, racial segregation, religion, and solitude as a critique of American society*21. Klein’s Life Is Good & Good for You in New York combines coarse grain, blur, close approach, and typography to transfer the city’s speed and congestion into the photobook’s design*22. Van der Elsken concentrated on named individuals, reunions, romantic relationships, family, and the photographer’s own voice. All three embedded their encounters with society in sequence and book design. Van der Elsken combined rough-edged images with writing, recurring characters, reused photographs, editing, and the voice of film, building affection and antagonism into the work’s structure. My Amsterdam, edited by Martin Parr, identifies shared traits in their viewpoint, humor, attention to detail, and commitment to making pictures; it also reproduces annotations and stains on the original prints in color*2. Later photographers’ reception of Van der Elsken likewise treats the photographer’s life, relationships formed while shooting, and edited materials as parts of a single body of work.

    Intimacy, Travel, and Authorship—Critical Force and Limits

    The critical force of Van der Elsken’s work appears in his decisions about whom to place at the center of a photobook. He repeatedly photographed bohemians in postwar Paris, workers and rebellious young people in Amsterdam, lovers encountered in the street, his family, and his own sick body. In Love on the Left Bank, Ann becomes the book’s central figure through her gaze, clothing, cigarettes, silences, and repeated appearances. Patti Smith later recalled the sense of freedom and the possibilities of self-formation that Vali Myers’s image offered young readers*16. This response indicates the effect of making an intimate acquaintance the protagonist of a photobook. By placing people he knew within the story of a fictional narrator, the photographer and editor assumed authority over how those people were shown*13. Bagara, published in 1957, was photographed at the invitation of a relative who also served as an administrator in French Equatorial Africa; the book moves from village life to safari and hunting*2. Because a relative in the French colonial administration enabled both the journey and the photography, that colonial system remains part of any reading of the book. Goldin’s inheritance, archival research, and the reassessment of the color work all invite attention to the photographer’s subjectivity, including preferences, editorial choices, interventions, and failures. The Rijksmuseum archive exhibition presented finished works alongside experiments and unpublished dummies, allowing authorship to be examined through the process of making*5.
