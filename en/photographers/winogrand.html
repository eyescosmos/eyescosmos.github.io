    Garry Winogrand

    ゲリー・ウィノグランド 1928–1984
    CountryUnited States Period1950–1960s ChannelCity and moment · STREET
    Abstract

    Garry Winogrand photographed streets, airports, zoos, political events, and crowds in great quantity with a wide-angle lens, leaving postwar American public space as an unpredictable photographic field. Tilted frames and events at the edge of the image helped him test what took shape when a real scene became a photograph. The 1967 exhibition New Documents and the undeveloped film left at his death reveal both his conception of photography and the problems of editing his work.

    What this photographer changed

    Winogrand placed the difference between an event in the world and the image cut from it by the camera at the center of his work. Through wide-angle lenses, proximity, and tilted frames, he made photographs in which center and edge, figure and background, chance and composition remain active at once He moved away from documentary photography organized around a single social narrative and made the medium’s unforeseen relations into the subject of the work.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From Magazine Assignments to the Street: The Image World of Postwar America

    Winogrand worked as a freelance magazine photographer in the 1950s while continuing to photograph the streets of New York. As television and color magazines altered the role of illustrated reportage, the expectation that a single photograph explain an entire article also changed, and he shifted more of his attention toward self-directed work*1. On the street, people, advertisements, automobiles, animals, and glances entered the frame at once, producing relations on the photographic plane that could not be predicted from the scene before editing.

    From Magazine Narrative to Photographic Fact: Why Did Winogrand Keep Shooting?

    In the 1950s Winogrand photographed for magazines, where pictures often served as illustrations supporting an article’s narrative. He increasingly refused to subordinate the photograph to a written conclusion and turned instead toward the result of the camera’s cut through reality. His working process came to depend on contact sheets, where he could discover arrangements of figures and gestures at the frame’s edge that he had not understood at the moment of exposure.

    New Documents in 1967: Changing the Purpose of Documentary Photography

    MoMA’s New Documents placed Winogrand alongside Diane Arbus and Lee Friedlander and identified a turn away from documentary photography organized around social reform toward work foregrounding the photographer’s own experience and vision. The exhibition materials described the three as using photography to “know life” rather than to “reform life”*2. This did not amount to indifference toward society. It weakened the premise that a photograph could organize society into one coherent conclusion.

    America Beyond New York: Movement and Regional Difference

    Winogrand photographed Los Angeles, Texas, airports, state fairs, and political gatherings as well as New York. Light, roads, distances between people, and the forms of public events varied by region, changing the density of his pictures. In the West, open space and automobiles create different intervals from the compressed streets of New York. Retrospectives covering the country present him as a photographer of regional differences in public space, not simply as a New York street photographer.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    Wide-Angle Lenses and Proximity: Photographing from Inside the Crowd

    Winogrand used a wide-angle lens and worked close to people while keeping surrounding crowds, buildings, and signs within the frame. Near figures appear large while other events continue in the background, preventing the image from resolving around a single protagonist. Proximity did more than heighten immediacy; it kept collisions among several glances and gestures in public space within one photograph.

    Tilted Composition: What Changes When the Horizon Breaks?

    In Winogrand’s photographs, horizons and buildings often tilt and figures are pushed toward the edge of the frame. This resulted from photographing while walking and giving priority to the overlap of people and background over a level horizon. The National Gallery of Art collection includes examples in which gestures and accidental juxtapositions around a central figure redirect the meaning of the picture*4. The tilt preserves the complicated spatial relations of a scene as they become photographic form.

    Why Tilt the Frame? Constructing Relations on the Picture Plane

    Winogrand cared more about the positions formed by figures, shadows, signs, and glances on the picture plane than about aligning the frame with the world’s horizon. Elements separated by depth in the scene collapse onto the same surface in a photograph. A tilted frame intensifies this compression and gives the edges of the picture structural weight. Its purpose lies less in dramatizing urban anxiety than in retaining the difference between reality and the photographed image as a visible relation.

    The Animals: Looking at Human Society from the Zoo

    In the photobook The Animals, cages, glass, and fences set human and animal postures and lines of sight beside one another. By including families, couples, and children who are looking as well as the animals on display, the pictures reverse the relation between observer and observed*5. Winogrand’s attention to public space turns here from the city to an institution through which humans exhibit and consume other living beings.

    Women Are Beautiful: Looking, Desire, and Criticism

    Women Are Beautiful, published in 1975, assembled a large number of photographs of women in streets and other public places. The series has been praised for registering bodily confidence and changes in urban life and criticized for the repeated structure of a male photographer looking at women he did not know. SFMOMA’s retrospective publication addresses both the social changes of postwar America and the author’s gaze*6. Formal vitality remains inseparable from the conditions that placed one person in the position of looking while the photographed subjects had no role in the use of their images.

    When the World Becomes a Photograph: Winogrand’s Conception of the Medium

    Winogrand said that he photographed in order to see what the world looked like in a photograph*7. The event encountered at the moment of exposure does not coincide with the relations that appear in the two-dimensional image. On contact sheets, he reconsidered the placement of figures and gestures at the frame’s edge, valuing what the photograph had produced beyond his prediction. This conception joins prolific exposure, delayed judgment, and tilted framing within one method.

    A Critique of Documentary: Photography Cannot Fully Explain Society

    The 1967 exhibition New Documents marked a shift from documentary work used as evidence for social reform toward photography that foregrounded the maker’s personal response*2. Winogrand photographed political events, demonstrations, press conferences, and parties among the wealthy, but he did not attempt to explain causes and conclusions in a single image. Glances, gestures, security, advertising, and bystanders remain together, showing how official messages and conduct on the ground fail to coincide. His criticism of contemporary news photography lies in this doubt that one picture can stand for the meaning of a social issue.

    The Edge of the Frame: When the Periphery Matters More Than the Center

    A face cut in half at the edge, a distant glance, or an arm entering by chance can alter the meaning of a Winogrand photograph as much as the central figure. The speed with which he worked left peripheral information unresolved in the viewfinder, and repeated viewing of the print reveals additional relations. This structure resists the photograph as a message grasped at once. By disturbing the hierarchy of center and margin, it retains several simultaneous events within public space.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Public Relations: Photographing Politics and Events from the Margins

    Public Relations examines press conferences, political rallies, ceremonies, sports, and social occasions in which society stages an official image of itself. Security guards, journalists, spectators, and background advertisements enter the frame alongside figures on the platform, creating gaps between an event’s official meaning and its peripheral gestures. Winogrand recorded the whole site of image production as a complicated photograph instead of making a representative picture that explained the occasion.

    Contact Sheets and Editing: Selecting Works from Massive Exposure

    Winogrand sometimes waited before evaluating his pictures and returned to contact sheets after the memory and excitement of exposure had faded*8. The delay allowed him to judge the forms left in the frame. He separated the act of making many exposures from the act of selecting a work, evaluating accidental relations only afterward.

    Undeveloped Film After Death: Who Determines the Work?

    After Winogrand’s death in 1984, roughly 2,500 rolls of undeveloped film remained, along with many rolls he had never seen and unsorted contact sheets. SFMOMA’s retrospective printed and presented part of this material, raising the question of how far images that never passed through the photographer’s own selection should be included in the oeuvre*6. Exposure, contact-sheet selection, and printing divide authorship across three distinct stages.

    Airport Photographs: Waiting, Movement, and Bodies That Look

    Winogrand repeatedly photographed airports, bringing people waiting for arrivals, aircraft behind glass, luggage, advertisements, and uniforms into the frame. The airport represents modern mobility while also serving as a place where many people stop and watch others. Within this waiting time, unlike the speed of the street, the arrangement of bodies and gazes becomes pronounced. The series extends his study of public space from roads to the infrastructure of postwar movement.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    After Robert Frank: From Social Narrative to Visual Excess

    Robert Frank’s The Americans used photobook sequence to construct the isolation and division of American society, while Winogrand retained unresolved information within individual frames. Both refused to condense postwar America into a unified story and presented it as a collection of contradictory gestures and glances. Frank connected fragments through the book’s narrative; Winogrand placed fragmentation in the speed of exposure and the information left at the picture’s edges.

    Winogrand and Lee Friedlander: Discovering Disorder or Structuring It?

    Friedlander carefully layers windows, reflections, signs, and his own shadow, organizing the city as a visual structure. Winogrand accumulated moments in which unpredictable arrangements emerged within crowds through walking and prolific exposure. The two appeared together in New Documents, but Friedlander incorporated obstructions into composition, while Winogrand allowed events beyond complete control to remain active all the way to the frame’s edge.

    The Responsibility of Posthumous Editing: Standards for Opening an Archive

    The undeveloped film left after Winogrand’s death blurred the boundary between an image made by the photographer and one selected by him as a work. Opening the archive supports research into his late methods and regional travel, but it also allows later editors to produce representative pictures according to present values. SFMOMA and the Center for Creative Photography distinguished finished works, materials selected by Winogrand, and images developed or chosen after his death, making provenance and editorial process visible.

    Volume and Delayed Judgment: Why Did He Keep Shooting Before Looking?

    Winogrand sometimes postponed evaluation and returned to contact sheets after the immediate memory of shooting had receded*8. He sought to judge the forms in the picture rather than the excitement of the event. The separation of exposure and selection gave the photographic result priority over the author’s expectation. The enormous quantity of undeveloped film from his final years shows the point at which production exceeded his ability to edit it*10. Posthumous access expanded knowledge of the archive, but the grounds for treating images he never saw as representative works remain disputed.

    The Male Gaze in Public Space: What Formal Freedom Does Not Excuse

    Women Are Beautiful has faced continuing criticism for treating women in public as objects of desire. Winogrand’s rapid approach and anonymous exposures sharply register street gestures while creating a relation in which the photographed person cannot participate in the later use of the image. Retrospectives and criticism have addressed the formal achievement and the male-centered gaze together*21. The claim that photography does not explain society does not neutralize the photographer’s power. The distinction between those positioned to look and those positioned to be looked at remains part of the series’ evaluation.
