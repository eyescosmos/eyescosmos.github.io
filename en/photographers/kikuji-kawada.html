    Kikuji Kawada

    川田喜久治 1933-2021
    CountryJapan Period1950–1960s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Kikuji Kawada joined close views of the Atomic Bomb Dome, war relics, flags, and fragments around American bases through dense contrast in the photobook The Map, turning postwar memory into a material experience of the book. Kohei Sugiura’s design, double-page spreads, foldouts, and recurring darkness made photography less an explanation of place than a structure for encountering history when it cannot be read easily.

    What this photographer changed

    In The Map, Kawada arranged walls inside the Atomic Bomb Dome, war relics, flags, and fragments of occupation culture across dense spreads and foldouts, tracing postwar memory through material residue. Through close-up, dark printing, and Kohei Sugiura’s book design, he transferred the survival of history as fragments in the present into the structure of the book He expanded the photobook from a container for images into a medium that requires readers to open, return, and test relations among photographs.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    VIVO and Postwar Japanese Photography: Making Photobooks Beyond Reportage

    Kawada worked in the photography department at Shinchosha before forming VIVO in 1959 with Shomei Tomatsu, Eikoh Hosoe, Ikko Narahara, Akira Sato, and Akira Tanno. Although the collective dissolved after a short period, it provided a setting in which photographers could publish self-directed work outside magazine assignments and determine both subject and editing*1. For Kawada, photographing the postwar period came to mean combining material remnants and fragments of memory in a photobook rather than explaining events chronologically.

    Twenty Years After Defeat: The Historical Distance of The Map

    The first edition of The Map appeared in 1965, twenty years after Japan’s defeat and the atomic bombings. Reconstruction and rapid economic growth were advancing, while the bomb, occupation, military bases, and war responsibility remained unresolved. The Kyoto City KYOCERA Museum of Art presents the book as an allegorical response to the political and social conditions of postwar Japan*2. Kawada did not photograph from the position of immediate aftermath; he approached memory through traces that had persisted and eroded over time.

    Shinchosha and VIVO: From News Photography to an Independent Series

    At Shinchosha, Kawada photographed gravure features for Shukan Shincho and learned how photographs were joined to articles, headlines, and print. When he left in 1959 and joined VIVO, he entered an environment in which the subject and mode of presentation of a series could be self-determined*1. This publishing experience became a foundation for The Map, whose meaning depends on multiple images and book design rather than on an isolated photograph.

    From Magazine Editing to The Map: Turning Information into Page-Bound Memory

    At Shinchosha, Kawada observed the process by which news photographs were organized into the meaning of an article. After joining VIVO, he photographed walls in the Atomic Bomb Dome, war relics, flags, and fragments around American bases, colliding distant images across spreads. He later described coming to see stains on walls and surviving objects as images capable of symbolizing memories of war*4. The form of The Map replaced the sequential explanation of reportage with a bodily encounter in which defeat’s remaining signs are approached by opening pages, returning to earlier images, and unfolding inserts.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    The Map: Why Did Kawada Photograph the Walls of the Atomic Bomb Dome at Close Range?

    At the center of The Map are close photographs of dark stains and peeling surfaces on walls inside the Atomic Bomb Dome. Removed from architectural overview and explanatory evidence, the wall becomes an abstract field resembling ground, skin, or astronomical imagery. The Met’s copy of the book shows these images joined on spreads with war relics, flags, advertisements, and photographs from areas around American bases*3. Close-up gives priority to material alteration inscribed on the surface over the name of the object. It is the distance at which history appears sedimented in matter.

    Close-Up and Darkness: Making Difficulty of Reading a Historical Condition

    Extreme proximity to the Dome walls removes the building’s overall form and location from the frame, leaving stains, cracks, and peeling surfaces. Kawada narrows information through proximity and concentrates historical duration in damage and residue. For a later photographer unable to record the instant of destruction, the limit of approaching history through material traces appears as difficulty of reading the image. The archive of negatives, contact sheets, notes, and books acquired by the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston, shows that these abstract pictures were selected through on-site work and careful editing*18.

    What Place Does The Map Show? From Geography to the Structure of Memory

    The title The Map does not function like an ordinary map that locates Hiroshima or Japan with precision. As the book proceeds, stains, soldiers’ belongings, signs of occupation, the Japanese flag, and Coca-Cola advertisements recur, placing different times and places within one dark terrain. An Aperture interview discusses The Map as a work that established the photobook as an artistic object and made political and social criticism through abstract, experimental form*4. Information does not move in a single explanatory direction; relations among fragments are tested through movement back and forth across pages.

    Kohei Sugiura’s Book Design: How Material Form Carries Historical Meaning

    Kohei Sugiura’s design for the 1965 edition incorporated a double slipcase, a folded cover, and heavy black printing, making the acts of opening and turning pages part of the work. Photographs emerge from dark pages, apart from isolated display on a white wall, and acquire different meanings through neighboring images*3. Design becomes a medium for the difficulty of approaching history and the bodily time of excavating memory, not an exterior decoration applied to photographs.

    Published on August 6, 1965: Presenting War Memory Twenty Years Later

    The Map was published on August 6, 1965, exactly twenty years after the atomic bombing of Hiroshima*7. At a time when memories of defeat were being organized through official commemoration and narratives of reconstruction, Kawada fragmented maps, flags, relics, occupation culture, and walls into a book that withheld a clear conclusion. Sugiura’s gate-like case, folded pages, and dark printing impede linear progress and create a sense of the past repeatedly entering the present. Book design thus resists fixing war memory into a concise lesson.

    What Do Symbols Communicate, and What Do They Leave Unclear?

    Kawada arranged clocks, medals, flags, soldiers’ belongings, and signs of American culture with restrained captions. Symbolic editing can hold defeat, sacrifice, the state, occupation, and consumer society in view at once without closing the postwar period into one claim. Images that do not identify whose experience they show also risk abstracting the specific voices of atomic-bomb survivors. The book’s critical force does not rest on ambiguity as an end in itself. It preserves the gap between explainable history and material memory that exceeds explanation.

    Reeditions and The Last Cosmology: Editing the Past Without Fixing It

    The Map has been reissued several times, with changes in paper, printing, format, and contents. Kawada later edited photographs of the cosmos, politics, cities, and signs of disaster in works including The Last Cosmology, Los Caprichos, and World’s End*2. Rather than repeating the early book, he connected the photographic archive to contemporary crisis. “The map” expanded from a completed volume into an ongoing method of giving the world new arrangements.

    The Double-Page Spread: Producing a Third Meaning Between Images

    The Map sets unlike photographs against one another across spreads: stains on a wall and a flag, a damaged object and an advertisement. The images do not simply add information. Their adjacency produces tension between things without a stated causal relation and generates a third meaning. Each return to a page places the same image in another chain and alters what comes before and after. The book’s criticism lies in the unexplained distance between images as well as in the symbolic strength of individual photographs.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    The Chizu Print and the Photobook: Is the Same Photograph the Same Work?

    MoMA holds a print titled Chizu as an individual object, presenting the formal qualities of one image removed from the book*5. The Met preserves the 1965 edition of The Map as a complete work, including print, sequence, and support*3. The same photograph produces different viewing time and context when encountered as a single print or as a page within the photobook.

    Darkness, Grain, and Close-Up: Techniques That Complicate Documentary Reading

    Kawada’s early work often gives black a broad presence, makes grain conspicuous, and withholds an easily identifiable whole. Fragmentary evidence changes meaning through relation to other images and words. Darkness and grain sustain the difficulty of reading historical traces. As stains on a wall begin to resemble maps or skin, close-up and high contrast open the subject to several associations. The photograph remains evidence while resisting confinement to one verbal explanation.

    Long-Term Production and the Date: Editing the Present as a Sign of Catastrophe

    Kawada continued photographing into old age and reedited images from different decades into new series. A 2024 exhibition at the Kyoto City KYOCERA Museum of Art presented the artist, born in 1933, as an active photographer who has pursued social and historical signs that are difficult to grasp with the naked eye*2. In this method, the meaning produced when a photograph later meets another image matters more than its immediate news value. The archive serves as material for editing the present rather than as a fixed storehouse of the past.

    Los Caprichos: Bringing Goya’s Title into the Present

    In Los Caprichos, Kawada borrowed the title of Goya’s print series and combined fragments of cities, politicians, crowds, television, and strange objects. The quotation brings an older language for questioning social reason and madness through fragmentary images into contemporary urban photography. Postwar Japan meets Goya’s era as the anxieties of different times correspond through photographic sequence.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    Kawada and Shomei Tomatsu: At What Distance Should Postwar Matter Be Seen?

    Tomatsu also photographed the persistence of war in objects and bodies, including a melted bottle, a stopped watch, and scarred skin in Nagasaki. Tomatsu retained the encounter with individual people and things, while Kawada collided heterogeneous fragments across photobook spreads. From the same VIVO milieu emerged both an approach grounded in material encounter and one that linked symbols through book design. Their difference shows that postwar memory did not settle into one dark style; it changed with distance and medium.

    Beyond the Canonical Photobook: The Political Tension of The Map

    The Map is now internationally valued as a rare photobook, a landmark of book design, and a canonical work of postwar Japanese photography. When market value and design innovation dominate discussion, its political tension around the atomic bomb, occupation, and national symbols can become decorative. A PGI exhibition emphasized Kawada’s continuing attention to the signs of his time after The Map, resisting the isolation of one book as a self-contained masterpiece*6. Its formal intensity remains tied to a postwar history that is difficult to read.

    A Critique of Postwar Realism: Keeping Evidence While Showing Its Limits

    The VIVO photographers inherited the ethical demand of postwar realism to confront actual conditions while moving away from confidence that one picture could stand for society. The images in The Map are grounded in concrete subjects such as the Atomic Bomb Dome and surviving objects, preserving historical places within abstract surfaces. Knowing the object’s name, however, does not complete the meaning of war. Recent scholarship has analyzed this tension between visibility and invisibility as a problem of representation in the atomic age*20. Kawada’s criticism made the limits of documentary explanation explicit through photobook form.

    An Artist Still Working: Not Ending Kawada’s Career in 1965

    Kawada continued to publish new work and reedit earlier photographs in the 2020s, remaining an active photographer as well as a historical figure born in 1933*2. After The Map, his subjects expanded to celestial phenomena, disaster, politics, and digitally recorded dates. The early concern with postwar memory differs from his later apocalyptic register, but the method persists: selecting fragments of an era and giving them relations through books and exhibitions.
