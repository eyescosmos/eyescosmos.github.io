Essay

Lee Miller (1907-1977) was an American photographer who moved from Vogue fashion modeling into photography, traversing Surrealist experiment, fashion photography, and war reportage across a single career*1*2. In Paris, through her collaboration with Man Ray around 1929-1932, she participated in the development of solarization and in photographic experiments that explored the unstable relation between body and light*2. From 1939 onward, working as a correspondent for Vogue, she photographed the European theater of World War II, including the Normandy landings, the liberation of Paris, and the liberation of concentration camps such as Dachau and Buchenwald*1.

The well-known photograph of Miller bathing in Hitler's apartment in Munich in 1945 has repeatedly been discussed as an image in which the personal body collides with a site marked by historical violence*1*3. Recent scholarship increasingly positions Miller not merely in relation to Man Ray, but as an autonomous photographer whose Surrealist eye and war reportage belong to a single, continuous investigation of unstable identity and visual power*1*2. The Imperial War Museums and the Lee Miller Archive have been central to this shift in reception, helping reframe her as a major figure in the history of photography rather than an adjunct to male Surrealism.
