Alfred Stieglitzアルフレッド・スティーグリッツ

Country: US
Era: 1890 — 1910s
1864–1946

Essay

In New York in the 1890s, photography was still widely treated as a mechanical copying process rather than something museums might exhibit beside painting and sculpture. Stieglitz's effort to raise the medium's status began as a direct confrontation with that prejudice*1. In his pictorialist phase he used soft focus, gum bichromate printing, and painterly composition to prove that photographs could rival painting. He defined the Photo-Secession, founded in 1902, as an attempt to unite Americans devoted to pictorial photography and secure recognition for it as a distinct medium of personal expression*2. Camera Work, launched in 1903, and the gallery 291, opened in 1905, were institutional strategies for placing photography in the same cultural frame as modern art. At 291 he mounted the first American exhibitions of figures such as Matisse, Cezanne, and Picasso, effectively making the question "Is photography art?" seem increasingly obsolete*3. After World War I, however, Stieglitz came to see pictorialism's basic premise as flawed: if modernism meant respecting the inherent properties of each medium, then photography could not justify itself by imitating painting*4. In the move toward straight photography, his Equivalents series, begun in 1922, produced roughly 350 cloud studies and proposed that photography could become an equivalent of inner feeling rather than merely a record of external subjects*5. The musical dimension of this idea is inscribed in the early series titles themselves: Music: A Sequence of Ten Cloud Photographs (1922) and Songs of the Sky (1923). Stieglitz told Georgia O'Keeffe that he wanted to make photographs which, when the composer Ernest Bloch saw them, would make him exclaim "Music! Music! Man, why that is music!" — and when Bloch did see them, that was precisely what he said*5. The theoretical grounding was Walter Pater's dictum of 1877 that "all art constantly aspires towards the condition of music": just as music arouses emotion through pure form without reference to external subjects, Stieglitz argued that photography could do the same, transmitting inner states through visual form alone, independent of what the image depicted*4. These pictures were among the first to insist systematically that photography could register abstract emotional states rather than merely document the world. Critic Hilton Kramer later argued that the Equivalents reached a lyric abstraction that American painting itself would not fully attain until the 1940s and 1950s*6.
