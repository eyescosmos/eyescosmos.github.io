    Takuma Nakahira

    中平卓馬 1970s
    CountryJapan Period1970–1980s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Takuma Nakahira photographed Tokyo at night, roads, underpasses, plants, waterfronts, and islands, moving from grainy black-and-white images to lucid color. Across PROVOKE, For a Language to Come, Circulation, Overflow, and Why an Illustrated Botanical Dictionary?, he examined photographic language, the photographer’s body, paper and printing, the systems that organize urban space, and the exhibition wall. Whenever an acquired visual style hardened into an authorial signature or a marketable product, he criticized it and changed how he photographed and presented his work.

    What this photographer changed

    Nakahira unsettled the idea that a photographer’s consistency should be demonstrated by repeating a single visual style. He refused to treat PROVOKE’s are-bure-boke—grainy, blurry, and out-of-focus imagery—as a final form, moving instead to the same-day installation of Circulation, the enumerative method of Why an Illustrated Botanical Dictionary?, and the color wall of Overflow. He showed both photography’s potential to escape established language and its tendency to harden into a code, a commodity, or an authorial brand through a sustained cycle of self-critique and methodological change.

    § 01 / 04 Before PROVOKE: Why Takuma Nakahira Moved from Editing to Photography

    Nakahira studied Spanish at Tokyo University of Foreign Studies. After graduating in 1963, he joined the editorial staff of Gendai no me (The Contemporary Eye). There he encountered Shōmei Tōmatsu’s work and helped edit the exhibition One Hundred Years of Photography. He left the magazine in 1965 and began working as a photographer and critic*16. Between 1965 and 1977, he published more than one hundred essays*19.

    Nakahira initially hoped to become a poet and joined a leftist study group focused on Latin American revolutions. He became increasingly skeptical of the way political and artistic language subsumed particular places and things under general concepts. He later compared the word “tree” with a single tree photographed at a specific time and place*8. Photography exposed critical abstractions to light, distance, time, and the surfaces of things, while the photographer’s body encountered the speed of the city and the resistance of its subjects. Daniel Abbe analyzes Nakahira during this period as a “body in the world”*23. Photography allowed him to test, through movement and sensation, a reality that concepts alone could not verify.

    In 1968, Nakahira, Kōji Taki, Yutaka Takanashi, and Takahiko Okada founded PROVOKE; Daidō Moriyama joined with the second issue. Its subtitle, “Provocative Materials for Thought,” stated an intention to remove photography from its role as illustration and present it as material whose meaning had not yet been settled*20. Tristan Ignas-Menzies reads the magazine as a collective practice of realism formed in response to a society undergoing rapid economic growth that no single viewpoint could encompass*24. Its unit of production was the collision among photography, criticism, poetry, editing, and the printed page.

    Coarse grain, camera shake, missed focus, and extreme tonal contrasts delayed the immediate attachment of journalistic explanations to the subject*4. Philip Charrier notes that an indistinct visual language intended to dislodge established codes could itself become a new code through repetition*25. The success of are-bure-boke forced Nakahira into his next act of self-critique.

    § 02 / 04 Photography and Language: For a Language to Come and Why an Illustrated Botanical Dictionary?

    For a Language to Come: Are-Bure-Boke, Paper, and Print

    Published in 1970, For a Language to Come sequences grainy black-and-white photographs of roads at night, underpasses, windows, people, animals, and signs. Designed by Tsunehisa Kimura, the book arranges 192 images across glossy double-page spreads*5. Full bleeds, black margins, repetition, and shifts in scale control the pace of page turning and create collisions between images.

    Jelena Stojković argues that deep blacks, blown-out highlights, coarse grain, and flash reflections make the viewer aware of the subject and the printed surface at the same time*17. Gyewon Kim considers urban signs, lettering, and walls alongside the material properties of paper, ink, and the page*26. Just as roads organize human movement, gutters and variations in ink density alter the order of looking. The roughness of the printed book challenged two assumptions: that a photograph transparently explains the city, and that paper transparently carries the photograph.

    Landscape Theory: Television News, Roads, and the Administration of the City

    The landscape theory, or fūkeiron, that Nakahira shared with Masao Matsuda, Masao Adachi, and others turned away from central protagonists and toward roads, underpasses, construction sites, suburbs, and reclaimed land. Stojković analyzes the Tokyo waterfront as an image of circulation networks carrying commodities, information, and labor*17. Franz Prichard reads it as a record of the spatial imprint of rapid economic growth and the Cold War order*18.

    Written in 1972, “The Illusion Called the Documentary” examined the belief that documentary images transmit events intact, against the backdrop of televised coverage of the United Red Army incident and the reversion of Okinawa*7. Yuriko Furuhata argues that news media produced “current events” by repeatedly showing political confrontation, while landscape theory located state administration in roads and urban infrastructure without visible protagonists*28. Nakahira redirected his camera from the protagonists of an event to the screens that circulated it and the infrastructure that governed everyday movement.

    Around 1970, the All-Japan Students’ Photo Association traveled to Minamata, Yokkaichi, Ashio, and other sites, incorporating photographs of damage, historical records, and statistics into its books. Kelly Midori McCormick places this activity within the formation of ecocritical photography in Japan*29. The association foregrounded victims and data, while Nakahira turned toward unpeopled roads. Both confronted the transformation of land by industrial and state systems.

    Circulation: Paris, 1971—Same-Day Shooting and Installation

    At the 1971 Paris Biennale, Nakahira photographed the city each day, developed the film at night, and added new prints to the wall the following day. The production of roughly two hundred images a day, his movement through the city, darkroom labor, the expanding wall, and the movement of viewers formed a continuous work*8. The work in SFMOMA’s collection is part of that unfolding activity*9.

    Abbe analyzes the work as a process in which the photographer’s body moved through flows of traffic, commodities, and information, then returned the resulting images to the exhibition site*23. Because the interval between exposure and display was compressed, the work had no fixed final form until the exhibition ended. Nakahira later recalled that this project marked the first time his statements and actions began to coincide*8. Walking, darkroom labor, installation, and audience response all took place within the city’s circuits.

    Why an Illustrated Botanical Dictionary? From Authorial Style to the Differences Among Things

    Why an Illustrated Botanical Dictionary? is a collection of essays published in 1973. Nakahira criticized the way are-bure-boke had become a recognizable style of dissent that advertising and publishing could consume. Chikuma Shobō describes the book as charting his move from criticism of his early work toward a photography that would record things as things*10. Charrier examines this shift through postwar photographic realism and mechanical objectivity*11.

    The “illustrated botanical dictionary” did not mean photographing plants alone. Nakahira placed plants, roads, buildings, and animals before the camera one by one, without using them as narrative symbols or substitutes for the author’s feelings. By refusing a single central image and granting unlike subjects equal status, he sought to limit the photographer’s power to assign meaning in advance. Prichard analyzes this enumeration as an open procedure in which fragments continue to accumulate*18.

    Scholars have also noted the influence of Alain Robbe-Grillet and J. M. G. Le Clézio. Stojković compares Nakahira with the nouveau roman’s effort to detach objects from psychology and narrative and describe their form, position, and repetition*17. Neil Matheson discusses Nakahira’s critique of placing the human subject at the center of the world*3. Abbe draws attention to his contemporaneity with Lee Ufan, Mono-ha, and the phenomenological idea of the “encounter”*23. The method depended on a viewing body and a thing meeting in the same place before the object was absorbed into a familiar name.

    The photographer’s body remained present in distance, framing, color, and the moment of exposure. Abbe and Prichard emphasize the reciprocity between the viewer and the viewed*19. Charrier questions whether the declaration that displaced the author actually achieved “true realism”*11. Nakahira turned to the dictionary because the success of his early photographs had created a new category of “Nakahira-like images.” He accepted as a photographic problem the contradiction that body and choice remain even in images attempting to evade classification.

    Overflow: Forty-Eight Color Photographs and Contemporary Art

    In Overflow (1974), forty-eight color photographs of manholes, subways, buildings, roads, signs, sharks, and other subjects occupied a wall approximately six meters wide. Taka Ishii Gallery relates the installation to the proliferation of photographic media and the sense that images were surging beyond the artist’s control*2. The reconstructed work is documented at approximately 1.6 meters high and six meters wide*12.

    All the photographs appear at once on the wall, and their combinations change with the viewer’s position. Prichard argues that the installation transformed a media surplus delivered in one direction into an arrangement that could be read along multiple paths*18. Yasufumi Nakamori identifies Overflow as a turning point in Nakahira’s engagement with photography and 1970s contemporary art*27. A photograph of the Tokyo waterfront also appeared on the cover of the catalog for the 1970 Tokyo Biennale, Between Man and Matter. By incorporating the wall and the viewer’s movement, Nakahira made photography operate within the same field as conceptual art and intermedia.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Photobooks, and Exhibition Methods

    La nuit and For a Language to Come

    In SFMOMA’s La nuit 1, a dark street and traces of human presence sink into coarse grain and dense black tones*6. The photobook repeats 192 such images and removes information that would return the city to a familiar place. Nakahira’s self-critique began when this indistinctness became a recognizable visual signature.

    Circulation: Date, Place, Events, 1971

    In Circulation: Date, Place, Events, Nakahira repeated exposure, development, and display throughout the exhibition. By eliminating the delay in which a completed work would normally be presented after the fact, he placed the body walking through the city, the darkroom, the gallery, and the audience within one production process.

    Overflow, 1974

    Overflow retains the varying sizes and orientations of its forty-eight color photographs, extending advertisements, plants, roads, and industrial equipment across the viewer’s peripheral field. The 2024 retrospective placed this work, Nakahira’s magazine work, the islands, and his post-illness color photographs within a continuous account of his work*1. The sequence of images is produced by the viewer’s movement along the wall.

    Okinawa, the Amami Islands, and the Color Photographs After His Illness

    Nakahira photographed Okinawa in 1973 and the Amami and Tokara Islands, among other locations, between 1974 and 1977*16. Prichard examines sea routes, military bases, ports, and roads as structures that produced relations between mainland Japan and its peripheries*22. Abbe reads the tilted frames, close distances, and unstable spaces of the Amami photographs as “records of encounter” that depart from a viewpoint seeking orderly possession of its subject*23. The visual footing from which the mainland might organize the islands becomes unstable within the frame.

    In 1977, Nakahira collapsed from acute alcohol poisoning and sustained serious impairments to memory and language. After recovering, he walked through Yokohama photographing in color, publishing the results in Aratanaru gyōshi (1983) and Adieu à X (1989), among other books. Recent reassessments have resisted dividing his work before and after the illness into two separate artistic identities*1. An SFMOMA film shows how walking and photographing became part of his daily life after the illness*14.

    The later work was not a simple return to the “illustrated botanical dictionary.” SFMOMA describes a growing emphasis on the differences among individual subjects that resist classification and generalization*16. Untitled, from Documentary uses lucid color and frontal composition unlike the early black-and-white work*15. Yet he continued to test difference through each act of walking and photographing, without quickly attaching the subject to an established meaning.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism, Reassessment, and Place in Photographic History

    Why Did Takuma Nakahira Criticize His Own Photography?

    For many years, Nakahira’s reputation outside Japan rested largely on PROVOKE and are-bure-boke. Abbe and Prichard note that the emphasis on this novelty obscured his more than one hundred critical essays and the methodological changes of the 1970s*19. Nakahira criticized his early style because its success had made it an imitable sensibility, turning a method intended to free images from explanation into another fixed sign.

    His writing analyzed where the preceding method had failed to grasp reality; the next photographs, edits, and exhibitions tested that analysis under different terms. Body and editing remained in the dictionary’s enumeration, just as selection remained in the same-day installation. Nakahira did not resolve these contradictions in a single theory. He continued testing them by changing visual style, production time, exhibition space, color, and location.

    Nakahira’s place in photographic history rests on his sustained test of the hope that photography, once independent from language, could reach reality directly—and on his recognition that photographs also become fixed as codes, commodities, and an artist’s name. He reworked the photographer’s body, paper and printing, editorial time, the exhibition wall, and the viewer’s movement as terms of production. The blind spots created by one successful method became the starting point of the next work.

    International Scholarship: Body, Collective Work, Paper, and Islands

    The Yokohama Museum of Art’s 2003 exhibition examined Nakahira’s post-illness work alongside his early photographs*13. The National Museum of Modern Art, Tokyo’s 2024 exhibition presented Overflow, his magazine work, the islands, and the post-illness color photographs as a continuous history*1. In 2025, At the Limits of the Gaze, the first English-language selection of his writings, was published*19.

    International scholarship has increasingly examined the circumstances from which Nakahira’s methods emerged. Ignas-Menzies addresses collective production*24. Charrier considers structuralism and codes*25. Kim analyzes paper and the materiality of the city*26. Furuhata situates television news in relation to landscape theory*28. McCormick examines contemporary pollution photography*29. Nakamori traces the intersection of photography and contemporary art*27. Abbe extends the discussion to the photographer’s body and the spaces of Amami*23.

    In 2026, the Spanish newspaper El País also introduced Nakahira as a theorist concerned with power, language, and the act of seeing*21. Nakahira is now being reconsidered beyond the author of a particular visual style, as a photographer and critic who tested the terms of photography through the body, collective work, printing, urban infrastructure, exhibition, and island spaces.
