    Sophie Calle

    ソフィ・カル
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelIssues in photo history · Conceptual
    Abstract

    After returning to Paris in 1978 from seven years of travel, Sophie Calle began following passersby as a way to reconnect with the city, recording their routes in photographs and journals. In Suite Vénitienne, the pursuer’s expectations and misrecognitions become central; The Hotel reconstructs absent guests through their possessions; and Take Care of Yourself turns 107 women’s readings of a breakup email into the work itself. Drawing together the procedures of conceptual art, the first-person voice of autobiographical fiction, and the evidentiary look of surveillance photography, Calle made the author’s desire and authority to narrate another person a subject of photography.

    What this photographer changed

    Conceptual art in the 1970s brought ideas, instructions, execution, and documentation to the center of the artwork, while feminist art opened love, domestic life, the body, and personal history to public debate. Calle joined these developments to the forms of detective fiction and surveillance photography. The blurred pursuit images in Suite Vénitienne reveal little about the man being followed, while the journal records the pursuer’s fixation and errors in detail. In The Hotel and The Address Book, the author’s conjectures enter portraits assembled from traces and testimony. Calle expanded photographic portraiture from reading character in a face to examining the approach, inference, and trespass involved in trying to know another person. Later autobiographical photography, investigative video, and performance would likewise be judged through the artist’s methods of gaining access, selecting evidence, and determining what could be made public.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period: Conceptual Art, “Individual Mythology,” and the Public Life of Private Experience

    When Following Became an Artwork: Conceptual Art and “Individual Mythology”

    Calle studied sociology at Paris Nanterre University and encountered the political movements that followed 1968. She then spent seven years traveling through the Middle East, Asia, and North America. When she returned to Paris at twenty-five, she had neither steady work nor a plan for making art. To bridge her distance from the city, she began following passersby into streets and shops she would never have chosen herself. The photographs and times she kept as evidence of these pursuits became the starting point of her work. She has recalled that she also needed to describe these activities as art to her father, a collector of contemporary art, in order to retain his financial support*1. Her turn to photography grew from the need to use another person’s movements to reconnect with Paris, more than from an interest in camera technique.

    Conceptual art in the 1970s treated the execution of an idea or instruction as an artwork alongside the finished object*2. In performance of the same period, photographs served as evidence after an action and also helped produce gestures and scenes conceived for the camera*3. Calle’s pursuits likewise begin with a predetermined action and are reconstructed through photographs, maps, timetables, and journals. The journals retain boredom, excitement, and mistaken sightings, allowing the record of a procedure to be read alongside the loneliness and fixation that made the procedure necessary.

    In France, work by Christian Boltanski, Jean Le Gac, and others was discussed under the term “individual mythology.” Photographs, documents, and objects resembling personal effects unsettled the line between personal history and a constructed narrative. Mudam places Suite Vénitienne within this lineage, noting how covert-looking photographs, pursuit maps, report-like prose, and private reflections produce factual documentation and autobiographical fiction at the same time*4. In 1980s France, “ethnology at home” also redirected the ethnographic gaze from distant cultures toward the subway, hotels, and everyday life within one’s own society*5. Trained in sociology, Calle made hotel rooms and an address book into subjects of observation while also recording the observer’s desire and overreach.

    The Sleepers: Recording Sleeping Bodies by the Hour and in Shifts

    For the early work The Sleepers (1979), Calle invited friends, acquaintances, and strangers to occupy her bed continuously for eight days. They slept in shifts while she photographed them every hour, brought them food, and recorded their conversations. MoMA describes the work, in which twenty-seven people shared the same bed, as a key early project composed of photographs and brief written records*6. Although the bed evokes intimacy, the work conveys times, postures, shifts, and short exchanges. It leaves a gap between the desire to approach another person and the camera’s ability to record only the surface of a body.

    The Sleepers began as a private experiment. Bernard Lamarche-Vadel, an art critic who took part, encouraged Calle to show it publicly, and she discovered that an everyday action could be read as art after editing and exhibition*7. A means of coping with solitude and boredom became a shareable work through rules for inviting participants, hourly photographs, and recorded conversations. It also raised questions of consent: who observes the sleepers, and how much of that observation may be made public?

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work: Recording the Desire to Know Another Person

    Suite Vénitienne: Recording the Pursuer’s Fixation and Misrecognitions

    Suite Vénitienne (1980) records Calle’s pursuit of Henri B., a man she met by chance in Paris and learned was traveling to Venice. She followed him in disguise. Her photographs of alleys, backs, hotels, and windows never identify their subject fully. The journal records the times of the pursuit, periods of waiting, information supplied by helpers, anxiety after losing him, and elation at finding him again. It reveals less about the man than about a pursuer who gives meaning to slight clues and turns another person’s life into a plot. As Mudam notes, blurred photographs, maps, and prose resembling a detective’s notes support the story’s plausibility while feeding the pursuer’s imagination*4. Calle retains mistaken sightings, failed attempts, and accidental assistance, making the pursuer’s inability to sustain the role of objective recorder part of the work.

    Setting Photographic Traces Against First-Person Conjecture

    Calle’s photographs preserve traces of people, rooms, and objects that stood before the camera. Her journals add times, actions, and emotions, along with the narrator’s expectations, memories, and conjectures. In Suite Vénitienne, the man’s back confirms that a pursuit took place, while the text gives that back desire and narrative. In The Hotel, clothing and trash indicate a guest’s life, and the written record converts those traces into a portrait. The discrepancy between photograph and text exposes the author’s judgments as she infers inner life from outward evidence. The Hasselblad Foundation has recognized Calle’s sustained examination of private and public life, fact and fiction, and the limits of photography*8.

    News captions identify places and events, while the prose of a photo essay links several photographs to a social account. Documentary text in conceptual art states an action’s instructions and execution. Calle writes in the first person about the thrill of waiting in ambush, failure, and performance, making the recorder’s emotions and judgments explicit*5. Faces are scarce; sheets, clothing, telephones, doors, and letters become clues. Viewers therefore construct a person from incomplete information and encounter the difference between what the photograph establishes and the plot the narrator assigns to it.

    The Hotel and The Address Book: Constructing a Person in Their Absence

    In The Hotel (1981), Calle worked as a chambermaid in a Venice hotel. While guests were away, she photographed their rooms and examined luggage, clothes, trash, unmade beds, diaries, and other belongings. As Siglio’s account of the work shows, the photographs and written records are organized by room number and date, accumulating traces of lives whose occupants never appear*9. A chambermaid is authorized to enter another person’s most private space for cleaning and expected to remain silent about what she sees. Calle redirected that position toward observation. The work retains both the curiosity that drives her reconstruction of strangers’ lives and the problem of using a service worker’s access for private investigation. Centre Pompidou records the project as a three-week study of hotel rooms and places it within the archive of “true stories” extending from The Sleepers and Suite Vénitienne, now centered on absent guests and the traces they left behind*10.

    For The Address Book (1983), Calle photocopied an address book she had found on the street before returning it to its owner. She then contacted the people listed inside and asked them about Pierre D., the book’s owner. The resulting testimony and photographs appeared as a serial in Libération. He had not been informed of the project and protested strongly after publication. When the work was later published in English, accounts again noted that a book edition had been withheld until after his death*11. As testimony accumulates, conflicting memories and relationships fracture the portrait. Calle, as editor, retains the power to select those fragments and deliver them to newspaper readers. The work demonstrates how a portrait assembled from third-party testimony can override the subject’s consent and self-description.

    Because: Reversing the Usual Order of Photograph and Explanation

    In Because (2018–), a curtain conceals each photograph while a short statement on the outside explains why Calle took it. Viewers read the text before lifting the cloth to see the image. Fraenkel Gallery observes that this sequence allows an explanation, usually supplied after a photograph is seen, to shape the viewer’s imagination in advance*12. The photograph may match the scene imagined from the text or depart from it. Calle uses the familiar assumption that words explain a photograph while making viewers experience how that explanation has already directed interpretation. After four decades of pairing photographs and writing, she extends the method to the way factual understanding changes according to which comes first.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media: Re-reading Private Experience Through Multiple Voices

    The Birthday Ceremony: Preserving Gifts and Counting Affection

    For The Birthday Ceremony (1980–93), Calle invited the same number of guests as her age to each birthday, added one stranger in case someone failed to attend, and kept every gift she received. The Museum of Contemporary Photography presents the project—its gifts placed in vitrines and shown with records of each year—as a central example of Calle’s autobiographical work*13. A birthday is a day on which affection is expressed, though feeling cannot be measured. By fixing the number of guests and refusing to discard the gifts, Calle translated the uncertainty of being loved into countable visitors and preservable objects. The items in the cases cannot convey the depth of each relationship. The ritual intended to provide reassurance also exposes her need to verify affection through possessions.

    Douleur exquise: Repeating Her Breakup Alongside Other People’s Pain

    Douleur exquise (1984–2003) has two parts. The first counts the days of a journey until Calle was due to meet her lover as “days until unhappiness”; the second repeatedly retells the breakup. Calle’s photograph and the same account recur beside testimonies she gathered about other people’s “most painful experience,” while her own text and image fade with each repetition. Centre Pompidou describes the alternation of her pain and others’ pain across seventy-two pairs of photographs and embroidered texts, and reads the disappearance of Calle’s account as a metaphor for recovery*14.

    The work establishes recovery through repeated narration, comparison with other people’s testimony, and the gradual erasure of Calle’s own image within the display. Personal experience becomes material that can be compared, relativized, and altered through editing. Take Care of Yourself extends the structure tested here—opening one’s pain to other people’s words—into specialist knowledge and collective production.

    Take Care of Yourself: Giving a Breakup Email to 107 Women

    Take Care of Yourself (2007) takes its title from the final words of a breakup email sent by Calle’s lover. She gave the message to 107 women, including a linguist, lawyer, proofreader, psychoanalyst, singer, actor, and sharpshooter. Each responded through her profession or skill, producing grammatical analyses, legal judgments, performances, translations, and enactments. Paula Cooper Gallery describes the project’s photographs, texts, and videos as opening a private breakup letter to numerous specialist readings*15. Calle gave others a text she could not fully read for herself instead of directly confessing the emotion of the breakup. She enlisted women’s knowledge, voices, and bodies to loosen the hold of one man’s words.

    The 107 responses do not converge on a single conclusion. Some read the email legally, others identify grammatical contradictions, turn it into music, or make it comic. A single message acquires different meanings across professions, languages, and cultures, and Calle’s breakup is dispersed among multiple readings. Presented in the French Pavilion at the 2007 Venice Biennale, the private letter and personal loss became a large-scale exhibition about language, gender, specialist labor, and collective production*16. The official archive of the participants and their interpretations further conveys the scale on which a private email became a many-voiced work in an international exhibition*17.

    The Last Image and Voir la mer: Those Who Cannot See and Those Seeing the Sea for the First Time

    In The Last Image (2010), Calle asked people who had lost their sight to describe the last thing they had seen, combining their portraits, spoken memories, and photographs that evoked those final images*18. In Voir la mer (2011), she brought Istanbul residents who had never seen the sea to the shore and filmed their backs as they looked, followed by their faces as they turned around*19. The last-seen image cannot be reconstructed, and the turned face cannot settle the emotion of seeing the sea for the first time. The participant’s words, Calle’s images, and the viewer’s imagination remain out of alignment. Working through photography, Calle uses the gap between testimony and image to show that experience cannot be fully replaced by a photograph.

    Calle moves among photographs, newspaper serials, artist’s books, video, sound, letters, and display cases according to the work. The newspaper format of The Address Book brought personal information to a mass readership; video and audio in Take Care of Yourself preserve 107 modes of interpretation; and the vitrines of The Birthday Ceremony give lasting form to her refusal to discard the gifts. Each medium determines who receives private information, at what scale, and for how long. The reach of that disclosure forms part of the work’s ethics.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History: Consent, Surveillance, and the Right to Tell

    Keeping the Imbalance of Power Produced by Pursuit and Intrusion Within the Work

    The hotel guests in The Hotel, the owner of The Address Book, and the man pursued in Suite Vénitienne could not determine in advance how they would become works of art. Calle carried forms of access obtained through hotel employment, a found object, and a chance encounter into museums and newspapers. The Walker Art Center connects her work to surveillance, privacy, and oversharing, and points to the viewer’s complicity in the curiosity of looking into another life*20. The work’s attraction depends on the pursuer’s ingenuity and on an imbalance in which the pursued cannot participate in editing or publishing the information.

    Surveillance cameras, social media, search histories, and location data have made it ordinary to infer another person’s life from fragments. In Calle’s work, small amounts of information—an address book, possessions, letters, blurred photographs—stimulate the viewer’s desire to know more. That desire propels the reading of the work and enables the intrusion. The ethical tension belongs to the act of viewing itself.

    Feminism and Autofiction: Self-Disclosure and Investigative Power

    Feminist art after the 1970s approached love, domestic labor, sexuality, mother-daughter relationships, and loss—experiences treated as private—as questions of social institutions and gender*21. Calle makes public her own relationships, her mother’s death, birthdays, and heartbreak. She also uses detective inquiry, lists, recorded times, and specialist analysis to organize emotion as evidence. The wounded participant and the author able to select and edit other people’s information coexist within the work. Its feminist difficulty lies in holding together a woman’s right to narrate her own experience and the danger that her narration may intrude into another person’s private life.

    Calle’s work meets autofiction in the author’s transformation of herself into a role within a narrative, as well as in the mixture of fact and invention. She edits herself as “the woman who follows,” “the abandoned woman,” or “the daughter watching her mother die,” and repeatedly performs those roles. As the Walker Art Center explains of The Shadow, which places a detective’s report beside Calle’s own account, the difference between a self recorded by another person and a self narrated by the artist also becomes the work*20. The publication of private life appears as an editorial act that reveals who selected the evidence and established the resulting persona.

    Gotham Handbook (1994) emerged from an exchange with Paul Auster, who had modeled the character Maria in his novel Leviathan on Calle. Calle then moved herself closer to Auster’s fictional figure by carrying out his “instructions for beautifying life in New York,” decorating a telephone booth and engaging passersby in photographs and written reports. Centre Pompidou describes the collaboration as a work in which a real artist performs a fictional character, reversing the causal relation between life and novel*22.

    After Calle: Examining the Investigating Author Within the Work

    In news captions and socially oriented photo essays, writing has often identified locations and events while suppressing the photographer’s personal emotion. Calle’s journals record waiting in ambush, excitement, misrecognition, jealousy, and editorial decisions, placing the photographer under the same scrutiny as the subject. In Suite Vénitienne, the pursuer’s fixation; in The Hotel, access granted by the chambermaid’s job; and in The Address Book, the authority to select other people’s testimony carry the same weight as the photographed objects. The work asks both what photography reveals about another person and from what position the author produced that knowledge.

    A central method Calle passed on was to make the author accountable for the actions surrounding the production of a photograph. Take Care of Yourself delegates the interpretation of heartbreak to 107 women, while The Shadow sets a detective’s record against Calle’s own account. The Hasselblad Foundation has recognized her considerable influence on later generations*8. The Walker Art Center further argues that the self-staging, pursuit, and voyeurism of her early work anticipated a social-media culture that publicizes one’s own life while tracking the lives of others*20. In autobiographical and investigative work after Calle, whom an artist follows, what she commissions, which information she publishes, and the role in which she stages herself can be examined as concrete production choices that shape both meaning and ethics.

    One culmination of this method was À toi de faire, ma mignonne, Calle’s 2023 exhibition at the Musée national Picasso-Paris. Across the entire museum, she connected new and earlier works concerning the absence of Picasso’s paintings, the deaths of her mother and father, cats, and visual impairment. The museum presented her long texts as integral parts of the work. The early pairing of photograph and journal had developed into an exhibition in which viewers read personal history and institutional memory while moving through an entire building*23.

    • Sherrie Levine — A Pictures Generation artist who reconsidered authorship and the ownership of existing images.
