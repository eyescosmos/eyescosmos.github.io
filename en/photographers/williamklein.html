    William Klein

    ウィリアム・クライン 1926–2022
    CountryUnited States Period1950–1960s ChannelCity and moment · STREET
    Abstract

    William Klein was a photographer and filmmaker who brought a background in painting and graphic design to urban photobooks through wide-angle lenses, close proximity, blur, grain, high contrast, and typography. In New York, rough street images collide with spreads and type, opposing the conventions of orderly humanist photography and photobook design. His fashion work and films form part of the same criticism of the image industries.

    What this photographer changed

    Klein joined blur, grain, and distortion—qualities often treated as technical failures—to printing, layout, and cinematic sequence. He integrated rough street images into spreads, typography, heavy black printing, and sequence, turning the city from a set of individual photographs into a printed field where advertising and crowds collide He moved across exposure, darkroom work, book design, fashion, and film as parts of one visual experiment.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From New York to Paris: Fernand Léger and Abstract Painting

    Born in New York, Klein moved to Paris after World War II and studied with Fernand Léger. He first made geometric paintings and movable panels, then entered photography through experiments in photographing and enlarging those forms*1. Because he did not begin within orthodox photographic training, he did not automatically avoid blur, grain, or compositional disorder as defects. He learned to treat them as large forms and rhythms made for print.

    From Abstract Painting to the Camera: Fixing Light in Motion

    Klein studied with Fernand Léger in Paris and made geometric paintings and architectural panels. His move into photography came through experiments that transformed rotating panels, light, lettering, and repeated forms into photographic images, as well as through a desire to record the street. ICP’s retrospective brought painting, photograms, photobooks, fashion, and film together because Klein’s photographic language did not develop in isolation from these other media*1.

    Against the Photography Magazines and Humanist Photography of the 1950s

    Humanist photography circulated widely in postwar Europe, presenting humane street moments through balanced composition. Based in Paris, Klein returned to his native New York and photographed it as an aggressive, noisy city, giving prominence to advertising, violence, crowds, and provocative children rather than warmth or lyricism. ICP’s retrospective positioned him as an artist working across photography, painting, film, and design who challenged established aesthetic rules*1.

    What Did Klein Oppose? The Beautiful Picture and the Benevolent Image of the City

    In the magazines and photobooks of the 1950s, balanced composition, the decisive moment, and sympathy toward human subjects formed a highly influential model of urban vision. When Klein returned to New York, he entered the crowd and included people’s reactions to being photographed instead of maintaining the distance of an observer. His recollection of conceiving New York as a kind of “tabloid gone berserk” shows that roughness was chosen through anger at the city and criticism of printed media*19.

    From Architectural Panels to Photograms: Photography as Movement on a Plane

    Klein made rotating panels for architectural spaces and photographed them with long exposures, producing abstract images of light traces and geometric forms. These photographs construct movement across a flat surface instead of explaining external objects. Seen in relation to these experiments, the tilt and typography of his later street work are supported by a formal sense that designs the entire image as a forceful shape. Even after turning to recognizable subjects, Klein continued to view the city as an arrangement of abstract forces.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    New York 1954–55: Why Did Urban Photography Become So Rough?

    The 1956 photobook Life Is Good & Good for You in New York collides children, crowds, advertisements, guns, festivals, and signs through photographs of different scales and blocks of type. It produces urban pressure through page-turning speed, abrupt enlargements, and black space instead of presenting the city through one balanced composition*1. Rough images became a visual language of the city through the editing of the whole book, not through chance at exposure alone.

    Wide-Angle Lenses and Proximity: Breaking the Distance of Street Photography

    A wide-angle lens includes a broad field, but at close range it also enlarges and distorts nearby faces and hands while pushing the background away. Klein used these properties to abandon the polite distance often maintained in street photography. In the photograph of a boy aiming a gun at the camera, the photographer’s presence provokes the subject’s response, and that tension becomes the image*3. The method makes contact and provocation visible rather than stealing an unnoticed moment.

    Blur, Soft Focus, and Grain: The Conditions That Turned Failure into Expression

    Klein did not eliminate blur, soft focus, or grain produced by dim light, fast movement, high-speed film, and enlargement. As contours break down, figures mix with signs and illumination, retaining the pressure of the crowd within the image. Tate presents his work as breaking conventional technical rules in order to produce a raw urban experience*4. Selection after shooting and the design of the printed page transformed individual imperfect frames into a coherent expression.

    The Idea of Roughness: Rethinking Photographic Correctness from the Printed Page

    Blur, soft focus, grain, and high contrast intensify urban disorder while arguing against standards that measured photographic quality by smoothness and tonal range. Klein inserted images rejected by technical instruction into mass print and unsettled the institutional question of what counted as a “good photograph” on the page. He did not treat the negative as sacred, using cropping, enlargement, blocked shadows, and collisions with type. The core of the method lies in moving photographic value from transparent information in the negative to the bodily speed produced by the printed page.

    Type and the Double-Page Spread: Constructing the City Beyond a Single Photograph

    In New York, photographs are cut to different sizes, cross the gutter, and collide with black space and large typography. Pages reject the quiet frame of art-viewing and move with a rhythm closer to newspapers, advertising, and film montage. ICP’s William Klein: YES treated his photography, graphic work, and films as one activity*1. Urban excess is produced through editing and design in reproduction as much as through the techniques used in the street.

    The New York Book That Was Not Published in America: Criticism Embedded in Form

    Life Is Good & Good for You in New York was published in France in 1956, but American publishers initially rejected it. Its crowded combinations of multiple images, headlines, black printing, and comic-strip-like sequences broke the convention of placing one photograph on a white right-hand page and joined a negative image of the city to an unfamiliar book form*19. Klein’s contemporary criticism therefore resided in more than subject matter. He brought the speed of newspapers, advertising, and cinema into a medium that had often served as a quiet container for art photographs.

    Fashion at Vogue: Moving the Disorder of the Street into Staging

    Beginning in the mid-1950s, Klein made fashion photographs for Vogue, placing models in streets, mirrors, rotating light, and wide-angle distortion. Fashion had to display commodities clearly, yet it also became a site for experiments with pose, city, and printed page*1. Street work used contingent encounters while fashion constructed situations, but both organized collisions between camera and subject within the frame.

    Children and the Camera: Street Aggression as a Joint Production

    Children in New York approached Klein’s camera, aimed toy guns, laughed, and exaggerated gestures. The camera provoked a response, and the aggressive image was jointly produced in a short encounter between the photographer’s proximity and the subject’s performance. Klein’s street photographs occupy a middle ground between distant observation and fully scripted staging.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Rome, Moscow, and Tokyo: Comparing the City Books

    After New York, Klein made photobooks on Rome, Moscow, and Tokyo. He did not simply impose one style on each city; page rhythms changed according to political events, street lettering, advertising, and the density of crowds. In Tokyo, signs, student protests, sumo, and urban imagery collide at high speed, constructing a media city that exceeds an outsider’s exoticism. Comparison among the books shows that Klein’s method responded to the image environment of each place.

    Who Are You, Polly Maggoo?: Critiquing Fashion from Within

    Klein also directed films, and Who Are You, Polly Maggoo? satirized the desires and fictions of the fashion industry. Experience working inside magazines became, in film, a criticism of the spectacle produced by models, editors, television, and consumers*1. Through sound, performance, and duration, the film exposes the mechanisms by which the image industry turns bodies into commodities.

    Contacts, Prints, and Reediting: “Anti-Photography” Was Precisely Constructed

    Klein’s photographs appear spontaneous, but they were carefully built through contact-sheet selection, cropping, enlargement, dense printing, and placement across spreads. He chose forceful collisions from several frames and combined them with type and space, converting disorder at the site into the rhythm of the book. His later contact paintings brought crop marks and editorial notation—normally hidden—to the surface of the work, exposing the decisions behind the rough style.

    Contact Paintings: Intervening in the Image After Exposure

    Klein enlarged selection frames and symbols from contact sheets and added red lines and writing as finished works. Marks usually hidden behind editing move to the foreground, making the choice of image and point of cropping part of the subject. The photograph acquires another form through selection, cropping, and printing after the shutter. He intervened in mechanical record after exposure and turned the trace of editing itself into an artwork.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    Klein and Robert Frank: Editing Alienation or Colliding with the City

    Klein and Robert Frank both addressed 1950s America critically through photobooks, but their methods differ. Frank’s The Americans edits fragments of a road trip through quiet intervals and repetition, producing an image of national isolation. Klein’s New York approaches his native city physically and compresses type, faces, and black-and-white collisions into the page. The distinction concerns distance from the city and photobook rhythm more than rank: one uses narration, the other an aggressive visual assault.

    Klein’s Influence on Daido Moriyama: From Grain to the Urban Media Environment

    Klein had a major impact on Daido Moriyama, and Tate Modern organized a large joint exhibition in 2012*8. Their common ground includes grain and blur, but also a view of the city as a place where advertising, print, and existing images circulate excessively and can be reedited in photobooks. Moriyama dissolves the self into the anonymous city and reproduced imagery; Klein retains direct confrontation with subjects and a strongly graphic authorship.

    A Place in History That Does Not Separate Photography, Fashion, and Film

    Klein’s street photography, painting, design, fashion, and film connect as a mobile criticism of media. ICP’s YES retrospective likewise presented these areas as mutually influential activities rather than a linear development by genre*1. The gesture of approaching subjects in the street and the editing that turns an entire publication into a field of visual collision belong to one production, questioning photographic quality and correctness from within mass culture.

    Fashion and Film: Critiquing the Image Industry from Within

    In Vogue, Klein drew models into streets, mirrors, wide-angle lenses, and groups of figures, turning photographs that explained clothing into visual events. His later film Who Are You, Polly Maggoo? satirized the exaggerations of the fashion industry and the consumption of women’s images*20. Participation in commercial media and criticism of the system occurred inside the same career. Klein used magazines and film to make visible the way images produce public identities and desire. The aggression of his urban photography shares this foundation in media criticism.

    The Limits of the Label “Anti-Photography”: What Happened After a New Norm Emerged?

    Klein’s work was initially described as rough, ugly, technically improper “anti-photography.” As blur, grain, and wide-angle distortion later became familiar devices, they were repeated as forceful effects of urban photography. His significance extends beyond inventing a surface style. He organized criticism of the 1950s city and print media through broken images, spreads, typography, and cinematic time, delivering that criticism to different publics.
