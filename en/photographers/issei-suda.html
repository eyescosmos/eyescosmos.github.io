    Issei Suda

    須田一政 1970s
    CountryJapan Period1970–1980s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Issei Suda photographed Tokyo alleyways, regional festivals, passersby, signs, and plants with a Rolleiflex in the 6×6 format. Around 1968, Provoke challenged the clarity of press and advertising photography through are-bure-boke—grainy, blurry, out-of-focus images—while Compora photography shifted attention from events to everyday life. Suda rejected any affinity with either tendency, saying they had “absolutely nothing to do” with him. Fushikaden, Waga Tokyo 100, and Human Memory show why he placed his response at the moment of exposure ahead of the ideas of a movement. They also reveal how precise square frames turned ordinary scenes into images whose circumstances remain unresolved.

    What this photographer changed

    Suda established a mode of street photography in which everything is sharply rendered, yet what is happening cannot be determined. The 6×6 negative records faces, clothing, lettering, and plants in detail, while the square frame cuts away the before and after of an event and gives people and objects equal visual weight. Provoke disrupted photographic transparency with grain, blur, and harsh contrast. Suda kept the surface clear while leaving the clues needed to explain the situation outside the frame.

    § 01 / 04 Issei Suda’s Starting Point: Rejecting Provoke and Compora

    Learning Composition from Photobooks and Traveling to Mount Osore

    Issei Suda was born in Kanda, Tokyo, and graduated from the Tokyo College of Photography in 1962. In an interview with the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum, he recalled that he “entered photography through looking more than through taking pictures.” He repeatedly studied photobooks by William Klein, Richard Avedon, and Irving Penn in foreign-language bookstores. When he tried to imitate one of Penn’s still lifes, he found that assembling the same kinds of objects did not reproduce its composition or color. The experience taught him that the structure of an image was a separate problem from the objects it depicted*3. While still at photography school, Hiroshi Hamaya’s Ura Nihon prompted him to travel to Japan’s snow country. In 1963, his series Osorezan received Nippon Camera’s annual award for its monthly competition*1. Looking closely at photographs and walking through unfamiliar places became the two foundations of his early work.

    The Avant-Garde of Tenjo Sajiki and a Practice Apart from Photographic Movements

    From 1967 to 1970, Suda worked as the staff photographer for Shuji Terayama’s experimental theater company Tenjo Sajiki*1. Tadanori Yokoo, Aquirax Uno, and others took part in an environment where theater, posters, film, and costume overlapped. Photography historian Ferdinand Brüggemann describes Tenjo Sajiki as a network connecting the avant-garde culture of the 1960s and situates Suda’s work within a postwar Japan where tradition and modernity coexisted*9. Suda did not derive a theory of street photography from Tenjo Sajiki. What he later emphasized was the intensity of a place directed toward making work, more than any inheritance of Terayama’s ideas*3.

    Photography in 1968: The Crisis of Reportage, Provoke, and Compora

    Around 1968, several developments in Japanese photography challenged the assumption that the medium could objectively transmit reality. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum places several events within the same moment of reassessment: the documentation of student protests, the exhibition Shashin 100-nen (“100 Years of Photography”), Camera Mainichi’s feature on Compora photography, and the launch of Provoke: Provocative Materials for Thought*5. Provoke used grain, blur, unstable focus, and severe contrast against the orderly language of press and commercial photography*6. Compora was less a movement with a unified manifesto than a label applied by editors and critics to a tendency that left events and heroes behind, photographing interiors, alleys, families, and anonymous passersby in a restrained register*7.

    “It Had Absolutely Nothing to Do with Me”: Refusing to Let Theory Precede the Photograph

    In 2014, asked whether he had ever felt an affinity with Provoke, Compora, or political movements, Suda replied, “It had absolutely nothing to do with me,” adding that his photographs concerned only his own interests*4. In another interview, he said that criticism could fix a style in place and extinguish the “excitement” of the next subject; one must not “let oneself be caught up in it”*3. These remarks indicate a refusal to let a movement’s ideology or a work’s meaning be settled before the photograph was taken, not an indifference to society or photographic history. Suda described his starting point as a “startling surface” that appeared when an object was viewed from a slightly different angle*3.

    His relation to contemporary photography becomes more concrete when the publication venues and finished images are considered together. Suda’s work appeared in Camera Mainichi and Nippon Camera, and was read within the same magazine culture that carried debates around Provoke and Compora. The Centre de la photographie de Mougins contrasts Provoke’s blur, grain, and intense contrast with Suda’s precise placement of subjects within the Rolleiflex square and his avoidance of overt graphic effects*8. Brüggemann likewise reads Suda’s close observation and suspended tension as a response distinct from Provoke’s aggressive imagery*9. Suda shared contemporary subjects such as passersby and alleyways, yet kept the image intact and left the event’s before and after, along with the person’s purpose, outside the frame.

    § 02 / 04 Walking and the 6×6 Format: How Suda’s Snapshot Method Took Shape

    Photographing Reflexively in Places He Walked Again and Again

    Suda carried a camera at all times and released the shutter reflexively while walking. FUJIFILM SQUARE described him as “the camera on two legs”*11. He could not work in exactly the same way in an unfamiliar place. Discussing his photographs of Kamagasaki, he described the difficulty of photographing before he had become accustomed to the area and noted that he had repeatedly visited the old downtown neighborhoods of Tokyo where he usually worked*4. Before a brief exposure came the time needed to learn the width of a street, the distance between people, the direction of light, and the pace of the city through the body. He did not wait for an event with an established meaning. He released the shutter when the direction of a face, an arm, a sign, a potted plant, and the pavement briefly formed a single arrangement.

    The Precision of 6×6: Legible Detail, Indeterminate Circumstance

    For much of his major work in the 1970s, Suda used a Rolleiflex in the 6×6 format. The JCII Photo Salon describes Fushikaden through its square frame, bold cropping, and finely detailed black-and-white rendering*10. The square lacks the strong directional pull of a horizontal frame, allowing a person, lettering, plants, and the street surface to press against one another within the same area. Wrinkles and fabric patterns remain legible, but cropped bodies and off-frame gazes cannot be reconstructed. Provoke destabilized the image itself. In Suda’s photographs, the surface remains sharp while the explanation of the situation is incomplete.

    The Mougins exhibition materials note that Suda avoided the climax of festivals, choosing moments that seem to fall just before or after an event and creating tension within exact compositions*8. A dancer is held between gestures, a child looks beyond the frame, and an object by the roadside carries the same visual weight as a person. The estrangement here does not require an abstract theme of an “other world.” It results from specific decisions of timing, distance, and cropping that withhold the causal sequence of everyday life.

    Looking Away from Landmarks in His Color Work

    The series Nihon no fūkei: toshi no shūen, photographed from 1982 to 1986, consists of 6×6 color transparencies. Suda wrote that he stopped at commonplace scenes around famous sights and picturesque views. If the landmark was the “light,” the images he gathered were everyday “shadows” falling across the same surface*11. Color did not change his method. He continued to avoid comprehensive views of tourist sites and concentrated on local arrangements formed by paint, clothing, signs, and artificial light. His form depended on walking, the square, and the cut beyond the frame, rather than the darkness or grain of black-and-white photography.

    § 03 / 04 Fushikaden, Waga Tokyo 100, and Human Memory

    Fushikaden: The Magazine Series, the 1978 Edition, and the Complete 2012 Edition

    Fushikaden appeared in eight portfolios in Camera Mainichi between December 1975 and December 1977, and was published as a photobook by Asahi Sonorama in 1978. Suda selected 138 photographs; the first edition included 100, while all 138 were published in 2012*8. Because the magazine pages, first edition, and complete edition differ in both number and order, the same body of photographs survives in several forms shaped by its medium. The official gallery shows festivals, children, streets, plants, and signs unfolding across a sequence of square images*14.

    The title comes from Zeami’s treatise on Noh, Fūshikaden. Suda said Zeami’s book contained everything from the attitude required to make photographs to the way they should be taken. He believed the subject was borrowed and that the photographer himself appeared in the picture*10. The title points to a way of photographing performers, spectators, and things in the street as forms encountered at the instant of his response, without trying to supply an ethnographic explanation of the festival.

    Waga Tokyo 100: Shitamachi Residents and the Objects Around Them

    Issei Suda: Waga Tokyo 100 was published in 1979 and assembled one hundred photographs made in Tokyo’s shitamachi, or old downtown neighborhoods, between 1976 and 1978*2. The book contains fewer comprehensive architectural views than alleys, fire drills, entertainment districts, passersby, and objects outside shops. The 2013 edition rearranged the same body of photographs and included 99 images*16. Shashasha’s bibliographic note likewise describes the book as centering portraits of residents within their surroundings more than the architecture or streets of Tokyo themselves*18. The different editions show that the photobook’s adjacencies were not fixed. A gaze, a shadow, or a bright white object created different correspondences each time it was paired with a photograph from another place.

    Human Memory: Mixing Periods and Placing Urban Individuals Side by Side

    Monogusa Shūi was published in Nippon Camera in 1980 and compressed people, animals, signs, plants, and fragments of interiors and exteriors without making festivals its subject*15. The 1996 photobook Human Memory drew from multiple periods and regions outside chronological order and received the Domon Ken Award the following year*2. Brüggemann notes that, compared with the groups and festivals of Fushikaden, Human Memory contains more figures standing alone in the city; he relates this change to population concentration and the transformation of communal life*9. This reading does not stand in for Suda’s own social theory, but it provides a basis for connecting the shift in his subjects—from groups at festivals to isolated urban figures—with postwar urbanization.

    § 04 / 04 The “Other World,” the Ethics of the Snapshot, and Suda’s Place in Photographic History

    Explaining the “Other World” Through Angle, Distance, Cropping, and Timing

    Suda’s work has often been described through phrases such as “a fissure in everyday life,” hare and ke, and “uncanniness.” The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum’s 2013 exhibition likewise presented the photographs as glimpses from reality into another space*12. Suda resisted having his work fixed by a single evaluation and distanced himself from claims that he had deliberately photographed a folkloric darkness*3. Festival dancers and Tokyo passersby occupy the square with the same density as signs, potted plants, walls, and shadows. The strangeness of the images can be explained through camera angle, distance from the subject, the cropping of bodies, and the moment of exposure, without presuming a specifically Japanese spirituality.

    The Asymmetry Between Photographer and Subject in Unpermitted Snapshots

    Reflexive snapshot photography contains an asymmetry: the photographer alone determines the frame. Conscientious Photography Magazine notes that, in an interview published in a retrospective catalog, Suda acknowledged photographing people reflexively without permission and recognized that the act could be disrespectful*13. In his later work in Kamagasaki, he encountered residents’ suspicion and used a model to divert attention from the surrounding photographs he was making*4. This change requires Suda’s snapshots to be considered through local custom, the subject’s wariness, and the presence or absence of consent, rather than praised unconditionally as the expression of a “photographer’s eye.”

    A Different Street Photography of the 1970s, Not a Lesser Variant of Provoke

    Suda received awards and exhibitions in Japan from an early stage, while recognition in Europe and North America came later than it did for Daido Moriyama or Nobuyoshi Araki. Conscientious Photography Magazine argues that presentations of Japanese photography in the West often centered a small group of well-known artists and exhibitions emphasizing “Japanese otherness,” leaving few opportunities to encounter Suda through a sustained body of work*13. Provoke offered a readily legible combination of magazine, manifesto, and are-bure-boke. Suda rejected affinity with the movement and changed the selection and order of his photographs across magazine series, photobooks, and exhibitions, resisting reduction to a single slogan.

    It is more accurate to compare Suda and Provoke as separate responses to the same post-1968 crisis of photographic description than to connect them through direct influence. Provoke used a distressed image to attack the expectation that photography transparently transmitted reality. Suda rendered his subjects precisely while withholding the event’s causal sequence, the person’s purpose, and the time before and after the exposure. He shared everyday subject matter with Compora photography, but selected moments when people and objects collided within the square instead of sustaining a flat, cool observation. Rupturing the image, observing everyday life in a low register, and withholding explanation while preserving clear detail were parallel forms addressed to the same crisis in 1970s Japanese photography. Suda occupies the third route.

    Suda’s introduction abroad did not begin in the 2020s. According to SFMOMA’s research on exhibition history, he participated in Japan: A Self-Portrait at the International Center of Photography in New York in 1979, alongside Shomei Tomatsu, Daido Moriyama, Masahisa Fukase, Miyako Ishiuchi, and others*17. FOMU in Antwerp, however, described its 2021 exhibition My Japan as the first retrospective outside Japan to survey roughly six decades of his work*19. The long interval between an early group exhibition and a retrospective spanning the artist’s career shows how individual photographs could circulate abroad long before the full range of his magazine series, photobooks, and color work became widely available.

    The 2020 photobook 78 was the first posthumous publication, selecting 78 previously unpublished photographs made between 1971 and 1983 from roughly 500 prints preserved by his family. Introducing the book, the British Journal of Photography noted that Suda had received less attention in the West than artists of the Provoke period such as Daido Moriyama and Takuma Nakahira*20. Later photobooks and retrospectives have presented a body of work extending from precise square images and Tokyo’s old downtown neighborhoods to regional festivals and urban color photography, rather than treating Suda as a missing figure at Provoke’s margins.

    § REL Related photographers & movements
    Related photographers
    • Daido Moriyama — His grain, blur, and engagement with reproduction in Provoke offer a clear contrast with Suda's precise 6×6 images.
    • Shomei Tomatsu — He recast postwar society through subjective documentation and helped establish the ground on which Japanese photography after the 1960s reconsidered the form of the record.
    Related movements
    • Provoke — The contemporary magazine with which Suda denied any affinity; the difference between its distressed images and his precise squares is central.
    • Street Photography — A framework for considering Suda's rapid street exposures and his repeated walks through the same places.
    • Documentary Photography — A framework for considering photographs that record festivals and the city while omitting an explanation of events.
