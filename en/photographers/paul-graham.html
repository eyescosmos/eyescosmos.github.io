    Paul Graham

    ポール・グラハム
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelIssues in photo history · Conceptual
    Abstract

    In early-1980s Britain, when black-and-white photography was widely treated as the proper language of socially engaged documentary, Paul Graham self-published A1: The Great North Road in color. Beyond Caring records the fluorescent lighting and worn interiors of unemployment offices; Troubled Land uses flags and painted curbs in Northern Ireland as concrete signs of politics in everyday life. Graham brought together the color of Eggleston and Shore, the social observation of Evans and Frank, and the editorial logic of the photobook. Through both images and criticism, he argued that photography made by observing the world carries an authorship equal to that recognized in contemporary art.

    What this photographer changed

    British social documentary had long derived its public authority from the gravity of black and white, emblematic expressions of working-class subjects, and photographs that explained events. Graham treated the corporate colors and plastic interiors of service stations in A1, the blue-green walls and fluorescent light of welfare offices in Beyond Caring, and the flags and painted curbs of Troubled Land as social evidence. Color became information through which Thatcher-era consumption, administration, and sectarian affiliation could be read. In American Night, he further linked overexposure to the social invisibility of poverty. His work demonstrated how color, bureaucratic interiors, exposure, and book design could establish the public stakes of a social subject. Alongside other figures in British New Color, he played a central role in establishing a photobook culture in which the traces left by policy and class on everyday life could be read through color.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period: Bringing Color into British Social Documentary

    Learning from Eggleston to Photograph Beyond the Event

    Graham studied microbiology at the University of Bristol and taught himself photography through photobooks and the magazine Creative Camera*1. One turning point was William Eggleston’s Election Eve. Graham locates Eggleston’s significance beyond the use of color, in the freedom to photograph roadsides, interiors, evening light, and other places that journalism would normally relegate to the background*2. This approach abandons the prior summary of an event and the search for an image that illustrates it, using photography instead to discover what appears in the world. It led to Graham’s later attention to roads, waiting rooms, suburbs, and brief everyday actions. According to the British Council’s artist record, his first exhibition, House Portraits (1980), focused on suburban homes, showing that his interest in reading social class and historical traces in ordinary architecture preceded the road projects*3.

    Photobooks by Walker Evans, Robert Frank, and Garry Winogrand were also important. John Szarkowski’s exhibitions and writing presented them in the museum as authors who constructed distinct views of the world through selection, framing, and editing, rather than neutral reporters of events. Graham learned from this argument that directly photographing reality could coexist with the making of an artwork*4. Living in western England, at a distance from museums and photography schools, he used the photobook both to encounter work and to study photographic history and methods of editing.

    Publishing A1 in Color in a Black-and-White British Photography Culture

    Around the age of twenty-four, Graham was unemployed and chose not to pursue commercial photography under a client’s direction. He regarded the making of art from an already existing world as photography’s highest challenge*2. In early-1980s Britain, industrial restructuring and recession altered work and daily life from region to region. Graham repeatedly drove the A1 from London’s financial district through industrial cities to Scotland, photographing drivers, cafés, cheap lodgings, closed factories, and rain-soaked service stations. Economic policy appeared along one trunk road through labor, rest, consumption, and regional difference. He later described A1, Beyond Caring, and Troubled Land as a related body of work recording recession and early Thatcher-era Britain from different sites*5.

    According to Graham’s recollection, British photography at the time was governed by a strong hierarchy centered on black and white, and A1 was discussed more for the novelty of its color than for its social concern*2. Graham wanted to preserve the actual appearance of the 1980s: the reds and yellows of service stations, corporate logos, vinyl-covered chairs, road signs, and clothing. Corporate color and consumer culture, lost in translation to black and white, made class and regional differences concrete. The use of color did more than bring the formal influence of American New Color to Britain; it expanded the kinds of information social documentary could record.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work: Joining Color, Exposure, and Focus to Social Content

    A1: The Great North Road: Reading Class Along a Trunk Road

    A1: The Great North Road (1981–82) was made through repeated journeys along the trunk road from London to Edinburgh, photographing roadside cafés, gas stations, cheap lodgings, people on the road, and degraded land. In Graham’s official archive, the images are sequenced as a journey north from the vicinity of the Bank of England*6. Graham selected the places where road users rested, ate, and worked instead of landmarks. Aging cafés, tired drivers, and regional disparities appear within a continuous modern transport network, allowing the book to trace uneven changes in living conditions from Britain’s center toward the north.

    Graham self-published the book in a period with few specialist photobook publishers or distribution networks. Creative Review describes the self-edited and self-published A1 as a turning point in British color photography*7. MACK’s material for the reissue likewise records how the 1983 first edition was produced outside established publishing structures*8. Self-publishing allowed the movement of the road, weather, and differences among places to be read across the entire volume before a museum or newspaper selected a “representative” image. By making the photobook central to production, Graham could present social observation in color without adapting it to the standards of existing news media.

    Beyond Caring: Photographing Welfare Through Waiting-Room Color and Bodies

    Beyond Caring is a series made discreetly from a low position around knee or chest height in unemployment-benefit and social-security waiting rooms across Britain in the mid-1980s. Graham’s official archive dates the project to 1984–86*9. Whitechapel Gallery presents it as a major work recording people and bureaucratic interiors in color within welfare offices during the recession*10. MACK records that the core photographs were made in 1984 and self-published in 1985*11. People waiting on benches, children, dirty floors, notices, fluorescent fixtures, and damaged walls share the same frames. Unemployment appears as time spent on hard chairs waiting for a number to be called, rather than as the isolated instant of a dismissal or demonstration.

    The dull blue-green walls, vinyl chairs, and fluorescent light convey the cheapness and fatigue of the environment through which the state receives people. A close view of a distressed expression can make unemployment appear as an individual tragedy. Graham includes bodies and interiors within the same frame, showing how an institution makes people wait and holds them in the same posture for extended periods. Color records the quality of the welfare office as political information that a face alone cannot convey. He has said that he retained the tilts caused by carrying the camera around his neck and photographing from near knee level because they corresponded to the listlessness of waiting.*5.

    Troubled Land: Reading the Northern Ireland Conflict in Flags and Curbs

    Troubled Land (1984–86) photographs the Northern Ireland conflict away from soldiers, explosions, and direct confrontation. British flags, Irish tricolors, painted curbs, murals, election posters, and surveillance structures appear along suburban roads, housing estates, fields, and rain-wet street corners. Residents can read political allegiance through these colors and signs; outside viewers may initially pass over them as peaceful scenery. Graham’s official archive presents the series as an account of sectarian and national conflict permeating the everyday landscape*12.

    Graham photographed political divisions that persisted before and after specific events. The red, white, and blue of flags, the Irish tricolor, and painted curbs mark boundaries and affiliations within residential districts. Accurate color is necessary to understand the conflict. Missing the signs reveals the viewer’s lack of knowledge about the locality’s codes. MACK notes that the gradual discovery of curbs, flags, distant soldiers, and helicopters connects landscape and conflict photography while departing from established formulas of Northern Ireland reportage.*13.

    New Europe and Empty Heaven: From the British Trilogy to Nation and Memory

    After the British trilogy, Graham traveled through Europe around the end of the Cold War for New Europe (1988–93), combining history retained in present-day landscapes with the tensions of transnational consumer culture. In Empty Heaven (1989–95), he brought Japanese advertising, cherry blossoms, images of the emperor, and memories of war and the atomic bomb into contact. His official archive describes these works as addressing a Europe in which the past penetrates the present and a Japan where the wounds of World War II coexist with dreamlike consumer imagery*12. David Campany characterizes the roughly fifteen years after the three British projects as a period in which Graham sought forms capable of responding to more complex subjects in Europe and Japan*14.

    During this middle period, Graham’s documentary expanded from following a specific road or institution within one book toward an editing method that set historical memory against contemporary consumer imagery across several countries. As Campany’s account suggests, Graham accumulated experience in producing political meaning through discontinuities among photographs*14. That editorial experience carries into American Night, where excessively bright images alternate with saturated color photographs and social class appears as a difference in visual conditions.

    American Night: Separating the Visibility of Poverty and Wealth Through Exposure

    American Night (1998–2002) shows poor people walking at the edges of American cities, nearly disappearing into extremely bright photographs. Other images render large suburban houses and manicured lawns in dense, clear color. MoMA PS1 explained that the work crossed established divisions among reportage, portrait, and landscape to present wealth and poverty in American society as differences in visibility*15.

    The brightest photographs require viewers to strain to find the figure. Elsewhere, large suburban houses, lawns, and cars appear in dense color and sharp outline, readily attracting attention. The difference in exposure restages a social difference: poor people fall outside public attention, while affluent life is actively visualized through advertising and residential imagery. Graham does not erase the figures completely; their outlines remain. The effort required to see them makes the social mechanism by which poverty exists yet goes unnoticed part of the act of reading the photograph.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media: Following One Event Across Several Photographs

    a shimmer of possibility: Preserving the Course of an Action Beyond the Decisive Moment

    a shimmer of possibility (2004–06) consists of twelve series that follow small events in American daily life across a few images or several dozen. Subjects include a man mowing a lawn, people waiting for a bus, a woman carrying groceries, and a roadside worker cutting grass. In one sequence, a man drinking newly purchased milk is divided into several photographs: holding the bottle, raising it to his mouth, drinking, and walking away. MoMA presents the work as an account of everyday duration that a single decisive moment cannot contain*16. The Photographers’ Gallery recognized the form—twelve volumes of varying thickness that follow brief everyday scenes—when awarding it the 2009 Deutsche Börse Photography Prize*17.

    Drinking milk, mowing a lawn, and waiting for a bus have beginnings, hesitations, repetitions, and endings. Events a newspaper or magazine might compress into one representative photograph remain in varying numbers of images. Motion is missing between frames, and focus and distance shift, distinguishing the sequences from continuous film. Each series departs from news priorities that grant the most space to major events and gives an anonymous person’s everyday action its own duration.

    Accepting Chance and Locating Responsibility in Selection and Editing

    Graham explains that when working from the real world, the movement of passersby, light, and overlaps among figures enter the photograph beyond the artist’s intentions*18. The photographer cannot command them, but can decide where to stand, when to expose, which images to retain, and how to combine them. Graham distinguishes accepting chance from abandoning authorship and assumes editorial responsibility for the results of responding to unforeseen facts.

    In a shimmer of possibility, out-of-focus photographs and figures nearly leaving the frame remain when they convey the middle of an action. Standardizing every event to the same number and degree of finish would absorb their differences into a fixed authorial form. Graham selects images according to the repetitions and interruptions that actually occurred, shaping chance into a specific sequence. That choice locates responsibility within observational photography.

    Twelve Photobooks: Giving Each Event Its Own Duration

    The first edition of a shimmer of possibility did not bind the twelve events into a single book. It placed twelve booklets in a box. Readers handle them one by one, encountering separately the brief durations of a person drinking milk, waiting for a bus, or mowing a lawn. MoMA notes that the series range from a small number of photographs to extended sequences*16. The thickness of each booklet, the order in which it is handled, and the pause while changing volumes alter the speed at which each scene is read.

    Separating the twelve books prevents their figures from becoming characters in a single national or class narrative and gives each an independent duration. Differences in image count correspond to differences in repetition, delay, and movement during the encounter. The book design moves editing away from the selection of one representative picture and toward preserving a distinct course for each event, materially changing how the viewer reads the work.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History: Arguing for the Authorship of Observational Photography

    The Unreasonable Apple: The Author’s Work in Observational Photography

    In his 2010 essay “The Unreasonable Apple,” Graham objected to a tendency in contemporary art to recognize the constructions and performances of Jeff Wall, Cindy Sherman, and Thomas Demand as authorial labor while describing the work Evans, Frank, Winogrand, and Shore made in real streets as simply photographing what was around them. In staged photography, the labor of sets, costumes, and performance is visible. In observational photography, invention appears in discovering a place and moment within a world whose meaning is unsettled, defining the frame, selecting photographs, and editing them into a sequence*2.

    The essay gives critical language to decisions Graham had already enacted in his work. Beyond Caring treats waiting-room interiors and bodies as equally political information; Troubled Land maintains a distance at which signs of conflict remain small; American Night aligns exposure with social visibility; and a shimmer of possibility edits chance encounters into event-specific durations. The authorship of observational photography lies in this chain of decisions: what to discover in the world and at what distance and in what medium to preserve it.

    Differences from Martin Parr, Stephen Shore, and Chris Killip

    Martin Parr also used color in 1980s Britain to photograph consumption, leisure, and class. Parr’s hard flash, close distance, and vivid palette often turn social gestures toward irony or humor. Graham maintains greater distance and withholds quick judgment through worn interiors, weak light, and easily missed signs. From Stephen Shore he learned to photograph ordinary places in color and to value the photobook, but Graham addressed institutions and class more explicitly through Thatcher-era unemployment, Northern Ireland, and poverty in the United States. Chris Killip entered mining and fishing communities over long periods and recorded industrial decline in black and white. Graham approached the same social transformation through roads, benefit offices, political colors, and other surfaces where institutions enter everyday life.

    Parr, Shore, and Killip each renewed postwar documentary in different ways. Graham’s distinct contribution joins Shore and Eggleston’s color observation of everyday life to a concern with class and industrial change closer to Killip’s. He also departed from Parr’s hard flash and satire through overlooked color, low light, and long periods of waiting. A1, Beyond Caring, and Troubled Land added to a black-and-white documentary tradition centered on suffering faces a way of reading consumer objects, bureaucratic spaces, and local political signs through the color photobook.

    Reworking Social Documentary Within British New Color

    In 1980s Britain, several photographers turned to color alongside Graham. Whitechapel Gallery identifies A1 as an important early New Color work that challenged the belief that socially “serious” subjects in Britain should be photographed in black and white*10. Peter Fraser, Graham, Jem Southam, and Martin Parr also shared an interest in color photography through the loose grouping Photographers Over the Rainbow*19. Within that development, Graham connected British social documentary’s concern with class and industrial change to the color observation of everyday life after Eggleston and Shore. In A1, corporate color along the road corresponds to class difference; in Beyond Caring, welfare-office interiors to unemployment; in Troubled Land, local color schemes to sectarian division. Color became a means of reading the specific traces that policy, consumption, and affiliation leave on daily life.

    This development broadened the criteria by which the public force of social photography could be judged: beyond a grave black-and-white tone, the moment of an event, or an emblematic expression of suffering, toward color printing, bureaucratic space, exposure, and book design. From the self-published A1 to the museum presentation of American Night and the twelve volumes of a shimmer of possibility, Graham tied the politics of observation to the specific form of the work. “The Unreasonable Apple” articulated that practice as the author’s labor of discovering and editing places and moments from reality. His place in photographic history lies in making the historical responsibility of social documentary and the authorship recognized by contemporary art legible within the same body of work, as part of the wider development of British New Color.

    Later institutional reception has treated this long development as a single sustained achievement. In 2011, Whitechapel Gallery and Museum Folkwang organized a retrospective tracing Graham’s work from A1 in 1981 through 2006*20. MoMA has collected the interiors, people, and landscapes of A1 as a coherent body of work, making the continuity established by the photobook part of its assessment*21. The 2012 Hasselblad Award also internationally affirmed the achievement extending from British color documentary to later sequential work in the United States*22.

    Related photographers
    • Martin Parr — Introduced color into documentary in contemporary Britain and photographed the surfaces of consumer society through a different visual strategy.
    • Walker Evans — An American photographer whose photobooks and "documentary style" formed a starting point for Graham.
    • Robert Frank — An important precedent for assembling fragments of a nation through travel on the road and the form of the photobook.
    Related movements
    • Documentary — Renewed the form from within through color, exposure, and sequence while retaining social subjects.
    • Color Photography — Turned color once dismissed as commercial into concrete information about British roads, unemployment, and conflict.
    • Social Documentary — Shows institutions and class through waiting, interiors, and signs in the landscape instead of concentrating on events.
