    § 131 — Photographer Index — Dusseldorf School

    Thomas Ruff

    トーマス・ルフ 1958–
    CountryGermany Period1980–1990s ChannelIssues in photo history · Conceptual
    Abstract

    Thomas Ruff moved from Porträts—photographs of friends made under standardized conditions and enlarged to roughly two meters high—to astronomical negatives, press photographs, night-vision imagery, and pornographic and news images found online. He entered the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf intending to become a photojournalist. Under Bernd Becher, he began to treat subject, shooting conditions, serial structure, printing, and display as parts of a single work. Interieurs and Porträts showed him how a photograph’s meaning is shaped by decisions made before exposure and by the viewer’s inferences. He subsequently brought together the large-scale tableau, appropriated imagery, and digital processing.

    What this photographer changed

    Appropriation and large-scale photography both had precedents, but Thomas Ruff shifted the center of photographic production toward images already made and circulating through society. He placed passport-style portraits, astronomical observations, press photographs, night-vision imagery, pornography, and JPEGs on museum walls as monumental tableaux. In doing so, he joined the Bechers’ serial procedures, appropriation, large-scale color photography, and digital processing within a sustained body of work. Ruff showed that photography could involve selecting anew the use, manipulation, compression, support, and display of existing images, as well as making new exposures. He thereby gave concrete form to a contemporary model of authorship in which operating a camera is no longer the work’s only point of origin.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    Kunstakademie Düsseldorf—From Photojournalism to Interieurs and Porträts

    As a high school student, Ruff was torn between astronomy and photography. Hoping to travel the world as a photographer for magazines such as National Geographic, he entered the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf in 1977. He later recalled choosing Bernd Becher’s class because it was then the only program at a German art academy that treated photography on the same footing as painting. He assumed that a place producing good paintings could also teach him to make good photographs*1.

    The travel and landscape photographs Ruff brought to the academy ran directly against Becher’s method, and Ruff later described them as kitsch*18. Critiques with students in painting, sculpture, and film prompted him to examine photography’s capacities and limits and to develop a photographic language of his own*1. His move from picturesque foreign landscapes to the repeated, controlled study of nearby rooms in Interieurs marked the beginning of a process in which he determined the subject, light, framing, color, and sequence of a series. The same procedure continued when he later worked with found images: he examined for whom and to what standard an image had been made, then established its meaning through selection, alteration, printing, and display*16.

    Interieurs—Postwar West German Domestic Space and Color Photography

    Interieurs (1979–83), Ruff’s first sustained series, consists of small color prints showing corners of his own home and the homes of relatives, family friends, and peers. Through Bernd Becher, he became interested in interior photographs by Eugène Atget and Walker Evans and chose the domestic surroundings in which his generation had grown up and lived as his subject*42. Curtains, wallpaper, carpets, wooden furniture, and light reflected in mirrors convey taste, class, and period without showing the inhabitants. Ruff left the furniture in place and used available daylight, while framing each room to condense its distinctive character and atmosphere*42.

    Color preserved differences among wallpaper, wood, fabric, and paint as information about domestic history. The Bechers had established precise black-and-white description as their standard; Ruff chose color because he considered it necessary to describe everyday interiors*43. He ended the series in 1983 as many of the rooms were remodeled in newer styles during the early 1980s*42. The work taught him that photography records detail while framing and color shape an impression of the subject, and that the surfaces of furniture and walls alone can prompt viewers to construct an image of a life.

    From the Bechers’ Serial Method to Monumental Color Portraits

    The Bechers photographed water towers and blast furnaces in even overcast light, from nearly frontal viewpoints, and without people in the frame. They arranged structures of the same type in grids so that differences in form could be compared*6. Ruff inherited their use of standardized conditions, repetition, and serial comparison. He changed both subject and format: black-and-white industrial structures became color faces; distant buildings became close views lit by flash; and grids of multiple images gave way to single faces occupying the wall.

    Around 1980, Ruff began studying portraiture, then scarcely represented in contemporary art. Drawing on photographs he made of the Düsseldorf band EKG and covers of television listings magazines, he photographed friends and people of his generation. He chose ordinary clothing, composed expressions, and a framing close to a bust portrait; in the earliest photographs, sitters selected their own background colors. He gradually narrowed the conditions to a light, plain ground, a frontal pose, even lighting, and gestures that offered fewer cues to character*53. Their passport-photo frontalness also carried a particular tension in West Germany at a time of heightened sensitivity to identification checks and surveillance*26.

    Between 1984 and 1986, Ruff tested different print sizes. When he enlarged the portraits to roughly 210 by 165 centimeters in 1986, the sitters’ gazes and expressions—and the photograph’s own physical presence—became more forceful*53. Another impetus came when friends treated small prints as substitutes for the sitters even when those people were present in the room*17. At monumental scale, the face exceeded the handheld dimensions of a personal surrogate and made viewers conscious of a photograph of a person, rather than simply the person represented. The format emerged from Ruff’s reconsideration of portraiture, his interest in standardized facial images, the conflation of a sitter with an image, and his experiments with print size.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    Across the series that followed, Ruff moved from images he exposed himself to images already produced by other people and institutions. He controlled the shooting conditions in Porträts; a police composite system shaped andere Porträts; an observatory determined the form of Sterne; newspaper offices supplied Zeitungsfotos; and military night-vision equipment defined the look of Nächte. The source material for nudes and jpeg had been compressed, copied, and circulated online, detached from its original use and provenance. Ruff made work about what these images depict and about how the purposes of identification, scientific observation, news reporting, surveillance, sexual consumption, and transmission determine format, color, detail, and resolution.

    Porträts—How Monumental Portraits Changed the Viewing of Photographic Portraiture

    Porträts depicts friends and members of Ruff’s generation with a 4 × 5-inch view camera, flash, and a long-focus lens designed for photographing objects*28. From 1986 onward, the conditions were restricted to a light, plain background, a frontal pose, even lighting, and a restrained expression*15. Skin, hair, and clothing appear in precise detail. Smiles, gestures, domestic settings, and occupational attributes are removed from the frame. The National Portrait Gallery describes the series as portraiture that avoids psychological revelation and presents the face as a public sign*7.

    In front of the enlarged faces, viewers can study pores, blemishes, hairstyles, and clothing at length, yet occupation, character, and emotion remain indeterminate*20. Ruff’s question concerns photography’s ability to guarantee access to an inner self. The camera records skin, hair, clothing, and reflected light; viewers supply personality through conventions attached to expression, dress, class, and occupation*53. The Metropolitan Museum of Art’s Portrait (A. Siekmann) demonstrates the format of a roughly two-meter-high color portrait presented as a single wall-based work*29. Works such as Portrait (O. Cieslik) preserve the gap between facial information and knowledge of the person, prompting viewers to notice the cues from which they infer personality*8.

    andere Porträts—Police Facial Composites and Fictional Identities

    In andere Porträts (1994–95), Ruff shifted his attention from a camera that records faces to a device that constructs them. Using a Minolta composite system employed by German police in the 1970s to reconstruct suspects’ faces, he superimposed two sitters from Porträts to create people who did not exist. The final images were screenprinted, bringing forward an institutional model of the face as interchangeable components—forehead, eyes, nose, and mouth*34. The police device converts a witness’s fragmentary memory into a standardized face and presents that image as a plausible suspect. Porträts examined the distance between a real person and a photograph; this series asks under what circumstances an image depicting no single individual can still be accepted as a portrait.

    Sterne and Zeitungsfotos—Scientific and Press Images as Photographic Material

    Sterne grew from Ruff’s childhood interest in astronomy. His own telescope could not achieve the resolution he wanted, and he was unable to obtain permission to photograph at an observatory with a large telescope. He therefore purchased high-resolution negatives made for scientific observation by the European Southern Observatory. From a panorama of the southern sky, he selected a section and determined its orientation, crop, and print size, transferring scientific material into a wall-sized image of the stars*17. The telescope and technician that made the exposure, the research institution that preserved the negative, and Ruff, who selected the image, all participate in the work’s production.

    Zeitungsfotos began with press photographs Ruff had clipped from newspapers for roughly a decade. He removed their captions and printed them at a uniform size close to the width of two newspaper columns. Images once used to report international politics, economics, sports, and culture often become scenes whose location, time, and event cannot be identified. Enlargement also brings the halftone screen of newspaper reproduction to the image’s surface*19. The issue is less the recovery of a photographer’s intentions than the decisions made by observatories and newspaper offices: what they record, which portion they select, and what language accompanies an image into circulation.

    John G. Hatch reads the trajectory from Sterne to Cassini and ma.r.s. as an inquiry into how a universe invisible to the unaided eye appears photographically through telescopes, probes, data processing, and assigned color*35. Scientific images also involve choices of wavelength, the combination of multiple datasets, and color mapping. Ruff retains their scientific utility while showing how the same data can function as an observational record, a view of the cosmos, and a large-scale artwork. The works ask which instruments and procedures establish what counts as a “visible universe.”

    Nächte—Gulf War Night Vision and the Optics of Surveillance

    Nächte arose from the military night-vision footage repeatedly broadcast during the 1991 Gulf War. Ruff fitted a light-amplifying device to his camera and photographed houses, roads, and gardens in Düsseldorf at night. Green grain, dark circular vignetting, and indistinct contours formed a visual language viewers had learned to associate with the battlefield*17. When Ruff directed the same device at his own neighborhood, the houses, trees, and roads in Nacht 3 I also acquired connotations of surveillance, danger, and possible attack*9. The suburban subject, the device that amplifies faint light, and viewers’ memories of military imagery work together to make a quiet place appear as a zone under watch.

    A conversation between Ruff and Tobias Zielony in Camera Austria describes night vision as an “optical prosthesis”*38. The device increases visibility in darkness while prescribing color, brightness, and the shape of the image field for military and surveillance purposes. It produces a vision optimized for locating and identifying targets. Reviewing Ruff’s work in Frieze in 2002, Tom Morton cautioned that linking suburban gardens and bombed Baghdad through the same green color does not in itself settle the work’s politics*39. The series addresses the moment when a military way of seeing, learned through television news, is applied to another place.

    l.m.v.d.r.—Miesian Architecture and the Memory of Architectural Photography

    l.m.v.d.r. (1999–2001) began with a 1998 commission from Julian Heynen of the Krefeld museums to photograph Haus Lange and Haus Esters, designed by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, for their reopening after renovation. Following the Krefeld exhibition, Ruff also photographed buildings across Europe for MoMA’s Mies retrospective. For structures that no longer existed and could not be photographed, he used archival material and processed it digitally alongside his own images*52.

    Ruff assigned each building a method he had previously tested, including color shifts, inversion, superimposition, blur, and stereoscopic effects. Completed-building photographs, drawings, collages, photomontages, and exhibition catalogs had repeatedly presented Mies’s architecture as an emblem of modern transparency and rationality. Helen Robertson interprets the series as a dialogue with Mies’s own visual methods*36. The works address both the buildings’ present appearance and the images that shaped their reputation. By placing extant and lost structures, newly made photographs, and historical documents in one series, Ruff makes tangible the role photography has played in sustaining architectural memory.

    nudes—Internet Pornography and Large-Scale Photography

    nudes consists of low-resolution pornographic images downloaded from the internet around 1999, altered in color, blur, contour, and composition, and converted into large prints. On the slow connections of the period, small thumbnails and compressed files allowed users to identify a nude body quickly and move on to the next image. Ruff treated the transition from grain to pixel and the ease with which compressed files circulated by email and on the web as a single problem*17. When approaching the traditional genre of the nude, he chose material already circulating online instead of photographing new models*2. Enlarged and blurred, the body and sexual act remain legible from a distance; up close, color bleed and digital manipulation dominate. The Met describes Nudes ree07 as an image privately consumed online that entered a public museum and came into contact with the Western history of displaying the female nude*27.

    That transfer carries an ethical tension. The finished works provide no clear account of the source photographer, conditions of production, or subjects’ consent, while Ruff selects the images and circulates them through galleries and museums as signed, large-scale works. The analysis of sexual-image consumption coincides with the insertion of those images into another system of value. Geoff Dyer observed that the blur can lend the nude a painterly lyricism while leaving intact the discomfort of its origins in the pornography industry*25.

    Morton also notes that the source pornographic photographs were already staged through pose, décor, lighting, camera position, and image processing*39. Ruff adds a second layer of treatment—blur, enlargement, and museum display—to images made as commodities. Their inexpensive commercial character recedes, but bodily poses and sexual use remain. Color fields evocative of the classical nude and the consumption structure of pornography occupy the same picture.

    jpeg—Low-Resolution Images, JPEG Compression, and 9/11

    jpeg enlarges low-resolution images circulating online until the compression blocks, organized in units of 8 × 8 pixels, become visible. Ruff photographed the collapse of the World Trade Center in New York on September 11, 2001, but no image registered on his film. He abandoned the idea of producing a personal eyewitness photograph and collected images repeatedly transmitted around the world through news websites*1. From a distance, jpeg de01 reads as the collapsing World Trade Center; at close range, smoke, building, and sky break into square fields of color*11. In MoMA’s jpeg msh01, the image likewise becomes a compression grid as the viewer approaches—the reverse of the added detail normally expected from a large photograph*10. JPEG reduces data to accelerate transmission, and on a small screen its losses are hard to detect. In a monumental print, the blocks that enabled circulation become as materially present as the event they depict*19. Titles, memory, and repeated news coverage supply missing information, making the recognition of an event from a degraded image part of the work.

    Ingrid Hoelzl and Rémi Marie describe JPEG as a codec that divides an image into 8 × 8-pixel blocks, converts information into numerical values, and reconstructs it*33. The procedure decides which differences to retain and which to discard, regenerating the image whenever it is viewed. Ruff’s enlargement gives computational processing—normally recessive on a screen—the same visible presence as smoke and architecture. Drawing on Vilém Flusser’s theory of the apparatus, Ian Rothwell locates the unease of jpeg in the way users appear to control images while a codec’s program automatically performs the selection of information*31. Emphasized block noise demonstrates how post-exposure calculation determines appearance. Moderna Museet’s 2007 exhibition similarly described the series as an oscillation between “horror and fascination”: war or catastrophe at a distance, abstract fields of color up close*40.

    Vered Maimon argues that evaluating jpeg solely as a large, autonomous wall work suppresses its conceptual use of anonymous documentary imagery. With authorship and original purpose lost, the source images function as “appropriated readymades” within the lineage of photographic documents used by 1970s Conceptual art*47. Online images are at once pictures that carry meaning and numerical information stored and exchanged as file size, pixel count, and compression ratio. Ruff’s works hold together three states: the anonymous document, the computed image, and the large, editioned print.

    § 03 / 04 Key Works, Methods, and Media

    The previous section followed the kinds of images addressed by each series. This section turns to the forms Ruff gives the work: print, support, display, and book. Enlargement, Diasec mounting, paper negatives, halftone screens, and writing on photographic versos determine viewing distance and how an image is read.

    Large-Scale Photography and the Tableau Form—How Porträts Changed Photographic Display

    In art history, the “tableau form” describes a photograph produced as a self-contained wall work and viewed frontally by an unspecified public in an exhibition space. Jean-François Chevrier introduced the term in relation to photography of the 1980s to describe the move from small reproductions sequenced across magazine and photobook pages to autonomous works occupying gallery walls. Olivier Lugon notes that this shift made dimensions, support, installation, and viewing distance matters of artistic judgment on the same level as photographic content*44.

    The enlargement of Porträts grew from Ruff’s experience of small prints being read as substitutes for their sitters and from his experiments with scale between 1984 and 1986*17. Once the face exceeded the viewer’s body, a photograph used to identify someone became a work that confronts viewers as a photograph. Diasec mounting adheres the print’s surface to a transparent acrylic sheet and reinforces the back with aluminum or another rigid material, largely eliminating visible paper edges, mats, and conventional frames*23. The glossy plane projects from the wall and intensifies color and detail. The Met argues that this scale and direct mounting recall both museum history painting and street advertising, giving the photograph a palpable objecthood*6.

    Michael Fried explains the growing importance of contemporary photography in the same exhibition spaces as painting and sculpture through the relationship between the tableau form and the beholder, citing Porträts as a major example*45. A face that would fit in the palm as an identification photograph becomes a public wall seen by several people at once when enlarged to roughly two meters. Fried compares the frontalness of Porträts to Manet’s portraiture and discusses the works as monumental photographs that face the viewer*45.

    The faces neither avert their gaze nor offer gestures that explain emotion. Viewers approach to inspect pores, hair, skin tone, and even illumination, then step back to grasp the whole image. This movement alternates between reading personality from a face and seeing the photograph as a plane of grain and acrylic reflections. The gloss of Diasec can also reflect gallery lights and the viewer’s body, bringing the image surface and exhibition space together. The tableau form thus incorporates bodily position, distance, and movement into the work’s appearance.

    In Ruff’s large photographs, size, support, surface, serial order, spacing, and viewing distance become decisions comparable to subject and composition. The photograph moves from a reproducible image consumed in a magazine or photobook to a work occupying a particular wall and confronting the viewer’s body. At the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf, photography was discussed in the same critical setting as painting and sculpture; debate with students in other media supported this approach*1.

    In 1980s West Germany, new galleries emerged alongside the boom in Neo-Expressionist painting, while museum and market support for photography also expanded. Ruff initially expected to earn a living through commissions rather than the sale of artworks, but he later recalled that the emergence of monumental Porträts coincided with the growth of galleries and museums prepared to show photography*1. Enlargement began as a formal response to the confusion of portrait and sitter, and an institutional setting capable of receiving that format developed at the same time. The convergence of artistic need and exhibition infrastructure established Porträts within contemporary art galleries.

    Treating the tableau form as photography’s “maturation” or attainment of equality with painting can obscure other paths shaped by Conceptual art, institutions, and social circumstances. Matthew Biro warns that Fried’s history risks making confrontation with the beholder its central criterion*46. Maimon likewise reads jpeg as a readymade selected from anonymous documentary images and disputes the claim that large wall photographs superseded Conceptual art*47. Ruff’s tableaux convert reproducible, functional images into singular viewing experiences; large prints, rigid supports, and limited editions also turn them into scarce artworks. Their direct address on the wall remains in tension with photography’s capacities for reproduction, circulation, and reuse.

    From Maschinen to press++—Negatives, Halftones, Versos, and Supports

    In Maschinen (2003), Ruff scanned glass negatives originally made for 1930s machinery sales brochures, recolored the machines and their backgrounds, and produced large prints. Herta Wolf calls Ruff a “historian of photography” because the work joins procedures from different periods—glass negative, product catalog, archive, scanning, and digital coloration—within a single image*37. Earlier commercial images were already produced through reproduction and retouching, while present-day digital processing remains dependent on those negatives and printed matter. Analog and digital appear as a continuous history of alteration.

    Here, the “material structure of photography” means concrete traces that support an image’s making and use: the dimensions of photosensitive paper, the reversal of a negative, fibers and damage in paper, halftone screens in newspaper printing, and stamps or writing on the back of a photograph. After experimenting with conventional photograms, Ruff decided that fixed paper dimensions and color, along with the impossibility of repositioning objects, constrained his ideas. For Fotogramme, he constructed a virtual darkroom in 3D software containing a photosensitive surface, objects, lenses, and light sources*30. In Negative, he digitally inverted old photographs and presented the blue cast of the negative—formerly an intermediate stage in printing—as the finished work*2. Tripe | Ruff enlarges 1850s paper negatives from the V&A collection until fibers, discoloration, cracks, and clouds or vegetation drawn from the verso become legible*22. In press++, Ruff scans and superimposes the front of a news photograph with the agency stamps, dates, editors’ annotations, and cropping instructions on its back. Enlargement brings the history of post-exposure cropping, description, transmission, and reuse into a single field*19. Digital processing makes the work of paper, chemistry, printing, and editing part of the image.

    The paper negatives in Tripe | Ruff are documents of photographic technique and colonial records made by Linnaeus Tripe in India and Burma during the 1850s on official assignment for the British East India Company. When, at the V&A’s invitation, Ruff enlarged their fibers, cracks, and manual additions, the original purpose of the photographs—as knowledge organized by a governing power about architecture and land—remained embedded in the material’s provenance*22. Sean Willcock argues that accounts of colonial photography centered only on finished positive prints miss the instability produced by damage, chemical reactions, and climate in paper negatives*49. Ruff’s enlargement reveals that instability while retaining the power relations through which the images were made by military and colonial administrations and are now preserved in European museums. The conservation of photographic technique and the transformation of a colonial archive into contemporary art occur within the same work.

    As Interview Magazine’s account of press++ explains, the series scans the front and back of each photograph separately and superimposes them with partial transparency, allowing the reported scene and its history of use to be read together*41. Stamps, captions, dates, and crop marks record editorial decisions about what event an image represented, when it appeared, and which portion was published. Combining recto and verso places the labor of agencies and newspapers on the same plane as the photographed subject.

    Photobooks and Retrospectives—A Body of Series Without a Unified Look

    Ruff changes subject, apparatus, source image, and printing method from series to series, choosing a different appearance for each question. The V&A describes his work—from monumental portraits to NASA images of Mars, including the editing and manipulation of pictures made by others—as a sustained examination of photographic processes, technologies, and concepts*14. Whitechapel Gallery’s retrospective organized the years 1979 to 2017 through portraiture, suburbia, the cosmos, the nude, utopia, and catastrophe, presenting Ruff’s work as an inquiry into contemporary image culture across disparate subjects*3. MCA Chicago likewise presented Porträts, press photographs, night scenes, architecture, and internet imagery as multiple lines of investigation into photographic use and appearance*5.

    In Japan, a major 2016 retrospective traveled to the National Museum of Modern Art, Tokyo, and the 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art, Kanazawa; the Kanazawa presentation included roughly 160 works from eighteen series*12. Gallery Koyanagi has maintained records and images of Ruff’s Japanese solo exhibitions since 1998*13. In the photobook jpegs, images of catastrophe, landscape, architecture, and war unfold across successive spreads, making the reader experience through the time of page turning how different events circulate in the same compression format*21.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History

    Before Ruff, photomontage, Pop art, Conceptual art, and appropriation had already incorporated existing photographs into artworks*16. The tableau form—the presentation of large color photographs as autonomous wall works—had also become an important format in contemporary art from the late 1970s onward*44. Building on the serial procedures he learned from the Bechers, Ruff brought passport-style portraits, scientific photography, press photographs, military imagery, pornography, and JPEGs—distinct systems of image production—into one photographer’s sustained body of work. As Maimon’s reading of jpeg through anonymous documentary imagery and the readymade suggests, Ruff’s work holds together the monumental tableau and the documentary inheritance of Conceptual art*47.

    What Is Meta-Photography? Making Rules, Uses, and Processing the Subject of Photography

    MAMC+ uses “meta-photography” to describe Ruff’s examination, through photographs, of the rules and procedures by which a subject becomes an image*4. In Porträts, the photographer specifies sitter, background, lighting, and expression. In Sterne, Zeitungsfotos, and Nächte, the institutions and apparatuses of astronomical observation, news reporting, and military surveillance shape images for particular uses. In nudes and jpeg, source files are compressed, copied, and moved between screens, detached from their origins. Kunstsammlung Nordrhein-Westfalen notes how Ruff has made visible the technical circumstances and paths of storage associated with negatives, halftones, transmission, compression, archives, databases, and the internet*19. Campany likewise treats Ruff’s camera-made photographs and his found-image works as a continuous project*16. What connects them is the way portraiture, scientific evidence, news, pornography, and propaganda each generate distinct pictorial forms.

    Dan Adler groups the camera, passport-photo standards, police facial-composite machines, observatories, newspaper offices, night-vision devices, and image compression under the term “apparatus”*32. These technologies and institutions establish what information will be retained, what will be excluded, and what the image will be used for before a photographer makes a selection. Ruff inherited repetition and standardized conditions from the Bechers. The Bechers compared formal differences among industrial structures; Ruff compared rules of personal identification, scientific observation, editorial selection, military vision, and data compression.

    Critiques of Thomas Ruff—Decontextualization, Aestheticization, and Recommodification

    Ruff’s works can appear analytic and impassive, yet color, blur, gloss, and enlargement produce considerable visual pleasure. David Campany discusses how the pixels in jpeg make the image oscillate between a distant scene and a close field of color, turning digital loss into an attractive surface*24. Writing on nudes, Geoff Dyer notes the coexistence of images in which blur lends the body lyricism and others that retain the discomfort of the pornography industry*25. As The Met observes, transferring privately consumed sexual images from the internet into the museum’s lineage of the nude activates both the boundary between private and public and the tradition of viewing the female body*27.

    nudes analyzes the rapid consumption of pornographic images while making those images scarce, large-scale objects of viewing and exchange. In jpeg, smoke, flame, and destruction from terrorism or catastrophe can become beautiful grids of color. The Smithsonian explains that the pixelation in jpeg de01 prevents the inspection of detail and points to the instability of collective memory*11. Ruff does not remove beauty from critique; he includes the viewer’s attraction to an image within the processes of its production and consumption.

    Removing captions, dates, and locations from found images can flatten history. Zeitungsfotos and jpeg clarify how language supports the meaning of press photographs, but placing war, terrorism, and catastrophe together as images of the same type can push each event’s causes, victims, and responsibilities into the background. Will Fenstermaker argues that Ruff’s reduction of photographs to surface and media grammar separates them from the historical relations in which they arose*48. press++ restores the text and use history found on the verso, yet the labor and ownership of source photographers, agencies, and editors are still transferred into a work signed by Ruff.

    The more recent tableaux chinois uses propaganda photographs from La Chine, a magazine published by the Chinese Communist Party for European audiences during the Mao era, and superimposes the halftone dots of offset printing with the pixels of a digital image*51. The word “tableaux” invokes the tableau form, while small, mass-printed propaganda pictures are transformed into Diasec works roughly 2.4 meters high. Ruff brings forward the procedures by which analog printing and digital manipulation produce idealized political images. Their constructed character becomes visible, yet the works circulate again as appealing large photographs, renewing the tension between critique and recommodification.

    Within the Düsseldorf School—From the Photographer to the Conditions of Image Production

    The term “Düsseldorf School” identifies a shared educational background, but its artists hold markedly different views of photography. Thomas Struth places a camera in city streets, museums, family groups, and scientific institutions, retaining within a single photograph the relationship among architecture, institutions, history, and the people present. Andreas Gursky uses elevated viewpoints and exacting composition to construct scenes in which crowds, production, consumption, and tourism appear as integrated systems*6. Ruff made the forms through which people and places are pictured his central subject. Across Porträts, Sterne, Zeitungsfotos, Nächte, nudes, and jpeg, the person operating the shutter shifts among Ruff, scientific institutions, press photographers, machines, and anonymous image makers. Ruff’s decisions about source selection, retained information, processing, dimensions, and support connect the works. Authorship moves from the photographer’s signature at the moment of exposure toward the investigation of how an image is produced and used, and the determination of how it will be presented. The inclusion of found images, scanning, computation, archives, and online distribution within photography is often described as post-photographic. Since Porträts in the early 1980s, Ruff has tested across different image systems how pre-exposure setup and post-exposure processing determine photographic meaning*17.

    Claus Gunti studies the Düsseldorf School’s digital turn as a set of individual choices. From the 1990s onward, Ruff, Gursky, and Jörg Sasse adopted digital technologies, while other artists from the same educational setting kept film exposure at the center of their work*50. For Gursky, digital processing became a means of integrating fragments he had photographed into a comprehensive view of social space. Ruff used composite devices, scientific data, printing screens, and compression systems as points of departure regardless of who made the source exposure. Responses to digital technology produced different definitions, within the same generation, of the photographic original and the photographer’s work.

    Viewed across photographic history, Ruff joined appropriation, Conceptual art, the monumental tableau, and the digital image—previously distinct trajectories—within one sustained body of work*16. A photograph could now begin with the selection, reprocessing, support, and display of images already circulating through society, as well as with an exposure made by a photographer before the world. From Porträts to jpeg and press++, Ruff gave this possibility concrete form in series spanning analog and digital photography.

    Related photographers
    • Bernd and Hilla Becher — Teachers whose typologies, repetition, and controlled shooting conditions formed Ruff's point of departure
    • Andreas Gursky — A contemporary who directed large scale and digital processing toward crowds, markets, and global spaces
    • Thomas Struth — A contemporary who photographed cities, museums, families, and scientific institutions through an institutional gaze
    • Sherrie Levine — An artist who rephotographed other people's photographs; a point of comparison for Ruff's transformations of observatory, press, and internet images
    Related movements
    • Düsseldorf School — The educational setting in which Ruff learned serial work under standardized conditions and approached photography as an autonomous work of art
    • Typological Photography — A method that creates comparative viewing through series made under consistent conditions
    • Large-Scale Color Photography — The display format that made the faces in Porträts larger than the viewer and generated a mode of viewing distinct from small photographs
    • Conceptual Art — A lineage that places rules, selection, and rearrangement at the center of the work
