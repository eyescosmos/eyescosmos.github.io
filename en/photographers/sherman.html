Cindy Sherman

Cindy Sherman transformed photography from a medium associated with evidence, likeness, and authorial self-expression into a way of testing how cinema, advertising, magazines, and art history produce recognizable roles. Working alongside the Pictures Generation in late-1970s New York, she used her own body, costume, makeup, setting, and camera position to unsettle the idea that a single image can reveal a stable person.

Essay

Born in New Jersey in 1954, Cindy Sherman made her Untitled Film Stills between 1977 and 1980, a series of sixty-nine black-and-white photographs that seem to belong to 1950s and 1960s Hollywood movies, film noir, and European art cinema. Sherman acted as director, performer, stylist, and photographer all at once, staging herself as types such as the ingénue, the working woman, the vamp, or the lonely housewife*1. It remains the only major series she made outside the studio. Each photograph looks like a still from a film that does not actually exist, which gives the work its layered fiction. MoMA acquired the full set in 1995*1 and organized a complete exhibition of the series in 1996-97*2. The Film Stills are now understood as a foundational work of the Pictures Generation that emerged in late-1970s New York. Alongside artists such as Richard Prince, Sherrie Levine, and Robert Longo, Sherman used pre-existing image codes from mass media to expose the structures of power embedded in the pictures that circulate everywhere*3. Feminist criticism has treated the work as a dismantling of the mechanism through which women are offered up as objects for the gaze. Her 1981 Untitled #96, from the Centerfolds series originally commissioned by Artforum, set a then-record price for a photograph at Christie's in 2011*4. MoMA now holds dozens of her works*5, and Sherman is widely regarded as one of the essential figures of postmodern photography. Across later series such as Centerfolds, History Portraits, Disaster, and Sex Pictures, she continued to make performance, masquerade, and the instability of identity her central subject. By using herself while simultaneously withholding any stable "self," Sherman produced a practice that has been discussed not only in art history but also through theoretical frames associated with thinkers such as Roland Barthes and Jacques Lacan. A major MoMA retrospective in 2012 confirmed the scale of that achievement.
