    Yto Barrada

    イト・バラダ
    Country1990s Period1990–2000s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Yto Barrada photographed vacant lots, advertisements, sleeping figures, and gazes directed toward the port in Tangier, bringing them together in A Life Full of Holes: The Strait Project. Instead of framing migration through the dramatic moment of crossing, she followed the traces left across the city by enforced immobility, making photography an indirect record of border politics.This page also considers why her work brings film, publishing, archives, and local cultural institutions into the same field of activity.

    What this photographer changed

    By the late 1990s, photographs of migration often pictured boats, checkpoints, and people crossing borders to convey the danger of passage. Such images could leave out the prolonged waiting before and after a journey, housing development, unemployment, and knowledge of border routes shared across a city. Barrada photographed sleeping figures, unfinished buildings, traffic signs, and transplanted plants in Tangier as a series, showing the border as a system that distributes time, housing, and work differently between those who can travel and those who cannot. In founding the Cinémathèque de Tanger, she complemented the international circulation of local images with an institution for preserving and screening films where they were made.

    § 01 / 04 Historical Background

    Tangier and the Schengen Regime—When the Strait Shaped Everyday Urban Life

    Barrada was born in Paris and raised in Morocco and France. She studied history and political science at the Sorbonne before studying photography at the International Center of Photography in New York.*13A Life Full of Holes: The Strait Project, begun in 1998, emerged as the expanding Schengen regime imposed tighter controls on travel to Europe and Tangier became a place of waiting for people unable to reach a North that seemed close at hand.*2MoMA describes the series as a record of the physical, psychological, and political effects of the Strait of Gibraltar on the city.*1Tangier retains the memory of its colonial-era status as an international zone. In the 2000s, new housing spread into former grazing land alongside the construction of port and industrial facilities. Barrada photographed windowless apartment blocks and waste from garment factories to show how border policy takes form in urban planning, employment, and housing.*20

    From Political Science to Photography—Reading the Traces Institutions Leave in Daily Life

    Political science explains the structures of law and international relations. In Tangier, Barrada observed how those structures shape the course of a day, the design of housing, the availability of work, and the way streets are read. By placing road signs, sleeping bodies, vacant lots, and closed windows within the same series, she translated border control from an abstract policy into recurring details of urban life. The MASS MoCA guide describes her work as responding to dominant narratives through close attention and indirect inference.*3Barrada’s own statements do not assign a separate purpose to every low vantage point or area of empty space. Within the images, however, subdued color and open space keep the information from resolving into a single event and give viewers time to compare several traces.

    § 02 / 04 Core Approach

    The Strait Project—Photographing the Border Beyond the Ferry Terminal

    Boats, checkpoints, and rescues in press photographs communicate the immediate dangers of borders. They often leave out the unemployment, family time, unfinished housing, and shared knowledge of alternative routes that precede an attempted crossing. The Strait Project includes ferries, roads leading to the port, a school overlooking the strait, people sleeping in public gardens, unfinished buildings, and images made for tourists.*5Seen as a series, these elements shift the border from a single point at sea to a system that distributes urban time, housing, and work over the long term. Jeu de Paume presented the project as an inquiry into passage between South and North, the Maghreb and Europe.*10The logo of a bus company served as both transport signage and a source of practical border knowledge, allowing young people to assess where they might hide beneath a vehicle and how stringent the security would be.*20The series draws its critical force from recording shared practical knowledge and the duration of waiting without reducing migrants to symbols of dangerous passage.

    Vacancy, Peripheral Views, and Sleep—Time Outside the Decisive Moment

    Barrada’s images repeatedly return to roadside walls, shuttered windows, tilted advertisements, neglected parks, and periods without work. An early interview with openDemocracy describes how the photographs linked the desire to migrate with changes in the city.*9When these scenes appear alongside photographs of the port and sea, waiting becomes visible as a duration that exceeds the news cycle. Empty spaces and stillness create intervals through which institutional effects can be read across several places and times.

    Plants, Urban Planning, and Colonial History—Natural History as Evidence of the Border

    Beginning with Iris Tingitana, Barrada has worked with native plants of Tangier, imported species, public gardens, fossils, and dyes.The classification and transplantation of plants also record colonial rule, tourist development, land ownership, and imported systems of value.The Guggenheim exhibition Riffs presented the works that followed The Strait Project, including projects on botany and education, as continuations of questions arising from the impasses of a postcolonial city.*2Jameel Arts Centre likewise describes her work as rooted in Tangier while moving across culture, landscape, economics, and politics.*15

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    A Life Full of Holes—How the Photobook Turns Fragments into an Urban Structure

    The series takes its title from the autobiography of the Moroccan storyteller Driss ben Hamed Charhadi, recorded by Paul Bowles.*11Individual photographs remain sparing in explanation, but in the photobook, the recurring ports, roads, people, signs, sea, and architecture reveal how the desire for movement and urban redevelopment arise from the same pressures. Witte de With presented the Strait project, ongoing since 1998, through both exhibition and publication.*11The photobook does not force these local fragments into a single conclusion. The distance between pages allows readers to reconstruct a political geography.

    Cinémathèque de Tanger—Building a Local Infrastructure for Preservation and Screening

    International exhibitions carry images of Tangier abroad. Preserving films, providing access to earlier works, and developing new audiences within the city required a local infrastructure for screening and collection. In 2006, Barrada and her collaborators restored the former Cinéma Rif and founded the Cinémathèque de Tanger. In a Walker Art Center conversation, Barrada and Bouchra Khalili describe the institution as an artistic intervention in urban space.*7Photography carries the city’s traces to international audiences; the cinema builds a local repository for film history and opportunities to see films. A BFI interview links the restoration of the cinema with Beau Geste, a film responding to redevelopment, as two parts of the same effort to preserve Tangier’s memory.*8The institution adds a continuing responsibility to preserve and share images where they were made to an art centered on the city.

    From Photography to Film, Textiles, and Sculpture—Extending Observation into Material Research

    Her later work in film, textiles, dyeing, sculpture, and publishing extends the observational methods of the early photographs into material research.Pace describes her projects with plant dyes and the educational space The Mothership as investigations into the ways history and material persist in present-day life.*12World of Interiors reported that her Tangier dye workshop and garden serve at once as a site for production, research, and local knowledge.*18The method of reading traces on urban surfaces continues in the colors of cloth, the cultivation of plants, and the reuse of architecture.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    From the Decisive Moment of Crossing to the Border Accumulating in the City

    From the late 1990s through the 2000s, migration and borders appeared frequently in press photography and international exhibitions. Barrada made series from streets, signs, gardens, and sleeping bodies to show how institutions turn human time into waiting and land into provisional construction. In its 2024 retrospective, ICP read more than two decades of her photography through the phrase “part-time abstractionist,” emphasizing the ways ordinary objects and urban forms carry history.*5SFMOMA presents photographs of the school overlooking the strait and public gardens as works that examine how reality is constructed at the margins of globalization.*6Her place in photographic history rests on shifting the focus of border imagery from the instant of passage to the effects that accumulate across a city.

    Politics and Play, Critique and Institution—How Barrada’s Position Has Been Assessed

    Barrada’s work has often been discussed through the pairing of politics and play, documentary evidence and handwork. Sculpture Magazine describes a body of work that uses found objects and pedagogical devices to draw out the multiple histories carried by things.*17An ArtReview interview shows how making, learning, and sharing give her institutional critique a constructive form.*16At MoMA PS1, Le Grand Soir turned monumental blocks that recall Tangier’s walls and structures of power into a setting through which visitors could move and play.*4Historically, Barrada has joined peripheral observation, archival work, public institutions, and material research in a continuing cultural project that extends beyond photography used chiefly to explain a political issue.A Vogue article on Barrada’s two 2024 New York exhibitions discusses both her view of walls as structures of power and Le Grand Soir as a work visitors could climb, sit on, and use for play.*19Because historical research appears through a work’s form, color, and use instead of becoming a lesson, politics enters through the viewer’s bodily encounter as well as through the presentation of knowledge.

    Bouchra Khalili, Zineb Sedira, and Barrada—Who Speaks for a Place, and Through Which Institutions?

    Among her contemporaries who addressed migration, diaspora, and postcolonial memory,Bouchra Khalili centers moving people’s own voices and maps in her films, while Zineb Sedira develops histories through her family’s multiple languages and memories that cross the sea.Barrada reads the terms of movement through streets, architecture, plants, and cinemas, while individual testimony remains less prominent. The Peabody Museum describes her as an artist who registers the effects of global trade and exchange through personal, everyday moments.*14The Cinémathèque de Tanger and The Mothership create infrastructures for producing and preserving images and knowledge in Tangier alongside the international circulation of her work through museums. Her commitment to sustained cultural stewardship in the place she photographs also distinguishes her position in photographic history.The asymmetry between photographer and community nevertheless remains.Any assessment must account for the tension between the international art-market circulation of her work and her support for film culture in Tangier.Conversations about the Cinémathèque de Tanger describe the institution as an ongoing intervention in the city.*7
