Essay

Walker Evans photographed rural poverty in the American South for the Farm Security Administration from 1935 to 1937, yet he kept a deliberate distance from the agency's propagandistic purpose. So ill-suited was he to overt political messaging that contemporaries remarked one could hardly imagine anyone less fit for propaganda work. Rather than illustrate policy, he pursued what he called the essential distillation of American everyday life, using an 8x10 camera, exact composition, and available light*1. He often ignored assigned routes in order to pursue his own subjects, from gas stations and storefront signs to Victorian houses. In 1938, MoMA gave Evans the museum's first solo exhibition devoted to a photographer, American Photographs, and published an accompanying book with an essay by Lincoln Kirstein. It stands as one of the key moments in the museum legitimation of photography as fine art*2. His collaboration with James Agee among white sharecroppers in Alabama failed to appear in magazine form and only reached the public in 1941 as Let Us Now Praise Famous Men, a book initially neglected and later canonized as a major work of American literature*3. Evans insisted on presenting pictures with minimal captioning, often confining explanatory text to the ends of sections in a book, because he believed photographs should meet viewers before language organizes them. In his subway portraits, made secretly in New York between 1938 and 1941 and published in 1966, he used a concealed camera to register anonymous fellow passengers as if their faces held compressed fragments of social reality*4. After 1945 he worked for two decades as a photo editor at Fortune and later taught at Yale. The poetry in Evans's pictures lies not in manipulation but in selection and framing: signs, worn walls, or the faces of tenant farmers are made to feel as if the present has already slipped into history. The Metropolitan Museum of Art has described this as a photographic equivalent of literary lyricism, irony, and narrative structure*1. His phrase "literal, documentary, style, lucid, severe, direct" became an influential paradoxical model for later American photographers from Robert Frank to Lee Friedlander*5.
