Albert Renger-Patzsch

Renger-Patzsch made the photographed object itself central, rejecting both pictorialist beautification and Bauhaus-style visual experiment in favor of precise structural description.

Essay

Albert Renger-Patzsch opened a third path in 1920s Germany, distancing himself from both pictorialist beautification and Bauhaus-style experimental photography. Where pictorialism made photographs painterly and Bauhaus photography treated the camera as a tool for transforming visual perception, Renger-Patzsch argued for showing the structural beauty of things themselves*1. His 1928 photobook gathered one hundred images of factory chimneys, railway bridges, plant details, ceramic reflections, cast-metal tools, and other objects. The title he wanted was simply Things; the publisher Ernst Wasmuth changed it for commercial reasons to Die Welt ist schön, The World Is Beautiful*2. The book was quickly received as a landmark. Helmut Gernsheim later described it as a turning point in modern German photography, and Thomas Mann publicly praised it*3. Walter Benjamin, however, objected in his 1931 essay A Short History of Photography that this photography could give things cosmic significance while remaining silent about the social conditions of human beings. His criticism directly challenged the work's concentration on the beauty of objects stripped of political and social context*4. Renger-Patzsch's photography of things recorded form, texture, and light with maximum technical precision. In contrast to August Sander, who tried to describe society as a whole, Renger-Patzsch pursued the visual purity of individual objects. Together with Sander's typological portraits and Karl Blossfeldt's magnified plant studies, his photographs of industrial forms and everyday manufactured things became one of the three pillars of New Objectivity photography*3. The macro close-up technique he developed through botanical work, pushing proximity and optical precision to reveal grain, reflection, and minute structure, was applied just as strongly to industrial products. It made visible an inner order of things that ordinary sight tends to overlook*2. Allied bombing in 1944 destroyed his studio near Soest and much of his collection, but after the war he continued to photograph mines and steelworks in the Ruhr with great energy*5. His approach became a foundation for the aesthetics of German industrial photography and product advertising, and it is also understood as one of the sources for Bernd and Hilla Becher's typological documentation of industrial architecture*6.
