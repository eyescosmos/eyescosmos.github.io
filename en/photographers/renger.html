Essay

Albert Renger-Patzsch opened a third path in 1920s Germany, distancing himself from both pictorialist beautification and Bauhaus-style experimentation*1. He insisted on the structural beauty of things themselves, and his book Die Welt ist schon made plants, machines, tools, and industrial forms into central subjects of modern photography*2. Critics quickly recognized the book as a turning point, even as Walter Benjamin later criticized its silence about the social conditions around the objects it depicted*3. Renger-Patzsch's descriptive precision and faith in the visual autonomy of things made him a major figure in New Objectivity. His approach would later feed into industrial photography and into the typological thinking of the Bechers*6.
