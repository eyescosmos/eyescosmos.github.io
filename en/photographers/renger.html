Essay

Albert Renger-Patzsch opened a third path in 1920s Germany, distancing himself from both pictorialist beautification and Bauhaus-style experimentation*1. He insisted on the structural beauty of things themselves, and his book Die Welt ist schon made plants, machines, tools, and industrial forms into central subjects of modern photography*2. Critics quickly recognized the book as a turning point, even as Walter Benjamin later criticized its silence about the social conditions around the objects it depicted*3. Renger-Patzsch's descriptive precision and faith in the visual autonomy of things made him a major figure in New Objectivity. Alongside August Sander's typological portraiture and Karl Blossfeldt's microscopic plant studies, his work on manufactured objects and industrial forms completed the three pillars of New Objectivity photography*3. The macro close-up technique he developed through botanical photography — pressing to the limits of proximity and optical precision to reveal the grain, reflection, and inner structure of materials — applied equally to industrial products, uncovering an order in things that ordinary sight overlooks*2. His approach would later feed into industrial photography and into the typological thinking of the Bechers*6.
