Essay

Diane Arbus grew up in a prosperous Jewish family on Manhattan's Central Park West, insulated from the Depression and from any direct encounter with hardship or difference. "I grew up feeling immune and exempt from circumstance," she wrote. "One of the things I suffered from was that I never felt adversity." That sense of having been quarantined from the texture of real life became the driving force of her practice*1. After years making fashion photographs for Vogue and Harper's Bazaar alongside her husband Allan Arbus, she enrolled in Lisette Model's classes at the New School for Social Research around 1956. Model's core teaching was "shoot from the gut" — trust instinct, go directly toward what compels you — and it converted Arbus's fear into a stimulus. Allan Arbus later said: "Three sessions and Diane was a photographer." The principle Arbus made her own was "Take pictures of what you fear," recorded in her 1971 class remarks and compiled in the posthumous Aperture monograph*5. The camera, she said, was "a license to go wherever I wanted and to do what I wanted to do." In practice this meant sustained visits to circuses, nudist camps, mental institutions, and the transvestite bars of Times Square — places her upbringing had forbidden her even to look at. She returned repeatedly to the same subjects over months and years, building the trust that made her close, frontal portraits possible. About the people she called freaks she wrote: "Most people go through life dreading they'll have a traumatic experience. Freaks were born with their trauma. They've already passed their test in life. They're aristocrats"*5. In New Documents at MoMA in 1967, John Szarkowski placed her alongside Lee Friedlander and Garry Winogrand as photographers redirecting documentary toward personal vision rather than social reform*2. The Identical Twins, Roselle, N.J. (1967) became one of the defining images of a new portraiture*3. ICP records her as someone who changed the way photography speaks*4. She died in 1971; the following year her work appeared at the Venice Biennale — the first American photographer to be included — and the Aperture monograph, edited by Doon Arbus and Marvin Israel, established her posthumous reputation worldwide*5.
