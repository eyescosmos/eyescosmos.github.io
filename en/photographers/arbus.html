Essay

Diane Arbus left fashion photography behind in the late 1950s and, under the influence of Lisette Model, turned toward people living at the edges of social normality*1. She spent long periods with her subjects and made frontal portraits whose intimacy challenged accepted ideas of proper distance between photographer and sitter. In New Documents she was presented as part of a generation redirecting documentary toward personal vision rather than social reform*2. Pictures such as Identical Twins became icons of a new kind of portraiture in which vulnerability, exposure, and strangeness could not be separated*3. After her death in 1971, her reputation grew rapidly, and her work came to mark a major turning point in the history of photography*5.
