    Abstract

    Lewis Baltz turned Southern California’s industrial parks, graded lots, and anonymous walls into problems of repetition and arrangement in The New Industrial Parks near Irvine, California and Park City. Against rapid suburbanization and a photographic hierarchy of subjects deemed worthy of attention, he privileged the structure of a body of work over the singular masterpiece. His move from industrial parks to surveillance and research facilities raises a central question: how much do architectural surfaces disclose about institutions and power, and where do they conceal them?

    What this photographer changed

    Baltz made the body of work as a whole the unit of the work. By arranging comparable walls, doors, parking lots, and graded surfaces, he revealed mechanisms of urban development that extended beyond any single building. Repetition, number, arrangement, the page, and exhibition space became integral to the work, expanding landscape photography from a view of land into an analysis of the institutions that support corporate architecture, information, and surveillance.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period

    Lewis Baltz grew up in Southern California as postwar housing development and technology industries expanded rapidly. In a 2009 Smithsonian oral history, he recalled learning about photography, art, and ideas from the Laguna Beach photographer William Current during his youth, and looking to Edward Weston as a model for an artist’s life*1. Shopping centers, roads, parking lots, and housing tracts filled his everyday surroundings. The gap between this environment and a photographic “hierarchy of subjects” that elevated Yosemite and celebrated figures led him to treat commercial buildings, erased signs, and street fixtures as the landscape of his own time*1.

    Prototypes, made at the San Francisco Art Institute, met strong resistance from teachers and students who expected expressive gestures or conventional photographic subjects. Baltz distanced himself from the insular culture of photography and moved toward questions of repetition and seriality pursued by Andy Warhol, Donald Judd, Josef Albers, and John Coplans*1. The 1975 New Topographics exhibition later grouped artists who had barely known one another under a single name; Baltz’s method had developed independently of that designation*1. By the late 1980s, he felt he had exhausted what he could say about buildings under construction and shifted his attention to the information, surveillance, and technology operating behind their walls. Color, large prints, multipanel works, and video entered his work as the question changed*1.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    “Prototypes” — Anonymous Walls

    The subject matter of Prototypes responded to the prevailing hierarchy that determined which places deserved to be photographed. Walls, entrances, windows, signs, and automobile bodies are cut away from establishing views that would explain their use and placed within a shallow space nearly parallel to the picture plane. Baltz described this space as close to low relief*1. He also observed that viewers could project themselves into the unoccupied images, while traces of absent human action remained embedded in the built environment*1. In “Santa Cruz 1970B,” an old car, a white wall, and a dark facade carry nearly equal visual weight. The space must be read through traces of design, ownership, and use.

    The New Industrial Parks — A Grid of 51 Photographs

    The 51 photographs in The New Industrial Parks near Irvine, California were selected to present the industrial park as a system of repetition. Baltz considered the entire body of images a single work*1. His experience of serial works by Warhol, Judd, and others informed this approach. At the Canadian Centre for Architecture, a three-tier grid places similar facades, doors, landscaping, and parking lots in simultaneous comparison, making minor differences and overwhelming uniformity perceptible*4. Repetition and comparison expose a logic of development that multiplies standardized buildings while weakening the specificity of place.

    “Neutral” Photography and Architectural Surfaces

    Baltz used restrained images to examine how objectivity is constructed. Early in his career, he sought a description that functioned like an “ordinary window”: shadows and details remained legible while the virtuosity of the print receded*1. He later understood this neutrality as a rhetoric of its own. When Allan Sekula criticized factory exteriors for failing to show the unequal social relations behind their walls, Baltz accepted the limitation and recalled that he was interested in photography’s opacity—its inability to encompass society as a whole*1. The walls that block the movement of workers, commodities, and capital mark missing information in The New Industrial Parks. They are also the blank faces that corporate activity presents to public space.

    Park City — The Time of Development

    Park City is a single work of 102 photographs charting the conversion of a former Utah mining region into a ski resort and residential development*7. Graded land, roads, half-built houses, completed homes, and discarded materials interweave stages before and after construction, natural and built elements, use and abandonment. Change appears as the time of real-estate development, an incremental process of substitution. Multiple viewpoints extend the scale of development beyond individual buildings and require the viewer to follow relationships among the photographs.

    Candlestick Point — Ground and Color

    Candlestick Point follows the conversion of a San Francisco landfill into a state recreation area. Twelve of its 84 photographs are in color, recording surfaces where soil, grass, water, rust, plastic, and debris intermingle*9. Color distinguishes accumulated materials and the formation of an administratively produced “nature.” Baltz later described the idea that a changed question demands a changed method as a guide for renewing his work*1. The move from frontal black-and-white photographs to color reorganized the terms by which the subject could be described.

    Sites of Technology — Inside Managed Space

    In Sites of Technology, Baltz moved from industrial-park exteriors into nuclear facilities, laboratories, communications infrastructure, and control rooms. Pipes, cooling tanks, control panels, and monitors indicate ongoing activity, while the purposes of the technology and the authorities directing it remain outside the frame. The large color photograph “Cooling tank, nuclear power plant, Gravelines, France” records a space ordered for management and safety through clinical light and abundant detail*12. The NGA exhibition pairing Prototypes with Ronde de Nuit, a work concerned with surveillance facilities, traced Baltz’s continuing interest from anonymous commercial architecture to environments that regulate information and human behavior*6.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    The New Industrial Parks near Irvine, California, 1974

    The New Industrial Parks comprises 51 black-and-white photographs of newly built industrial parks around Irvine. At the CCA, the work was installed as a three-tier grid, placing sealed facades, building corners, parking lots, and landscaping at a distance that permits comparison*4. The title identifies a specific region, yet the photographs reveal little about the businesses operating there. The combination of precise place names and anonymous images presents the shared form through which industrial parks homogenize land, architecture, and corporate activity.

    Park City — Development as a Series

    “Park City” is an extensive series that breaks a mountain landscape under development into graded surfaces, houses, roads, materials, and distant views. The Whitney Museum’s collection record treats 102 gelatin silver prints as a single work, confirming that the series and its editing constitute the work*7. Linking the viewpoints preserves the gradual replacement of the land by another form.

    Candlestick Point and Sites of Technology

    Candlestick Point mixes black-and-white and color photographs in the exterior terrain of a landfill, while Sites of Technology enters high-technology facilities through large color prints. Getty’s project material describes this movement as a continuum extending from early single photographs to later work incorporating video and installation*10. Exterior walls and institutional interiors look different, yet both series focus on spatial structures that enable human activity.

    Bodies of Work, Grids, and Books

    Baltz conceived the completed work at a scale larger than a single exposed negative. Grids of closely spaced small prints invite simultaneous comparison among facades and surfaces. Books create, through the time of turning pages, the sensation of recurring spaces. His account of treating “the entire body of work” as the unit as early as Prototypes confirms that exhibition and publication were central to his method*1. The NGA exhibition Prototypes/Ronde de Nuit juxtaposed 83 small black-and-white photographs with 12 large color panels, demonstrating how number, dimensions, color, and arrangement changed with the scale of the institution under examination*6.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Historical Position

    Conceptual Photography Through Arrangement

    Baltz’s work challenged the assumption that a photograph’s meaning resides wholly within a single image. Repetition, number, arrangement, comparison, and exhibition space moved to the center of production. In his conversation with David Campany, Baltz discussed a persistent tension between what can be seen and what can be known, architectural surfaces that refuse to communicate meaning, and his equal concern with the page and the wall*15. Blank facades make the absence of information visible and turn the gap between seeing and knowing into a subject. Baltz also treated landscape as institutional space produced by real estate, corporate architecture, surveillance, and research facilities, opening an apparently “objective” documentary style to the viewer’s act of comparison*1. The NGA’s placement of his early work alongside Judd and Richard Serra, and its extension of the same concerns into his later surveillance spaces, supports the connection to Minimalism, Conceptual art, and installation*6.

    Robert Adams, Stephen Shore, and Baltz

    Among the photographers associated with New Topographics, Robert Adams placed the losses caused by development and the light that remained on the land within the same image, bringing an ethical conception of landscape to the foreground. Stephen Shore photographed roads, meals, motels, and intersections in color, linking everyday travel with photography’s descriptive capacity. Baltz repeated industrial buildings and graded sites to approach the anonymity of the corporations, institutions, and technologies that sustain a place. From the shared subject of the human-altered landscape, Adams foregrounded ethics, Shore everyday experience, and Baltz systems.

    From Landscape Photography to Contemporary Art

    The central question linking Baltz’s early black-and-white work and later color projects concerns the systems through which contemporary environments are designed, used, and managed. The Getty Research Institute’s acquisition of material spanning 1967 to 2013, together with the NGA’s simultaneous presentation of Prototypes and Ronde de Nuit, makes it possible to read his movement from industrial-park facades to spaces of surveillance and information as a renewal of that question*9. LE BAL’s research fund likewise situates Baltz’s work at the intersection of rigorous form and social and political issues*14. By controlling the number, arrangement, scale, and limits of visibility in photographs, he brought into landscape photography a method for examining the institutions that produce space. That method underlies his continuing influence in contemporary art.
