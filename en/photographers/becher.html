    § 048 — Photographer Index — Typological Photography

    Bernd & Hilla Becher

    ベルント＆ヒラ・ベッヒャー 1931–2007 / 1934–2022
    CountryGermany Period1980–1990s ChannelSeeing side by side · TYPOLOGY
    Abstract

    Bernd and Hilla Becher worked as an artistic partnership, using large-format cameras and standardized shooting conditions to document water towers, blast furnaces, winding towers, and cooling towers disappearing with the decline of coal and steel. Their book Anonyme Skulpturen and their gridded typologies transformed records of industrial architecture into works that allow viewers to compare buildings with the same function and read historical and regional variation. This page examines how their method connected postwar German photography, New Objectivity, Minimal and Conceptual art, New Topographics, and the Düsseldorf School, and considers the critical implications of that connection.

    What this photographer changed

    The Bechers photographed vanishing industrial structures under consistent conditions and built an archive spanning several decades. Their grids and photobooks shifted the center of the work from the finish of a single print to a system that places buildings of the same type side by side so that similarities and differences can be read. Each photograph records an existing structure, while the series makes visible a plan: what to collect, under which conditions, and how to classify it. The photograph’s evidentiary force, the sculptural appearance of the subject, Minimalism’s repetition, and Conceptual art’s rules converge, bringing photography into contemporary art’s debates about space, classification, and institutions.

    § 01 / 04 The Vanishing Industrial Region and the Turn to Photography

    Siegen’s Industrial Landscape—Demolition Made Photography Necessary

    Bernd Becher grew up in the Siegen region, where his grandfather and uncles worked in iron mines and blast-furnace plants. Industrial facilities shaped both his family history and the everyday landscape. Hilla explained that the project also allowed Bernd to “preserve his childhood.” As industry contracted during the 1950s and 1960s, the mines and factories he had been drawing closed and were demolished faster than he could complete sketches or prints. He began taking small-camera photographs as aids to memory, then recognized that photography could respond to the speed at which the structures were disappearing.*1

    Their early collaborations included timber-frame workers’ houses in the Siegen region. Repeated frontal views of the dark structural grids crossing white walls joined a record of local social history with a comparison of variations within one building type. Hilla Wobeser had formal photographic training and experience in commercial photography. She brought precise technical description of materials and structures to Bernd’s developing interest in industrial landscapes. They began working together in 1959.*25

    The Met’s 2022 retrospective returned Bernd’s mining drawings and photographic collages, along with Hilla’s pre-collaboration photographs of steel wire, metal components, botanical specimens, transmission towers, and industrial-exhibition architecture, to a shared account of their development. It examined their working process through materials extending beyond finished photographs.*19 The evidence resists a simple division in which one partner supplied nostalgia and the other technical assistance. Memory of the industrial region converged with a way of isolating and clearly describing objects, and the combination developed into a long-term collaborative undertaking.

    Subjective Photography and New Objectivity—Reframing Objectivity in Postwar Germany

    When the Bechers began collaborating in the late 1950s, Otto Steinert’s Subjective Photography was an influential force in the reconstruction of West German photography. Steinert used photograms, stark tonal reduction, severe cropping, long exposures, negative reversal, and multiple exposure. He emphasized the creation of an image autonomous from straightforward description. His approach sought to restart avant-garde photography, interrupted by the Nazi regime and the war, through the individual artist’s formal imagination.*27

    The Bechers chose fixed viewpoints, even light, and repeated shooting procedures. Authorship moved away from dramatic effects within a single image and into the rules of photography and the design of a series. The Met places their method between Steinert’s expressive direction and the lucid description associated with New Objectivity in the work of August Sander, Karl Blossfeldt, and Albert Renger-Patzsch.*3 SK Stiftung Kultur, which maintains the Becher Archive, likewise identifies early photography, nineteenth-century painting, New Objectivity, and a systematic commitment to empirical verification among the lineages that informed their work.*18 The Bechers did not simply revive New Objectivity. They used comparisons among multiple images to show how structures serving the same function assumed different forms across periods, regions, and technologies.

    The method is described as Conceptual because the organizing principle lies in the plan, not because the photographs detach themselves from reality and become “concepts.” Each image remains a specific record of an existing building. The work as a whole is governed by a plan: photograph structures with the same function under the same conditions, collect them over long periods, classify them, and arrange them. Authorial decisions are distributed across the choice of subject, shooting conditions, duration of collection, taxonomy, and sequence. The plan becomes visible as the work. The Met distinguishes this rule-based method from Steinert’s Subjective Photography while placing it in relation to Minimal and Conceptual art.*3 MoMA’s history of its photography department similarly describes Information (1970) as an exhibition in which photography was deeply integrated into Conceptual work, including that of the Bechers.*21

    The Decline of Coal and Steel—Industrial Architecture as Social Infrastructure

    When the Bechers began photographing, the coal- and steel-based industrial infrastructure of West Germany was entering a period of closure and demolition driven by technological change and shifting markets. MoMA describes Winding Towers as part of a project that recorded, for more than forty years, industrial structures in Europe and the United States that were disappearing during the transition from an industrial to an information society.*24 SK Stiftung Kultur likewise notes that many facilities lost their overall form soon after the photographs were made, through shutdown and demolition—a condition that shaped the archive from the outset.*18 Water towers and winding towers belonged to production systems that linked extraction, transport, smelting, and storage while organizing regional employment, housing, movement, and daily schedules.

    The Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex in the Ruhr encompassed mine shafts, a coking plant, railways, waste heaps, miners’ housing, and welfare facilities.*23 The Bechers’ frontal photographs extract one structure from such a network and make its function and form comparable with others. MoMA’s educational material explains that they waited for overcast skies to obtain neutral light and deliberately excluded workers, whose labor would ordinarily animate the scene.*20 The buildings consequently appear with exceptional clarity, while ownership, labor conditions, accident risk, noise, smoke, and environmental damage remain difficult to read directly from the photographs. As The Met’s Zeche Hannover demonstrates, older equipment vanished rapidly during technological modernization.*30 Isolating a structure from its social setting enables comparison, while also registering the process of deindustrialization through which equipment moved from productive tool to “heritage” or “sculpture.”

    § 02 / 04 What Does Typology Make Visible?

    Overcast Skies, Frontal Views, and Large-Format Cameras—Rules for Comparison

    The Bechers photographed water towers, winding towers, blast furnaces, cooling towers, gas tanks, grain elevators, and other structures with large-format view cameras, usually from the front or side. They waited for softly diffused overcast light, avoided people, vehicles, strong shadows, and dramatic clouds, and kept the subject’s scale and proportion within the frame consistent. Hilla explained that they excluded the “moods” created by sunshine, snow, or moonlight so that photographs could remain comparable and the sky would not become a second subject.*1 The Canadian Centre for Architecture’s catalog record for Water Towers describes a project comprising 224 towers photographed over roughly twenty-five years, in some cases from as many as eight directions. Waiting for cloud cover, adjusting the viewpoint with ladders or scaffolding, and securing cooperation from facility managers to clear people from the frame show that the apparently uniform surface was produced through sustained decisions in the field.*33

    Rules did not eliminate judgment. The photographers still had to locate a front, back, and side within the dense pipework of a blast furnace, find a position from which a smokestack carried sufficient visual weight, and adjust verticals and margins. Hilla said that large-format photography demanded decision and experience, and that each subject required them to “invent our way of seeing it.” Their objectivity was designed to bring buildings from different places and periods into a common visual field where comparison could occur.*1

    What Is a Typology? From the Single Photograph to a Field of Comparison

    A typology arranges several buildings with the same function in grids of four, nine, sixteen, thirty, or another number of prints. A single water tower can be read as a record or portrait of a building. In a group, the contours of tanks, supporting legs, materials, braces, and regional variations become measures against one another. Numerous variants emerge within the general category “water tower.”*3

    Typology did not appear suddenly as a format into which photographs were fitted after the concept had been completed. In a 2025 art-historical study, Andrew Moisey treats the grid as an outcome of close research into industrial architecture. It preserves the specificity of each building while making human design choices for a shared function available for comparison. The photographs remain documents; standardized presentation turns those documents into a visual classification distinct from a numerical table.*36 Issues in Science and Technology similarly notes that the Bechers understood typology as a “grammar” for comprehending and comparing different structures. Classification did not erase individuality through abstraction. It revealed differences of region, material, and period within a common function.*37

    MoMA likewise explains that the unified format reveals a shared basic form while preserving details specific to each structure.*29

    The format took shape through experiments of the late 1960s and early 1970s. The Met’s Framework Houses, Germany combines one overall view with nine details in a “one-plus-nine” arrangement. The museum explains that an archive standardized in formal and compositional terms made comparison possible, and that an “ideal type” or prototype forms in the viewer’s mind only when multiple variants are placed together.*28

    According to Hilla, the grouping began when three photographs of cooling towers were placed on a white floor and the images “started to dance.” In books, they adjusted the rhythm through page sequence; on walls, they used horizontal rows, nine-part grids, and diagonals. Typology brought the taxonomic chart and formal composition onto the same surface, turning the ordering of information into a perceptual experience.*1

    This shift meant that photographic meaning no longer ended within a single frame. It emerged through comparison among images. MoMA explains that the grid allows individual towers to be compared with ease, each retaining its specificity while appearing as a variation on an ideal form.*29 Photography expanded from a single act of discovery into collection sustained over decades, and the work expanded from one print into a system encompassing selection, sequence, and spacing. An exhibition text from the Staatliche Museen zu Berlin also shows how horizontal, vertical, and diagonal reading paths generate the grid’s formal meaning.*22 Typology’s critical force lies in establishing a structure through which buildings with the same use can be read for differences of region, material, date, and alteration.

    “Anonymous Sculptures” and the Readymade—From Selection to a Long-Term Archive

    The term “anonymous sculptures” describes industrial structures whose forms were shaped less by a named designer than by function, technology, and accumulated modification. Blast furnaces and coal-preparation plants were built by teams of engineers and workers, then altered as machinery and pipes were added. Hilla explained that they did not emerge all at once from a single design; they changed over time. Once isolated from their surroundings and grouped together, everyday equipment appears as an autonomous system of forms.*1

    The Met reads Anonyme Skulpturen as an implicit response to Marcel Duchamp’s readymade because the Bechers also selected existing objects, isolated them photographically from their functional environment, and gave them a new name and exhibition setting. In both cases, value shifts from manual fabrication alone toward decisions about what to choose, where to place it, and how to name it.*3

    The Bechers’ project, however, began with the need to preserve buildings facing demolition. Hilla described field research, large-format photography, repeated standardized procedures, classification, and arrangement as developments arising from that initial need.*1 A readymade can be established through the selection of a single manufactured object and a change of exhibition setting. The Bechers’ acts of selection extended across a decades-long archive, and the photograph served both as a recontextualized work of art and as evidence of lost architecture. The present archive maintains cross-references among negatives, prints, classifications, and arrangements used in exhibitions and publications.*18

    Its Conceptual character therefore lies in making the criteria of collection and the procedures of classification into part of the work. It does not replace each building with an abstract idea. Every print conveys a specific structure; the grid displays the rules through which the structures can be compared. MoMA’s departmental history places the Bechers’ exhibitions within a sequence in which Information (1970) brought photographically based Conceptual work into the museum and Projects (1975) unsettled the distinction between photographers and artists who used photography.*21 A project that began as preservation entered the institutional space shared by photography and contemporary art through procedures of selection, repetition, and classification.

    Typology and Industrial Landscape—Comparing Form, Recording Place

    The best-known grids suppress much local information, allowing buildings from distant countries to carry equal visual weight. At each factory or mine, however, the Bechers also photographed industrial landscapes that included relationships among structures, rail lines, roads, housing, and terrain. MoMA presented the frontal typologies as images with the clarity of an engineer’s drawing and the industrial landscapes as a form directed toward the experience of a particular place, describing them as two poles of photographic description. Images that isolate the object and images that return it to place move between formal comparison and an understanding of the industrial system.*4

    This range is also visible in retrospectives and collections. Centre Pompidou’s 2004 retrospective included about forty industrial landscapes and a documentary study of the Zollern II mine made in the early 1970s, extending the account of their work from comparison among individual buildings to the record of an entire mine.*32 In Dia Art Foundation’s two-part Mines of Giraumont, Lorraine, France, attention shifts from isolated exterior views to the beams and trusses that form an underground interior structure.*34 Frontal views, industrial landscapes, and photographs of interiors or details functioned as different descriptive units through which the form and location of a facility could later be cross-checked.

    Collaboration and Hilla Becher’s Printing

    In the field, they used two cameras and divided limited periods of access, each returning with selected viewpoints. Hilla made most of the prints in the darkroom, while both examined negatives, camera positions, tonal density, and arrangement. From locating subjects through editing grids, each partner’s decisions revised the other’s, producing a collaborative vision that is difficult to separate into individual authorship. The professorship established in 1976 was formally held by Bernd, although institutional rules prevented their simultaneous appointment.*3

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Photobooks, and Exhibitions

    Water Towers, 1963–80—One Function Seen Across Sixteen Prints

    Water Towers is a representative typology that combines towers from different places and periods in equal-sized prints, allowing the tank and its supporting structure to be compared. The Met’s work consists of sixteen photographs made between 1963 and 1980, compressing geographic distance and more than a decade into one wall-based arrangement. Buildings that fulfill the same function take on different contours in response to regional technology, materials, capacity, and connections to existing equipment. The variation becomes visually legible before it is explained in words.*6

    Cooling Towers/Steel-Wood, 1972—Measuring Material Difference in Two Prints

    Cooling Towers/Steel-Wood is a two-part work from 1972 that places a steel cooling tower beside a wooden one. In the National Gallery of Art’s work, the consistent framing of the two large structures makes differences in cladding, framework, and visual weight legible as effects of material.*7 The two-part Winding Towers from the same year compares the frameworks of the towers and the configuration of their machine houses, showing that even two images can reveal both the shared traits of a type and the individuality of each example.*8 The National Museum of Art, Osaka, also holds an eight-part Cooling Towers from 1986, making a typological arrangement available in a Japanese public collection.*16 The number of prints was not a fixed stylistic formula; it was chosen in relation to the material being compared and the exhibition space.

    Anonyme Skulpturen, 1970—From Photobook to Contemporary Art Exhibition

    The photobook Anonyme Skulpturen: Eine Typologie technischer Bauten was published in 1970, joining function-based sequences with the designation “anonymous sculptures.” In the same year, a series of cooling towers appeared in MoMA’s Information, placing the Bechers’ photographs in the same exhibition space as Conceptual art organized around information, rules, and classification.*21 MoMA presented a solo Projects exhibition in 1975.*10 That year, they also participated in New Topographics with Robert Adams, Lewis Baltz, Stephen Shore, and others, becoming part of a new photographic approach to human-altered landscapes characterized by visual restraint.*26 Together, these events show their work being received through three distinct channels: the photobook, the contemporary art exhibition, and the landscape photography exhibition.

    At the 44th Venice Biennale in 1990, their photographs of industrial structures received the Golden Lion for sculpture.*11 The Met argues that their standardized frontal views and grids resonated with the serial and programmatic structures of contemporary Minimal and Conceptual art, challenging the assumed separation between documentary photography and fine art.*19 The award in the sculpture category signaled recognition not simply of the subjects’ forms, but of a display structure built through repeated, uniformly scaled prints and a plan of classification—concerns that extended beyond conventional accounts of photography.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History

    How Neutral Are Photographs That Appear “Objective”?

    The Bechers’ photographs are often described as “objective” or “impassive.” In 1977, critic Hilton Kramer reportedly compared them to photographs made for a real-estate company, a comparison Hilla accepted.*2 Yet the images contain decisions about waiting for cloud cover, raising the camera on a ladder, locating the most revealing side of a structure, and determining how much surrounding land to include. Their uniform appearance results from choices that suppress emotional staging and intensify comparison. The Met argues that this apparent objectivity connected documentary photography with Minimal and Conceptual art and unsettled the boundary between documentary and fine-art photography.*5

    What Remains of Labor When People Are Excluded?

    The Bechers removed people as elements that would interfere with comparison, clarifying the buildings’ outlines and construction. MoMA’s material notes that workers would ordinarily animate these scenes through labor and that the Bechers deliberately excluded them from the compositions.*20 The rule allows the photographs to convey the forms of towers and furnaces in detail, while offering little direct information about ownership, working conditions, accidents, or industrial pollution. Objectivity determines what can be compared and what remains outside the frame. Art historian Blake Stimson argues that this separation temporarily withdraws the industrial equipment’s social, political, and economic values, allowing an aesthetic concentration on form to take their place. Neutrality here contains a tension: it does not deny social meaning, but opens another way of seeing without directly explaining that meaning.*35

    Human work nevertheless remains on the structures’ surfaces. Writing on Water Towers, architectural critic Reyner Banham observed that human presence is excluded while human handiwork is visible everywhere. Welds, braces, added pipes, and repaired joints carry the collective labor of unnamed engineers and workers. The absence of people makes labor less visible, while preserving its material results at high density. That contradiction raises the question of how fully photography can carry the memory of industrial society.*6

    The Record of the Entire Mine Behind the Grid

    Later reception made typology the Bechers’ representative form and obscured bodies of photographs that recorded factories and mines from many directions. In his review of the 2022 retrospective, James Welling suggested that the easily recognizable format sought by contemporary art may have overshadowed broader documentary projects such as the Concordia mine.*9 Industrial landscapes, working notes, and records made from multiple viewpoints show a project extending beyond a collection of forms. It also served as a documentary undertaking from which an industrial facility could later be reconstructed.

    From the Single Photograph to a Wall-Based System—Connecting Photography with Other Visual Arts

    When The Met acquired the sixteen-part Water Towers in 1980, the Bechers’ repetitive and seemingly prosaic project puzzled conventional photography collectors and museums. The museum states that by the late 1980s they had become among the first photographers to bridge the divide between photography and the other visual arts.*6 In a display that treats the single print as a window onto the world, each photograph remains an independent unit. In a grid, each print becomes an equivalent module and the wall as a whole becomes the work. The Met’s retrospective connects their serial arrangements with Minimal and Conceptual art because repetition, spacing, and overall scale organize the act of viewing alongside the content of the images.*19 Looking extends laterally and diagonally instead of remaining fixed before one image. Städel Museum explains that reality in the Bechers’ typologies is not represented by a single photograph but perceived through the simultaneous arrangement of multiple images. It relates their strict repetition, industrial forms, and work assembled in stages to Minimal and Conceptual art. Photography approached the other visual arts through a form that designed the entire wall as a work, as well as through sculptural subjects.*31

    Each photograph conveys details of an existing tower, while the complete grid is perceived as a type. The photograph’s evidentiary relation to the world, the objecthood of the subject, Minimalism’s repetition, and Conceptual art’s rules operate on the same wall. Photography thereby entered discussions of space, repetition, institutions, and classification that had long been associated with painting and sculpture. The Met’s claim that the Bechers challenged the boundary between documentary photography and fine art refers to this double character.*19 The Golden Lion in the sculpture category in 1990 marked the art institution’s recognition of that shift.

    The Düsseldorf School—Inheriting Rules of Production, Not a Visual Formula

    Bernd taught at the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf from 1976, and his class included Thomas Struth, Thomas Ruff, Candida Höfer, Andreas Gursky, and others. They inherited a method of repeating subjects and making shooting conditions and series structure integral to the work’s logic. Industrial architecture and black-and-white photography were not the defining legacy. A 1997 exhibition at the National Museum of Modern Art, Kyoto, presented the Bechers alongside their students and described the movement between distant observation and close attention to detail as a lineage within postwar German photography.*15 Städel Museum’s Photographs Become Pictures argues that this teaching established photography as an expressive medium equal to other arts, while students further unsettled boundaries among photography, painting, and sculpture through scale, color, display, and digital processing.*31

    Gursky’s vast social spaces, Ruff’s portraits and reproduced images, Struth’s streets and museums, and Höfer’s public interiors do not share one visual appearance. They nevertheless extend an approach built on repetition, distance, rules, and comparison into different subjects, color, large prints, and digital processing. The Düsseldorf School’s influence lies less in a style of “deadpan photography” than in the idea that photographers design rules through which to examine the world.*3

    The Industrial-Heritage Archive—Reading History Through Comparative Form

    The Bechers’ work altered the institutional position of photography and art while also creating a body of material through which vanished industrial architecture can be studied. Since 1996, SK Stiftung Kultur has continuously developed the Becher Archive, preserving works, negatives, classifications, and arrangements used in exhibitions and publications according to the order established by the artists.*18 The Erasmus Prize recognized that their systematic series, made under consistent conditions, unsettled the distinction between documentation and art and became a source for industrial-heritage research.*13 The Bechers stated that they would use the award money to preserve and organize the work, opening unprinted negatives, maps, plans, and travel records to future researchers as a cross-referenced archive.*14

    On-site preservation and a photographic archive retain different kinds of knowledge. Industrial heritage sites such as Zollverein physically convey the relations among shafts, transport networks, housing, and equipment.*23 The Bechers’ photographs place forms from different countries and periods—including facilities that have been demolished—on the same surface for comparison. A preserved site presents the spatial whole of one industrial system. The photographic archive crosses distance and time to test the many solutions modern industry devised for the same function.

    International recognition has addressed the project’s formal character, long-term documentary plan, and influence. The Hasselblad Award in 2004 honored the rigor of their method and its impact on subsequent generations.*12 Through roughly two hundred works, SFMOMA’s 2022–23 retrospective reconsidered the documentation of disappearing industrial architecture, typology, and industrial landscape within a single body of work.*17 The Met exhibition from which it developed drew on extensive access to the archive and working materials, presenting early drawings and collages through mature grids as the development of one method.*19 Recent reassessment has treated the Bechers’ work as a long-term project spanning photography, research, preservation, classification, and exhibition, rather than extracting only the finished typologies.

    Recent scholarship has focused on the difficulty of reducing their method to either art or research. Moisey argues that the grid became an instrument for qualitative observation of variations among buildings with a shared function while preserving the architectural precision of each photograph.*36 Issues in Science and Technology describes the Bechers as “natural historians” of the industrial landscape, connecting New Objectivity, visual classification, and the end of the industrial era within a single method.*37 Their contribution to photographic history extends beyond presenting industrial architecture as art. They preserved each photograph as evidence of an existing structure, used the grid to make multiple pieces of evidence comparable, and employed industrial landscapes to return structures to sites of production and everyday life. SK Stiftung Kultur places the work at the intersection of photography, art, industrial history, and scientific classification because the method exceeds any single medium or discipline.*18 The Bechers turned a question into photographic rules, an archive, and a wall-based form: how can multiple images be made, gathered, and arranged so that standardization and regional variation, technological renewal and obsolescence, and the history of function and form become visible? That is their central legacy in photographic history.

    § REL Related photographers & movements
    Related photographers
    • August Sander — A precedent for typology and the systematic archive. The Bechers encountered his consistency after developing their own method and held his work in high regard.
    • Walker Evans — A point of comparison for treating vernacular architecture and disappearing industrial forms through restrained description.
    • Andreas Gursky — A student who extended the Bechers' rule-based observation into images of the global economy made with large-format color and digital processing.
    Related movements
    • Typological Photography — A method that photographs comparable subjects under consistent conditions and uses arrangement to reveal similarity and difference.
    • Conceptual Art — A contemporary field in which repetition, classification, rules, and photography as information became central.
    • New Topographics — A photographic approach that, from 1975 onward, reconsidered human-altered land through restrained description.
    • Düsseldorf School — A lineage emerging from the Bechers' teaching and method, later developed through series, large-scale photographs, and analyses of contemporary social structures.
