Essay

Arthur Rothstein was one of the best-known photographers of the Farm Security Administration and an important figure in the history of American documentary photography*1*2. His images of drought, migration, labor, and rural hardship helped define what documentary photography looked like in the New Deal era, and they circulated widely in magazines, reports, and government publications.

Rothstein matters because his photographs became central to the public visualization of the Great Depression. At the same time, his career raises enduring questions about staging, selection, and the ethics of documentary representation*1*2. In the history of photography, he is important not only for iconic images such as the Dust Storm sequence, but because his work shows how documentary authority is produced through publication, institutional context, and the photographer's choices about where and how to frame social reality.
