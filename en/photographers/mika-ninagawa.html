    Mika Ninagawa

    蜷川実花（ミカ・ニナガワ） 2000s
    CountryJapan Period2000–2010s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Mika Ninagawa photographs flowers, goldfish, and people in intense color, and has extended her work across photobooks, magazines, advertising, film, and exhibitions. Her reception began with the 1990s discourse of “girl photography” and later expanded to Neo-Pop, mass culture, self-image, representations of women, and spatial installation. The development of her color, and the praise and resistance that greeted magazine and advertising imagery in museums, offer another way to read that history.

    What this photographer changed

    Mika Ninagawa did not treat her portraits for magazines and advertising as separate work unrelated to her personal projects. In her 2008 retrospective, portraits of more than 200 people made for magazines and advertising appeared alongside photographs of flowers, goldfish, and early works*17. Abroad, the reach of her photobooks and publications beyond the art market was identified as a point of difference from Japanese Neo-Pop*24. Another critic described the same retrospective as akin to “flipping through a glossy magazine”*25. A central question in Ninagawa’s reception became how museums should assess the vivid photographs that had circulated widely through magazines and advertising in the 2000s.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period

    Mika Ninagawa became strongly drawn to photography while she was still in elementary school. She has said that growing up around her father, theater director Yukio Ninagawa, and her mother, actor Tomoko Mayama, gave her an almost impatient desire to become someone who expressed herself through art. She tried drawing and making things, but photography felt different because so little seemed to stand between emotion and expression; what she felt could become an image almost directly*12. By around age ten, she was using a compact camera to photograph herself in a mirror and a Barbie doll placed among the volcanic rocks of Onioshidashi at Mount Asama*12. She later studied graphic design at Tama Art University while pursuing photography more seriously, and in 1996 received recognition through the Hitotsubo Exhibition and Canon’s New Cosmos of Photography*21. She later recalled that having her photographs recognized through open-call competitions, and being called an “artist,” was a liberating experience*4. At the time, photographs by young women were becoming increasingly visible through compact cameras, open-call competitions, and magazines. Ninagawa gained attention within a trend described as “girl photography” or “girly photo.” In her 1996 series Happiness Self Portrait 1996, she explained that she spent more time selecting, sequencing, and combining photographs than defining a theme before shooting, showing that editing after the shoot had been central to her work from an early stage*9. In 2001, she received the Kimura Ihei Photography Award with Yurie Nagashima and HIROMIX. She continued to photograph flowers, goldfish, people, and herself. In 2008, the large-scale retrospective Earthly Flowers, Heavenly Colors reorganized work made since 1995*13. Her official biography now lists more than 120 photobooks, over 150 solo exhibitions, and work in film, video, and spatial installation*2.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of Her Work

    How She Responded to the “Girl Photography” Boom

    The late-1990s “girl photography” boom brought Ninagawa’s work to a wider audience. She has described the timing as fortunate and said she consciously took advantage of the attention as an opportunity for people to see her work*12. Yet from the beginning, she had mixed feelings about a label that treated her work and her public persona as one and the same. Yurie Nagashima later examined the very category of “girl photography” constructed by critics and editors*8. Ninagawa chose to turn that attention into opportunities to keep working. Looking back, she said that the fear of being consumed, other people’s desires, intense jealousy directed at her by men, and the experience of being a woman during this period affected her later photography and films*12. When she received the Kimura Ihei Photography Award in 2001 together with Nagashima and HIROMIX, she also recalled being left with the question, “Why not one person?”*12. In later interviews, she has continued to describe this period through both the reception of her work and the image imposed on her as a photographer.

    Why She Began Taking Photographs, and How Her Color Took Shape

    Ninagawa’s photographs are known for intense primary colors, yet she says she rarely decides in advance exactly how she will shoot. She presses the shutter when something moves her, and only later, looking back at the photographs, notices how often color seems to dance in the light*3. Her attraction to photography also came from this direct link between emotion and expression, the sense that what she felt could become an image with very little mediation*12. The finished images can appear meticulously staged, but the act of photographing begins with an immediate response to the subject. A Nippon.com profile describes an early method in which she deliberately used a color copier with faulty color controls to push the image toward oversaturated primary colors*21. In Happiness Self Portrait 1996, she likewise explained that much of her time went into selecting, sequencing, and combining photographs after shooting*9. Photographing what moves her and strengthening color and sequence afterward are separate stages of the same process. She has also cited two influences: the environment of Tokyo, where different things mix and are consumed at tremendous speed, and the colors of ukiyo-e and kimono*12. As for cherry blossoms, she remembers the blossoms falling and moving in the wind in her father’s NINAGAWA Macbeth, which she saw at age eight, as both frightening and beautiful. She has suggested that this memory may be one reason she photographs cherry blossoms repeatedly*12. Her color took shape through childhood memories of the stage, Tokyo’s visual environment, her immediate reactions while shooting, and the printing and editing that followed.

    Keeping Vivid Color and Black in the Same Frame

    Ninagawa’s color does not depend solely on the intensity of red, blue, yellow, and green. The Hara Museum contrasted “Ninagawa Color,” associated with vitality and splendor, with works marked by distortion, decline, stagnation, and death, describing noir as a series in which black and color enter into one another*1. At the 2008 Tokyo Opera City exhibition, every work from Acid Bloom was arranged as a panorama surrounding the visitor*19. At the entrance, noir covered a window surface six meters high and sixteen meters wide*16. Beyond the color of any single photograph, filling the field of vision with a sequence of similarly intense images also heightens the experience of color. In a 2011 two-person exhibition with Daido Moriyama, Moriyama found both glare and unease in Ninagawa’s flowers, while Ninagawa responded to the density of shadow in Moriyama’s photographs of Hawaii*7. Blossoms at their peak, black backgrounds, reflections on water, and damaged surfaces are all pushed forward with equal force, leaving traces of decay and unease within the vivid color.

    Flowers, Artificial Flowers, and Goldfish: Beauty Shaped by Human Desire

    Ninagawa’s flowers, artificial flowers, and goldfish are bound up with the human desire to preserve beauty and reshape it. She has said that flowers make life and death, light and darkness especially visible because they begin to wither as soon as they bloom*20. In the photobook Everlasting Flowers, she photographed artificial flowers left at graves. Although made of plastic, they carry the wish to offer the dead flowers blooms that never wither; Ninagawa has said that this led her to reconsider the distinction between the “real” and the “fake”*20. Her interest in goldfish brings human desire into the image even more directly. She sees goldfish as creatures shaped by the desire to possess something strange: the longer the fins or the larger the growths, the shorter their lives may become, even as people find these altered forms beautiful and cute*20. Flowers wither, artificial flowers are made not to wither, and goldfish have their bodies altered in the pursuit of beauty. Ninagawa’s recurring motifs bring together the loss of life with the human wish to keep beautiful things close and make them more beautiful still.

    Self-image: Reediting the Self Across Time

    The Hara Museum exhibition Self-image placed monochrome self-portraits, made intermittently since Ninagawa’s early career, alongside noir and PLANT A TREE*1. In Happiness Self Portrait 1996, being alone with the camera in her room was described as a feeling of traveling inside herself*9. Nearly twenty years later, Ninagawa selected those early self-images again and placed them in the same exhibition as her best-known color works. Their meaning changes through the act of editing past photographs from a later point in time, rather than fixing the self at the moment of exposure.

    In Film, Desire Unfolds Through a Character’s Time

    Ninagawa has said that in photography she captures emotion first and words come later, whereas in film she begins with what she wants to communicate*20. In Sakuran, she linked goldfish that cannot survive if returned to nature and cannot leave their tanks with courtesans unable to leave Yoshiwara*20. The goldfish she had repeatedly photographed became a motif linking a body made beautiful to an enclosed environment. In Helter Skelter, the story centers on the body of a top model who maintains her beauty through full-body cosmetic surgery and is praised, consumed, and ultimately begins to collapse*6. Ninagawa has explained that the film drew on her own experience of being seen from the outside through the “girl photography” boom and her relationship to a famous father, as well as the insecurities she had carried as a woman*14. The short lives of flowers and goldfish, manufactured beauty, and images made for consumption become, in film, stories of characters driven by desire and changing over time*20.

    From Anger to Preserving a Moment

    Ninagawa has recalled that, when she was younger, anger at gender inequality and at being rejected before people had even seen her work drove her to keep making work*4. As she began to feel the limits of working from anger alone, her father died in 2016. Imagining how the world might have looked to him as he was dying allowed her to feel that she could turn her attention toward beautiful things in everyday life*4. During roughly a year and a half of the COVID-19 pandemic, she visited botanical gardens and other places, photographing moments that moved her with an almost obsessive intensity, then selected the exhibition works from approximately 40,000 images*3. In a recent interview, she said that the beauty of everyday life can disappear easily, and that she wants to preserve those moments in photographs*5.

    § 03 / 04 After “Girl Photography”: Shifts in Reception

    What Remained After the Boom

    By the late 2000s, Ninagawa’s reception was moving beyond the generational category of “girl photography.” A 2008 program at UCLA’s Hammer Museum described the broader tendency as a mix of aspirations toward fine art, glossy fashion magazines, and the snapshot sensibility of mobile phones*22. A UCLA record from the same year noted that diary-like methods had been rapidly absorbed into mainstream photography and that some of the movement’s stars quickly disappeared, while Ninagawa and Mikiko Hara had developed mature individual styles and come to be recognized as independent international artists*23.

    Compared with Neo-Pop: What Set Her Apart from Takashi Murakami?

    In 2009, Germany’s Priska Pasquer Gallery placed Ninagawa, at first glance, close to the Japanese Neo-Pop associated with Takashi Murakami*24. The gallery drew a distinction, however: Murakami’s T-shirts and watches remained largely within debates around contemporary art and pop culture, while Ninagawa’s photobooks and publications reached a broader audience directly. It also noted that she did not feel the need to justify her use of pop imagery from within the art world. In 2008, Vanity Fair likewise compared Ninagawa with Takashi Murakami and Sofia Coppola, presenting her as a figure who joined high fashion with heightened drama*27. By this point, international comparisons had expanded beyond her peers in “girl photography” to artists working across art, film, and fashion.

    An Exhibition Like a Magazine: Praise and Resistance

    This proximity to mass culture generated resistance as well as praise. A 2009 Tokyo Art Beat review compared walking through Earthly Flowers, Heavenly Colors to flipping through a glossy magazine*25. The critic read the early works as editorial pages, the goldfish, celebrities, and artificial flowers as feature spreads, and the portraits of women as advertisements or information pages, arguing that the intellectual content was weak compared with the sensory stimulation*25. The same critic described the display of large and small prints pinned directly to the wall in noir as reminiscent of Wolfgang Tillmans*25. MoMA describes Tillmans as an artist who pioneered the widespread use of taping and pinning photographs directly to walls*26. The comparison concerns a display method; it does not establish Tillmans as an influence on Ninagawa. Priska Pasquer Gallery valued Ninagawa’s ability to reach broadly into mass culture*24. The Tokyo Art Beat critic saw that same magazine-like sensibility as a weakness in a museum*25. By the late 2000s, the question surrounding Ninagawa had shifted from whether she belonged to “girl photography” to how museums should assess a visual language already in wide circulation.

    Toward Self-Image, Female Representation, and Spatial Installation

    From the 2010s onward, critics and institutions approached Ninagawa through a wider range of subjects. The Hara Museum’s 2015 exhibition Self-image presented her well-known color works alongside monochrome self-portraits made since her early career, noir, and PLANT A TREE, bringing the artist’s own image and a sense of death to the foreground*1. In film scholarship, Helter Skelter was discussed in 2016 through female voice, the reception of Western imagery, and the process of adapting Kyoko Okazaki’s manga for the screen*28. In 2025, FOLLOWERS was examined as a work through which to consider Japanese postfeminism beyond a solely Western-centered frame*29. Also in 2025, Vogue Italia compared her immersive exhibition in Venice to a Sofia Coppola film and reported Ninagawa’s view that, even as she added film and installation to photography, the center of her interests had not changed*30. Her later reception no longer settled into a single label, extending across pop culture, self-image, representations of women, film, and spatial installation.

    § 04 / 04 Commercial Images and the Move Toward Collaboration

    The Speed of Tokyo and the Experience of Commercial Photography

    Ninagawa describes Tokyo, where she was born and raised, as a place where disparate things mix and are consumed at tremendous speed, and identifies human desire as a subject deeply tied to the city*12. In the same interview, she also cites the colors of ukiyo-e and kimono as influences*12. In a Tokyo where fashionable people, products, styles, and new images continually appear, Ninagawa herself first drew attention through “girl photography” and later continued to create images of others for advertising and magazines. In a 2026 interview, she said that advertising work is also part of who she is, and reflected that two experiences have long been intertwined: having an image imposed on her through the “girl photography” boom and her relationship to a famous father, and creating images of others through advertising*15. In the 2008 retrospective, portraits of more than 200 people made for magazines and advertising appeared alongside flowers, goldfish, and early works*17. She has also explained that Helter Skelter drew on her experience of being seen from the outside and the insecurities she had carried as a woman*14.

    Taking Photographs Alone, Building Spaces with a Team

    The 2008 retrospective brought together more than 500 photographs*13. The exhibition was divided into nine sections, including early works, flowers, goldfish, travel, and portraits, while noir covered a window surface six meters high and sixteen meters wide*16. In the goldfish room, Liquid Dreams light boxes were combined with a giant video projection*18. At the Tokyo Metropolitan Teien Art Museum in 2022, she opened the curtains to admit natural light, allowing the photographs to change with the time of day and the weather*3. For the 2023–24 TOKYO NODE exhibition, all eleven works were newly created for the site, combining photography, video, three-dimensional elements, architecture, music, and stage design*11. Ninagawa has said that filmmaking taught her the pleasure of mixing her ideas with those of others, shifting her sense of creation from “I” to “we”*4. At the Kyoto City KYOCERA Museum of Art in 2025, EiM—a team including a data scientist, set designer, creative director, and lighting director—co-produced an exhibition incorporating video and three-dimensional work*10. The solitary act of pressing the shutter remains the starting point. When those photographs enter exhibition space, she now works with others to build light, sound, architecture, and moving images around them.
