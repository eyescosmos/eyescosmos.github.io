    Kazuyoshi Nomachi

    野町和嘉 1970s
    CountryJapan Period1970–1980s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Kazuyoshi Nomachi spent years photographing the Sahara, the Nile, Ethiopia, Tibet, and Mecca, recording terrain, labor, pilgrimage, and praying bodies in color. The clear composition of advertising photography and the international color magazines of the 1970s shaped his visual language. A boy’s prayer witnessed in the Sahara, wide-ranging travel by car and aircraft, and repeated returns to the same places shaped his subjects and working duration. In Sahara, Bahar, Tibet, and Mecca Pilgrimage, he moved away from the instant of an event to report on the time of river basins and pilgrimage routes. The same books also raise a question: how does cross-cultural photography organize other cultures into a story of “land and prayer”?

    What this photographer changed

    Nomachi shifted the unit of photojournalistic inquiry from events to deserts, river systems, and pilgrimage routes. Aerial views and long shots show the terrain and the movement of people; close views record individuals engaged in prayer and labor. His photobooks bring together the dry and rainy seasons, river source and mouth, and the departure and arrival of pilgrims across several years. He joined the communicative force of advertising photography and color magazines to long-term reporting. At the same time, his use of an “archetype of prayer” to connect different religions and societies risks reducing their historical and political differences.

    § 01 / 04 Advertising, International Color Magazines, and the Sahara: The Formation of Nomachi’s Method

    Bringing the Compositional Force of Advertising into Overseas Documentary Photography

    Kazuyoshi Nomachi was born in Kochi Prefecture in 1946. After studying with photographer Takashi Kijima, he became independent in 1971*1. Before independence, he worked in advertising photography, learning how composition could communicate a product’s subject quickly, how to organize areas of color, and how light could attract attention in print. Nippon.com explains that Nomachi consciously carried the clear messages and vivid color of advertising into his later documentary work*7. He retained these skills after moving into reportage, using them to convey the relation among terrain, people, and ritual in a single image to international readers who did not share the language or historical background of his subjects.

    The Sahara in 1972: From the Horizon to Water and Daily Life

    The turning point came in 1972, when Nomachi visited the Sahara at the age of twenty-five. He has written that he traveled to North Africa out of a fascination with the horizon, then shifted his attention from photographing sand and rock to people searching for water, tending livestock, and moving across the desert*3. His interest in terrain moved from uninhabited spectacle to the ways water sources, pasture, trade routes, and differences between daytime and nighttime temperatures shaped daily life. Placing figures within the expanse of the desert allowed him to show the distances people walked and the places where they found water and shade, as well as the scale of the environment.

    A Boy at Prayer and the Working Hypothesis of an “Archetype of Prayer”

    Nomachi’s interest in religion was decisively shaped by the prayer of a sixteen-year-old boy who traveled with him in North Africa. He later described how the boy, who had been joking moments earlier, stood beyond a dune, bent, knelt, and repeatedly pressed his forehead to the ground*10. Photography cannot directly record an inner state of faith. It can register the body’s orientation, contact with the ground, repeated movement, distance traveled, and the flow of a crowd. From this encounter came a method that placed the desert, plateau, religious architecture, and pilgrimage route surrounding the body within the same body of work as close portraits.

    Nomachi understood deserts and plateaus as “religious spaces” in which individuals confront a scale beyond themselves. After witnessing full-body prostration in Tibet, he began to seek an “archetype of prayer” across Islam, Christianity, and Buddhism*8. For the 2005 exhibition A Photographer’s Pilgrimage, he wrote that globalization was standardizing places around shared values of efficiency and convenience, while a renewed attachment to one’s own culture was intensifying through religious revival*16. The “archetype of prayer” became a working structure for considering what sustained local cultures amid increasing uniformity, as well as a search for bodily actions held in common. He linked standing, kneeling, touching the forehead to the ground, circling a sacred site, and walking great distances across the Sahara, Tibet, Mecca, the Ganges, and the Andes. This emphasis on commonality can also reduce differences of state, ethnicity, class, and religious institution.

    Overseas Travel and Color Magazines as a Publishing Network in the 1970s

    Nomachi’s long-term work abroad depended on more than individual resolve. During the 1970s, overseas travel from Japan became less costly and logistically easier. A travel boom and growing interest in other cultures helped international color magazines such as National Geographic and GEO reach large audiences*7. Sahara (1978) began as a dummy book submitted to Heibonsha and developed into a collaborative project that included the Italian publisher Mondadori, leading to editions in several countries*7. Color printing and international co-publication helped sustain hundreds of days of fieldwork as a profession. They also created pressure to condense complicated regions into forceful single images that could be understood at once.

    § 02 / 04 Cars, Aerial Photography, and Return Visits: Photographing Broad Geography Over Extended Time

    Combining Wide-Ranging Travel with Long-Term Stays

    Nomachi returned to the Sahara ten times through 1993, spending more than eight hundred days there in total. For his work on the Nile from 1980 to 1982, he used a Land Cruiser shipped from Japan as well as aircraft. Across five trips, he spent roughly four hundred days traveling through the basins of the White and Blue Nile*7. A short visit tends to center unfamiliar scenery; a stay in one place cannot account for the routes connecting source and mouth, dry and rainy seasons, or pastoral and agricultural life. Cars and aircraft allowed him to understand the terrain as a whole. Repeated visits then allowed him to photograph seasons, festivals, labor, and family time, presenting a region as a connected field of movement and daily life instead of a collection of isolated points.

    Long Shots and Close Views: Geography as a Whole and the Individual Body

    Luminous Landscape identifies Nomachi’s movement between long shots that place figures within vast terrain and close views of faces and bodies as a defining feature of his work*10. The long shots make water sources, grazing land, pilgrimage routes, rock-hewn churches, and the paths circling the Kaaba legible. The close views record fatigue, age, hands and feet, clothing, meals, and proximity to livestock. In Nomachi’s photobooks, elevated or aerial photographs of terrain and crowds are followed by close views of individuals made at ground level. The first describes routes of movement and collective structure; the second shows the bodies that travel those routes.

    Color and Dramatic Light: The Selectivity of Immediate Legibility

    Nomachi’s color photographs organize sand, rock, water, clothing, and religious architecture through firm contours and areas of color. White pilgrimage garments, red earth, blue shadows, golden sand, and the darkness inside rock-hewn churches allow readers across languages to distinguish climate, time of day, material, and ritual function quickly*7. Sunrise, reflected light on dunes, and beams entering a church also shape contemporary lives into epic and monumental scenes. This direct communicative force underlies international comparisons with Sebastião Salgado and Steve McCurry*10. The same form selects terrain, clothing, ritual, and light as the elements most likely to be remembered, while roads, urbanization, commodity circulation, and political institutions receive less attention.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works: The Sahara, the Nile, Tibet, Mecca, and Siberia

    Sahara: From Desert Scenery to Lives in Motion

    Sahara brought oases, nomadism, trade, gathering, and prayer into a regional sequence alongside the geometry of sand and sky. On the official Sahara Horizon page, Nomachi’s text and photographs trace his movement from uninhabited mineral landscapes toward the lives of people bound to the desert*3. Compositions pairing the horizon with a small figure show distances of travel and water. They can also condense a life into the emblem of a person enduring the land.

    Bahar: Photographing the Nile as a Basin Across Borders

    Bahar: Africa Flows was published in 1983, structuring the sources and mouth of the White and Blue Nile as a continuity of peoples, pastoral life, religion, and terrain*2. Views of the river, floodwater, cattle, settlements, rituals, and close portraits alternate, bringing several countries and ways of life connected to the water system into one book. Bahar and Sahara Everlasting earned Nomachi the third Domon Ken Award in 1984*1. The river itself, instead of a single event, became the unit of reportage.

    Tibet: Altitude, Distance, and Full-Body Prostration

    Nomachi’s coverage of China’s Long March, begun in 1988, led him to the Tibetan Plateau and was published in 1994 as Tibet: The Heavenly Land*2. The official Tibetan Plateau page brings Mount Kailash, Lake Manasarovar, grazing land, monasteries, and pilgrimage routes into the same album*4. Distant views of the plateau establish the length of the pilgrimage. Worn clothing, gloves, foreheads, and marks on the ground record what full-body prostration leaves on the body. The sacred site appears as an itinerary of altitude, cold, walking, and lodging, as well as a work of architecture.

    Mecca Pilgrimage: Permission, Conversion, and a View of the Crowd

    In 1994, a Saudi publisher commissioned Nomachi to photograph Medina. That year he recited the shahada and converted to Islam. Nippon.com records both his long-standing interest in Islam, beginning with the Sahara, and the practical conditions that made work in the holy cities possible*7. From 1995 to 2000, he photographed Mecca and Medina, recording the pilgrimage through tawaf, Arafat, Mina, and Muzdalifah from aerial views of crowds and ground-level views of individuals*6. The official Mecca page shows the circular movement around the Kaaba, prayer, travel, and rest as a continuous sequence*5.

    The English-language book Mecca, the Blessed, Medina, the Radiant combines Nomachi’s photographs with texts by the Islamic scholar Seyyed Hossein Nasr and historical materials*11. A Photographer’s Pilgrimage brings the Sahara, Tibet, Mecca, and the Andes together as a “path of prayer”*12. The combination of photographs and explanatory texts situates religious ritual through architecture, history, and routes of pilgrimage; it also integrates different faiths into a single spiritual history.

    Siberian Prison Camp 1992: State and History Beyond Prayer

    Reducing Nomachi’s work to prayer and pilgrimage omits another axis of his career as a photojournalist. In March 1992, immediately after the collapse of the Soviet Union, he photographed a corrective labor camp near Khabarovsk in the Russian Far East. Exhibition materials from the Japan Professional Photographers Society describe the series as a record of two connected subjects: a system that used prisoners as labor, and the uneventful daily routines that continued inside it. The work was made during a brief period when state control had loosened*19. Here the expansive land does not serve as a setting for religious transcendence. It is the place where the state isolated and managed people, and the prisoners’ repetitive lives register institutional time instead of faith. The series shows that Nomachi’s long-term fieldwork and his placement of figures within an environment could record political systems and history as well as sacred places.

    § 04 / 04 Access to Sacred Places, Cross-Cultural Imagery, and Nomachi’s Place in Photographic History

    Becoming a Participant Does Not Remove the Conditions of Access

    Non-Muslims are not permitted to enter Mecca. Nomachi’s photographs depended on a Saudi commission, special permissions, conversion, and participation in the pilgrimage on several occasions. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum situates his work from 1995 to 2000 as a long-term record of Mecca and the pilgrimage*6. Conversion allowed him to enter the ritual route, but did not make the photographer a complete insider to the community. The places he could photograph, the aerial viewpoints available to him, and the scenes that could be published were structured by religious institutions and state authorization. The exceptional perspectives of the work cannot be separated from these conditions of access.

    The Risk That Long-Term Documentation Produces an “Unchanging Tradition”

    The ethnographic impulse to preserve a culture in a supposedly pure form before it disappears has been criticized as a “salvage paradigm”*13. When roads, state institutions, tourists, communications technology, and urban labor appear infrequently in Nomachi’s frames while ritual and the natural environment dominate, the people photographed can be read as bearers of a culture unchanged since antiquity. A 2015 FUJIFILM SQUARE exhibition described the work as “a documentary of land and people,” while also presenting the living environment and spiritual cultures of the photographed regions as “almost unchanged”*15. This reception shows both the photographs’ ability to connect land and life and the risk of interpreting a changing society as an enduring tradition. National Geographic has itself acknowledged a history of depicting non-Western people as exotic and close to nature while giving insufficient attention to race, power, and politics*14. Nomachi’s “archetype of prayer” can likewise push local institutions and histories into the background when it gathers bodily actions from different sects and societies into a single universality.

    Nomachi’s fieldwork, however, was not a succession of one-time journeys. He returned to the Sahara, the Nile, Ethiopia, and Tibet, photographing different seasons and decades. The Setagaya Art Museum’s 2025 exhibition placed early photographs beside later returns and took political instability, urbanization, mobile phones, and other changes in the photographed regions as part of its premise*9. Reading these recorded changes alongside the monumental scenes of prayer allows the work to be assessed as more than the preservation of a “vanishing tradition.”

    From Events to River Basins and Pilgrimage Routes: Nomachi’s Place in Color Documentary Photography

    When Nomachi began working abroad in the 1970s, the weekly edition of LIFE had ended. Monthly magazines such as National Geographic and GEO were gaining influence by presenting distant lands and cultures in large-format color*7. Nomachi retained photography’s reportorial ability to communicate distant circumstances, but he did not use the time of a war or political event as the basic unit of inquiry. Over several years, he photographed lives crossing deserts, the Nile basin, routes taken by pilgrims, and seasonal festivals, assembling aerial photographs, long shots, and close views in photobooks.

    The compositional discipline of advertising, the expansion of overseas travel, international color magazines and co-publication, and movement by car and aircraft gave Nomachi’s form its visual and material conditions. Repeated visits totaling hundreds of days and his hypothesis that an “archetype of prayer” could be sought through terrain and the body shaped its duration and subject. Its historical distinction lies in retaining the communicative force of photojournalism while shifting the subject from the event to the river basin and pilgrimage route, and the duration from an instant to several years. The same clear color and monumental composition organize different societies into a universal account of “land and prayer.” The strength of the long record and the limitations of cross-cultural imagery made for international magazines must be examined within the same photographs.

    International reception has valued both the reportorial and formal qualities of Nomachi’s work. Le vie del sacro, held at MACRO/La Pelanda in Rome from 2013 to 2014, was his first European retrospective. It divided roughly two hundred photographs into seven sections; the official materials praised pictorial compositions in which figures and landscape merge, and light that remains descriptive while carrying a sense of transcendence*17. In 2014, the Photographic Society of Japan presented Nomachi with its International Award for contributions to international photographic culture*20. Alongside the duration of his fieldwork, this reception depended on images whose strong color, horizons, and crowds could be grasped across national borders.

    Later retrospectives have moved beyond defining Nomachi solely as a photographer of sacred places. A 2023 exhibition at the Museum of Art, Kochi assembled 215 photographs in three parts: “A Documentary of Land and People,” World Heritage sites, and Siberian Prison Camp 1992. It placed work made in religious spaces and within state institutions in the same career*18. Understanding Nomachi’s form requires both the photographs that place praying bodies against the scale of the land and those that record bodies confined by the state. The method of organizing broad geography and extended time within a single image and across a photobook remains consistent, while the forces it describes differ among religion, nature, and the state.
