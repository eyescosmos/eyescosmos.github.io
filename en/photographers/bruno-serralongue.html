    Bruno Serralongue

    ブルーノ・セラロング
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Bruno Serralongue has photographed international conferences, summits, migrant-support networks, the sans-papiers movement, the Calais “Jungle,” and the ZAD at Notre-Dame-des-Landes without press credentials, using a 4×5-inch view camera from the same spaces available to ordinary participants. His series attend to people waiting, temporary structures, peripheral conversations, and the time in which assemblies disperse instead of the dramatic peak of an event. The conditions under which a photograph becomes news become part of the subject.

    What this photographer changed

    Since the 1970s, critical documentary has questioned the photographer’s power to represent others and the mechanisms through which news institutions select events. Serralongue turned that critique into a repeatable working procedure: traveling independently to conferences and protests, maintaining distance from press accreditation, making a limited number of photographs with a 4×5-inch view camera, and returning them through exhibitions and photobooks to a temporality different from that of the news image. At international conferences he photographed outside accredited viewpoints; in Calais, daily life organized around waiting to move; in the sans-papiers movement, the repeated public appearance of a collective; and at the ZAD, the overlap between protest and communal living. He shifted the center of political photography from dramatic confrontation to the time and material support that sustain a movement, making shooting conditions and routes of circulation part of the political facts shown by the image.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    Questioning Photographic Access within the Media Space of the 1990s

    Bruno Serralongue studied art history in Paris, graduated from the École nationale supérieure de la photographie in Arles in 1993, and completed his studies at Villa Arson in Nice in 1995*20. From the mid-1990s he began photographing political assemblies, international conferences, and protest movements. In the period of globalization after the Cold War, meetings such as the G7, the World Social Forum, and the World Summit on the Information Society became vast media events in which politicians, corporations, NGOs, activists, and news organizations gathered in the same city. Alongside the event itself, accreditation, press platforms, media centers, and transmission deadlines determined what could be seen. Cnap describes Serralongue’s work as an inquiry into the procedures through which information is produced and circulated as images*1.

    He travels to sites in a manner similar to a photojournalist but works without an agency assignment or press card, photographing from places open to ordinary participants. In a text preserved by Air de Paris, he describes traveling to Kosovo at his own expense and using a view camera alongside reporters without an official press card or invitation*2. KADIST notes that he repeated this method across different events, including local festivals, world expositions, and political gatherings in Chiapas, creating a speed and position distinct from those of press photographers*19. This self-imposed limit inscribes the photographer’s position as something produced by both the politics of the subject and the institutions that regulate access.

    Documentary after the “Decisive Moment”

    Twentieth-century photojournalism often privileged moments in which the meaning of an event appeared compressed into a single image. A clash between police and demonstrators, a handshake between leaders, or a border crossing is readily used by newspapers and agencies. Most political activity, however, consists of preparation, waiting, meetings, travel, meals, the construction of tents, repeated marches, and failed negotiations. The Centre Photographique d’Île-de-France explains that Serralongue accepts the slowness of a large-format camera, moving away from the shocking single image to photograph backstage areas and the margins of his subject*3.

    This method records preparation, waiting, and peripheral figures that news images tend to remove. Agency photographs are transmitted immediately with captions; Serralongue’s pictures are developed, selected, and organized into series before appearing in exhibitions months or years later. Fondation Pernod Ricard describes him as producing information independently since the mid-1990s, with exhibitions as its primary destination*4. The delay turns the photograph from an alternative news report into material for reconsidering the understanding produced by the news.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work

    How a 4×5-Inch View Camera Changes the Photographer’s Speed and Power

    The 4×5-inch view camera used by Serralongue requires a tripod, composition on a ground glass, and the insertion of a film holder before exposure. It cannot follow a crowd or shoot rapidly like a handheld small-format camera. Baronian’s artist materials describe his method of attending events without press credentials, using a large-format camera, and presenting uncropped prints*5. The slow equipment changes the photographer’s body from one chasing an event to one remaining in place and observing over time, alongside its association with a classical photographic aesthetic.

    During the long preparation, subjects become aware of the camera and the photographer shares the duration of the site. The relative anonymity of the quick snapshot diminishes; photographing becomes part of the site’s continuity. Research published in Depth of Field analyzes the slowness produced by a technical camera and tripod as a counter to the speed of snapshot and press photography*6. The high-resolution negative records the wording of banners, temporary architecture, objects on the ground, and peripheral figures with equal clarity. It gives the material and labor that constitute a political site the same visual density as its central action.

    Photographing the Process by Which Events Become Images

    In the series made at international conferences, news crews, barriers, waiting participants, tourists, and the areas outside conference halls often replace political leaders. Cnap’s text on Société de l’Information states that Serralongue questions the production and circulation of informational images and uses the slowness of the large-format camera to make room for backstage areas and margins*7. Through the press corps, fences, waiting participants, and exterior space, the photographs show how a conference is made visible instead of explaining its decisions.

    This position gives priority to the procedures by which photographic documents are produced over a simple opposition between objectivity and subjectivity. Jeu de Paume notes that photographs carry historical information while remaining relative and ambiguous, and describes Serralongue as constructing an independent model of narrative at a distance from photojournalism*8. He retains photography’s evidentiary force while making visible how camera position, equipment, selection, captions, and exhibition shape the appearance of evidence. The work enters the information system through another procedure.

    Waiting, Backstage Activity, and Collective Duration as Political Time

    Serralongue’s photographs often show the intervals before and after dramatic confrontation. People wait for buses, gather around tents, eat, stand holding banners, and look into the distance. Such ordinary scenes show how political communities are sustained through repeated time, care, movement, and material supplies. Galerie Francesca Pia’s materials for Pour la vie present long-term struggles and sites of living, including Notre-Dame-des-Landes, as series situated outside the logic of news*9.

    In these quiet images, waiting postures and surrounding spaces shape the roles of figures alongside banners and uniforms. The persistence of a collective comes forward in place of an individual heroic image. Series including Sunday treat days without an event and peripheral time as what sustains the continuity of political action. An essay in Mousse argues that Serralongue’s work addresses the boundaries between event and non-event, the visible and the unseen, and information and everyday life*10.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Calais and the “Jungle” — Competing Image Systems in the Same Place

    Since the mid-2000s, Serralongue has repeatedly photographed Sangatte, Calais, and the area around the English Channel: people attempting to reach Britain, support workers, police, and temporary settlements. The Centre Pompidou exhibition Calais. Witnessing the “Jungle” placed Serralongue’s photographs beside AFP news images and testimony from former residents, comparing the different functions, roles, and statuses of images*11. Agency photographs reported incidents at the border; Serralongue’s series showed tents, roads, fences, and waiting as a long-term environment; residents’ testimony supplied experiences of movement and daily life missing from both sets of photographs.

    Calais raises differences in mobility between photographer and subject in addition to differences in shooting speed. A French artist able to move freely photographs people attempting to cross a border and transports their images into museums. Serralongue avoids resolving this asymmetry through a single sympathetic portrait. By participating in an exhibition that placed press photographs, an artist’s series, and residents’ words together, he made visible whose images are trusted as information and whose experience is relegated to explanatory text. FRAC Grand Large’s Getting to England likewise moves border crossing away from a decisive instant toward repeated attempts, places, and infrastructure*12.

    Sans-Papiers and the ZAD — The Difficulty of Representing a Collective

    In the series on the Paris sans-papiers movement, people with precarious immigration status repeatedly enter public space through assemblies, marches, and meetings. CHRD incorporated images from Manifestations d’un collectif de sans-papiers and an interview with the artist into an exhibition on migration history*13. The issue is the continuing process by which people rendered legally difficult to see create public presence as a collective, rather than the concentration of the movement in a single emblematic face. The series records the repetition of the same demands across different dates and places as a political form.

    At the ZAD of Notre-Dame-des-Landes, opposition to airport construction developed into agriculture, building, meals, meetings, and collective work within an occupied territory. The sans-papiers series addresses a movement that appears in public space; the ZAD photographs follow protest as it becomes a long-term living environment. Francesca Pia’s project page presents assemblies, landscapes, temporary structures, and people within the same series*14. By giving political aims and the miscellaneous work of maintaining collective life equal density, the photographs extend the image of protest from heroic confrontation toward social endurance.

    Turning Exhibitions and Photobooks into “Slow News Media”

    Serralongue’s completed works join the exposed negatives to selection, sequence, captions, and spacing in an exhibition. As Fondation Pernod Ricard observes, the exhibition becomes the principal outlet for his independently produced information*4. In a newspaper, a single image carries the main point of an article and is replaced by other news the next day. An exhibition allows several photographs to remain present at once; viewers can return, compare, and take time.

    Cnap’s research report records his documentary work through the relations among research, photography, serial form, and institutional support*15. Photobooks connect different cities and dates through page sequence, relocating events outside news time. As immediacy recedes, the meaning of a photograph remains open to later change. Serralongue uses the art space as another public sphere in which images can be compared over time.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    Does an Anti-Spectacular Image Guarantee Anything beyond Boredom?

    Photographs that avoid spectacular moments can criticize the theater of the news, but they may also weaken political urgency and reach only audiences who visit museums. Distance from press accreditation does not remove the difference in position between photographer and participants. Serralongue retains the authority to select others through a large-format camera and to transfer the resulting works into the art market and public collections. Jeu de Paume’s retrospective materials emphasize objectivity and documentary ambiguity in order to understand his method as an experiment comparing image systems, distanced from any self-image of innocent witnessing*8.

    With this limit in view, the meaning of anti-spectacle lies in comparing the different ways visibility is organized in each event, rather than in an ethics automatically granted by photographing the margins. International conferences are structured by fences and press platforms; Calais by temporary settlements and border infrastructure; the sans-papiers movement by collective repetition; and the ZAD by the buildings and labor that sustain life. Serralongue’s quiet compositions risk flattening these differences into one aesthetic of waiting, yet they are used to compare conditions of appearance specific to each event.

    Critical Documentary after Allan Sekula and Martha Rosler

    Since the 1970s, Allan Sekula and Martha Rosler have criticized documentary photography for classifying its subjects and exercising benevolence and power at the same time. Serralongue carries this legacy forward by changing the conditions of photography rather than relying primarily on textual denunciation. He travels to the site and makes highly detailed evidentiary images while disclosing access, speed, selection, and circulation as parts of the work. Exhibition materials from Galerie des Galeries present his photographs as an inquiry into media rules and political appearance*16.

    Here “post-documentary” describes an attitude concerned with both photography’s capacity to convey reality and the institutions that shape that conveyance. Cnap’s exhibitions and research materials place Serralongue within political situations, investigative procedures, and public display, showing that his method has been received through both photographic aesthetics and institutional critique*17.

    Place in Photographic History — Turning Institutional Critique into a Photographic Procedure

    Allan Sekula and Martha Rosler had shown that a photograph’s meaning is produced by writing, classification, archives, and news institutions as well as by its subject. Serralongue’s distinction lies in translating that prior critique from a claim made in accompanying text into a consistent photographic procedure: traveling outside systems of accreditation, using a large-format camera, limiting the number of exposures, avoiding cropping, building series, and exhibiting the results.

    Through this procedure, the subject of political photography expands beyond demonstrators and migrants to include press platforms, barriers, tents, captions, and gallery walls. Air de Paris’s portfolio shows the method repeatedly applied to different events through series on international conferences, migration, protest movements, and occupied territories since the 1990s*18. His work adds to photographic history a model that examines both the image of an event and the institutions that produce and publicize it, moving beyond an appeal simply to “look slowly.” It creates an alternative informational time outside the news while leaving the power of the art institution that owns that time as an unresolved question.
