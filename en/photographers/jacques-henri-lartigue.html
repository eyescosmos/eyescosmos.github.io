    Jacques Henri Lartigue

    ジャック＝アンリ・ラルティーグ 1894–1986
    CountryFrance Period1890–1910s ChannelIssues in photo history · Private photography
    Abstract

    Jacques Henri Lartigue preserved family life, play, airplanes, automobiles, sports, and fashion in photographs, diaries, and albums from childhood onward. Fast shutters and experiments made within the family rendered jumps and moving vehicles as distortions unlike ordinary vision. Half a century after many of these images were made, the 1963 MoMA exhibition, Life magazine, and Richard Avedon’s editing established them as precedents for the modern snapshot.

    What this photographer changed

    Lartigue’s place in photo history lies less in participation in an art-photography movement than in the later discovery of domestic picture making by museums and publishers. A private practice that preserved fleeting pleasure and movement through diaries, albums, and photographs was recognized half a century later by museums and publishers as a major example of snapshot photography The distance between the moment of exposure and the moment of recognition is itself central to his historical significance.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    A Child’s Camera and the Belle Époque: The Family’s Technological World

    Born into a wealthy family, Lartigue began photographing at about eight years old after receiving a camera from his father. The official chronology records his continuing interest in family members, pets, models, automobiles, airplanes, sports, stereoscopic photography, and experiments with movement*1. Photography began as a domestic activity alongside diaries, drawings, and inventive play, not as professional training, and accumulated according to standards different from the exhibition finish expected by contemporary art photography.

    Saving Vanishing Moments in a Diary: Why Did the Child Keep Photographing?

    Lartigue did not begin with a plan to become a professional photographer. In a prosperous household where his father used photographic equipment, he employed the camera from around age seven to preserve everyday surprise and pleasure before they faded, combining photographs, writing, and drawings in his diary. The official archive and a Fujifilm retrospective describe an acute awareness of memory’s fragility and the pleasure of saving nearby events as the durable core of his work*16. Photography continued as a way of keeping pace with the passage of his own time, rather than as a project to produce representative images of society.

    What Did Amateur Photography Record? Leisure, Class, and Modern Life

    Seaside holidays, horse racing, tennis, skiing, motor races, aviation experiments, and elegant clothing recur throughout Lartigue’s early photographs. They preserve the early twentieth century’s enthusiasm for speed and leisure, while recording modern life from a privileged family environment rather than representing French society as a whole*2. The brightness of these images depended on social conditions that determined who could possess a camera and free time.

    The Class Conditions of the Belle Époque: Whose “Everyday Life” Was This?

    Automobiles, airplanes, seaside holidays, horse racing, and fashionable dress register new forms of life in the early twentieth century, but regular access to them belonged to a limited social group. Lartigue’s albums are internal records of a bourgeois household able to enjoy technology, travel, and leisure; they do not describe average life across France. Materials from The Photographers’ Gallery explicitly identify family wealth and access to early equipment as conditions of the work*13. Acknowledging those conditions preserves the buoyancy of the pictures while placing the resources that sustained happiness and speed back around the frame.

    Cameras and Shutters: The Technical Conditions That Made Modern Speed Visible

    Lartigue’s action photographs depended on more than personal talent. Glass plates, portable cameras, and fast shutters were beginning to enter domestic life. A family culture in which he handled machines with his father and brother and tested new devices encouraged him to aim the camera at racing cars and airplanes*9. His photographs record modern technology from within domestic play and invention. Camera, automobile, and aircraft belonged to the same world, making the albums a concrete account of early twentieth-century amateur culture.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    Stopping Speed: How Lartigue Photographed Automobiles and Airplanes

    Lartigue photographed racing automobiles, jumping bodies, and airplanes at the unstable peak of movement. In 《Grand Prix of the Automobile Club of France》, the car body and wheels warp while spectators stream diagonally across the frame*3. The distortion resulted from a focal-plane shutter exposing a rapidly moving subject in sequence. The combination of shutter scan, panning, and vehicle speed communicates mechanical velocity through deformation across the image rather than through precise contour.

    Distorted Speed: Play and Equipment, Not Futurist Theory

    The slanting elongation of the car and wheels in 《Le Grand Prix de l’A.C.F.》 resulted from the interaction of high speed and the scanning action of a focal-plane shutter*9. No evidence shows Lartigue presenting a Futurist theory of machine civilization. He repeatedly photographed flight, jumping, falling, and racing, testing as family play the camera’s ability to produce forms of movement different from ordinary vision. Formal novelty grew from repeated games and the properties of contemporary equipment.

    Bodies in Midair: Preserving Success and Failure as a Sequence

    Photographs of cousins jumping from stairs, women suspended in the air, and athletes chasing balls select the instant when the body leaves the ground. Lartigue did not seek a single successful exposure; he photographed the same games repeatedly and placed successful and failed attempts together in albums*1. Images of speed and the body emerged through this cycle of family performance, exposure, and shared results.

    Avenue du Bois de Boulogne: A Private Street Photography of Fashion

    In 《Avenue du Bois de Boulogne》, clothing, hats, and posture are caught quickly as women move through the street, making urban fashion an object of observation. Lartigue described these women in his diary as “beautiful people,” turning the street into a private collection of attractions*4. The gaze resembles later fashion and street photography, but at the time personal fascination and memory preceded any magazine commission.

    Photography and the Diary: An Apparatus of Image, Word, and Memory

    Lartigue combined photographs with diaries, handwritten accounts, weather records, and drawings, arranging them in a vast series of albums. Because descriptions of events and feelings remain around the images, photography functions as an index for replaying life rather than as an autonomous artwork*1. When later exhibitions and books extracted photographs from this setting, continuous family time was converted into independent images attributed to a named author.

    The Myth of the Innocent Genius: Separating the Time of Making from the Time of Discovery

    Lartigue is often introduced as a genius who saw modernity through the eyes of a boy. His photographic reputation, however, was formed half a century after many exposures, when images were reselected, enlarged, exhibited, and published in magazines. The official archive treats his career as a double chronology in which the time of production overlaps with the time of discovery*5. Including the people who selected particular pictures prevents childhood vision from being carried directly into present-day evaluation as a myth.

    Stereo Photography and Continuity: Play Beyond the Single Flat Image

    The official chronology records Lartigue’s experiments with stereoscopic photography, sequential exposures, and spirit photographs*1. Multiple frames of similar scenes remain in the albums, indicating how family members compared attempts to reproduce depth and motion. Concentrating only on the later selection of single images diminishes the play and repetition through which equipment was tested. His treatment of speed developed within that process of trial and error.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    The 1963 MoMA Exhibition: How Was “Lartigue the Photographer” Produced?

    In 1963, MoMA mounted a solo exhibition of Lartigue’s photographs under John Szarkowski. The show concentrated mainly on early images of movement and leisure, and Life featured the work that same year, turning a man long publicly active as a painter into an internationally recognized photographer*6. The museum extracted images of speed, abrupt framing, and chance from a private archive and presented a newly defined author.

    Richard Avedon and Diary of a Century: Reediting the Family Album

    Richard Avedon was deeply drawn to Lartigue’s photographs and diaries and participated in the editing of Diary of a Century in 1970. The official archive presents their relationship as an instance in which the maker and a later selector jointly shaped the image of an oeuvre*5. Page sequence, cropping, reproduction size, and the placement of text reorganized domestic records into an autobiographical narrative spanning the twentieth century.

    The Donation to the French State: Turning Private Records into a Public Archive

    In 1979, Lartigue donated his negatives, albums, and diaries to the French state. The Donation Jacques Henri Lartigue now oversees preservation, research, exhibitions, and rights management, maintaining private records of life as a public archive*1. This institutionalization allows relationships among different prints, albums, and texts to be studied, and makes the “discovery” of the 1960s available for reconsideration as a historical act of editing.

    A Long Career as a Painter: The Late Arrival of the Title “Photographer”

    Lartigue pursued painting as his public profession throughout his life and also worked in color photography, film, and travel photography. When later history narrows its attention to the speed photographs of his youth, his adult painting, color work, and film largely disappear. The official chronology records photography, painting, and cinema continuing in parallel before 1963*1. The canonical works produced after rediscovery represent one part of a life spent moving among several media.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    A Precursor of the Snapshot? Avoiding the Projection of Later Forms onto the Past

    Lartigue’s tilted frames, cropped bodies, and accidental movements share formal traits with later snapshot photography. At the time, he was preserving his own and his family’s experience in albums. The 1963 MoMA exhibition selected from that private production the speed and abruptness later viewers recognized as modern*6. Lartigue entered photo history as a case in which images made for another purpose converged with a later conception of photography.

    Who Historicizes Private Photography? The Question After Lartigue

    Lartigue’s reception shows the conditions under which amateur photographs, family albums, and diaries enter the museum. When representative images are removed from the sequence shared by a family, the relations among the people photographed, the writing, and the temporality of the pages change. Materials from The Photographers’ Gallery connect the work to Belle Époque life, technology, and the class conditions of the family*13. His historical importance includes both the attraction of private photographs and the process through which museums and editors remade their meaning.

    A Critique of Photographs of Happiness: Where Are War and Social Conflict?

    Although Lartigue lived through two world wars, his best-known photographs center on family play and leisure, with war and poverty seldom at the front of the image. Describing him as a photographer of “the twentieth century itself” carries this imbalance of vision. His albums show what one class and one household chose to see and what it left unphotographed. Images of happiness are selections of the experience he wished to preserve, and the same album retains both their appeal and their limits as historical evidence.

    MoMA and Avedon: The “Modernity” Selected from a Private Album

    The 1963 MoMA exhibition concentrated on dynamic images from childhood through the 1920s, producing a particular Lartigue from several decades of work. Avedon’s subsequent editing of Diary of a Century arranged photographs, diaries, and handwriting as a twentieth-century autobiography and consolidated an international reputation*17. The value of the work was formed jointly by its original domestic use and by later exhibitions and editing. The speed, cropping, and chance selected by museum and editor became established as Lartigue’s representative style.

    § REL Related photographers & movements
    Related photographers
    • Henri Cartier-Bresson — A photographer known for the concept of the ‘decisive moment,’ resonant with Lartigue in the snapshot methodology of seizing everyday moments.
    • Brassaï — A photographer who likewise recorded the daily life of Paris, positioned with Lartigue as visual testimony of French living culture.
    • André Kertész — A snapshot practitioner who valued the moment and a poetic gaze, compared with Lartigue in cutting the everyday from a private viewpoint.
    Related movements
    • Street Photography — Lartigue’s early snapshot practice is cited as a precursor of the ‘capture of the moment’ later systematized as street photography.
    • Modernism — MoMA’s 1963 ‘discovery’ was an institutional reappraisal that repositioned Lartigue’s photographs within the context of modernist photography.
