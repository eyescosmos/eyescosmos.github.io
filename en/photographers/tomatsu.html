    Shomei Tomatsu

    東松照明 1930–2012
    CountryJapan Period1950–1960s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Shomei Tomatsu photographed postwar Japan as a continuing presence in matter and bodies, from melted glass and scarred skin in Nagasaki to English signs around American bases and the sea and rituals of Okinawa. In 11:02 Nagasaki and The Pencil of the Sun, symbolic close-ups, chains of fragments, and photobook sequence produced a view of the atomic bomb, occupation, Americanization, and Okinawa that resists consolidation into a single national story.

    What this photographer changed

    Tomatsu did not organize the postwar as a completed event. He photographed its duration in melted bottles, skin, signs, chewing gum, fences, sea, and ritual. Moving away from distant views that claim to explain society as a whole, he linked fragments of matter and bodies to photograph how occupation, the atomic bomb, Americanization, and Okinawa persisted within everyday life This approach substantially renewed the critical language of postwar Japanese photography.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From Iwanami Shashin Bunko to VIVO: Changing the Method of Social Photography

    Tomatsu worked on Iwanami Shashin Bunko in the 1950s, gaining experience with photo essays designed to explain regions and industries. In 1959 he formed VIVO with Eikoh Hosoe, Kikuji Kawada, Ikko Narahara, and others, shifting from commissioned social documentation toward self-directed subjects and editing*1. He retained an intense concern with reality while questioning the reduction of postwar Japan to one representative image, turning instead toward material fragments and unstable compositions.

    From the Fieldwork of Iwanami Shashin Bunko to VIVO: Postwar Daily Life in Fragments

    Through Iwanami Shashin Bunko, Tomatsu photographed social and industrial sites and observed how editorial policy and captions converted photographs into public knowledge. In his later independent work, he approached signs around American bases, damaged objects, consumer goods, and fragments of bodies instead of typical characters or representative events. Postwar daily life contained both resistance to occupation and desire for American culture, a contradiction that could not be contained by a simple moral judgment or reconstruction narrative. VIVO provided a place to transfer that contradiction into sequences of fragments and photobook editing.

    The Bases Remain After the Occupation: Photographing the Postwar in the Present Tense

    Japan regained sovereignty in 1952, but American military bases and cultural influence remained visible across the country. In Yokosuka, Sasebo, Okinawa, and elsewhere, Tomatsu photographed soldiers, bars, signs, women, fences, chewing gum, and Coca-Cola, showing occupation persisting as an everyday visual field. The National Museum of Asian Art introduces Chewing Gum and Chocolate as a series tracing the American military presence and transformations in Japanese society*2.

    After Ken Domon and Ihei Kimura: Inheriting and Displacing Realism

    Tomatsu inherited from Ken Domon, Ihei Kimura, and postwar realism the ethical demand to go to actual places and photograph society. He also moved away from making a representative person or decisive event stand for the whole and approached signs around bases, a melted bottle, a stopped watch, and scarred skin—fragments whose meanings did not resolve into one statement. Participation in VIVO became one setting for reconsidering social contradiction through symbolic objects and bodies.

    A Critique of Realism: From the Representative Image to a Chain of Fragments

    Tomatsu retained realism’s ethical commitment to confronting actual conditions while keeping his distance from the assumption that a forceful photograph could transparently represent a social issue. Research at Osaka University argues that his images do not fix postwar Americanization as either celebration or rejection, but register attraction and humiliation coexisting in everyday life*11. Tilted frames, close-up, and symbolic objects prevented contradictory feelings from being resolved into one explanation. The form was built to preserve that contradiction.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    11:02 Nagasaki: Showing the Atomic Bomb Without a Photograph of the Explosion

    Tomatsu began photographing Nagasaki more than a decade after the bombing and could not directly record the explosion or the immediate ruins. He turned to museum relics, survivors’ bodies, the rebuilt city, and religious sites, photographing the time the atomic bomb had left in matter and daily life. 《11:02 Nagasaki》 takes the stopped time of a clock as its title, joining a historical instant to the present of the later photograph*3.

    Why Tomatsu Kept Returning to Nagasaki: From Past Catastrophe to Living Bodies

    Tomatsu arrived in Nagasaki more than ten years after the explosion. His subject was not the flash preserved in historical photographs, but survivors’ lives and transformations in matter. A melted bottle, a stopped watch, and scarred skin are symbols of the atomic bomb and concrete presences that continued into the time of exposure. Japan Society’s retrospective Skin of the Nation treated Nagasaki as a long-term subject rather than a single assignment; Tomatsu returned for more than half a century*5. Duration restored the city from a fixed stage of tragedy to a place shaped by urban life and generational change.

    The Melted Bottle: How Much History Can Matter Carry?

    In the close photograph of a glass bottle deformed by heat, the object remains an accessioned relic while appearing as a twisted shape suggestive of a body. The National Museum of Asian Art identifies the image as one of the emblematic works of the Nagasaki series*2. By pressing the deformation left by atomic heat toward the surface of the image, Tomatsu converted historical knowledge into a visual encounter with matter.

    The Skin of Survivors: The Ethics of Photographic Proximity

    The Nagasaki series includes portraits and bodily details of people injured by the atomic bomb. Close-up brings harm back from abstract numbers to individual lives, while also risking the circulation of wounds as forceful symbols. The Smithsonian’s Nagasaki exhibition places relics, the city, and survivors’ lives within the same history rather than reducing the project to one shocking picture*4. Tomatsu’s formal proximity cannot be separated from the duration lived by the people photographed.

    Signs and Bodies Around the Bases: Photographing Americanization in Fragments

    Around American bases, English signs, uniforms, cars, entertainment venues, and Japanese youth enter the same frames, presenting occupation culture as a mixture that cannot be simplified into imitation or resistance. Tomatsu located Americanization in the everyday details of products, language, clothing, and bodily distance. Japan Society’s Skin of the Nation brought Nagasaki, the bases, and Okinawa together as changes registered on the “skin” of the postwar nation*5.

    Okinawa and The Pencil of the Sun: Bases, Ritual, and Sea Within the Time of One Place

    From 1969 onward, Tomatsu repeatedly visited Okinawa and the Southwest Islands, photographing American bases, island rituals, the sea, vegetation, and people’s bodies. In The Pencil of the Sun, strong light, local color, and distinct ways of life establish the rhythm of the book alongside political questions viewed from mainland Japan*1. Okinawa confronted him with histories and cultures that did not share the mainland’s chronology and required a transformation of the method developed in Nagasaki.

    How Okinawa Changed Tomatsu’s Photography: From Evidence of Occupation to the Time of the Land

    In Okinawa, Tomatsu photographed island ritual, sea, plants, older people, youth, and intense sunlight as well as bases and American soldiers. The increased use of color in work previously associated with monochrome can be understood as a refusal to subordinate the materiality and light of the southern islands to a mainland image of the postwar. Okinawa appears as a place from which the contradiction of a sovereign mainland still dependent on military bases could be viewed in reverse. The change in form shows a photographer continually altering his language in response to place.

    The Occupation Series: The Exchange of Chewing Gum and Chocolate

    The title Chewing Gum and Chocolate recalls children receiving goods from American soldiers after defeat. The series includes huge military machines, leisure, commodities, bodies, and English entering Japanese everyday life. American culture does not appear solely as an external aggressor; it is also desired and imitated, so attraction and resentment coexist in the image. Occupation becomes a lived experience extending from political institutions to taste and gesture.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Symbolic Close-Up: Photographing Society Without Showing the Whole

    Tomatsu’s close-ups fill the frame with bottles, clocks, skin, artificial eyes, and signs, reducing surrounding information. The object remains identifiable while scratches, reflections, and distortions on its surface expand social associations. Tate’s holdings show material fragments functioning as a central vocabulary for his account of postwar Japan*6. Through sequences of fragments, history that no single picture could represent accumulates across the work.

    Photobook Sequence: How Are Nagasaki, the Bases, and Okinawa Connected?

    Tomatsu edited photographs from different years and places into books including 11:02 Nagasaki, Nippon, and The Pencil of the Sun, as well as publishing them in magazines. Repetition and contrast across pages connect the atomic bomb, occupation, consumption, and the southern islands as several temporalities of postwar Japan. SFMOMA’s artist materials and videos trace his continuing revision of subjects through series and photobooks*7.

    The Turn to Color: Releasing Okinawan Light from Monochrome

    Tomatsu is known for high-contrast black-and-white work, yet he used color extensively in Okinawa. Color does more than reinforce a tropical image; it gives clothing, plants, sea, and artificial objects a density different from monochrome. The difference between compressing Nagasaki relics into dark tonalities and opening Okinawan color shows an artist who changed his vocabulary according to place rather than remaining fixed within one style.

    Plastics: Photographing the Surface of High-Growth Japan

    Tomatsu also photographed plastic goods, packaging, and artificial color and light, registering new surfaces produced by postwar consumer society. A bottle deformed by atomic heat and mass-produced plastic carry different histories, but his interest in reading an era through material form connects them. Within the linear story from ruins to reconstruction, close views of commodities and waste show how prosperity altered bodily sensation and environment.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    Tomatsu and Kikuji Kawada: Encountering Traces on Site or Symbolizing Them in a Book

    Kawada’s The Map also addresses walls of the Atomic Bomb Dome, war relics, and signs of occupation, but organizes the entire photobook as a dark symbolic system. Tomatsu preserves encounters with particular people, objects, and places and develops postwar distortion from their specificity. Both emerged around VIVO and postwar memory, yet Tomatsu places greater weight on contact at the site, Kawada on collision across the page. Their difference shows that postwar Japanese photography cannot be reduced to a single “dark” expression.

    Updating the Label “Representative of Postwar Japan”

    International exhibitions have often introduced Tomatsu as a central representative of postwar Japanese photography. The label helped establish his reputation abroad, but it can merge the distinct histories of Nagasaki, military bases, and Okinawa into one national image. Tomatsu repeatedly moved toward places, things, and bodies that fell outside a unified account of Japan. As collections and retrospectives at MoMA, Tate, SFMOMA, and elsewhere have consolidated his status, the work is increasingly resituated within the specific times of survivors, Okinawa, and communities around bases*8.

    The Force and Limits of Symbolic Fragments: Returning to the Histories of Those Represented

    Tomatsu’s bottle and clock became international emblems of postwar Japan. Their strength lies in concentrating complicated history within a single object, but wide circulation can push the specific experiences of survivors and communities around military bases into the background. Recent scholarship and exhibitions have distinguished the political situations and dates of work in Nagasaki, Yokosuka, and Okinawa instead of treating them only under the summary phrase “Japan’s postwar”*12. Tomatsu’s criticism remains active when symbolic form is held together with the histories of the people it came to represent.

    Looking at Mainland Japan from Okinawa: Reversing Center and Periphery

    Tomatsu moved to Okinawa and photographed the islands over long periods, creating distance from a postwar history centered on Tokyo. After the 1972 reversion to Japan, he continued to follow the persistence of bases, Ryukyuan culture, ritual, nature, and tourism. Time spent living in place brought relations and changes of color unavailable to short assignments. Nagasaki and Okinawa have different histories and durations of exposure, and Tomatsu’s emblematic images remain grounded in the concrete time of people living there.

    Related photographers
    • Eikoh Hosoe — An ally who co-founded the photographers’ group VIVO and established ‘subjective documentary.’
    • Ken Domon — Set alongside as a photographer with whom he shared the atomic-bomb record (Hiroshima-Nagasaki Document).
    • Daido Moriyama — Cited as a younger photographer of the ‘Provoke’ generation influenced by Tomatsu.
    • Takuma Nakahira — Likewise a member of the ‘Provoke’ group placed under Tomatsu’s influence.
    Related movements
    • Documentary — ‘Subjective documentary,’ fusing pure record and expression, is given as Tomatsu’s method.
    • Social Documentary — Corresponds to the subject of recording from inside the realities of postwar Japan — Occupation, the atomic bomb, and high-speed growth.
    • Provoke — Positioned as the next-generation movement influenced by Tomatsu.
