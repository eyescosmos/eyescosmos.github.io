    Andreas Gursky

    アンドレアス・グルスキー 1955–
    CountryGermany Period1980–1990s ChannelSeeing side by side · TYPOLOGY
    Abstract

    Andreas Gursky moved from early photographs of people at ski resorts and riverbanks to ports, stock exchanges, discount stores, apartment blocks, and factories. From distant viewpoints and through digital editing, he assembled arrangements of people, commodities, architecture, and information that no single position could encompass, presenting them as prints several meters wide. Salerno, 99 Cent, and Rhein II show how the descriptive precision he learned from Bernd and Hilla Becher became a means of addressing globalized space, the relationship between photography and painting, the contemporary sublime, and the risk that capitalism itself may be transformed into spectacle.

    What this photographer changed

    Gursky made spaces that could not be surveyed from one position or exposure legible within a single photograph, using multiple exposures, stitching, deletion, and adjusted perspective. His prints of ports, exchanges, discount stores, and apartment blocks reveal, from a distance, the structures that move people and objects; at close range, they disclose differences among individual figures and commodities. He retained photography’s descriptive detail while making large quantities of information about contemporary society comparable within one image.

    § 01 / 04 Commercial Photography, Folkwang, and the Becher Class

    Gursky was born in Leipzig in 1955, moved to West Germany as a child, and grew up with a father and grandfather who worked as commercial photographers. He studied at the Folkwang University of the Arts in Essen before attending the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf, a chronology also documented by the National Art Center, Tokyo*1. Although he learned photographic technique at an early age, he later recalled that he first had to relinquish the aesthetics of advertising photography*2. Influenced by the West German student movement and reluctant to continue in advertising, he studied visual communication at Folkwang in search of a form of expression that engaged with society*3. During a stay in New York in 1978, he met Thomas Struth, whose recommendation led him to apply to the Kunstakademie Düsseldorf. Bernd and Hilla Becher attracted him through a mode of photography removed from advertising production and a life organized around the medium, from darkroom and studio to library*4. Their small class offered a concrete model of a photographer’s life built around sustained attention to a single subject*2.

    The Bechers photographed water towers and blast furnaces under consistent lighting, from frontal viewpoints and comparable distances, presenting groups of images as typologies. The “Düsseldorf School” was not a movement organized around a single manifesto; the term was applied retrospectively to artists who had studied in the Bechers’ class. Struth, Thomas Ruff, Candida Höfer, Axel Hütte, and Gursky pursued different subjects, but shared an education that emphasized distance from the motif and exact description*5. Using a handheld 6 × 7 camera, Gursky photographed people gathered at ski resorts, riverbanks, and tourist sites, observing how landscapes were used by the desires associated with leisure and tourism*6. While developing Klausenpass (1984), he noticed hikers scattered as tiny figures across the mountainside—details he had not registered while photographing—and has described this as a point when his attention turned toward people within socially organized environments*7. From afar, people appear as an arrangement; within the crowd, bodies press close together and room for movement contracts. His interest in both states continued in later photographs of stock exchanges and concert venues*8.

    The turning point came with Salerno (1990). Photographing the port of Salerno from an elevated position, Gursky was struck by the accumulation of cars, containers, cargo ships, and the old city within a single frame. He has said that the image changed both the subject and the composition of work that had previously centered on figures*9. The photograph records vehicles and freight packed into the harbor under evening light, combining a broad field of view with sharp detail*10. Tokyo Stock Exchange and his photographs of a Siemens factory, made in the same year, led to airports, hotels, factories, and discount stores*9. The early photographs had shown people arranged along landforms and leisure facilities. This concern now extended to spaces that support logistics, exchange, production, and consumption. Gursky chose such sites because the mechanisms that move large numbers of people and objects, organize labor, and direct purchasing take concrete form in architecture, corridors, shelves, and desks.

    Airports, hotels, and discount stores are designed to process movement, lodging, and purchasing on a mass scale. Later scholarship has discussed these spaces through the French anthropologist Marc Augé’s concept of the “non-place”: an environment where function takes precedence over local history and communal ties, and where users pass temporarily through systems of signs, rules, and commodities. Gursky did not select his subjects according to Augé’s theory. Kristen Sharp uses the concept to argue that his photographs reveal forms shared by airports and hotels around the world without rendering each location entirely interchangeable*11. The photographs hold standardized systems together with the architectural and human differences that remain specific to each site.

    § 02 / 04 Elevated Viewpoints and Monumental Prints—Whole and Detail

    Salerno and Tokyo Stock Exchange—How Space Organizes People and Objects

    The elevated viewpoints in Salerno and Tokyo Stock Exchange (1990) place harbor space, vehicles, desks, and people on a continuous plane. A closer view might reveal expressions and incidents, yet it would make it harder to see how containers, corridors, machinery, and the rules of exchange position and direct those individuals. Gursky has spoken of his interest in both the density felt from within a crowd and the arrangement visible from a distance*8. The elevated view allowed him to include the spaces that condition individual action.

    Monumental Prints and Painting—Whole and Detail at Different Viewing Distances

    In prints more than three meters wide, shelves, windows, and crowds form grids or bands when seen from a distance. At close range, product names, gestures, clothing, and differences among interiors emerge. Overall repetition and individual variation cannot be apprehended from a single viewing distance. Monumental scale preserves both.

    Gursky did not originate large-scale color photography on his own. From the 1980s onward, Jeff Wall, Ruff, Struth, and others also presented photographs on the scale and in the exhibition spaces traditionally associated with painting and sculpture. A conversation between Gursky and Wall shows that both incorporated post-shoot construction into their work and referred to the history of painting while retaining photographic detail*12. Gursky’s distinction lies in the quantity of information filling these several-meter-wide surfaces. History painting and abstraction have long organized figures, events, and fields of color across large canvases; Gursky arranged actual commodities, windows, people, and equipment so that each remained identifiable in detail. The Museo Reina Sofía likewise identifies his sustained dialogue with painting, alongside scale, compositional rigor, and color, as a defining feature*13. Comparing Gursky with Wall, Katia Mai examines how monumental printing and digital technology brought photography to the wall-scaled format of the tableau*14. Gursky presents complex spaces at the scale of painting while preserving the medium’s descriptive specificity. Large photographs and paintings could therefore be compared in terms of scale, density of information, and viewing distance, rather than through a simple model of imitation.

    The Contemporary Sublime—A Comparison with Barnett Newman

    Alix Ohlin has discussed Gursky’s monumental photographs in terms of the “contemporary sublime”*15. In eighteenth- and nineteenth-century accounts of the sublime, the uncontrollable scale of storms and mountains produced fear and pleasure at once. A K20 exhibition guide argues that Gursky transfers this role to finance, logistics, consumption, information, and crowds—systems made by people that exceed individual control*16. The people filling an exchange, the goods in a discount store, the windows of an apartment block, and arrays of solar panels form human-made orders whose quantities exceed a single person’s field of vision and comprehension.

    Barnett Newman’s Vir Heroicus Sublimis (1950–51) provides a useful point of comparison. Its red field, approximately 5.4 meters wide, fills the viewer’s vision and brings the body into direct relation with the narrow vertical lines Newman called “zips.” Gursky also works at a wall-filling scale, but his surfaces teem with windows, products, people, and equipment. Approaching Newman’s painting immerses the viewer in color; stepping back from Gursky reveals an order of repetition, while moving closer restores individual differences. Bence Nanay argues that high resolution, post-shoot editing, and monumental output make this movement between macro and micro possible*17. In Gursky, the sublime lies in the inability to grasp human-made institutions and material abundance all at once.

    A “World Without Hierarchy” and the Rhythm of Repetition

    Gursky adjusts perspective through multiple exposures and image processing, bringing the descriptive density of different areas of the picture closer together. Discussing Amazon (2016), he described making a “world without hierarchy” in which every element carries equal importance*18. Products in a discount store, inventory in a warehouse, and people and information in an exchange appear as equivalent visual units while retaining differences of use and value. The Art Gallery of New South Wales notes that in Centre Georges Pompidou, digital flattening and a grid direct the eye laterally across the image instead of drawing it into depth*19. Repetition produces an order close to abstraction, yet individual products, workers, and architectural features remain identifiable. The coexistence of abstraction and description is central to Gursky’s construction of the image.

    § 03 / 04 Digital Construction and Major Works

    Paris, Montparnasse—The Apartment Block as a Whole and the Differences Between Units

    Paris, Montparnasse (1993) was made by photographing a housing block designed by Jean Dubuisson from several positions and stitching the images into an elongated façade unavailable from any single viewpoint. The building’s edges and perspectival convergence are suppressed, so the windows appear to continue beyond the frame*20. Differences among apartments emerge within the repeated windows. Many lives occupy the standardized building, and variations in curtains, lighting, furniture, and inhabitants remain within the same frontal structure. Discussing the 2025 reworking Paris, Montparnasse II, Gursky said that its 1,122 windows reveal the residents’ habits, tastes, and ways of arranging their interiors*21. The stitching turns the architecture into an abstract grid while making differences among lives within standardized housing comparable in a single image.

    99 Cent and Chicago Board of Trade I—Images of Consumption and Exchange

    99 Cent (1999) presents a Los Angeles single-price store as a field of commodities in which shelves, columns, and ceiling reflections extend laterally*22. Goods with different uses, origins, and qualities are gathered beneath the same price signs, translating economic equivalence into repeated shelving and dense color*23. From a distance, the products become units of color; at close range, differences in branding and packaging return.

    In Chicago Board of Trade I (1997), figures on the exchange floor swirl like particles of color. The elevated view removes individual trades and voices, making exchange itself appear as an autonomously moving pattern. Christopher Williams-Wynn relates Gursky’s photography and digital processing to the logic of exchange value, which converts different commodities and actions into the common measure of price*23. Just as price arranges goods in 99 Cent, the exchange converts human actions into units of trading and price fluctuation. Color and repetition show the operation of the institution while making individual labor and judgment difficult to distinguish.

    Rhein II—Constructing Reality Through Deletion

    Rhein II (1999) organizes grass, a footpath, the river, the opposite bank, and the sky into horizontal bands*24. Industrial buildings, boats, and passersby present at the site were digitally removed, producing a river landscape that does not exist there in that form*4. Alexandra Danzig analyzes the accumulated layers of capture, deletion, color adjustment, and output as a photographic “palimpsest”*25.

    Rhein II is compared with Newman because the river, grass, and sky are reduced to horizontal bands of color, giving the work the appearance of a monumental abstract painting. The textures of water, grass, and path remain, as does the photograph’s connection to a specific river running through the industrial city of Düsseldorf. As the Parrish Art Museum’s placement of Gursky’s landscapes within a history extending back to the nineteenth century suggests, the work inherits a central problem of Western landscape painting: how nature is represented as an ordered image*26. It also discloses that this quiet nature was produced through selection and removal. The real site, an idealized landscape, and digital editing remain in tension within the same photograph.

    § 04 / 04 Reception, Criticism, and Place in Photographic History

    Monumental Photographs Linking Production, Distribution, Consumption, and Waste

    Gursky has called his long-term project an “encyclopedia of life.” It records leisure, labor, production, logistics, consumption, politics, entertainment, energy, and waste as situations in which space organizes people and objects*6. In 2001, Michael Rustin argued that these photographs show a world already fully modernized, not one still in the process of modernization*27. The Hayward Gallery likewise describes the work as a record of globalization’s effects on everyday life and the natural environment, seen through sites of commerce, industry, and tourism*28.

    This cycle continues beyond consumption. Untitled XIII (2002) shows a landfill outside Mexico City as waste extending to the horizon; closer inspection reveals logos on containers and people searching for reusable material*29. In the Bangkok series, Gursky has explained his intention to show beauty and river pollution together by placing reflections on an oily surface alongside packaging and other contaminants*7. By following commodities through production, transport, exchange, and disposal at different sites, he links ports, discount stores, factories, and landfills within a single economic cycle.

    Critique of Capitalism or Spectacle?

    Gursky has said that he tests whether a finished image carries social meaning or stops at formal effect, and discards works he believes do only the latter*2. Scale, repetition, and color follow from decisions about which areas of contemporary society to show and how extensively to show them. Yet images that order commodities, crowds, and architecture into vivid color also turn consumer society into an attractive spectacle while offering it for analysis. Jörg Colberg has criticized the possibility that this form grants visual authority to the values of free-market society. Discussing that argument in Aperture, Brian Sholis notes that it is a leap to treat Gursky’s work as direct propaganda for capitalism, and that compositing or editing does not automatically carry a fixed politics*30.

    Distance and elevation disclose the overall structures of ports and exchanges while making individual labor, transactions, and bodily strain harder to identify. Comparing Gursky with Allan Sekula, Zanny Begg distinguishes photographs that present globalization as a seamless whole from those grounded in the specific sites of labor and transport*31. A debate between Katy Siegel and Alex Alberro published in Artforum similarly considers opposing accounts of whether the work critiques global consumer society or reconstructs its spectacle in another form*32. The issue cannot be settled by choosing either critique or beautification. The same form that clarifies the whole reduces individual labor in scale and renders institutional order spectacular.

    The sublime sharpens this ambiguity. Finance, logistics, and consumption are human institutions that can be changed, but when their quantities and scale exceed the viewer’s grasp, they can appear as inevitable and overwhelming as natural phenomena. Ohlin discusses monumentality and repetition as an experience of the contemporary sublime*15. Gursky’s images reveal the scale of institutions while risking their acceptance as unchangeable landscapes.

    Review—Newman’s Sublime and Political Power

    Review (2015) is approximately 4.77 meters wide and seats Gerhard Schröder, Helmut Schmidt, Angela Merkel, and Helmut Kohl before Newman’s Vir Heroicus Sublimis. The vertical window frames repeat Newman’s “zips.” The Broad relates this arrangement to the Cold War history in which Abstract Expressionism was used politically as a symbol of American freedom and creativity in opposition to Socialist Realism*33.

    The work brings together the postwar American history that associated Newman’s Abstract Expressionism with individual freedom and cultural authority, and the authority of parliamentary democracy, state administration, and political leadership represented by the German chancellors. The Broad describes the photograph as prompting an open consideration of the values attached to Western art and political traditions*33. Gursky places the authority of modern art beside that of political leaders, asking how the two forms of authority sit together and stage one another’s appearance.

    The work is based on a newspaper photograph taken during 2009 discussions over a bailout for the automaker Opel. Gursky constructed a fictional scene from this source, combining a scale close to Newman’s painting, digital editing, and the politicians’ turned backs to bring the truth claims of press photography, corporate rescue, the state, and capitalism into one image*34. Before the vast red field, the politicians become small. State power appears to use the authority of modern art as a backdrop, while the same art diminishes the politicians themselves. Newman’s transcendent field of color is returned to the specific histories of postwar politics and economic policy.

    Gursky’s Place in Photographic History—Documenting Beyond a Single Viewpoint

    Composite photography, large-scale photography, and staged photography all predate Gursky, so digital editing and monumentality cannot be claimed as his inventions. His distinction lies in joining the Bechers’ methods of exact description and comparison to multiple exposures, the removal of elements, adjusted perspective, and color prints several meters wide. The Bechers placed photographs made under standardized conditions side by side, allowing viewers to compare similarities and differences among industrial structures. Gursky inherited their distance and precision but moved comparison inside a single image. Matthew Biro treats the Bechers’ analog photography and Gursky’s digital work as a continuity between precise description and conceptual construction, rather than as a simple break*35.

    Apartment blocks, ports, discount stores, and exchanges contain simultaneous movements of people, commodities, circulation routes, and information, making their overall operation difficult to capture in a single exposure from one position. Paris, Montparnasse places the entire building beside differences among its units; 99 Cent brings commodities into relation with the mechanism of price; Rhein II holds a real river and an idealized landscape within the same image. Gursky stitches multiple photographs, removes parts of a site, and adjusts perspective, yet retains details derived from the location—figures, products, windows, and ground surfaces. The final print does not preserve a single moment of vision intact, but neither is it a fictional image severed from reality.

    In Gursky’s work, the photograph is both a record bearing traces of the site and a constructed diagram for comparing the spatial arrangements that move people and objects. Seen from a distance, the monumental print reveals the order of social space; approached closely, it restores differences among figures, commodities, interiors, and ground. A limitation remains: the clearer the whole becomes, the smaller individual expressions and labor appear. Gursky made the scale of contemporary society available for examination within a single photograph, while exposing the risk that such totalization can lose sight of the individual. This double movement defines his place in photographic history.
