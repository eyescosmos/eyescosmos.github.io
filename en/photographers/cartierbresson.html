Essay

Henri Cartier-Bresson encountered Surrealism from 1926 onward and, through Rene Crevel, came into contact with Andre Breton. He brought Surrealism's attention to the dreamlike charge hidden within ordinary reality into photography, and from the moment he picked up a Leica in 1931 he rejected retouching and cropping, making the simultaneous grasp of form and event inside the viewfinder his central principle*1. His well-known formulation - the simultaneous recognition, in a fraction of a second, of the significance of an event and of the precise organization of forms that give it expression - remains the core of his photographic thought*2. Taken prisoner by the German army in 1940, he spent three years in a camp before escaping on his third attempt in 1943. Back in France, he documented the liberation of Paris and also worked with Jean Renoir. In 1947 he co-founded Magnum Photos with Robert Capa, David Seymour, George Rodger, and William Vandivert. Magnum was the first major photo agency built around photographers retaining copyright and editorial control while reporting the world's decisive events independently of any single magazine*3. His 1952 French book Images a la Sauvette appeared in English as The Decisive Moment, with a cover by Henri Matisse. As a Magnum photographer he covered Indian independence, Gandhi's assassination, and the Chinese Revolution, helping set the aesthetic and ethical terms of postwar photojournalism*4. In 1955 he became the first photographer to be granted an exhibition in the Louvre, a symbolic milestone in photography's acceptance as fine art. After 1975 he largely withdrew from photography to focus on drawing, and in 2003 he established a foundation in Paris to preserve his work*5. His commitment to the poetry of the instant over technical display, along with his refusal to use flash because he believed artificial light killed that poetry, made him a foundational figure for generations of street photographers around the world.
