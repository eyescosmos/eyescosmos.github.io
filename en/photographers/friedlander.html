    Lee Friedlander

    リー・フリードランダー 1934–
    CountryUnited States Period1950–1960s ChannelCity and moment · STREET
    Abstract

    Lee Friedlander layered windows, reflections, shadows, signs, television screens, cars, and monuments within single frames, photographing the “social landscape” of postwar America as a complex composition. In New Documents, Self Portrait, The American Monument, and America by Car, the photographer’s shadow and even the interior of the car enter the image, recording how society is perceived through advertising, architecture, and reproduced imagery.

    What this photographer changed

    Friedlander brought to street photography a composition that organizes visual disorder with great precision and a viewpoint in which the photographer appears as part of that disorder. By placing reflections, shadows, window frames, signs, and his own body in the image, he showed that seeing reality is already mediated by advertising, automobiles, architecture, and other pictures He removed the assumption of a transparent observer from documentary photography and made both the image environment and the photographer part of the social landscape.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From Jazz Record Photography to the Street: Beginning Within Reproduction Culture

    Friedlander moved to New York in the 1950s and photographed jazz musicians for record covers. The commercial experience of visualizing musical performers within the square printed format contributed to his later interest in photobooks and reproduced objects*1. On the street, he turned from landmarks to television sets, posters, signs, and shop windows—objects that had already converted American daily life into images—and sought places where physical reality and media overlapped.

    From Jazz Portraits to the Social Landscape: A City Surrounded by Reproduced Images

    In the 1950s Friedlander photographed jazz musicians and supplied images for record jackets. Seeing a musician’s identity converted into a commodity through photograph, lettering, and square design formed a background to the frequent presence of advertising, television, posters, and signs in his later street photographs. His “social landscape” includes the environment of reproduced images and words surrounding life, not only people and architecture. Fraenkel Gallery’s observation that he organizes a mass of visual information describes a method that grew from photographing streets already saturated with images*6.

    New Documents in 1967: Documentary Photography That Does Not Explain Society

    MoMA’s New Documents introduced Friedlander, Garry Winogrand, and Diane Arbus and marked a shift from documentary photography aimed at social reform toward the photographer’s individual vision and experience*2. Friedlander does not explain a social problem through a single subject. Through the arrangement of windows, signs, reflections, and people in the street, he records the visual environment in which society appears.

    Decades of Photographing America: A Repeated Method Across Changing Subjects

    Friedlander photographed roads, streets, factories, family members, self-portraits, and trees over several decades. Obstruction, reflection, and frames within frames recur across the series, but function differently in cities, deserts, offices, and automobiles. Rather than finishing a subject quickly, he accumulated the ways local conditions altered viewpoint and composition through long-term work.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    The Social Landscape: An American Environment Made by Signs and People

    Friedlander called his photographs a “social landscape,” treating advertising, television screens, words, cars, and fences as elements of landscape alongside roads, buildings, and people. MoMA’s artist materials identify the continuing intrusion of human-made signs into ordinary urban environments as a central concern*1. The social landscape is not condensed into an emblematic image of society. It repeatedly records the conditions under which people live surrounded by visual information.

    The Social Landscape: Society Appears in More Than Human Figures

    Friedlander’s social landscape includes signs, television, advertisements, and window reflections alongside people, buildings, and roads. Postwar society appears as an environment in which reproduced images and words surround daily life. MoMA identifies his ability to organize ordinary visual obstructions and overlaps into complex compositions as a defining feature*1. He records how advertising, windows, and lettering shape everyday sight without reducing society to one symbolic subject.

    Windows and Reflections: Putting Several Places into One Photograph

    In 《Glenwood Springs, Colorado》, the reflected street, the interior beyond the glass, lettering, and the photographer’s position overlap within one image. Reflection divides the space recorded by the photograph into several places*3. Foreground and background refuse a single depth, leaving the mediation of sight by glass, merchandise, and architecture visible.

    Reflection and Framing: Exposing How Reality Is Compressed into a Picture

    A shop window can show interior space, the street, the photographer, and buildings behind him at the same time, preventing photographic depth from settling into one order. Friedlander uses this overlap to expose the camera’s compression of a three-dimensional place onto a flat surface, not merely as an optical trick. Window frames, car windows, and mirrors become secondary frames that place different viewpoints and temporalities within one image. The core of the form lies in making the photograph’s organization of reality into a subject of the picture.

    Self Portrait: Can the Photographer’s Shadow Become a Self-Portrait?

    In the photobook Self Portrait, Friedlander’s shadow, mirror image, and parts of his body enter streets and interiors. In 《Self-Portrait》, the author’s face is not presented frontally but divided by windows and surrounding figures*4. The trace left by the camera-bearing body within light, architecture, and other people’s space becomes the content of the self-portrait.

    A Critique of the Transparent Observer: Shadow and Reflection as Intrusion

    The shadows, mirror images, and bodily fragments in Self Portrait differ from the traditional self-portrait as an expression of interior identity. In the work held by The Met, Friedlander’s shadow falls across a woman’s back, making his presence humorous and intrusive at once*4. His trace breaks the documentary promise that the photographer looks objectively from outside the image. Every position of seeing carries a body that acts upon other people and spaces.

    Frames Within Frames: Structuring the Act of Looking

    Window frames, doors, fences, automobile mirrors, and television screens create additional rectangles within the photographic frame. Friedlander uses them to divide figures, combine images at different depths, and produce compositions without a single center. The Fundación MAPFRE collection shows this layered framing recurring in New York street scenes*5. Chance provides the initial complexity, but the image is precisely controlled through its edges.

    Humor and Visual Error: How Photographs Are Made from Misrecognition

    In Friedlander’s street photographs, lettering on signs overlaps heads, statues and passersby repeat gestures, and the photographer’s shadow stretches bodies into awkward forms. Fraenkel Gallery characterizes his early street work—filled with people, buildings, advertising, and reflections—through the wit and complexity of the social landscape*6. Accidental correspondences are selected all the way to the frame’s edge and become visual jokes. Humor makes concrete the photograph’s ability to give unexpected relations to elements of reality.

    Television Screens: Photographs Already Present Inside Reality

    Television sets in shop windows and advertising photographs recur in Friedlander’s early street work. When a passerby and a televised face occupy the same frame, living and reproduced bodies share one surface. MoMA’s holdings include photographs in which television, advertising, and posters appear beside people in the street*1. Friedlander treats media as material already embedded in everyday vision, not as information arriving from outside society.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    The American Monument: Returning Monuments to the Background

    In The American Monument, public statues and memorials are blocked by trees, cars, signs, pedestrians, and buildings and returned to the ordinary city. Friedlander avoids arranging national heroes into frontal grandeur and records how monuments are overlooked and absorbed by contemporary street life*7. Roads, signs, parked cars, and moving people continually disturb the history monuments claim to tell. The monument becomes one element of the social landscape, its meaning changing through the surrounding visual environment.

    Factory Valleys: Photographing Labor and Industry Through Systems of Equipment

    For Factory Valleys, commissioned by the Akron Art Museum in 1979, Friedlander photographed industrial areas along the Ohio River, recording factory exteriors, machines, pipes, workers, housing, and valley terrain as one regional system. The museum identifies the series of approximately fifty vintage prints as a major commission*19. Repeated viewpoints show bodies at work incorporated into the dimensions of equipment and architecture, without organizing the factory as a heroic industrial landscape.

    America by Car: Turning the Automobile Interior into a Camera Obscura

    In America by Car, Friedlander keeps windshields, side mirrors, and dashboards in the frame, photographing the American landscape through the automobile’s structure. Multiple panes of glass and mirrors divide the road view from direct sight. By making the vehicle a visible condition of looking, the series extends his earlier method of windows and reflections into car-dependent society.

    Maria and Family Photography: Intimacy Within a Complicated Composition

    Photographs of his wife Maria made over more than sixty years combine domestic intimacy with Friedlander’s characteristic obstruction. The New Yorker presents the portraits, from youth to old age, as a sustained record of married life*18. Mirrors, furniture, windows, and the photographer’s shadow conceal parts of the body and preserve within the frame who is looking, and from where. His family photographs bring the relation between photographer and subject into the composition itself.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photo History

    Friedlander and Winogrand: A Body Receiving Chance or a Frame Producing Layers?

    Winogrand approached crowds with a wide-angle lens and left the speed and unpredictable gestures of public space in the frame. Friedlander also uses street chance, but more often layers windows, shadows, and words into an image with several designed paths of reading. Within New Documents, Winogrand responded bodily to an excess of events, while Friedlander structured how excess collided on one surface.

    After Walker Evans: Complicating Frontality

    Walker Evans photographed signs, architecture, and storefronts with lucid frontality, identifying types within American everyday life. In Friedlander’s pictures, similar artifacts are broken apart by reflection, his own shadow, and obstructions. The frontal description of the object becomes multiple under the advertising, television, and automobile windows that fill the postwar street. Comparison with Evans shows documentary clarity moving into another compositional form as the image environment changed.

    The Photographer’s Position: Documentary Self-Reference

    In self-portraits and street photographs, Friedlander’s shadow, arm, camera, and reflection enter the image, placing the photographer on the same plane as the recorded subject. In Self-Portrait, the author’s shadow overlaps another person’s body and the street, showing that the observer occupies no transparent position*4. This self-reference weakens the authority of documentary photography to explain society from outside and preserves who photographed from where as part of the image’s structure.

    The Critical Force and Limits of Ambiguity: Is Avoiding Explanation Neutral?

    Friedlander’s photographs rarely state a direct political conclusion, leaving several signs and people within the same image. This ambiguity relativizes the clear messages projected by advertisements and monuments, while also leaving labor, class, race, and other backgrounds unexplained. Its critical force does not reside in complex composition alone. Both the signs emphasized within the frame and the social circumstances left outside it give the documentary openness and its limits.

    Trees: Moving from the Artificial Social Landscape to Nature

    In later series of trees and Western landscapes, branches cover the frame, obstruct depth, and create complex lines where signs once appeared in street photographs. Their overlap blocks a direct path toward one visual center and restricts the photographer’s position. Friedlander moves away from the expansive vista associated with Ansel Adams and transfers the problem of framing developed in the social landscape to plant growth and interlacing form.
