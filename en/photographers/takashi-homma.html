    § 148 — Photographer Index — New Topographics

    Takashi Homma

    ホンマタカシ
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Takashi Homma first encountered the instability of photographic meaning while working at an advertising agency and at i-D in London, where publication format, text, and use could alter the role of a photograph. He carried this question into the post-bubble suburbs of Tokyo, fashion, family albums, and architecture. Tokyo Suburbia brought the color and human-altered landscapes associated with William Eggleston and Robert Adams to Tokyo from a measured distance distinct from the strongly embodied or autobiographical urban photography of Daido Moriyama and Nobuyoshi Araki. Tokyo and My Daughter and “New Documentary” examined how photographers, custodians, author credits, and museums determine whose memory or artwork a photograph becomes; the camera-obscura projects extended that inquiry to architecture and light.

    What this photographer changed

    Homma recorded Japan’s 1990s suburbs in restrained color while making the changing use and authorship of images—across magazines, photobooks, family albums, and museums—part of the work itself. Tokyo Suburbia placed built landscapes and consumer culture within the same frame; Tokyo and My Daughter separated photographer, custodian, and the author named on the cover; and “New Documentary” brought advertising photographs, snapshots, and artists’ works into a single exhibition. In contrast to a lineage of urban photography represented by Daido Moriyama and Nobuyoshi Araki, in which the photographer’s body or private life grounds the work, Homma made the media and institutions that classify a photograph as record, commodity, private memory, or artwork into a concrete subject of urban photography.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    Advertising and i-D—Learning How Media Changes a Photograph’s Meaning

    Takashi Homma studied photography at Nihon University College of Art and began working as a photographer for a Tokyo advertising agency in 1984*2. In 1991–92 he moved to London and worked for i-D, a magazine devoted to youth culture, music, and fashion. Tokyo Opera City Art Gallery describes this period as one in which he encountered photographers presenting work through a wide range of methods*24. In a magazine, clothing, headlines, typography, adjacent images, and readership all shape a photograph’s function and meaning. Homma’s commercial experience showed him that the same image could operate as an advertisement, a record of the city, or an artist’s work.

    Advertising often demands a recognizable authorial signature, yet Homma avoided inscribing the photographer’s emotion heavily into the frame. Using a 4×5 camera, he aligned horizontal and vertical lines, stepped back from his subjects, and treated people, roads, houses, and signs as equivalent elements in the picture. He has explained that this position allows viewers to imagine where they themselves might be standing*21. The controlled surface leaves room for viewers to assess the scene through their own experience.

    The same thinking extends to his commercial work. Homma places models, clothing, roads, signs, and architecture within a shared setting, and describes fashion photography as a document of the city and body of its time*12. The construction of a desirable commodity image and the recording of a city coexist within a single photograph.

    Tokyo Suburbia and the Suburb Boom—Selections and Contradictions Identified by 藤田紅於

    When Tokyo Suburbia appeared in 1998, Japanese debate focused intensely on new towns, roadside development, convenience stores, and standardized residential districts. After the 1997 Kobe child murders, newspapers, magazines, and sociological writing increasingly associated suburbia with a weakened sense of reality, fictionality, and the erosion of family and local community. 藤田紅於 compared contemporary reviews with suburban theory centered on Shinji Miyadai and traced how Tokyo Suburbia was received as visual evidence for this “suburb boom”*4.

    According to 藤田, the sixty-three photographs concentrate on newer suburbs developed after the 1980s and largely omit accumulated signs of daily life such as laundry, potted plants, supermarkets, and banks. Nostalgic images of childhood also recede. Instead, the book repeatedly shows game centers, family restaurants, convenience stores, children in American and European brands, and landscapes suggestive of American film. These choices visually reinforced contemporary accounts of diminished reality, dispersed family life, and Americanization*4.

    At the same time, the location list at the back returns apparently anonymous landscapes to places such as Tama, Makuhari, and Yokohama, while one photograph of adults with an infant unsettles the impression of a community composed only of children. 藤田 notes that contemporary newspaper and magazine reviews largely overlooked these contradictions and praised Tokyo Suburbia for visualizing a standard image of the suburban boom*4. The essay also warns against treating Homma’s suburban upbringing as sufficient proof of an insider’s viewpoint. The book carefully constructs the conventions of suburban discourse, then inserts place names and exceptions into them. Its critical force lies in this double movement.

    Eggleston, Robert Adams, and Nobuyoshi Araki—Three Predecessors Named by Homma

    Homma identifies William Eggleston, Robert Adams, and Nobuyoshi Araki as important predecessors*13. Eggleston brought color associated with advertising and family snapshots into art photography, treating ordinary objects, cars, interiors, and roadsides as events of color and composition*27. Within New Topographics, Robert Adams photographed the meeting of housing developments, roads, and plains in the American West from a quiet distance*26. From them, Homma learned that color and human-altered landscapes could be photographed as everyday environments in their own right.

    Nobuyoshi Araki transformed private life into photobooks while working at an advertising agency, closely linking the photographer’s biography, titles, and publication. Homma cites Araki as one reason he became a photographer, yet in Tokyo and My Daughter he used a first-person title to prompt an autobiographical reading while mixing photographs of a friend’s daughter with snapshots made by her parents and rephotographed images*13. Homma’s concern extended from private subject matter to the power of the photobook and author credit to produce an image of the artist.

    Daido Moriyama and Takuma Nakahira rendered Tokyo in the 1960s and 1970s as an urgent field of perception through the walking body, blur, grain, and print. Homma’s 4×5 photographs approach the later city from a fixed position several steps back, giving people, houses, roads, and signs comparable weight. The distinction lies in whether the photographer’s body anchors the urban image or attention shifts toward the arrangements and consumer signs that structure suburbia. These histories reached Homma by different routes. Eggleston, Robert Adams, and Araki are predecessors he named himself; Moriyama and Nakahira serve as points of comparison within Japanese urban photography.

    § 02 / 04 Core Approach

    The 4×5 Camera—Restraining the Photographer’s Gesture, Recording Suburban Arrangement

    Homma chose the 4×5 camera for more than its descriptive precision. The camera requires a tripod, careful alignment of horizontal and vertical lines, and the placement of people and architecture within the same frame*10. By stepping back, he removes facial expression from the center of psychological drama, allowing roads, houses, sky, and signs to become equal conditions of the image. He has said that this position helps viewers imagine where they themselves might stand*21.

    Through the 1980s, a major current in Japanese urban photography entered the street with a small camera and registered the photographer’s speed and reaction in the frame. The 1990s suburbs Homma photographed were planned spaces of recurring intersections, houses, convenience stores, and construction sites. Proximity and quick response could intensify an individual scene; a fixed distance made the repetition of planned space more legible. The 4×5 format showed where a person stood among roads, buildings, and commercial signs.

    What Homma calls an “open photograph” withholds the photographer’s emotion as a final conclusion and allows viewers to compare the arrangement in the frame with their own memories*13. His ordered 4×5 pictures still involve decisions about place, distance, weather, and people. Directing those decisions toward suburban arrangement while keeping expressive gesture out of the foreground defines his measured distance.

    Magazines, Photobooks, Museums—Changing the Use of the Same Image

    A defining feature of Homma’s publishing work is the repeated movement of the same image through different media, making changes in use and evaluation part of the work’s structure. A photograph used to present clothing in i-D becomes a record of the post-bubble city in Tokyo Suburbia, then material for examining the institutional division between commercial and artistic photography in “New Documentary”*6. Existing images reappear with traces of their earlier uses, weakening the expectation that each book must close around an entirely new body of work.

    Homma has produced more than sixty publications and has said that he prefers thin books made for particular exhibitions or moments*20. In Japanese photography, Daido Moriyama and Nobuyoshi Araki placed magazines and photobooks at the center of production and changed photographic meaning through print. Homma brought advertising, fashion magazines, family albums, and museums—each with different systems of ownership and evaluation—into that history.

    What distinguishes Homma’s work from the ordinary integration of shooting, selection, and book design is the recirculation of identical images. As a photograph moves from one medium to another, it may be treated as commodity, urban record, family memory, or artwork. Making that shift a sustained subject separates his books and exhibitions from a conventional editorial process.

    Tokyo and My Daughter—Separating Photographer, Custodian, and Author Credit

    In Tokyo and My Daughter, the author’s name on the cover and the first-person title suggest an autobiography following the photographer’s own child. The girl is in fact a friend’s daughter, and the book mixes photographs by Homma with snapshots by her parents and rephotographed images*6. Before establishing each photograph’s provenance, viewers use the title and author credit to construct a single family story.

    In a family album, the person pressing the shutter, the family represented, the household preserving the photographs, and the album’s owner often overlap. Tokyo and My Daughter separates these roles and gathers images by different photographers under Homma’s name. Photographer, custodian, selector, title-giver, and publisher each participate in the photograph’s meaning and sense of ownership.

    The work therefore shifts from a single photographer’s private confession to the mechanism by which family photographs are recognized as someone’s memories. Its subject is the division of labor among shooting, preservation, selection, naming, and publication. Different people perform these tasks, yet their combined work establishes whose story the book appears to tell.

    Camera Obscura—Examining Authorship Through an Optical Apparatus

    Since the 2010s, Homma has darkened hotel rooms and houses, admitted daylight through a small aperture in a window, and projected an inverted city onto walls or photosensitive material. Because the entire room functions as a camera, its architecture, the direction of the window, the weather, and long exposure times directly shape the image. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum presented the camera obscura as a central element of his recent inquiry into photographic apparatuses and photographic history in Revolution 9: Homma Takashi*23.

    In The Narcissistic City, rooms in Tokyo, New York, Milan, and other cities become camera obscuras, allowing the city to leave its own image on photosensitive material through architecture*8. Nonaka-Hill describes this as using the city as an apparatus to photograph itself, with inversion, chance, and repetition as constitutive elements*7. The early 4×5 photographs and the camera-obscura works differ greatly in technique and appearance. They share a distance from the idea that the author’s instantaneous perception alone grounds expression. In the 4×5 pictures, the photographer’s gesture recedes; in the camera obscura, architecture, aperture, daylight, and exposure time divide responsibility for the image.

    The available sources show that Homma developed the camera-obscura work as research into photographic history and architecture; it also casts his earlier understanding of authorship in a different light. At a time when smartphones and digital systems produce and circulate images instantly, sealing a room and waiting for light restores the duration by which light becomes an image. Homma refers to Nicéphore Niépce, Nobuo Yamanaka, and Jun’ichirō Tanizaki, using the dark chamber as an entry point into photographic history and the modern organization of vision*5.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Tokyo Suburbia, 1998—Thick Pages and an Externally Framed Suburbia

    Tokyo Suburbia comprises sixty-three color photographs of residential districts, roads, commercial facilities, children, and young people. Its board-book construction, made from laminated card, gives the volume considerable physical weight, while full-bleed printing brings the reader into direct contact with the page surface. The light palette of the city meets the book’s physical heaviness, introducing bodily resistance into the act of viewing suburbia as an abstract image*4.

    In 1999, the Kimura Ihei Award, followed by a 2000 exhibition at Fotomuseum Winterthur, brought Homma’s restrained color photographs wider international recognition*3. Current reassessments consider both the book’s reinforcement of 1990s suburban discourse and the ways its location list, infants, and the gazes of its figures fracture that typology*22. The coexistence of formal clarity and social selectivity remains a central issue in the book’s reception.

    Tokyo and My Daughter, 1999–2010—The Family Album and the Displaced Author Credit

    Tokyo and My Daughter follows a girl’s growth from 1999 alongside Tokyo streets, houses, airports, and family snapshots. She is the daughter of a friend, and the book includes images made by her parents as well as rephotographed existing photographs*13. Grain, color, and distance remain uneven within the volume, placing images preserved in the home on the same timeline as the artist’s urban photographs. The autobiographical expectation created by the words “Tokyo and My Daughter” conflicts with the actual provenance of the pictures, and that gap structures the book.

    The Narcissistic City and Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji

    In The Narcissistic City, rooms become large cameras that turn fragments of buildings, water towers, roads, and sky into inverted images*14. Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji refers to Katsushika Hokusai’s series and photographs a mountain endlessly repeated through tourism, World Heritage status, and daily life from camera obscuras in different locations*11. Both series record how an image changes as viewing position, architecture, and exposure time change, instead of presenting a single representative view of a famous site.

    From Le Corbusier’s Windows to Portraiture and Moving Images

    Looking Through: Le Corbusier Windows follows architecture across Europe and India, examining how windows determine light, view, and the position of the body*17. A joint project by the CCA and the Window Research Institute connects the shared structure of windows and camera obscuras to the histories of modern architecture and photography*19. According to the CCA, the research began with Homma’s 2002 photographs of Le Corbusier’s Petite Maison, continued in Chandigarh, and developed into an examination of the common structure of the window and the camera aperture*9. The subject expanded from recording a building’s exterior to studying how architecture itself organizes sight and view.

    Portrait of J gathers portraits made since 2002, presenting public figures, friends, and children without a hierarchy of status*15. In 2022, an i-D project photographed nine young queer people on Tokyo streets and placed their portraits alongside their own words*16. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Videodiary developed near-empty Tokyo, domestic interiors, and family voices across twenty-eight videos*18. Across photobooks, magazines, moving images, and architectural research, Homma chooses image duration, sequence, and circulation according to the subject.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    Praised Restraint and Critiques of “Ordinariness”

    Homma’s reception formed around the bright color of Tokyo Suburbia, the distance that treats people and architecture with comparable temperature, and his movement between magazines and photobooks. In its 2000 exhibition, MU praised his ability to work inside the media while directing a critical gaze toward them; Fotomuseum Winterthur identified socioeconomic fractures beneath the work’s apparent lightness*1.

    Aperture’s review of “New Documentary” noted that its discussion of deskilling—mixing amateur photographs with professional technique—sometimes repeated familiar art-historical arguments, and distinguished works in which the concept operated forcefully from those led by institutional explanation*6. The criticism clarifies that titles, editing, design, and display produce critical force only when they act concretely upon each series’ social setting. Homma’s restrained surfaces gain their strength when they serve a precise inquiry into society and media.

    The Pictures Generation and Tillmans—Separating Direct Influence from Contemporary Comparison

    In the late 1970s, the Pictures Generation appropriated images from magazines, film, and advertising to criticize an art system centered on original authors and originals*28. The available sources do not establish that Homma directly referred to the group. The useful comparison lies elsewhere: those artists moved mass-circulated images into art, while Homma began within commercial image production and moved his own advertising photographs, family snapshots, and urban pictures among different media.

    Working at roughly the same time for publications including i-D, Wolfgang Tillmans treated photographs, photocopies, and reproductions of magazine pages as equivalent in a 1993 installation, shifting the hierarchy between printed matter and original prints*25. The sources likewise do not confirm a direct line of influence between Tillmans and Homma. Tillmans rearranged hierarchies of size, support, and mounting within the gallery; Homma traced how suburbia, family albums, and Japanese magazine culture classify images as advertisement, record, private memory, or artwork.

    This distinction avoids presenting Homma as a synthesis of Euro-American currents. Eggleston, Robert Adams, and Araki are predecessors he named. The Pictures Generation and Tillmans offer comparisons for authorship and circulation developed in different regions and institutions. Homma’s specificity lies in placing advertising, magazines, suburbia, family albums, and museums within a single field of inquiry in 1990s Japan.

    Making the Boundary Between Magazine Photography and Museum Art a Subject in 1990s Japan

    Questions of photographic circulation and authorship predate Homma. Daido Moriyama made the printed surfaces of magazines and photobooks integral to the photograph; Nobuyoshi Araki linked private life and authorial identity across a vast publishing output; and art of the 1980s criticized authorship through the reuse of existing images. Homma’s work cannot be adequately described as a personal synthesis of these histories.

    Its specificity lies in the circulation systems of 1990s Japan. In Tokyo Suburbia, a color palette and distance developed through fashion magazines were received as visual evidence for suburban discourse. In Tokyo and My Daughter, photographs preserved within a household appeared under the name of a single photographer. In “New Documentary,” magazine covers, commercial photographs, family pictures, and video entered a museum, and the exhibition made changes in their use and evaluation visible*24.

    Homma’s place in photographic history depends on treating commercial assignments, private snapshots, artists’ works, and architectural research as connected stages in the changing meaning and evaluation of images. The Tokyo Photographic Art Museum’s 2023 exhibition reorganized his early suburbia photographs, seascapes, mushrooms, Le Corbusier studies, and camera-obscura works around the acts of seeing photographs and examining the camera apparatus*23. Movement between magazines and museums had clear precedents. Homma’s particular contribution was to circulate advertising, suburban photography, family albums, and architectural research within one body of work and thereby show, in post-1990 Japan, how photographic use and authorship are assigned.
