    Kishin Shinoyama

    篠山紀信 1960s
    CountryJapan Period1950–1960s ChannelIssues in photo history · Japanese photography
    Abstract

    Kishin Shinoyama worked through advertising, magazines, photobooks, and museum exhibitions, photographing celebrities, nudes, architecture, Tokyo, and traces left after disaster as public images of his time. Through Hareta Hi, Ie, TOKYO NUDE, ATOKATA, and Shinorama, he showed how photographing, editing, printing, circulation, and exhibition shape public images of people and the memory of places.

    What this photographer changed

    Shinoyama’s contribution to postwar Japanese photography was to place the production conditions of advertising and magazines at the center of photographic expression. Hareta Hi edited the people and events of 1974 into the duration of a book; Ie recorded traces of life in buildings across Japan; Shinorama and TOKYO NUDE used multiple cameras and large-scale staging to construct a changing Tokyo. His historical position is most clearly seen in the way he designed who would encounter a photograph, through which medium, and at what scale.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period

    Light Publicity and Photography Magazines: Making Images for a Mass Audience

    Kishin Shinoyama’s approach took shape in the 1960s, when advertising production, photography magazines, and museums were closely connected. While studying photography at Nihon University College of Art, he received the APA Award at the Japan Advertising Photographers’ Association exhibition in 1961 and gained experience at the advertising production company Light Publicity. His graduation work appeared in Camera Mainichi in 1963; in 1966 he was the youngest participant in Ten Photographers of Contemporary Photography at the National Museum of Modern Art, Tokyo; and in 1968 he began working independently*1. In advertising, commissions, planning, models, lighting, art direction, design, printing, and placement all contribute to the finished image. Shinoyama used this collaborative process as the means by which an image reached society. The pace of advertising and magazine serials taught him to photograph contemporary people, buildings, commodities, and events continuously, then deliver their reproduced images to a large readership. He saw photography as a way to take part in the transformation of present-day people and places into public images, preserving the surface of an era through mass-produced print.

    “Reproducing the Times” and Kettō Shashinron: Locating Photography in the Present

    Shinoyama stated that “a photographer can photograph only the era in which he lives,” and described his own work as “a reproduction of the times”*1. This idea included events in the world, the pose of a star, advertising light, a magazine spread, the order of a photobook, and the scale of an exhibition. Each revealed how an era shared images of people and places. In 1977 he published Kettō Shashinron with Takuma Nakahira, presenting their dialogue on photography and reality, the photographer’s position, and contemporary expression*12. In Shinoyama’s view, documentation and staging, artwork and commodity, individual expression and mass media belonged to the same production process. He entered the places where society manufactured images of stars and bodies, and made their desires, technologies, speed, and circulation both the subject and the conditions of his photographs. His critical concern lay in working from the center of the present, making images that would later become historical documents.

    § 02 / 04 Core Approach

    Hareta Hi: Editing 1974 at the Speed of a Magazine

    The photobook Hareta Hi grew from a six-month series in Asahi Graph that began in May 1974, supplemented with other photographs made that year. Art, entertainment, politics, professional and amateur sports, and everyday life appear in the same volume; figures such as Shigeo Nagashima, Kōichi Wajima, and Yoko Ono share the chronology of 1974 with public events*1. The book foregrounds the editorial fact that images of different subjects and scales existed at the same time, without imposing a single visual style. Shinoyama spoke of the mass character inherent in photography, and the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum has described the book as a critique of the Shōwa era*1. The reporting cycle of a magazine created speed and proximity to the present, while the page sequence of the photobook created time for looking back over a year. He approached people and events before their historical importance had been settled, then edited heterogeneous images into the memory of a single year. The method positioned the photographer as both a selector of events and an editor of public imagery.

    Ie and Kaikan no Yakata: Photographing Time in Architecture

    Ie was serialized in the monthly magazine Ushio from January 1972 through December 1975. Shinoyama photographed sites from Hokkaido to Okinawa, including the State Guest House, former Imperial General Headquarters ruins, rural homes, an abandoned coal-mining settlement, Tokyo apartments, and a Kyoto teahouse*1. In Ie, held by the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum, furniture, walls, light, wear, and empty rooms reveal the life that once occupied the building as clearly as its exterior form. The museum’s object page presents the image and identifies it as a 1975 silver dye-bleach print*3. As housing and communities changed rapidly during Japan’s high-growth period, Shinoyama photographed marks of use, the tastes of occupants, regional differences, and time held within a space. For the 2016 Hara Museum exhibition Kaikan no Yakata, he photographed thirty-three models inside the museum and installed seventy-seven works in spaces corresponding to where they had been made*6. Ie records time accumulated in a place; Kaikan no Yakata layers the photographed building with the building occupied by viewers, turning the exhibition into a new event. Architecture determines the positions of bodies, light, memory, and the spectator.

    Shinorama and TOKYO NUDE: Constructing Tokyo with Multiple Cameras

    Developed from 1983, Shinorama joined multiple cameras for simultaneous exposures and assembled adjacent frames into a wide image. In TOKYO NUDE, postmodern buildings, roads, artificial light, and bodies arranged as groups form a city image with visible seams. Materials from the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum document a large production process that included scouting locations, numerous models, makeup, and lighting*1. Small differences in viewpoint and time remain between adjoining frames, and Tokyo appears as a stage where several scenes unfold at once. Nude bodies are organized as urban elements alongside architecture, roads, and advertising light. Shinoyama applied the collaboration, lighting, and direction of advertising photography at the scale of the city, presenting a changing Tokyo across joined frames. Shinorama offered more than an expanded angle of view. It divided the unified viewpoint of a single photograph and transferred the city’s excess of information and artificiality into the structure of the image.

    § 03 / 04 Key Works, Methods, and Media

    From Myōjō to Monumental Exhibitions: Circulating Public Images of People

    Beginning in 1971, Shinoyama photographed covers for Myōjō and participated in the process through which singers and actors became the shared mass-cultural image of the “idol”*1. In Carmen Maki, in the Tokyo Photographic Art Museum collection, pose, background, and lighting shape an image that can be recognized immediately in print*4. Kobayashi Akira shows a similar clarity in the force with which the sitter occupies the frame*7. In celebrity portraits and nudes, clothing, body, gaze, lighting, and background determined how a person appeared as a social image. These portraits moved from magazine covers to photobooks, advertisements, and exhibitions, acquiring a different scale in each medium. The touring exhibition The People by KISHIN, launched in 2012, presented photographs of celebrities and public events as monumental prints and traveled to thirty-two venues across Japan through 2019. Gallery AaMo records works more than eight meters wide and a cumulative attendance of about 990,000*5. Shinoyama treated print run, image size, and viewing distance as parts of production, designing the conditions under which a public image of a person reached an audience.

    ATOKATA: Recording Objects and Places After the Disaster

    Made after the Great East Japan Earthquake, ATOKATA redirected Shinoyama’s long-standing concern with recording an era toward places marked by loss. Between May and autumn 2011, he made four visits to the disaster area, photographing trees felled by the tsunami, stranded boats, collapsed buildings, quiet landscapes, and traces of domestic life left in houses*1. TIME described the photobook, published that year, as a record of damaged wood, metal, and stone in the immediate aftermath of the disaster*18. Ie photographed time accumulated in dwellings; ATOKATA records, through objects and space, places where that accumulation was severed at once. The large-scale lighting and direction used in celebrity photography and Shinorama recede, leaving the position and surface of objects and their surrounding landscape to register directly. Shinoyama described photography as “a record made as things are dying,” connecting glamorous public images and post-disaster traces through a commitment to carry the present into the future*1. His form changes with the subjects and media demanded by each period, but the impulse to reproduce the present before it disappears remains.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    From Commercial Photography to a Record of an Era: What Shinoyama Changed

    Advertising, magazines, photobooks, the Venice Biennale, and museum exhibitions ran concurrently throughout Shinoyama’s career. Circulation, celebrity, production crews, printing technology, and exhibition scale all entered into the making of an image, joining commercial photography and museum work within a single history of production. In 1976 he presented work based on Ie in the Japan Pavilion at the Venice Biennale, moving a magazine series into an international exhibition space*6. His contemporary Daidō Moriyama used a small camera, chance encounters in the street, and coarse grain to record the unstable experience of the city. Shinoyama organized concepts, lighting, models, architecture, printing, and large-scale displays to produce the shared public images of people and cities. Both used magazines as a primary medium, yet they approached reproduction and urban life through different methods. Hareta Hi gathered the events of 1974 into a book; Ie recorded living environments through architecture; TOKYO NUDE constructed bubble-era Tokyo across multiple frames; and ATOKATA followed post-disaster loss through objects and places. Shinoyama incorporated the production conditions of advertising and magazines into photography, designing image-making, editing, printing, distribution, and display as a continuous expression. He changed form for stars, nudes, houses, cities, and disasters, recording how an era presented itself, what it lost, and what it remembered. His distinct place in postwar Japanese photography lies in treating photography’s mass circulation as a condition of historical critique.
