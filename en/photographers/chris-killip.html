    Chris Killip

    クリス・キリップ 1946-2020
    CountryUnited Kingdom Period1970–1980s ChannelDocumentary as reading · DOCUMENTARY
    Abstract

    Chris Killip used a large-format camera to record shipbuilding, coal mining, coastal labor, housing estates, young people, and families in Northeast England in black-and-white photographs. At a time when photojournalism compressed events and victims into brief explanations, he lived in the region and photographed the years in which industrial collapse permeated work, housing, leisure, and bodily habits, while retaining evidence of his own status as an outsider. In In Flagrante, Seacoal, and Skinningrove, exact description coexists with traces of the photographer and the sequencing of the book, raising the question of who represents these facts and through what order and language they become history.

    What this photographer changed

    Killip resisted social documentary’s pressure to reduce hardship to dramatic incidents or representative victims by recording history as it unfolded through ordinary time. In In Flagrante, shipyard walls, housing, coastlines, clubs, labor, and leisure form an account of lived time that policy histories often miss. The preface, the photographer’s shadow, and the tensions among images place documented facts and the photographer’s authority to select and publish them within the same book.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Era

    Cartier-Bresson and Evans: Choosing Photography as a Way to Think about Society

    Killip first recognized photography as an expressive form when he was seventeen. While studying hotel management, he saw Henri Cartier-Bresson’s Rue Mouffetard, Paris in Paris Match and later recalled being struck by an image that existed in its own right, apart from advertising or commemorative portraiture*22. After making souvenir photographs on the seafront, assisting an advertising photographer, and working commercially, he saw Walker Evans, August Sander, and Paul Strand at MoMA in 1969. He left behind images made to serve a client and returned to his native Isle of Man to use photography to think about the society and history in which he lived*23.

    Killip valued Evans as a photographer who separated visible facts from conjecture and used them to think about society*22. He criticized the paternalism of the “beauty of poverty” he found in Strand’s Mexican Portfolio, where a photographer could arrange another person’s hardship into an ennobling narrative*14. Accurate description and a refusal to make the photographer’s interpretation absolute became two conditions of his documentary work.

    When Killip began working in the 1970s, a photographic culture independent of the press was developing in Britain. Galleries, magazines, educational institutions, and local production centers opened new routes for presenting documentary photography. Tate describes this history as moving in the 1980s toward an intersection of social documentary, conceptual approaches, and color photography*27. Side Gallery was opened by the Amber Film & Photography Collective in 1977 and specialized in documentary photography, which was rarely exhibited at the time. Killip was a founding member, exhibition curator, and adviser and served as director from 1977 to 1979*1. He also said that John Berger’s Ways of Seeing taught him that acts of looking include the maker’s political position*14. His documentary work therefore addressed both the social circumstances pictured and the question of whose lives were being recorded and who would make those images public.

    Deindustrialization in Northeast England: Life beyond the Factory Gates

    In 1975 Killip moved to Newcastle on a Northern Arts fellowship and began photographing shipbuilding, coal mining, housing estates, and coastal labor. When factories and shipyards refused him access, he turned toward the people living around industrial sites*6. Closures and job losses became visible in fences, houses, vacant land, pubs, young people’s gestures, and family time. Against an official industrial history centered on companies, machinery, output, and policy, Killip photographed history as it was experienced within everyday life. Campany contrasts this with “written history,” describing it as history “lived from the inside”*23.

    Killip’s work in the Northeast extended from 1973 to 1985 and crossed several governments. In later years, In Flagrante was read as an indictment of the Thatcher era, although the destruction of industrial employment had begun earlier*15. Killip continued to observe local events, seasons, conflicts, and changes in individual lives, photographing the same people working, playing, becoming angry, and waiting. Josef Koudelka advised him that photographs change when a few weeks of reporting become several years of residence, and Killip recalled living in Newcastle for fifteen years*14. He began approaching the sea-coal workers in 1976, was accepted as a photographer in 1982, and moved into a caravan on the beach the following year*7. Extended residence allowed him to record different moments and gestures without fixing the same person in a single role such as worker, unemployed person, or victim.

    § 02 / 04 Core of the Work

    Large-Format Cameras and Working Distance: Keeping People and Environment in the Same Frame

    Killip used a 4×5-inch large-format camera to describe people along with hands, ground, houses, sea, and factories in fine detail. Campany argues that Killip and Graham Smith chose the slowness and difficulty of large-format photography as a discipline*24. Focusing and changing film produced a different time from rapid shooting and held facial expression and living environment within the same image. Any ethical significance of the equipment depended on the photographer’s continued presence and the relationships formed with the people he photographed.

    In Skinningrove, Killip used a large Linhof Technika. Getty explains that after he had become a familiar presence to residents, he could work unobtrusively with this conspicuous camera and photograph intimate moments*7. At Seacoal he spent years gaining acceptance and lived in a caravan on the beach. With the large-format camera, he made both frontal portraits in which subjects look directly at the lens and close views during conversation and work.

    Seacoal: Building a Life from Industrial Waste

    Seacoal follows people on the Lynemouth coast in Northumberland who recovered coal, dumped into the sea by mines, with horses, carts, and nets. Killip wrote that industrial waste and labor that appeared medieval existed in the same place*3. After trying to gain access from 1976, he was accepted in 1982 and lived intermittently for fourteen months in a caravan on the coast*7.

    The series records the process of recovering coal along with horses, wheels, waves, soot, children, families, and rest. The photographs show the community’s everyday economy of making a living from waste, documenting skill, pride, humor, and family relations alongside economic hardship. The Martin Parr Foundation describes the work as conveying both the severity of the labor and the closeness among the people*9.

    Skinningrove: Sea and Body in a Closed Fishing Village

    Skinningrove was photographed from 1982 to 1984 in a fishing village on the North Sea. Killip recalled a community that could be hostile to outsiders, especially those carrying cameras*4. People appear looking toward the sea, walking, embracing, working, and waiting, and the pictures retain a duration of life that cannot be contained by a single account of industrial decline.

    Reflected light from the sea carries details of faces, clothing, and cliffs through a continuous range of tones. Getty explains that Killip was drawn to this reflected light and worked at close range with a large-format camera*7. The environmental detail situates each person in relation to the sea, home, and companions.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Photobooks, and Media

    In Flagrante: A Photobook That Places Fact beside the Photographer’s Position

    Published in 1988, In Flagrante gathers fifty photographs made in northern England between 1973 and 1985. Getty places it among the most influential British photobooks of the postwar period and examines the editing process through maquettes and contact sheets*6. In the first edition’s preface, Killip called the book “a fiction about metaphor.” At its beginning and end he placed photographs of his own shadow stretching beside a large-format camera*26. The places and people are recorded with specificity, while the structure also acknowledges the photographer’s authority to select and order them as fragments of history.

    The book links shipbuilding, coal mining, fishing, unemployment, and leisure through recurring gazes, gestures, walls, smoke, sea, and vacant land. Precise details retain the facts of place, while sequence revises the meaning of each image through another photograph. Pride is followed by fatigue or play; communal solidarity sits beside isolation and violence. The people therefore do not settle into a single role as “victims of deindustrialization.” Campany argues that, as in Evans’s American Photographs and Brandt’s The English at Home, combinations of seemingly lucid photographs unsettle the viewer’s certainty*26. The editing preserves facts while preventing them from being absorbed into one explanation.

    In Flagrante Two: Re-editing an Interpretation Fixed on the Thatcher Years

    In In Flagrante Two (2015), Killip removed layouts that crossed the gutter, corrected blocked-up blacks in the printing, and added photographs that supplied further historical information. Killip cited the way the first edition had become too firmly fixed as a critique of the “Thatcher years” as one reason for re-editing it. He added a thumbnail index with place names and concise historical notes*18. Getty’s exhibition guide records that the new edition included three photographs absent from the first*7. The re-edit responded to the way printing, margins, sequence, and factual information shape the historical reading of a photobook.

    Archives and Retrospectives: Returning from a Regional Label to Individual Series

    Recent retrospectives have expanded reception beyond In Flagrante by presenting Isle of Man, The Station, Pirelli, Seacoal, and Skinningrove as records of distinct periods and relationships. Baltic’s retrospective included more than 150 works, documents, and previously unseen color photographs and presented Killip’s sustained engagement with northern industry and communities as a central thread across the entire body of work*17. The Martin Parr Foundation archive likewise organizes prints, contact sheets, and book materials by project*8.

    Showing the series by place, date, and project allows each photograph to be reread as a record of a particular coast, village, club, or factory. The archive also makes it possible to examine locations, relationships with subjects, and editorial decisions that were not explained when the photographs were first made.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    The Ethics of Documentary: Empathy, Outsider Status, and Misreading

    Killip’s dramatic light and precise detail create compelling individual images, while also carrying the risk that a person may be detached from the series and made to stand for an entire region. Campany argues that well-known photographs are easily simplified into icons of social conditions, whereas within the book their meanings are pulled back and revised by surrounding images*26. Emphasizing dignity alone can reduce the losses of deindustrialization to a story of individual endurance. Emphasizing political injury alone can render people as helpless victims. Killip’s wariness of Strand’s “beauty of poverty” arose because this problem could not be separated from photographic form.

    In the preface to In Flagrante, Killip wrote that he was superfluous to the people in his photographs and that his own life did not depend on their struggle*2. The photographer retained the freedom to leave and to carry the images into museums and publications. Killip stated this asymmetry in the preface and positioned himself outside the community. Decisions about whom to photograph, which images to retain, and in what order to publish them became part of the work’s structure.

    Exhibition titles and articles also alter how people are seen. MoMA’s 1991 exhibition presented the work under the title British Photography from the Thatcher Years, compressing a complicated period of production into the era of one prime minister. Some British newspapers used the phrase “Boozers and Losers,” turning the people pictured into a contemptuous type*25. This reception shows how exhibition titles, captions, and journalism remake the political meaning of documentary photographs. In Flagrante Two was also a re-edit intended to correct an interpretation fixed to the Thatcher era*18.

    A Shift in Postwar British Documentary: Showing the Record and the Photographer’s Position Together

    Killip inherited from Evans, Sander, and Brandt a method of describing people and places in detail. Around Side Gallery and Amber, he participated in locally rooted forms of production and exhibition that valued working-class life as culture. In In Flagrante, he coupled the claim that these lives were worth recording with a reservation about the photographer’s authority to speak for society. The preface, the photographer’s shadow, and sequences that bring conflicting emotions together show both recorded facts and the position from which they are selected and published*26.

    In discussing Evans’s photographs, Killip explained that he listed what was visible in the frame separately from conjecture, establishing a factual basis for writing*22. This approach retains details of people, homes, work, and leisure and limits description to what the photograph can support. In the book, different times and emotions sit beside one another, restraining the impression of a single image from expanding into an explanation of an entire region.

    In the 1980s, Martin Parr, Paul Graham, and others photographed British society through color, consumption, and the welfare state, extending documentary in several directions*27. Killip recorded regional history in concrete terms while treating the photographer’s position, photobook editing, exhibition titles, and journalistic typecasting as part of documentary’s own problem. By making explicit the asymmetry between the lives pictured and the photographer’s ability to carry those lives into publication and exhibition, his work remains connected to current questions of documentary ethics.
