Essay

W. Eugene Smith helped define the modern photo essay at LIFE by turning long periods of immersion with his subjects into narrative picture stories rather than isolated news images*1. For Smith, documentary work was not neutral description but a moral cry, which is why projects such as Country Doctor became landmarks in photojournalism*3. His constant battles with editors over control of his images eventually pushed him away from magazine journalism and into larger independent projects, including the vast Pittsburgh essay*2. In the 1970s his work in Minamata, Japan, transformed industrial pollution into a global visual indictment, and his photograph of Tomoko Uemura in her bath became one of the most powerful environmental images of the century*4. Smith remains central to the idea that documentary photography can be narrative, partisan, and ethically charged at the same time*5.
