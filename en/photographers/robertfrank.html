Essay

Robert Frank was born into a Swiss Jewish family in Zurich and moved to New York in 1947, where he initially found work in fashion photography for Harper's Bazaar. Yet the real subject of his photography was not fashion but the America he saw as an immigrant outsider, what lay beneath its public surface*1. In 1954, with a recommendation from Walker Evans describing him as the most talented of the younger photographers, Frank received a Guggenheim Fellowship and spent two years making more than 28,000 exposures across the United States. His experience of being stopped and detained in Arkansas because of his appearance and foreign accent sharpened the outsider perspective that runs through the work*2. From those thousands of images he selected eighty-three for The Americans, first published in France in 1958 and then in the United States the following year with an introduction by Jack Kerouac, who famously wrote that this shy, pleasant Swiss with a little camera had absorbed the sad poem of America into film*3. Contemporary magazines such as Popular Photography attacked the book's blur, grain, rough exposure, and tilted horizons as incompetence, but later critics would call it perhaps the most influential photobook of the twentieth century*4. What Frank refused was the bright, orderly, optimistic visual language of 1950s LIFE magazine, with its carefully composed images of prosperity and national cohesion. His photographs instead registered segregation, loneliness, and inequality with the peculiar closeness and distance of someone who did not entirely belong*2. Though poorly received at first, the book was radically revalued in the 1960s as civil rights struggle and antiwar protest made its bleak vision of America newly legible. ICP has called The Americans one of the most revolutionary books in photographic history, and it became a touchstone for later photographers such as Garry Winogrand, Lee Friedlander, Danny Lyon, and Joel Meyerowitz*2. After publishing the book, Frank moved increasingly into documentary filmmaking, including Pull My Daisy (1959), made with writers and poets of the Beat generation*5.
