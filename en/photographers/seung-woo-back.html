    Seung Woo Back

    ソン・ウー・ベク
    Country1990s Period1990–2000s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Seung Woo Back made Real World, which photographs a theme park of scaled-down global landmarks as if it were an actual city; Blow Up, which enlarges peripheral details from photographs approved during a trip to North Korea; and Utopia, which reconstructs architectural images made for propaganda. The photographs show reality already fashioned as tourism, censorship, and state imagery before the camera arrives.

    What this photographer changed

    Photography criticism in the 2000s often sought to verify facts by detecting digital manipulation and returning to an unaltered source image. In Back’s Real World, the source scene is a model; in Blow Up, the original photograph has already passed through censorship; in Utopia, the materials are architectural images produced for state propaganda. Through documentary precision, enlargement, and reediting, he traces how tourism, censorship, and propaganda turn reality into an image before the shutter is released. The truth of a photograph becomes a question of the entire process: who controls what can be seen and which images circulate as reality.

    § 01 / 04 Historical Background

    Post-division Korea and the Image Environment—The Impossibility of Seeing a Nation Directly

    Back studied photography in South Korea and moved to London in 2001, where he studied fine art and theory at Middlesex University.*18His work emerged from the division of the Korean Peninsula, restricted access to North Korea, rapid urbanization, tourist attractions, and the spread of digital image processing. MMCA selected him as a finalist for the 2016 Korea Artist Prize, describing an artist deeply versed in photographic language who did not remain within the established role of “photographer.”*8His DOOSAN biography records his participation in exhibitions on the democratization movement and mass culture, as well as projects reconsidering photography in Korean art after 1989.*17Back’s point of departure therefore lies in Korean visual culture, where state control of images in a divided nation coincided with the rapid circulation of photographs after democratization and internationalization. A general distrust of digital manipulation is only part of this background.

    “Picture-Grapher”—Defining the Artist as One Who Handles Images

    Back calls himself a “Picture-Grapher,” including already-made images, printing, enlargement, reediting, and models within his photographic work. Tokyo Art Beat introduced the term as his self-definition in relation to the contemporary image environment.*3He does not locate authorship solely in the moment of pressing the shutter. To follow who makes, selects, censors, prints, and exhibits an image, he treats photography as a broader set of operations performed on pictures.

    § 02 / 04 Core Approach

    Real World—Why Photograph a Model with Documentary Precision?

    Real World I photographs scaled models of famous architecture at Aiins World, a theme park near Seoul. The MFAH explains the perceptual delay in which the image first appears to show an actual city and only later reveals itself as a model through its details.*1A digitally composited fictional city would make its unreality legible as an intervention added by the artist. Back instead used deep depth of field and the precision of large-format color photography to show that the Statue of Liberty, Eiffel Tower, and World Trade Center had already lost their geography and history inside a tourist attraction, becoming signs that could be visited in one day. Because the pictures retain the appearance of conventional urban photography, viewers discover the contradictions of scale only afterward and recognize that reality itself was already producing fiction.

    Real World II—Toy Soldiers Bringing the Memory of War into Everyday Life

    Real World II is a series in which toy soldiers appear in nighttime residential areas, roads, lawns, and kitchen windows, introducing a miniature army into quiet daily life.An essay from the Seoul Museum of Art explains that the model soldiers evoke wartime memory and the persistence of a Western view of Korea shaped by the Korean War.*4In #8, held by the MFAH, soldiers gather around houses, sidewalks, and a telephone booth while the surrounding people remain unaware of the approaching threat.*13Real World I reduces global architecture to the scale of a tourist model; Real World II reduces national history to the scale of toys and examines how that memory persists as a frame through which Koreans are viewed abroad. The Eye of Photography likewise distinguishes the two Real World series through their different use of model cities and toy soldiers at night.*20

    Blow Up—Reading Another Pyongyang from the Edges of Censored Photographs

    During a trip to North Korea, Back was allowed to photograph only designated places and directions, and his film was inspected when he left. He had no opportunity to return and photograph an alternative Pyongyang directly; all that remained were images approved by the state. Blow Up enlarges figures, windows, rough ground, and architectural details that happened to appear at the edges of those photographs. The Art Gallery of New South Wales explains the title’s reference to Antonioni’s film Blow-Up and the method by which enlargement draws another story from apparently harmless photographs.*11Enlargement leaves grain, blur, and missing information intact. It also reveals the outsider’s desire to read the margins that escaped the state-approved center. The MFAH collection record makes available works photographed in 2001 and printed in 2007, showing the delay through which approved travel photographs became material for later rereading.*12

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Utopia—Making North Korea’s Ideal Architecture “More Ideal”

    In Utopia, Back cuts out architectural photographs from North Korean posters and postcards, distorts them, and places them against vivid monochrome grounds. Simply recirculating propaganda photographs would repeat the state’s selection of buildings, viewpoints, and skies. By exaggerating composition and color, Back makes visible the extent to which images with the appearance of factual records had already been designed to produce belief in an ideal society. ArtReview argues that the work intensifies the constructed nature of propaganda imagery and exposes the original picture as an already staged ideal.*10Digital manipulation functions here as a comparative tool for reading the prior selection, omission, and exaggeration performed by state imagery.

    Forty Enlargements, the Series, and the Exhibition Wall—A Structure That Suspends Judgment

    Blow Up arranges numerous small enlargements across a wall, producing an act of looking that continues to search among fragments rather than settle on the content of one picture. MISA SHIN Gallery points to the ambiguity produced by enlarging 35mm film and to a sense of reality distinct from the ideal-city imagery disseminated by North Korea.*9Viewers obtain no decisive evidence and become aware of their own desire to assign meaning to particular fragments.

    Archive Project, Memento, and Seven Days—Reordering Other People’s Photographs

    In works from the 2010s, Back reclassified collected personal photographs, images organized by days of the week, and existing archives, constructing narratives removed from the original photographer’s intent. A SeMA interview records his reexamination of others’ private photographs in Memento and his mixing of images from Monday through Sunday in Seven Days.*5Asia Art Archive’s Nobody Reads Pictures brings Real World, Blow Up, and Utopia together with the later archival projects in one volume.*15

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    Testing Photographic Objectivity Through Scale, Censorship, and Printing

    Art Sonje Center explains that Back questions photography’s objectivity, immediacy, and universality, working between the real and unreal and between stories already told and those that remain unseen.*7Vancouver Biennale similarly summarizes his work through the intervals between real and unreal, narrated and invisible stories.*16Back changes the terms in each series. Real World addresses scale and viewpoint; Blow Up, censorship and enlargement; Utopia, state selection and digital editing. This specificity moves beyond the general claim that “photographs lie” and traces how an image acquires the appearance of reality at each stage.

    The Politics of Imaging the Korean Peninsula—How an Unseen Country Is Imagined

    For outside viewers, North Korea is imagined through news reports, state propaganda, tourist photographs, and satellite imagery. Blow Up and Utopia make the procedure by which outsiders construct stories from limited images their subject. Yonhap introduced his image editing and the term “Picture-Grapher” in the context of the Korea Artist Prize.*19Blow Up directs attention to the margins of approved photographs; Utopia further alters images of ideal architecture, revealing a continuity between the state’s selection and the viewer’s imagination. The North-South division appears through flags and the military demarcation line, and through unequal access to images.

    Within Contemporary East Asian Photography—Documentary That Finds Staging in Reality

    SFMOMA’s Photography Now: China, Japan, Korea included Real World and reflected a broader reconsideration of photography’s documentary status amid rapid social change in East Asia.*14Walid Raad and Joachim Koester explored the fictionality of archives; Back directs his camera toward realities already staged as model cities, censored travel photographs, and state architectural imagery.His work makes clear that fiction in contemporary documentary can arise both from interventions added by the artist and from staging already embedded in reality before the photograph is taken.ICP catalog records and photobook holdings show that his work has circulated internationally through publications that allow series to be compared, as well as through large-scale prints.*6Because it addresses both the political specificity of Korea and the global decontextualization of images, the work has been read beyond regional surveys of East Asian photography.

    From Verifying Truth to “Deferred Judgment”

    The retrospective title Deferred Judgement succinctly names the method of his work.*7The viewer’s judgment remains suspended between model and city, propaganda and record, accident and intention.Collections at MFAH, SFMOMA, and AGNSW show that this uncertainty has been received as a means of thinking about photography in the digital era beyond the specific politics of Korea.*2Alongside contemporary post-documentary and archival work, Back made the procedures of judging truth into his subject through the specific image environments of the divided Korean Peninsula, tourist models, state propaganda, and censored film.

    The “Original Image” in the Digital Era—Tracing the Image Back to Reality Before the Photograph

    Debates about digital photography commonly compare a manipulated image with an unaltered original and treat the latter as the standard for verification. In Back’s work, modeling, censorship, and propaganda already shape that supposed origin. The source scene of Real World is a tourist model; the original photographs in Blow Up are film that passed through censorship; the materials in Utopia are architectural photographs selected for state propaganda. ArtReview discusses ubiquitous digital processing as a starting point for Back’s use of archives.*10Models and state propaganda produce a first layer of plausibility; Back’s photography and editing produce a second; the viewer’s misrecognition and revised judgment produce a third. An examination limited to traces of manipulation cannot identify the earlier selection, censorship, and modeling. Back presents the subject, permission to photograph, selection, printing, and exhibition as one continuous process, locating photographic truth in the institutions that determine what may be photographed, selected, printed, and shown.
