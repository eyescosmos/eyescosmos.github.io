    Wang Qingsong

    王慶松
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Wang Qingsong has examined marketization, educational competition, urban development, and consumer culture in China through staged photographs such as Follow Me, Night Revels of Lao Li, and The Bloodstained Shirt, filling the frame with performers, monumental sets, text, and commodities. He brings the visual languages of socialist realism, classical painting, advertising, and group portraiture into collision, presenting rapid social change as a single, fully constructed event. This page considers his move from painting to photography and why large-scale collective staging became necessary to his social critique.

    What this photographer changed

    From the late 1980s through the 1990s, Chinese photography developed along two parallel paths: a new documentary photography that followed changes in cities and everyday life, and experimental work that used the body and staged action to examine social institutions. Wang Qingsong created collective tableaux that restaged classical handscrolls, socialist propaganda paintings, English-language television lessons, and advertising with large casts and monumental sets, compressing post-reform society into mural-scale photographs. Their significance lies in more than size. The production itself—its financing and collective labor—became part of an image of how education, consumption, class, and work assign roles to people. By joining the narrative scale of history painting to photography’s traces of bodies and labor, Wang established a forceful form for treating urbanization and education as systems that generate individual events.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    Post-Reform China and the Shift from Painting to Photography

    Wang Qingsong studied oil painting at the Sichuan Fine Arts Institute and moved to Beijing in 1993*9. When he began working in the 1990s, China was undergoing market reform, urban redevelopment, an influx of foreign capital, and a rapid expansion of advertising and television. Socialist vocabulary and consumer desire began to occupy the same streets and homes. The International Center of Photography explains that Wang shifted from painting to photography in the late 1990s to convey a caustic view of Chinese society amid this economic expansion*1. The change moved production from the hand of a single painter to a site where performers, costumes, stage construction, lighting, lettering, and a camera crew worked together. The production process temporarily created its own social roles and hierarchy.

    Discussing the period in which he made Night Revels of Lao Li, Wang described both the idea of restaging a classical painting as the disappointment of contemporary intellectuals and the production process required by a vast studio set*2. In another interview, he explained that he was drawn to the movement of video but chose photography because of the production costs he faced at the time*3. A choice shaped by limited resources developed into a form that compressed figures, commodities, language, and historical references into a single frame. In the monumental prints, social contradictions appear at once, allowing the viewer to compare central and peripheral figures and the hierarchies between commodities and bodies in minute detail.

    Socialist Group Portraits and the Spectacle of Advertising

    The public imagery of Wang’s youth included socialist-realist arrangements of workers, peasants, soldiers, and exemplary students. After reform and opening, corporate logos, English words, consumer goods, and models of successful living began to occupy the same public space. The San Diego Museum of Art describes Wang’s works as monumental tableaux that cite Chinese and Western art history while satirizing global consumerism, the expanding middle class, and China’s growing connections abroad*4. Wang treats the old and new visual systems as forms that imitate one another within the same frame. Propaganda and advertising both arrange large groups, offer models of happiness and progress, and invite the viewer to participate. His staged photographs exaggerate this shared structure.

    The excess operates as a structure that lets a social problem permeate the entire picture. Writing for Huxley-Parlour, Martin Barnes argues that spectacle and contemplation coexist in Wang’s work: immense scale produces an immediate shock while also requiring time to inspect the details*5. The performers occupy positions within the collective forces of education, consumption, labor, and desire instead of acting out individualized psychologies. The advertising-like pull of the surface exposes the unease of finding people aligned with commodities, words, and architecture inside the image.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work

    Night Revels of Lao Li — Restaging a Classical Handscroll through the Contemporary Intellectual

    Night Revels of Lao Li (2000) transfers the elongated composition of The Night Revels of Han Xizai, attributed to the Five Dynasties court painter Gu Hongzhong, to a contemporary banquet and the art world. The original unfolds the private festivities of the official Han Xizai across several scenes. Wang casts the critic Li Xianting as “Old Li” and arranges food, music, alcohol, nudity, transactions, and exhaustion across a single extended photograph. He has described the work as a turning point concerned with the frustration and disillusionment of contemporary Chinese intellectuals*3. The original painting’s image of an intellectual under official surveillance overlaps with the contemporary artist surrounded by the market and fame, allowing political control and commercial temptation to appear as a continuum.

    The work in the collection of Art Tower Mito measures 120 centimeters high and 960 centimeters wide*21. Viewers cannot grasp the whole from one position. They move along the wall as they would read a handscroll, following shifts in posture, commodities, naked bodies, and gazes across the same banquet. This lateral duration places several scenes inside a photographic instant and creates a social model in which intellectual life, the art market, and consumption repeat through one another.

    According to official materials, the work was built in a vast studio according to the proportions of the original painting and financed through the sale of Wang’s oil paintings*2. The quoted classical image is only one component. The money and labor spent constructing the set, performing, and photographing also enter the work. Photography turns the historical quotation into a production site where contemporary social relations actually operate, retaining their bodily, financial, and material traces in a single image.

    Follow Me — English Lessons, Corporate Logos, and the Surface of Knowledge

    Follow Me (2003) takes its title from an English-language educational program broadcast in China during the 1980s and places Wang himself before a blackboard in the role of teacher. The Mori Art Museum notes that the program taught English while also offering a window onto Western society*6. English words, equations, product names, corporate logos, and fragments of politics and culture crowd the board until any hierarchy among subjects disappears. The teacher’s pointer seems to organize knowledge, yet the picture as a whole presents a competition to acquire as many signs as possible, regardless of what they mean.

    Here photography becomes a model of a situation in which knowledge is consumed as imagery. The closer a viewer moves in order to read the writing, the more individual meanings dissolve into the noise of advertising, examinations, news, and branding. From a distance, the teacher and blackboard form a clear composition; up close, the image becomes an overflow of information. Wang uses the viewing distance of a large print to hold together an affirmative image of education as an entry into modernization and a critique of knowledge accumulated at the level of surfaces. The ICP presents Follow Me and related works as productions in which Wang stages large casts on monumental sets, linking Chinese classical culture to newly acquired material wealth*1.

    The Crowd as an Instrument for Making Social Structure Visible

    In Wang’s tableaux, the crowd becomes the principal bearer of social structure. Uniformed students, construction workers, banquet guests, naked consumers, and local residents each perform positions assigned within an institution. In The Bloodstained Shirt (2018), Wang restaged Wang Shikuo’s 1959 drawing Bloodstained Shirt, an image of peasant struggle, with more than seventy participants in a disused factory near Detroit. The University of Michigan Museum of Art explains that the work joins histories of unequal property development in Beijing and Detroit within one frame, exposing the human costs of profit and the force of collective action*7.

    . A revolutionary image from the past changes as it moves into contemporary urban redevelopment. When local residents in an American factory after industrial decline reenact a heroic composition about Chinese land reform, unequal ownership and labor become visible across both societies. In Skyscraper, the Hammer Museum notes, thirty migrant workers erected a golden scaffold but disappeared from the finished image, drawing attention to the anonymous bodies behind rapid growth*8. In Wang’s photographs, the actual labor of constructing a set overlaps with the social roles enacted in the completed picture.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Organizing the Photo Shoot Like a Film Production

    The ICP compares Wang’s production process to that of a film director, arranging dozens of models across immense stages*1. The finished work emerges from a film-like production but stands as a single tableau. Costumes, postures, text, commodities, lighting, and backgrounds are planned before the exposure so that several social signs can be read at once. Materials from Blindspot Gallery trace his work across photography, video, and installation and his sustained attention to rapid economic growth and cultural change*9. A single photograph fixes the people and objects assembled at the production site, then enlarges them in the exhibition space to a scale that confronts the viewer physically.

    The detail of the large print prevents the image from being consumed at a glance. It has the clear center of a poster or propaganda painting, while its edges retain conflicting gestures, discarded goods, text, and exhausted bodies. A Collector Daily review of the ICP exhibition considers the theatricality and scale of the installation while reading each photograph’s social allegory through the sequence of the exhibition*10. Wang collectively manufactures moments that appear decisive and makes their constructed nature explicit. The method reveals how news images, advertising, and history painting circulate as though they were natural accounts of reality.

    Follow Him and Preschool — Education as a Stage for Competition

    In Follow Him (2010), a figure buried among books and examination papers embodies the acquisition of knowledge and the exhaustion of the body at the same time. Google Arts & Culture presents the work as part of Wang’s sustained series on education*11. In Preschool (2002), a large group of children is arranged inside a vast classroom, turning education into a tableau of population, discipline, and competition. The San Diego Museum of Art used the work as a key image for Social Mobility, making Wang’s attention to education, social mobility, and globalization central to the exhibition*4.

    Wang returns to education because the school carries both hope for the future and mechanisms of social selection. Blackboard, textbook, uniform, and examination make the abstraction of “modernization” concrete by distributing it across bodies. The many figures aligned in the image evoke China’s population while showing individual desire directed into standardized routes. An official critical text on Wang links Follow Me, Follow Him, Follow You, and Crazy Readers to an educational system that encourages the accumulation of information at the expense of sustained understanding*12.

    How Photobooks, Exhibitions, and Monumental Prints Set the Pace of Viewing

    On a reduced web image, Wang’s work can easily be consumed as an exotic spectacle of bright costumes and crowds. In the museum, the monumental print first overwhelms through its overall composition; the eye then moves to expressions, clothes, language, and props. An exhibition at MdbK Leipzig presented Wang’s works as large-scale photographs through which to consider China’s rapid transformation and global capitalism*13. White Rabbit Gallery traces Wang’s movement from oil painting and “Gaudy Art” toward large-scale staged social allegories around 2000*14.

    In a photobook, panoramic spreads and enlarged details create a different mode of reading. Because the whole cannot be taken in at once, the reader moves through the image over time, echoing the reference to classical handscrolls. Across monumental prints and books, Wang turns a photograph from instantaneous evidence into a prolonged encounter with several scenes and systems of signs. Galerie Loft’s artist materials trace the circulation of these series through international exhibitions since the 1990s*15.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    The Tension between Social Critique and Spectacle

    Wang critiques consumer society by using the monumentality, saturated color, provocative bodies, and advertising polish that consumer culture itself favors. Critics have therefore considered the risk that works satirizing their subject may also supply the international art market with the spectacular image of a “rapidly changing China” that it expects. An Artsy essay argues that photographs of the disorder and excess following economic growth must be read together with the social aftermath that lies behind them*16. This tension forms the center of the work: Wang remains inside the attraction of the surface to show how that attraction organizes people.

    A reading limited to political satire can reduce Wang’s figures to parts of an allegory. Yet the collaboration with local residents in The Bloodstained Shirt and the absent laborers in Skyscraper show how the production process changes the meaning of the finished image. The works restage how history painting and advertising have represented collectives and expose the mechanisms of that representation instead of offering a definitive account of society. An essay on the history of Chinese art photography in China Books Review places Wang within the post-reform expansion of photography from documentation toward constructed and critical images*17.

    Tableau Photography after Jeff Wall and China’s Distinct Image Histories

    Since the 1980s, artists including Jeff Wall and Cindy Sherman have staged photographs and connected the medium to the scale of film, painting, and advertising. Wang belongs to this international history of tableau photography. Defining him as a regional version of Western staged photography, however, obscures the image histories specific to China. The Night Revels of Han Xizai, socialist realism, English-language television lessons, and corporate advertising after reform and opening are concrete media that produced public models of the human figure at different historical moments in China. Materials published by Paris·B include Wang’s own discussion of the connections among Night Revels of Lao Li, Chinese intellectuals, advertising, and photographic production*18.

    Wang uses photography as both a trace of reality and a collective apparatus for producing reality. The performers existed, the sets were built, and the shoot required money and labor*2. These physical facts pull the picture back from digital allegory toward an actual event shaped by work and expense. QAGOMA places Night Revels of Lao Li within Wang’s examination of modernization and power in contemporary China through historical citation, noting a structure that renders the sequential time of the handscroll visible all at once*19.

    Place in Photographic History — Expanding Chinese Experimental Photography into Collective Social Allegory

    By the 1990s, Chinese experimental photographers were already using archival materials, the body, performance, and digital manipulation to move photography away from transparent social record toward constructed images. Wang’s distinction lies in expanding that turn into collective narratives comparable in scale to handscrolls and history paintings, linking education, consumption, urban development, and labor within a single picture. Alongside approaches that followed social change through events in the street, his work demonstrated how a social institution could be reorganized for the camera and read structurally across a wall.

    The ICP exhibition and its production documentary presented the completed monumental photographs alongside their sets, performers, and working process*1. The Mori Art Museum’s acquisition of Follow Me shows how this large-scale image of education and globalization entered a Japanese contemporary art collection as social allegory*20. Art Tower Mito’s collection of the 960-centimeter-wide Night Revels of Lao Li gives the photograph an institutional setting in which it can be read at the wall scale of a handscroll or history painting*21. Wang’s work made clear that photography could preserve the traces of actual bodies and labor while forming a collective tableau about the organization of society. It did not direct all of Chinese photography along a single course; it established a forceful lineage for structuring post-reform society at the scale of history painting.
