    Yang Fudong

    楊福東
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Yang Fudong has portrayed the hesitation of young people and intellectuals living through China’s rapid transformation in works including An Estranged Paradise, Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest, and The General’s Smile, using black-and-white 35mm film, staged photography, and multichannel video. He brings memories of classical literature, literati painting, 1930s Chinese cinema, and European film into contact with contemporary bodies. Sparse exposition gives way to fragments, pauses, repetition, and displaced gazes, through which historical transition is felt. His work treats still and moving images as parts of a continuous temporality, giving the photographic instant a sense of what came before and what may follow.

    What this photographer changed

    From the late 1990s through the 2000s, photography and moving image brought the lived time of urbanization into an international reception of Chinese contemporary art that had largely centered on painting. Yang Fudong used the large-format photographic triptych The First Intellectual, black-and-white 35mm film, and multiscreen video as a single visual language. Ancient literati, memories of Republican-era cinema, and post-reform youth overlap in scenes whose conclusions remain suspended. His subject is the silence, repetition, and waiting of people who defer judgment between inherited ideals and new forms of urban life. Yang’s distinction lies in fixing historical figures and contemporary youth in monumental still photographs as well as films, building a temporal structure in both media that prompts viewers to imagine events before and after the image and several historical periods at once.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From Painting at the China Academy of Art to Cinema as a Medium of Time

    Yang Fudong studied oil painting at the China Academy of Art in Hangzhou and moved to Shanghai in the mid-1990s. His training remains visible in the placement of figures, the handling of light and empty space, and the proportions between landscape and body. Yet he pursued the duration in which images appear and disappear, moving beyond the self-contained composition of a painting. Marian Goodman Gallery describes Yang as an artist with a background in painting who uses film, photography, and installation to address individual emotions and experiences in relation to history, society, and politics*1. He began shooting his first feature, An Estranged Paradise, in 1997. After interruptions caused by a lack of funding, he completed it in 2002. MoMA Post notes that Yang called it a “small intellectual film” about an educated social group and that it addressed loss and alienation among a younger generation*2.

    In late-1990s China, market reform and urban development accelerated the pace of daily life while contemporary art connected rapidly to international exhibitions. Yang minimizes direct explanations through construction sites or commodities. Old houses, lakes, mist, hotels, bamboo groves, fashionably dressed youth, uniforms, and banquets appear in black-and-white images where the boundaries between periods become unstable. This slowed duration gives form to subjects who cannot fully grasp the meaning of change and seek an image of themselves between inherited culture and new urban life.

    International Reception after Documenta 11 and His Distance from a Singular “Story of China”

    An Estranged Paradise was completed with support from Okwui Enwezor and screened at Documenta 11 in 2002. HIAP recounts Enwezor’s role in supporting the production and the work’s path to Documenta 11*3. This international appearance made Yang visible as an artist of a transforming China, while his works translate social circumstances into gestures, silence, and sustained views of landscape. Museo Esteban Vicente describes his cinematic world as poised between dream and reality, centered on the identity, desire, and solitude of intellectuals in contemporary China*4.

    In Yang’s treatment of modernity, old and new occupy the same present. Ancient legends, Republican-era cinema, socialist uniforms, and the fashions of the global city coexist on screen. Anachronism becomes a sign that contemporary subjects wear several pasts at once. In a recent M+ interview about Sparrow on the Sea, Yang explained that props from earlier works such as An Estranged Paradise and Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest returned to his mind, describing memory as something that repeats across projects*5.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work

    Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest — Translating Ancient Reclusion into the Gestures of Contemporary Youth

    Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest (2003–2007) reworks the legend of seven Wei-Jin literati said to have withdrawn from politics to exchange poetry, music, wine, and philosophy in a bamboo grove. Contemporary young men and women perform the figures across a five-part film. M+ identifies the nearly five-hour cycle as a major work and explains that it brings into the present an ancient ideal realm repeatedly pictured in poetry, music, and literati painting*6. In the first part, seven figures in urban clothing walk through the mountains. Later parts move through rural areas, the seashore, the city, labor, and pleasure. Seeking a community, they continue to move among mountain, countryside, coast, and city without finding a stable place of belonging.

    The classical past becomes a measure for the gestures of contemporary youth. The legend of literati who withdrew from politics overlaps with young people after reform and opening who pause among social participation, success, freedom, and solitude. Marian Goodman Gallery describes the five-part reenactment of ancient intellectuals by contemporary youth as a work about tensions between traditional values and contemporary society*7. Walking, smoking, silence, and glances that fail to meet stretch the time before decisive action. Repeated gestures take precedence over narrative causality, and historical transition appears as the bodily sensation of stopping before a path has been chosen.

    How Black-and-White 35mm Film Unsettles the Distance between Present and Memory

    Yang’s black-and-white imagery does more than style the present as nostalgia. Grain, strong backlighting, deep blacks, and slow camera movement bring contemporary scenes close to memories of old cinema while layering several cinematic periods in a single frame. Orientations notes that Yang chose 35mm film over digital formats to give his images a tactile quality*8. Film makes the present appear as though it has already entered memory, destabilizing its temporal location.

    In his photographs, too, figures are caught in mid-action or with their gazes misaligned. The still image may resemble a publicity still, yet it points toward an event continuing beyond the frame. Tate collects and presents Yang’s films, photographs, and installations as parts of the same body of work*9. His photographs function as independent works by showing only a portion of time and prompting the viewer to imagine what surrounds it, even when they share a world with his films.

    Staging and Improvisation — Finding the Image on Location Instead of Executing a Finished Script

    Yang’s films appear meticulously composed, but the production process places considerable weight on discovering a scene on location instead of reproducing a fixed script. He has said that he begins with an indistinct idea and finds the scene through the relation among place, actors, and camera*10. A conversation published by ShanghART similarly discusses the figures, locations, and contemporaneity of Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest as a process that opens the legend to present experience instead of explaining it*11.

    Improvisation allows chance to enter a prepared scene. Costume, location, light, and camera are selected in advance, while pauses among actors, glances, wind, weather, and deviations in movement remain. As staging and actuality move against each other, gestures escape the roles assigned to the performers. Yang photographs the distance between contemporary actors and the historical figures they enact. That distance gives form to intellectuals who know inherited ideals but are still seeking their place in present life.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    An Estranged Paradise — Hangzhou’s Landscape and the “Small Intellectual”

    An Estranged Paradise (1997–2002) is a feature film set in Hangzhou, following a young intellectual, Zhuzi, as he drifts among physical illness, love, urban life, and landscape. MoMA Post places the work within the loss and alienation experienced by China’s young educated classes in the 1990s and relates it to independent Chinese cinema of the period*2. West Lake is a canonical ideal landscape in Chinese painting and literature. In Yang’s film, mist, rain, interiors, and the surface of the lake correspond to the protagonist’s malaise and sustain a world whose outlines have yet to settle.

    The title’s “estranged paradise” describes the distance that opens between the protagonist and a landscape that remains before him, saturated with cultural memory. The five-year production history also joins the China of the late 1990s, when filming began, to the early 2000s, when the work appeared. Its screening at Documenta 11 connected this personal, slowly paced film to an international exhibition concerned with multiple modernities in a postcolonial world. EMMA later exhibited Part I of Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest, presenting it as a central work that transfers the classical image of the literatus to contemporary youth*12.

    The General’s Smile — Banquet, Authority, and the Theater of Life

    The General’s Smile (2009) unfolds a banquet surrounding an elderly general across multiple screens, bringing authority, memory, aging, and celebration into one setting. In Yang’s first solo exhibition in Japan, the Hara Museum presented it as a major moving-image installation that approached the universality of life through a banquet honoring a senior military officer*13. Toasts, music, dancing, silence, and the old man’s expressions repeat in place of a clear plot. The multiple screens show the same event simultaneously from different angles, dispersing the central figure’s authority into the surrounding durations.

    Each time viewers move among the screens, their point of view and the order of events change. Unlike the fixed seat of a cinema, the installation gives viewers control over sequence and length of attention. Yang’s multichannel works turn film from a container for narrative into an environment in the gallery, asking viewers to compare moments as they would images in a photographic series. ACMI’s Yang Fudong: Filmscapes connected his work to Chinese painting and film noir, presenting a moving-image practice that constructs a single scene through multiple viewpoints and fragments*14.

    The First Intellectual — Producing Before and After within the Still Image

    The photographic work The First Intellectual (2000) is a triptych showing a wounded, bleeding young man in a suit holding a brick on a deserted urban street. ShanghART records each panel as a color chromogenic print measuring 193 by 127 centimeters*19. The attacker and the brick’s intended target remain outside the frame, and the causal sequence stays incomplete even across all three panels. Anger and helplessness repeat through different postures.

    The work clarifies the difference between a photograph that looks like a cinematic scene and a still extracted from a film. Three stationary images generate an imagined sequence before and after them, requiring the viewer to construct an event beyond the frame. The monumental prints make the young man larger than life. The type of the intellectual who has lost his place during social transition appears as a specific wounded body marked by hesitation. A ShanghART critical text reads him as a contemporary intellectual uncertain whether to locate the source of the problem in himself or in society*17.

    Ms. Huang at M. Last Night (2006), an independent digital print produced for Parkett, is held by MoMA as a work in the Department of Drawings and Prints*20. In Yang’s work, photographs may emerge as by-products of a film or develop as independent images from related stages and figures. This movement between media changes the still image from evidence that completes an event into a scene that suspends the historical and narrative belonging of its figures.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    Does Visual Beauty Weaken Social Critique?

    Yang’s refined cinematography, costumes, actors, and landscapes often lead museums and film festivals to describe his work as “poetic” or “dreamlike.” Its beauty can be criticized for obscuring China’s political and economic transformations by absorbing them into universal melancholy. Yet Yang marks an era through subjects who waver without choosing a clear position. An ART iT interview traces his movement between reality and dream, individual and society, and tradition and the contemporary through the artist’s own words*15.

    The beauty of Yang’s images is both a polished surface and one of the means through which classical culture, film, advertising, and fashion teach subjects to imagine themselves. Figures remain physically apart within carefully composed scenes; their gazes drift and actions stop midway. The attraction of the image and the interruption of narrative express the distance between an ideal life and lived reality. An exhibition text from Kunsthall 3,14 describes Yang’s work as a cinematic space where historical memory intersects with contemporary psychological states*16.

    The Cinematic Turn in Chinese Contemporary Art

    From the 1990s through the 2000s, video, photography, and performance became important media for describing new experience in Chinese contemporary art, challenging an international reception centered on painting. Yang used the vocabulary of film history to represent modernization through youthful expressions, silence, landscape, and waiting. A ShanghART critical text discusses An Estranged Paradise and Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest through the figures of the “small intellectual” and the contemporary Chinese intellectual*17.

    Wang Qingsong compresses social institutions into monumental single-frame allegories, while Yang extends the time of figures who pause before choosing their roles. Both use staging. Wang’s photographs render social structure visible; Yang examines the process by which that structure becomes delay and hesitation within a subject. This difference broadened staged photography and moving image in China from political allegory toward forms of time, memory, and feeling.

    Place in Photographic History — Building the Same Unfinished Time into Still and Moving Images

    Cinematic stills and staged photographs that suggest stories existed before Yang. His distinct place in photographic history lies in bringing classical Chinese literati, the visual language of Republican-era film, and post-reform youth into the same unresolved duration. In The First Intellectual, an unsettled event repeats across three images; in Seven Intellectuals in a Bamboo Forest, the ideal of community remains suspended across a five-hour film. Whether the image is still or sustained, its figures occupy a historical interval.

    By working across Documenta 11, the Venice Biennale, museum multiscreen installations, and editioned photographs, Yang established a body of work in Chinese contemporary art that treats photography and film as equal media, extending the same figures and sense of time at different lengths. MoMA’s collection of his films and independent prints within the same body of holdings reflects this reception*18. His work does not stand for Chinese moving-image art as a whole. It added the duration of people living with deferred judgment as a shared subject of photography and film, alongside methods that presented social change through emblematic events or urban landscapes.
