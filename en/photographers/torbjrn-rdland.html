    Torbjørn Rødland

    トルビョルン・ロドランド
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Torbjørn Rødland photographs bodies, lotion, fruit, hair, shoes, sculptures, and landscapes with the polished light of advertising and fashion photography, tactile surfaces, and gestures that resist explanation. Although carefully staged, the works preserve physical contingency from analog photography and resistance from their subjects, allowing beauty and discomfort, truth and fiction, devotion and vulgarity to operate at once. Through Fifth Honeymoon and The Touch That Made You, he turns photography from a sign that can be decoded quickly into an image whose meanings become less stable the longer it is viewed.

    What this photographer changed

    After Cindy Sherman and Jeff Wall, staged photography became an established critical form for showing how photographs are constructed through cultural codes and production. Rødland questioned a situation in which viewing could end once that construction had been recognized, leaving the viewer safely distanced from desire or disgust. He brought the lighting of advertising and fashion photography, gestures from religious painting, Romantic landscape, and kitsch symbolism into contact with actual skin, liquids, fruit, aging, and the responses of models, producing interpretation and bodily reaction at the same time. Instead of weakening commercial seduction by presenting it as a quotation, he made the viewer’s attraction part of the critique. He reintroduced weight, sensation, and a near-devotional allure into late-twentieth-century staged photography and defined a line of work in which critique and seduction remain active together.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Historical Setting

    From Postmodern Quotation to “Re-Enchantment”

    Torbjørn Rødland was born in Stavanger in southwestern Norway and began working in the early 1990s. At the time, postmodern staged photography exerted a major influence on art photography by quoting existing images from advertising, cinema, magazines, and pornography to expose their artificiality and power. Rødland focused on what remained after a viewer had identified the source or construction. Even after a landscape is recognized as Romantic or a photograph as advertising, the sheen of skin, sublimity of landscape, and gaze of a figure can still prompt bodily response. To make that response central, he maintained the technical polish of commercial imagery and introduced unease from within its seduction. Serpentine Galleries describes his work as a practice that introduces unpredictable outcomes into carefully staged analog photographs and invites sustained viewing*1.

    In the early series In a Norwegian Landscape, Rødland photographed Nordic mountains, forests, and solitary figures that recall Caspar David Friedrich. Working after postmodern critique, he examined moments when a kitsch version of natural sublimity still retains emotional or near-religious force. The quotations from Romantic landscape contain both a critical dismantling of the style and a state in which viewers remain attracted to the image while understanding its conventions. A retrospective essay in The New Yorker connects this early work to a post-postmodern desire for “re-enchantment”*2. Astrup Fearnley Museum identifies the work as an early series begun in 1993 and places it as a search for photographs that would not be exhausted by irony, despite his awareness of the Pictures Generation*13.

    From Norway to Los Angeles — Working inside Commercial Image Culture

    Rødland later made Los Angeles one of his bases, working in an environment close to the visual languages of fashion, advertising, celebrity, and cinema. David Kordansky Gallery presents his work across photography, video, and publishing as a sustained pressure placed on ordinary objects and bodies through symbolic and psychological associations*3. The light of Los Angeles and the techniques of commercial photography form a production environment that actively shapes the desires of both subject and viewer.

    His photographs make deliberate use of advertising’s smooth surfaces. Skin, fruit, plastic, liquid, and fabric appear with seductive detail. Strange contacts, excessive viscosity, awkward expressions, and combinations without a clear purpose interrupt the usual movement toward communicating a product’s use or a model’s appeal. Fondation Louis Vuitton holds Rødland’s work as photography in which everyday motifs shift into unsettling psychological and symbolic relations*4. Commercial seduction draws the viewer close before an uncanny contact suspends the expected movement toward purchase or identification.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work

    Holding Staged Photography and Photographic Truth Together

    Rødland treats staging as the condition under which contact with reality begins. Staged photography in the 1980s foregrounded sets, costume, and performance in order to challenge the assumption that a photograph offers a natural window onto reality. Rødland inherits this achievement while returning attention to the fact that light touches skin and objects, models react, and liquids and materials change during the shoot. His statement at Bonniers Konsthall—“Even if photography often begins as observation, my dream is a more deepened engagement”—signals an attempt to add physical contact among photographer, subject, and image to the analysis of signs*5.

    He chose this method to retain the weight, moisture, age, and resistance of bodies and objects beyond cultural decoding. Costumes, lighting, and composition may be prepared, but actual bodies, liquids, and film change unpredictably. Instead of fully controlling the scene, Rødland brings a concept into contact with physical material and photographs the resulting friction. An interview in Even Magazine presents his position as one that holds constructed scenes together with photography’s indexical relation to the world and gives priority to physical proximity before it is verbalized*6.

    The “Wet Process” of Analog Photography and the Tactility of the Surface

    Rødland has continued to work primarily with color negative film and darkroom or lab processes after digital imagery became commonplace. In an Artnet interview, he speaks of his attraction to the “wetness of the process” in analog photography and emphasizes the chemical and bodily nature of production*7. Analog photography here is a physical production through film, chemistry, and print. Film color, grain, exposure, and the surface of the print return the photograph from transparent information to a material event.

    Scenes in which lotion covers skin, hair touches food, fruit juice adheres to fingers, or ceramic approaches flesh turn looking into an imagined sense of touch. Their discomfort comes from both an uncanny scenario and the sense that the surfaces are too close. Moderna Museet connects Rødland’s motifs to Romantic painting as well as kitsch and popular photography*8. Technical finish brings matter excessively near and intensifies the viewer’s bodily response.

    Bringing Desire, Disgust, and Devotion into the Same Contact

    Rødland’s photographs include young models, naked bodies, food, devotional gestures, older people, children, animals, and ornaments.His focus falls less on unusual combinations of objects than on the moment when a familiar gesture is tipped toward another meaning by a single thing.In Folded Hands (2012), a muscular man bows his head in a posture associated with prayer, while underwear is wrapped around his wrists. Devotion, restraint, and a sexualized gaze overlap in the position of the hands, allowing several allegories to compete*9.

    In The Measure (2010–13), a blond boy sits quietly inside a dog cage. His compliant posture, the cage’s purpose, and lighting polished like an advertisement invite protection and confinement, innocence and a fetishizing gaze at once*9. In another photograph, a child brings a mouth toward an apple embedded with coins and dripping with honey. Biblical temptation, commodity decoration, sweetness, and wounded flesh collect at a single point of contact*2. Rødland retains advertising polish so that the viewer genuinely feels attraction and then recognizes that attraction and anxiety arise from the same act of looking.

    His “deepened engagement” concerns the persistence of the viewer’s response more than an approach toward the artist’s intended answer. After the construction of the photograph has been understood, the sheen of skin, wetness of food, age of a figure, and shape of a hand remain, and the balance among desire, disgust, tenderness, and control changes with the viewer’s experience. Staging is selected as a condition for bringing real bodies and objects into contact with several conflicting cultural memories, not for fixing a single meaning.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    Fifth Honeymoon — Colliding Meanings across a Series

    Fifth Honeymoon is a major project that combines portraits, body fragments, fruit, landscapes, still lifes, and uncanny contacts across a photobook and exhibitions. Images placed beside one another form a chain in which meanings contaminate each other instead of a continuous narrative. The romantic title suggests intimacy or a new beginning, while the “fifth” repetition introduces unease into a model of happiness performed again and again. In an interview with Living Content, Rødland discusses the editing of the photobook, repeated motifs, and a structure that keeps meaning moving in several directions*10. Bergen Kunsthall’s publication notice records that the project was produced collaboratively by institutions in Bergen, Stockholm, and Helsinki and included thirty new photographs and his first video work in eleven years*18.

    The series changes classifications such as “strange advertisement” or “uncomfortable portrait” through the pressure of adjacent images. The tenderness of one photograph changes the violence of the next; moisture from food transfers to a picture of skin; the sublimity of landscape drops into an inexpensive interior object. The book joins works through visual and emotional rhyme. Kiasma presented Fifth Honeymoon as an exhibition centered on photography while organized around the video Between Fork and Ladder*19. For Rødland, editing is central to maintaining several possibilities of meaning.

    The Touch That Made You — Making Touch the Structure of an Exhibition

    The Touch That Made You was held at Serpentine Galleries in 2017 and brought together works from the 1990s onward without arranging them as a chronological survey. The exhibition catalog was published by Serpentine Galleries and Koenig Books.Serpentine described it as an exhibition of carefully constructed everyday scenes that preserved chance and multiple readings, keeping viewers within the act of looking*1. The “touch” of the title extends from contact pictured in the photographs to the touch of light on a subject and the effect of an image on the viewer’s memory and desire.

    The catalog gathers major works into one volume and creates new sequences among bodies, objects, and landscapes*11. In the exhibition, print size and spacing regulate tactile intensity. At close range, details become excessive; from a distance, compositions resembling religious painting or advertising emerge. Rødland’s photographs activate the eye that interprets meaning and the body that reacts to surfaces at the same time.

    132 BPM — Extending Photographic Composition into Moving Duration

    132 BPM (2005) transfers Rødland’s sense of photographic composition into video. For a 2006 solo exhibition, MoMA PS1 described the work as a moving image that links forests, waterfalls, and coastal vegetation in western Croatia to a steady tempo of 132 beats per minute*12. BPM measures the speed of dance music, but the video restrains rapid editing and sustains a fixed pulse across details of the natural world.Plants, light, and moving water are perceived as musical time.

    During filming, Rødland manipulated branches and leaves and sometimes illuminated them with a flashlight, introducing small staged actions into the landscape*12.Like the still photographs, the work prepares a composition while admitting unpredictable movements of wind, water, and plants.Bringing the fixed pulse associated with advertising or music video into contact with Romantic images of nature sets artificial speed against physical duration within a single work.

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    A View of Photography beyond “Strange” and “Surreal”

    Because of its abnormal props and awkward bodies, Rødland’s work is often summarized as “strange,” “surreal,” or a form of “uncomfortable beauty.” Those labels obscure why the photographs retain a polish borrowed from advertising, fashion, and devotional painting. He treats the surface itself as the site where desire, faith, and memory take shape.An Aperture essay describes his concrete method of leaving contingency within meticulously staged situations and making images that resist classification even outside their original series*14.

    Symbols call several cultural memories into play. Fruit can suggest sexuality, decay, abundance, a commodity, and an offering in religious painting at the same time. A model’s hand can hold tenderness and control together. An interview in Autre addresses the way his works produce bodily proximity and psychological distance simultaneously*15. The photograph continues to change according to the viewer’s own history of desire and disgust after any authorial message has been understood.

    After Cindy Sherman and Jeff Wall — Returning Matter and Sensation to the Constructed Image

    Cindy Sherman performed the types produced by cinema and magazines, while Jeff Wall reconstructed events at the scale of painting and film. By the 1990s, the construction of photography through staging and the codes of advertising or cinema had become shared knowledge within contemporary art. Rødland accepts this premise and shifts attention toward the physical event in which light and matter still meet inside a constructed image. An interview in Sleek presents his view of staged photography, photographic truth, observation, and analog production as parts of one problem*16.

    His distinction lies in preserving a double response: viewers recognize a landscape as Romantic and still feel moved by it; they recognize advertising light and remain attracted to skin or fruit. This goes beyond returning Romantic allure or sensuality as a post-critical sentiment. During analog production, models, liquid, film, and light deviate slightly from the artist’s plan, and those deviations create friction with cultural codes. Staged photography thereby becomes a site where signs, matter, and the viewer’s reactions alter one another, beyond a picture whose work ends when the source of a quotation is identified.

    Place in Photographic History — Turning Commercial Seduction into the Viewer’s Own Critique

    It is more accurate to say that Rødland reintroduced another criterion into the critique of construction established at the end of the twentieth century than to claim that he moved staged photography forward along a single line. He preserved advertising appeal and made attraction, disgust, and devotional feeling operate within the work. His statement at Bonniers Konsthall about moving from observation toward “a more deepened engagement” signals a shift from viewing that explains an image toward viewing that accepts responsibility for one’s own reaction*5.

    The contact of analog light and matter, the model’s response, the print surface, adjacency within a photobook, and distance in an exhibition were joined as a single method. The Touch That Made You, presented at Serpentine Galleries and Fondazione Prada, brought photographs and videos from the 1990s onward together outside a fixed chronology, allowing images from different periods to alter one another’s intensity of seduction and unease*17. Rødland’s place in photographic history lies in challenging the assumption that staged photography requires coolness or an explicit quotation in order to be critical. He established a form capable of examining the viewer’s desire while retaining commercial seduction, Romanticism, and near-religious feeling.
