    Philip-Lorca diCorcia

    フィリップ＝ロルカ・ディコルシア
    Country1980s Period1980–1990s ChannelIssues in photo history · Conceptual
    Abstract

    Philip-Lorca diCorcia learned from 1970s conceptual art to use photography as the record of an action, then encountered a documentary tradition shaped by Walker Evans at Yale University. He designs a location in advance with electronic strobes—artificial lights that emit a brief, intense flash. In Hustlers, he directed men hired for the photograph; in Streetwork and Heads, he photographed passersby who entered the prepared light by chance. By joining construction before exposure with the singularity of a street encounter, he challenged the street-photographic assumption that an unguarded moment is inherently more truthful.

    What this photographer changed

    Henri Cartier-Bresson’s “decisive moment” emphasized the photographer’s response to an instant when form and meaning briefly coincide in the world. Postwar street photographers such as Garry Winogrand developed that premise in different directions, answering the unpredictable city through the movement of body and camera. Staged photography in the 1980s foregrounded the artist’s construction of a scene. In Streetwork, diCorcia left the gestures of passersby to chance while determining in advance where light would fall and who would appear to occupy the leading role. The effect by which an unguarded expression seems to reveal a person’s “truth” is therefore produced by lighting and camera position. In Hustlers, his subjects perform themselves while titles retain actual occupations, fees, and places. His work made it difficult to judge street photography’s credibility solely as documentation or staging. Lighting, focus, and titles also had to be considered as mechanisms that make a person appear truthful.

    § 01 / 04 Background and Period: Between Conceptual Art and Walker Evans

    Conceptual Ideas and Yale’s Documentary Tradition

    When diCorcia studied at the School of the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston in the mid-1970s, conceptual art placed greater weight on ideas, language, actions, and institutional questions than on the material finish of an object. In projects such as The Miracles of Everyday Life, he used photographs as records of actions that made ordinary phenomena appear strange. In Suspense, a flash arrests an object at the instant it falls, treating camera and light as instruments for executing an idea. He has recalled that he did not then regard photography as a medium of truth and felt distant from mainstream documentary photography’s investment in evidentiary reliability*1. MoMA’s Fred (1986) also marks this starting point: an early tableau that uses a real person and place while severing their ordinary relation and deliberately withholding what came before and after*2.

    At Yale’s graduate program, the environment was reversed. Walker Evans’s presence remained strong, and training was grounded in large-format cameras, black-and-white photography, and the precise description of real places. DiCorcia began photographing people there, directing the conceptual-art premise that images are culturally constructed signs toward concrete places and individuals in the wake of Evans. The ICA Boston locates the position sustained from his early work between the modernist documentary of Evans, Winogrand, and Arbus and a conceptual art that treated images as signs*3. His work holds together the postmodern understanding that media shape reality and the density contributed by actual people, places, and accidents. MoMA’s sustained collection of his work since New Photography 2 in 1986—from early staged photographs of family and friends through Streetwork and Heads—also shows the longevity of a method that suggests narrative while withholding its surrounding information*4.

    Electronic Strobe: Deciding in Advance What Will Appear Important

    DiCorcia supported himself through magazine and advertising work, learning commercial methods that involved large crews, careful location scouting, exposure tests, and electronic strobes. An electronic strobe can be positioned away from the camera and emits a brief flash that makes part of a person or place brighter than its surroundings. Before anyone appears, the photographer can determine the direction, intensity, and reach of the light. Centre Pompidou explains that diCorcia brought the complex lighting he learned in commercial work into the street, combining natural and artificial illumination within a single image*5. In advertising, such methods made products legible and desirable. DiCorcia used the same effect to establish the conditions under which an ordinary scene could resemble an event from a film.

    In the 1980s, staged photography by Cindy Sherman, Jeff Wall, and others cited the conventions of film, advertising, and painting to demonstrate that photographs are constructed. Street photography of the same period retained the improvisational observation and singular encounters developed by Winogrand and others. DiCorcia combined the lighting design of staged photography with the unpredictable people of the street. Schirn Kunsthalle describes his career as a sustained inquiry into the boundary between staged and unstaged scenes*6. However carefully the image is prepared, facial expressions and encounters with other people exceed the artist’s plan. The same photograph can therefore reveal chance within construction and the artifice of lighting and framing within a chance encounter.

    § 02 / 04 The Core of the Work: Unpredictable People Enter Controlled Light

    Hustlers: AIDS, Payment, and Performance in Title and Image

    Hustlers (1990–92) portrays male sex workers and men who had come to the streets around Santa Monica Boulevard while seeking success in Hollywood. Its background included diCorcia’s brother’s death during the AIDS crisis and conservative attacks on public arts funding around the Robert Mapplethorpe exhibition. DiCorcia has described the series as a response to a political climate in which gay bodies were treated through disease and moral condemnation*7. The Museo Reina Sofía reads the work as connecting a collapsed American dream, sexual double standards, and the margins between the film industry’s fictions and social reality*8. The Met records both the requirement that NEA grant recipients pledge not to produce “obscene” work and diCorcia’s symbolic response: distributing the grant as fees to people subjected to that climate of censorship*9.

    Before each exposure, assistants scouted the location, installed strobes, and checked the exposure with Polaroids. DiCorcia approached men on the street, paid what each would ordinarily receive for a transaction, and asked him to stand in a predetermined position. Titles record the subject’s name, age, place of origin, and the amount paid*10. Even when the figure is lit like a film actor, the amount in the title returns the image to the brief employment and economic difficulty that made the photograph possible. A performing body remains alongside an actual occupation, fee, and place, allowing the staged image itself to carry social fact. SFMOMA’s Marilyn publishes the image together with a title listing the subject’s name, age, origin, and a payment of thirty dollars, preserving both cinematic glamour and the specificity of the transaction*11.

    Streetwork: Lighting the Place Where a Decisive Moment May Occur

    For Streetwork (1993–99), diCorcia installed electronic strobes along sidewalks in New York, Tokyo, Hong Kong, Mexico City, Los Angeles, and other cities, photographing passersby from a distance with a telephoto lens*6. He determined location, framing, focus, and light level, leaving the city’s flow to decide who entered the illuminated zone. Clothing, expressions, strides, and overlaps with other figures remained singular events. The Reina Sofía presents the method as a reinterpretation of street photography’s “decisive moment”*8. Cartier-Bresson and Winogrand responded to the form of an event; diCorcia prepared the lighting conditions that would make an event appear dramatic.

    The strobed figure and naturally lit background differ in brightness and color temperature. A passerby remains one person among many on the same sidewalk yet appears as the protagonist of a film or the subject of an event. What changes is less the street occurrence than the hierarchy through which the photograph assigns importance. The brightly lit expression acquires psychological weight, while figures behind it appear secondary. That hierarchy comes from the reach of the light, focus, and the decision to release the shutter, not from the subject’s social importance. Streetwork demonstrates that the truth effect of a decisive moment can arise from lighting designed before the event as well as from the photographer’s response to it.

    Heads: Light That Gives Psychological Weight to Unguarded Expressions

    For Heads (2000–01), diCorcia aimed a powerful strobe at a fixed section of sidewalk in Times Square and photographed passersby from a distance. Most were unaware of the exposure; a single face or upper body emerges from a dark background. Schirn Kunsthalle explains that he photographed thousands of pedestrians and used intense light to transform anonymous faces into images resembling religious painting or a cinematic close-up*6. The subject is an unidentified person who happened to pass through the site, yet the large print magnifies pores and eyelids and presents the instant as a monumental portrait.

    Closed eyes, a tired expression, or an inward gaze were shown without the subject’s awareness of being photographed. The large print and dramatic light nonetheless invite readings of loneliness, anxiety, or revelation. By withholding personal information, diCorcia emphasizes how lighting gives narrative weight to an ordinary expression. Heads asks how viewers receive orchestrated visibility as evidence of an inner life.

    Re-editing Hustlers: Censorship, Funding, and Circulation Across the Series

    Hustlers was presented in MoMA’s 1993 exhibition Strangers and attracted attention as a form of social portraiture combining cinematic lighting, factual information about each subject, and the fee recorded in the title*12. The amount describes the subject and preserves the brief employment relation between photographer and sitter. Because diCorcia used an NEA grant to pay the men, the series treats public funding, censorship, and sexual labor as interconnected production conditions during the AIDS crisis and the culture wars.

    In 2013, a substantially expanded book and exhibition incorporated previously unpublished images. A series once known through a small number of portraits was re-edited to trace the occupations, movements, and payments of men drawn to the area around Hollywood. Material published by David Zwirner shows how this expansion prompted renewed consideration of the grant’s use, payments to subjects, and labor in a city that promises success, alongside the cinematic attraction of individual photographs*13.

    The Met included Hustlers in an exhibition examining the relation between “truth and fiction” in contemporary photography*14. This framing recognizes that a staged photograph can record an actual contract and social situation, while a title listing facts can still direct the viewer toward a particular persona through lighting and pose. Hustlers shifts discussion from classifying an image as document or staging toward examining how production conditions and circulation shape the meaning of the person depicted.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media: Making Pre- and Post-Shoot Decisions Part of the Work

    A Storybook Life: Editing More Than Twenty Years of Photographs Afterward

    A Storybook Life (1975–99) selects seventy-six photographs made over more than two decades and forms a single work through spreads and sequence. The images come from different people, places, and periods—family life, interiors, streets, and travel. DiCorcia did not plan a single story while taking them; later editing uncovered repetitions of gaze, color, light, and gesture. LACMA’s retrospective presented the long-term project as a key work linking his early family photographs to the street series*15. The Art Institute of Chicago describes the seventy-six photographs as a distinct work that produces meaning through free association between adjacent images*16.

    Despite the word “storybook” in the title, the work supplies no conventional plot or conclusion. Viewers imagine family relations, travel, waiting, and loss from neighboring photographs, although those narratives may not correspond to causal events at the time of exposure. The project separates photography’s record of an instant from editing’s later creation of relations among facts. Against a history centered on isolated masterpieces, A Storybook Life shows the importance of rereading years of negatives and discovering repetitions the photographer had not known were there.

    Thousand: Production Decisions Preserved in Test Polaroids

    Thousand (2007) displays roughly one thousand Polaroids used over about twenty years to check exposures and test lighting. Alongside scenes resembling finished works are assistants, empty sets, figures checking positions, failed exposures, and preparations for commercial assignments. LACMA describes the dense wall installation as a way to reveal the trials and choices behind finished photographs*17.

    Polaroids ordinarily functioned as expendable working material for a final image. Thousand retains those that were never used, revealing how diCorcia’s precise photographs emerged from numerous checks and failures. Seen all at once, however, the circumstances of individual shoots recede, and fragments of private life and commercial production become an enormous visual archive. By making preparation public, the work shows the labor behind images viewers might otherwise accept as either “natural moments” or flawless staging.

    Large Prints: Viewing Chance Passersby at the Scale of History Painting

    DiCorcia’s photographs are often shown as large color prints. In Streetwork, viewers can take in the intensely lit figure and the surrounding pedestrians at once. In Heads, pores and eyelids are enlarged, turning an anonymous instant into a monumental portrait. Scale adds information and transfers the public authority of advertising, the cinema screen, and history painting to the image of an unknown person. Museum De Pont explains that his photographs leave viewers uncertain between everyday reality and a cinematic scene*18.

    Film shows what precedes and follows a scene in time; diCorcia’s photograph retains a single moment. In Hustlers, titles provide names, origins, and fees, while Streetwork removes names altogether. Lighting and composition suggest a prepared narrative, yet the event’s cause and outcome remain unknown. Guided by a cinematic appearance, viewers supply the figure’s past and future. His use of advertising technique avoids product explanation because the viewer’s imaginative completion is itself part of the photograph’s content.

    Editorial and Fashion Work: Moving Between Commercial and Fine-Art Photography

    Magazine and fashion work was more than a separate means of earning a living for diCorcia. Techniques for controlling models, clothing, architecture, and artificial light while making a commissioned product or figure legible supported his early staged photographs and the production design of Hustlers. In turn, the lighting and unresolved narratives of his art photographs—placing a person within what looks like a film scene—entered his fashion stories for magazines.

    In 2018, Vogue commissioned a series based on the Metropolitan Museum of Art exhibition Heavenly Bodies. A recollection by editor Phyllis Posnick, published by David Zwirner, describes how museum display, religious imagery, clothing, and editorial photography intersected on a single set*19. DiCorcia used lighting refined in advertising for artworks, then returned the unsettling narrative quality of those artworks to fashion photography. This two-way movement unsettled a division between commercial and fine-art photography based solely on use.

    § 04 / 04 Criticism and Place in Photographic History: Reconsidering the Truth Effect of the Decisive Moment

    The Heads Lawsuit: Who Owns a Portrait Made in Public?

    Erno Nussenzweig, the subject of one photograph in Heads, sued on the grounds that his image had been taken, exhibited, and sold without consent. In 2007, the New York Court of Appeals upheld dismissal because the claim was filed beyond the statutory period; the majority did not rule on the First Amendment issue. A separate opinion by Justice Tom and others argued that the work remained protected artistic expression on a matter of public interest even when sold in a limited edition*20. The result cannot be reduced to a simple victory of art over privacy or publicity rights. The subject still did not participate in the enlargement, exhibition, or sale of his face. Heads therefore brings together the freedom of street photography and the authority to convert an anonymous passerby into a portrait for the art market.

    In Hustlers, subjects know they are being photographed and receive payment. In Heads, they are unaware of the exposure. The terms of consent differ, but in both series the photographer controls the negative, print, exhibition, and sale. DiCorcia’s light does more than make a person visually compelling; it sharpens the public-space question of who may own a stranger’s instant as a work of art.

    Differences from Jeff Wall, Nan Goldin, and Garry Winogrand

    Jeff Wall also uses cinematic preparation and large prints, but often constructs scenes approaching history painting through actors, sets, and digital assembly. DiCorcia retains actual people and places and leaves substantial room for chance within the plan. Nan Goldin made photographs from sustained life with friends and lovers, grounding the work in intimate relationships with her subjects. DiCorcia chose a contract lasting a few hours in Hustlers and anonymous encounters in Streetwork and Heads, making the thinness of the relationship itself an issue. Garry Winogrand responded to the city’s flow with a handheld camera; diCorcia installed his equipment first and waited for passersby to enter the light. His work intersects with Wall’s production scale, Goldin’s attention to vulnerable real people, and Winogrand’s chance gestures, while focusing on the narratives that advance design imposes on an unguarded subject.

    Reconsidering the Credibility of the Decisive Moment Through Apparatus and Light

    Cartier-Bresson’s “decisive moment” made the anticipation of, and rapid response to, an instant when form and meaning coincide in reality a central photographic skill. Through tilted frames and intersecting figures, Winogrand answered urban disorder bodily. DiCorcia transferred part of the emphasis those photographers derived from reaction at exposure to lighting designed beforehand. In Streetwork, he left expressions and gestures to chance while preparing, through electronic strobe, telephoto lens, and fixed focus, the conditions under which one person alone would appear important. In Heads, an unguarded face seems to convey psychological truth; in Hustlers, titles attached to performing figures provide actual fees and places of origin. By designing light and distance, the photographer makes an ordinary passerby’s instant resemble a film protagonist or a confessional subject.

    Across these series, the criteria for assessing street photography’s credibility expand beyond the alternatives “Was the event real?” and “Was the person acting?” They include whom the light makes important, what the frame elevates into an event, and which social facts the title preserves. The ICA Boston identifies diCorcia as an influential photographer of the preceding three decades because he brought cinematic lighting into the street while retaining chance subjects and making visible the conditions that cause those subjects to appear truthful*3. The social portraiture of Hustlers has also been credited with influencing fine-art photography, photography education at Yale, fashion advertising, and editorial portraiture*7. Yale University Press’s study Unfamiliar Streets, which reinserts an artist shaped by fashion, editorial work, and conceptual art into the history of street photography, likewise shows how the boundaries of that history have been reconsidered after diCorcia*21.

    § REL Related photographers & movements
    Related photographers
    • Jeff Wall — Shares large color prints and cinematic preparation, but differs in the degree of control over the event.
    • Nan Goldin — Photographed contemporary urban life and people, but contrasts with diCorcia in intimacy with subjects and immediacy of exposure.
    • Garry Winogrand — A predecessor who turned street chance into photographs; diCorcia delimited that chance with lighting and a fixed position.
    Related movements
    • Cinematic Photography — Introduces cinematic lighting and open-ended narrative into the still image.
    • Staged Photography — Constructs the conditions of place, light, and figure before exposure.
    • Street Photography — Works with chance and unaware figures in public space while transforming the method through artificial light.
