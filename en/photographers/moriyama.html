Daido Moriyama

Daido Moriyama is a key figure for understanding the history of photography around Provoke and Japanese Photography. This page follows the photographer's place in photography history through Provoke, are-bure-boke, and urban photography, and the representative work Japan, a Photo Theater, related photographers, movements, and sources.

Essay

Daido Moriyama was born in Ikeda, Osaka, in 1938 into a family whose frequent moves — Tokyo, Hiroshima, Chiba, Shimane, Osaka — gave him what he later described as an instinct for wandering that became foundational to his practice. After assisting Takeji Iwamiya and Eikoh Hosoe, the decisive turn toward a personal and improvisational approach came through his encounter with William Klein's photobooks. "A huge shock," he called it: Klein's rough, aggressive pictures of city life showed that photography could be a record of collision rather than calm observation, and that directness was more honest than polish*1. He joined Provoke magazine from its second issue in 1968. The magazine's subtitle — "provocative materials for thought" — expressed a precise critical position: political language had failed after the collapse of the 1960 Anpo security treaty struggle, and clean, well-made images were suspect because Japanese photographers had used them to support imperial propaganda during the war. A sharp, composed photograph could lie; grain, blur, and lost focus could not*2. The are-bure-boke aesthetic — grainy, blurred, out of focus — was therefore not a stylistic preference but an ethical claim: the only honest image was one that showed the limits of the photographer's control, a trace of a body moving through a world that resisted rational ordering. Moriyama described his approach in terms of instinct rather than method: "When I go out into the street, I have no plan. I walk down one street and feel like turning a corner, so I turn. I am really like a dog — I decide where to go by the smell of things." This connects directly to his best-known single image, Stray Dog, Misawa (1971)*3. His concept of "copy of a copy" — drawn partly from Warhol — placed the emphasis not on the photographer's unique vision but on the circulation of images as a cultural process. Farewell Photography (1972), which mixed his own images with those of other artists and cropped negatives, pushed the dismantling of authorship as far as it could go; it later entered the Tate collection*3. His international reputation was shaped by a sequence of institutional moments: John Szarkowski's New Japanese Photography at MoMA in 1974, where Szarkowski wrote that what was nihilistic suggestion in Tomatsu became in Moriyama "frank and effective statement"; the first major American museum retrospective at SFMOMA in 1999; and Aperture's argument that the label "postwar Japan" only scratches the surface, and that his real concern was dismantling the image itself*6. A 2012-13 Tate Modern exhibition pairing him with William Klein drew the response from critic Sarah Kent that Moriyama "deserves to be seen as a law unto himself, rather than an acolyte of a more familiar western photographer"*2. In 2019 he received the Hasselblad Award, whose jury chair described him as one of the most important and influential photographers in history*4. MoMA holds his work in its permanent collection*5.
