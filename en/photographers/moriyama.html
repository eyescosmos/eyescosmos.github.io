Essay

Daido Moriyama was born in Ikeda, Osaka, in 1938. After assisting Takeji Iwamiya and Eikoh Hosoe, he went independent in 1961 and joined the photography journal Provoke from its second issue in 1968. Though the magazine lasted only three issues, it transformed Japanese photographic expression. Its subtitle, "provocative materials for thought," captured the intellectual and political charge of the moment*1. Moriyama's contribution was the rough visual language later summarized as are, bure, boke - grain, blur, and out-of-focus imagery - which rejected established ideas of clean, well-made photography. In high-contrast black and white, his fragments of the city gave direct form to the instability and disorientation of high-growth Japan*2. In 1972 he published Farewell Photography, a book that pushed the dismantling of the photographic medium itself further than almost anyone had before, and which later entered the Tate collection as a landmark of postwar photographic experimentation*3. Since the late 1960s he has produced more than 150 photobooks, continually reworking both subject and form. A 2012-13 Tate Modern exhibition pairing him with William Klein was widely seen as the first substantial attempt to think seriously about the relation between their practices*2. In 2019 he received the Hasselblad Award, whose jury chair described him as one of the most important and influential photographers in history*4. MoMA holds his work in its permanent collection*5. Drawing on figures such as Klein and Walker Evans while forging his own language on the streets of Tokyo, Moriyama established a hand-held, fast-moving practice that made photography feel inseparable from walking, collision, and urban noise. His Provoke-era work ran parallel to the intensity of student protest in 1968-69 and preserved the sense that photography could become a tool for confronting the contradictions of society itself. From Japan: A Photo Theater to Shinjuku, Koori, and Farewell Photography, he repeatedly renewed the photobook as a form, and even in later life moved fluidly between film prints and images made on a mobile phone, sustaining a lifelong inquiry into what photography can be.
