    § 042 — Photographer Index — Color Photography

    William Eggleston

    ウィリアム・エグルストン 1939–
    CountryUnited States Period1970–1980s ChannelDocumentary as reading · DOCUMENTARY
    Abstract

    William Eggleston photographed homes, cars, people, and signs in Memphis and the Mississippi Delta as frames in which color, line, and distance enter into momentary tension. He brought the compositional awareness he gained from Henri Cartier-Bresson, painting, drawing, and color film to 35mm photography, linking the colors of the visible world to Southern life and history. Through dye-transfer printing, his 1976 MoMA exhibition, and William Eggleston’s Guide, he made private, minimally explained fragments of everyday life central to the language of color photography.

    What this photographer changed

    Eggleston changed color from information attached to a subject into a structural element that carries composition and the time of a place at once. Red ceilings, blue skies, car bodies, clothing, and signs determine shape and distance while retaining the consumption, class, race, and memory of the postwar South. The 1976 MoMA exhibition and William Eggleston’s Guide brought private surroundings, synthetic color, abrupt cropping, the materiality of the print, and open-ended sequencing into the center of critical discussion about art photography.

    § 01 / 04 How Eggleston Came to Photography: Painting and Color Film

    The Decisive Moment and the Turn to Leica Photography

    Eggleston grew up on a plantation in Sumner, Mississippi, surrounded by cameras, recording equipment, the piano, and drawing. In college he encountered Henri Cartier-Bresson’s The Decisive Moment and committed himself to photography*6. He later described his early work as an attempt to approach its compositional discipline*8. Around 1960 he began photographing the Memphis area in black-and-white 35mm, searching for the point at which people, cars, interiors, and roads briefly came into balance.

    Tom Young and the Decision to Photograph the Familiar South

    The painter Tom Young, a visiting artist at the University of Mississippi, prompted Eggleston to turn his camera toward the South he already knew. Young urged him to continue photographing, study contemporary abstract art, and photograph the surroundings that seemed least stimulating*18. Eggleston shifted away from the search for distant subjects and concentrated on shopping centers, housing developments, and gas stations appearing on agricultural land*5. Malloy reads William Eggleston’s Guide as a record of the agrarian South moving through development, commercialization, and suburbanization*25.

    Painting and Drawing: Color, Line, and the Frame

    Eggleston studied painting in college, admired Franz Kline, Willem de Kooning, and Jackson Pollock, and made abstract paintings of his own. He later said that painting and drawing helped explain the construction of his photographs*5. His sketchbooks preserve combinations of fields of color and line, along with evidence of his interest in Kandinsky*18.

    He learned the use of diagonal composition from Degas and attended to the organization of color within the frame in Hitchcock’s films*7. Eggleston has also described turning prints upside down to inspect the arrangement of power lines, signs, buildings, and cars*9. The compositional sensibility he developed through painting and film operated within a photographic process that selected actual objects and colors in a brief instant.

    Art in the 1950s and 1960s: Abstract Expressionism, Color Field Painting, and Pop

    Abstract Expressionism in the 1950s tested the terms of painting through gesture, bodily action, paint, and large scale; Color Field painting brought the effects of expanses of color to the fore*12. In the 1960s, Pop art introduced commodities and reproduced images into art*14. Minimalism developed geometric form and industrial materials*15.

    Eggleston belonged to none of these movements. His photographs nevertheless join formal attention to color, line, and plane with a postwar environment crowded by cars, signs, commodities, and suburban houses. Their connection to contemporary art lies in the way color can organize a picture without erasing the histories of actual things and places.

    From Black and White to Color: Photographing the Contemporary South

    Eggleston began working in color in the mid-1960s and identified a photograph of a young man pushing shopping carts at dusk as his first successful color picture*18. He explained that he remembered the world in color and that black and white failed to retain enough of the experience present at the moment of exposure*7. The colors of product packaging, cars, fluorescent lights, and road signs shape his compositions while recording a postwar South being altered by consumer culture and suburbanization*21.

    § 02 / 04 Color, the Frame, and the “Democratic Way of Looking”

    35mm and the Instant of Deliberate Selection

    Eggleston moved through familiar spaces with a 35mm camera, looking upward from low positions, cutting objects at the edge of the frame, and leaving diagonals and areas of open space. Fondation Henri Cartier-Bresson identifies everyday motifs and unconventional framing as characteristics of his work from the beginning*8. Eggleston rejected the label “snapshot,” stating that the pictures were carefully conceived*20. He also rearranged black-and-white work prints while considering exhibition sequences, making post-exposure selection part of his process from an early stage*18.

    The “Democratic Way of Looking”: Withholding a Hierarchy of Subjects

    The “democratic way of looking” names an approach that withholds a hierarchy of subjects before the photograph is made and distributes attention widely across the visible world. The Whitney describes his sustained attention to moments close at hand as the working form of this idea*6. Family members, cars, refrigerators, ceilings, power lines, and debris in the street acquire different weight in each photograph through color, cropping, and distance. Young’s advice encouraged Eggleston to suspend preference and aversion as primary criteria*18. The foundation likewise defines its mission through a “democratic way of looking at the world around us”*23.

    Color as Composition and the Time of Place

    In Eggleston’s photographs, color serves composition, historical reference, and psychological pressure at once. MoMA’s 1976 materials describe a new generation moving color from a descriptive or decorative supplement to a central determinant of photographic content*2. Coca-Cola red, skin, fluorescent green, and the brown of rusted metal direct the picture’s movement and depth while preserving traces of commodity circulation, artificial light, and regional memory.

    Dye Transfer: Commercial Saturation Applied to Everyday Life

    Eggleston’s early color pictures were made with commercial film and processed by drugstore laboratories. Seeking greater control over tone and detail, he adopted dye-transfer printing in the early 1970s*18. The costly process, which layered dyes from three color-separation negatives, was used chiefly in advertising and fashion*19. Eggleston directed its saturation toward bare bulbs, old cars, refrigerators, and rural roads, intensifying the wear of objects and the time accumulated in a place.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Photobooks, and Printing Methods

    Greenwood, Mississippi: The Red Ceiling and the History of an Interior

    In Greenwood, Mississippi, a red ceiling crossed by a bare bulb and white wiring fills the frame. The dye-transfer red seems to press the ceiling forward, compressing the sliver of wall and images below*11. The building was a former servants’ quarters attached to a residence, with rooms painted blue, yellow, and red*7. The saturated field of color retains the building’s history, artificial light, and a private social world. Eggleston considered it an important picture, and it has been described as a work that led to his 1976 MoMA exhibition*22.

    Untitled (Memphis), 1970: The Tricycle and Suburban Space

    Untitled (Memphis) looks up at a tricycle from near ground level, placing its front wheel in the frame as an enormous circle. The low vantage gives the toy sculptural scale, while the houses, lawn, and sky recede around it*10. Red, blue-green, black, and blue establish the picture’s center of gravity. With no child present, the road, houses, and automobiles behind it register suburban life. Malloy reads the Guide, including this photograph, as a record of agricultural land under development and a Southern life increasingly shaped by commerce*25.

    William Eggleston’s Guide: A Sequence of Forty-Eight Photographs

    William Eggleston’s Guide is a 112-page photobook containing forty-eight photographs selected from 375 pictures made in Tennessee and northern Mississippi. It was MoMA’s first publication devoted to color photography*3. Tricycles, people, tables, roads, and interiors follow one another without explanatory text; recurring color, viewpoint, and distance connect a field of everyday life. The book includes an introduction by John Szarkowski and was reissued in 2002*27. Szarkowski described the photographs through the camera’s capacity to order experience within the frame, leaving subject-based categories aside*2. Eggleston likewise valued the photobook as a medium that allowed photographs to be studied over time*20.

    Election Eve and The Democratic Forest: Photographing the Edges of Events

    Election Eve was made in 1976 along the road to Plains, Georgia, Jimmy Carter’s hometown. Its subjects are houses, churches, vacant lots, and roads, with the candidate and campaign rallies outside the sequence*17. In The Democratic Forest, more than 1,000 photographs selected from roughly 12,000 exposures made in the 1980s were edited into ten volumes, extending the same attention from Memphis to Berlin*16. Ben Child interprets the continuity of roads, houses, and commercial spaces as a map of a “postsouthern” landscape where regional memory and national consumer culture coexist*26. Eudora Welty emphasized the capacity of ordinary details to evoke unspoken histories and desires*9.

    Stranded in Canton: The Order of the Still Image and the Southern Night

    The black-and-white video Stranded in Canton, made around 1973–74, shows friends and musicians in Memphis, Mississippi, and the New Orleans area amid dark rooms, alcohol, conversation, guns, and blues music. The 2008 retrospective placed the film beside photographs and drawings, presenting the range of Eggleston’s work outside the still photograph*6. Schjeldahl connects the turmoil released in the film with the severe compositional order of the still images and calls Eggleston “a blues photographer”*28. The voices, bodies, violence, and music of the film show that the stillness of the photographs comes from selecting a single instant out of an excess of reality.

    § 04 / 04 The 1976 MoMA Exhibition, Critical Stakes, and Photographic History

    The 1976 MoMA Exhibition: New Criteria for Color Photography

    MoMA’s Photographs by William Eggleston ran from May 24 through August 1, 1976, and the Guide appeared during the same period*1. The exhibition included approximately seventy-five works; the book contained forty-eight selected from 375 pictures, making exhibition and publication distinct selections*2. Responses that criticized the work as ordinary or boring reveal the collision between established criteria and photographs of private surroundings, synthetic color, diagonal composition, and fragments with little explanation*21.

    Earlier Color Photography: Eggleston and Ernst Haas

    MoMA had already mounted Ernst Haas: Color Photography in 1962, establishing a precedent for solo exhibitions of color photography*4. The institutional first in 1976 was William Eggleston’s Guide, the museum’s first publication devoted to color photography*3. Eggleston’s work joined color, form, private memory, and regional surroundings in a single photographic language, directing the saturated palette developed in advertising and film toward worn cars and inexpensive interiors.

    A Critique of Photographic Institutions: What Counts as a Work?

    Eggleston’s photographs carried no explicit political statement, yet they created a problem for institutions that judged photography. Colors associated with family snapshots and drugstore prints, along with roads, refrigerators, tricycles, and ceilings, entered the museum and a meticulously produced photobook, unsettling the convention that a subject’s importance secured a photograph’s value. Eggleston rejected the label “snapshot” and explained that the apparently accidental appearance of his pictures arose from careful construction*20. The effects of color, cropping at the frame’s edge, print density, and the spacing between photographs became subjects for critical attention.

    The Postwar South: Equal Attention in an Unequal Society

    Eggleston’s South contains signs of race, class, and the Confederacy alongside commodities and suburban housing. In Untitled, c. 1970, a rusted Confederate-flag license plate functions as a compositional shape and an object bearing regional history*13. The photograph of Eggleston’s uncle and Jasper Staples, a Black employee, contains both intimacy and the unequal positions defined by employment and race*21. The High Museum places Eggleston among photographers who have questioned the South’s violent history and cultural myths*24. Richard H. King likewise connects the long concern with how the South is seen to a “democratization of seeing”*29. The democratic element lies in the distribution of attention, while the society pictured remains unequal.

    Withholding Explanation: Critical Force and Its Limits

    The civil rights movement and segregation rarely appear as direct subjects, and Eggleston offered no explicit conclusion about Southern history. The photographs retain intimacy and inequality, the newness and wear of consumption, beauty and threat. Southern Spaces contrasts Walker Evans’s lucid image of the South with Eggleston’s diagonals, depth, bleeding color, and private scenes without a script*21. Schjeldahl describes the surrounding details that prevent meaning from settling into a single interpretation as an “excess of reality”*28. Sparse explanation leaves contradictions in place, while risking the absorption of social difference into atmosphere.

    The time embedded in roads, abandoned buildings, and rusted signs has also been discussed through comparison with Southern literature. Gerlero places Eggleston beside Cormac McCarthy and argues that photographic sequences and novels map a “new South” through ruins, memory, and the overlap of reality and imagination*30. Eggleston photographed moments when a cluttered reality acquired order through color, line, object, and distance, while retaining differences of life, class, and history within the frame. That tension gives the work its critical charge.

    § REL Related photographers & movements
    Related photographers
    • Walker Evans — A predecessor who described Southern architecture, signs, and everyday objects through frontal black-and-white photographs. Eggleston used color and oblique viewpoints to register the consumer culture and unstable present of the postwar South.
    • Stephen Shore — A contemporary point of comparison who reconfigured everyday America through color in the 1970s.
    • Joel Meyerowitz — A photographer of the same generation who moved from street photography to color and treated color and light as a unified field of perception.
    • Nan Goldin — A later photographer who used color to record private life and the memory of an immediate community.
    Related movements
    • Color Photography — The history through which color moved from supplementary description toward a condition that determines form, time, and feeling at once.
    • New Color — A movement in the 1970s that made suburbs, roads, and household objects substantial subjects for color photography.
