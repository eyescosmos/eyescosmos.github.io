    Roe Ethridge

    ロー・エスリッジ
    Country1990s Period1990–2000s ChannelQuestioning the image · CONCEPTUAL
    Abstract

    Roe Ethridge brings fashion shoots, advertising outtakes, family photographs, rotting fruit, Southern landscapes, and images found online into the same exhibitions and photobooks. He retains the polished surface of commercial photography and makes the changing value and meaning of photographs—their movement among magazines, commodities, personal records, and museums—the subject of the work.He showed that the distinction between commercial and fine-art photography is produced through publication and circulation.

    What this photographer changed

    Postmodern photographic criticism brought advertising images into museums to analyze commodity culture. That approach often left the labor of making advertisements, and the movement of the same techniques and images among commissioned work, family records, and art, in the background. Ethridge treated the commercial photographer who produces commodity images and the family member photographing Southern suburbia as two roles occupied by the same person. He reedited his advertisements, outtakes, family photographs, and online images across magazines, exhibitions, and photobooks. The divisions between commerce and art, private memory and commodity value, emerge from circulation and adjacency more than from style.

    § 01 / 04 Historical Background

    From Atlanta to New York—Giving Commercial Work and Family History the Same Visual Vocabulary

    Ethridge studied photography at the Atlanta College of Art and made art while working on catalogs and magazines in New York.*6From the late 1990s through the 2000s, the routes connecting fashion magazines and museums, film and digital media, personal photographs and online images increasingly overlapped. ICA Boston introduced him early as an artist who applied a sensibility formed in fashion and advertising to landscapes, portraits, and still lifes.*4In a 2026 conversation, he recalled the middle-class suburbs of Miami and Atlanta, his father’s photography books, and his work as a catalog-photography assistant as foundations of his visual vocabulary.*20An Aperture conversation likewise places his early-1990s interest in the Düsseldorf School alongside his experience in catalog photography.*15Suburban family photographs and the professional task of showing products clearly without shadows converged into one basic visual vocabulary.

    Why Photography?—Combining Painterly Composition with Reproducible Labor

    Ethridge has said that he never wanted to make films but did want to paint.*2Photography could accommodate both the painterly desire to compose a single frame and the professional reality of making a living through commissions, printing, reproduction, and reuse. Postmodern appropriation criticized commodity culture by citing other people’s advertisements, but that approach would leave Ethridge’s own labor as a maker of commodity images outside the frame. Keeping commercial portfolios and personal work separate would likewise preserve the institutional division he wanted to examine. He therefore reused his own commissioned photographs, outtakes, and family pictures within one archive. ICA Boston presents his photograph of pigeons, made with film left over from a commercial shoot, as an example of how a commission, chance, and later selection flowed into personal work.*5

    § 02 / 04 Core Approach

    Operating Several Photographic Vocabularies Within One Archive

    Ethridge’s exhibitions bring together studio still lifes, models’ faces, a family refrigerator, Southern roads, corporate architecture, online images, and decaying food. A single signature style would leave only one of his parallel positions—the commercial photographer, family member, person shaped by Southern suburban memory, or fine artist—as the authentic author. He keeps those roles together by retaining these different image types within one archive. MoMA’s New Photography 2010 describes his assembly of web images, fashion photographs, rotting fruit, and runway images as a “visual fugue.”*6The range of still lifes, portraits, landscapes, and printed images on MoMA’s artist page likewise indicates that editing, more than a shooting style, is the core of the work.*7

    Bringing the Clarity of Product Photography into Private Memory

    High resolution, even lighting, saturated color, and frontality come from the clarity of catalog and product photography. Ethridge applies the same clarity to rotting fruit, inexpensive signs, family interiors, and Southern roads. ICA Boston describes his flattening close-ups as turning ordinary things into deadpan icons.*4Private memory consequently takes on the finish of a commodity image, while commodity images acquire the accumulated time associated with family photographs. By retaining advertising’s appeal, the image can also show how that appeal produces desire and repulsion regardless of its subject.

    Sequence—How Adjacency Changes the Genre Assigned to a Single Image

    Viewed alone, even lighting and vivid color quickly suggest advertising, while a family interior is readily classified as a private record. Ethridge alters these judgments through sequence: a family refrigerator appears beside a corporate logo, and a model’s portrait is followed by decaying matter. Private memory then reads like a commodity image, while advertising begins to carry the time of family life. Andrew Kreps Gallery explains that commercial studio images, constructed still lifes, and improvised cell-phone photographs produce open-ended meanings through their grouping.*10In Gagosian’s artist materials, Ethridge says that a sequence needs to “sing.”*9Sequence adds new meanings to individual photographs and demonstrates how adjacency remakes the categories of commerce, art, and family record.

    § 03 / 04 Major Works, Methods, and Media

    New Photography 2010—Photography After Appropriation

    MoMA’s 2010 exhibition introduced Ethridge as one of four artists working with images accumulated through print and film.*6He recombines his commissioned work, outtakes, online images, and everyday photographs, asking how an image functions in a given setting rather than whether the artist took it. MoMA’s Studio with Red Bag shows how an incidental corner of a working studio becomes both still life and self-reference through exhibition.*8

    Le Luxe, Sacrifice Your Body, and Shelter Island—Mixing Family History and Commodity Images in Photobooks

    In photobooks, spreads, repetition, and page rhythm turn heterogeneous images into a shared visual language. Sacrifice Your Body edits family history in Florida, cars, wetlands, and memories of crime with the same clarity as advertising; Shelter Island places summer family photographs and fashion images within the chronology of a shared vacation. MACK’s list of publications shows the central role of the photobook in Ethridge’s work.*12The exhibition catalogs from Le Consortium and M Museum Leuven were also published as attempts to carry relationships formed in the gallery into print.*14Because the same photograph acquires different neighbors in each book, the balance between personal history and commodity value changes with each republication.

    American Polychronic—Editing Two Careers Through Opposing Chronologies

    The large 2022 monograph American Polychronic covers work from 1999 to 2022, moving fine-art photographs forward chronologically and commercial work backward, with many exceptions. The New Yorker noted that the book’s more than 450 pages thoroughly mix editorial assignments and gallery work until the distinction becomes meaningless.*17The title’s “polychronic” structure refers to the parallel careers of a single photographer and the multiple presents in which images reappear.MACK describes the book as a nonlinear monograph moving through more than two decades of personal, editorial, and commercial work.*11After the book’s publication, Gagosian returned the same archive to the scale of the wall, showing that neither the photobook nor the exhibition serves as the final version of the other.*19

    § 04 / 04 Critical Reception and Place in Photographic History

    Reading the Boundary Between Commerce and Art Through the Circulation of the Same Image

    Commercial photographers have long made fine-art work, often separating commissions and personal projects into different portfolios, exhibitions, and standards of judgment. That division conceals the passage of the same techniques, models, studios, and files between the two fields. Ethridge repeatedly circulates the same negatives and digital files through magazines, exhibitions, and photobooks. In Gagosian Quarterly, he revisits photographs from the late 1990s and early 2000s, showing how commissioned, family, and regional images entered one archive.*3Because one image can acquire different values as a client’s advertisement, an artist’s work, or a fragment of family history, style alone cannot explain the boundary between commerce and art. Ethridge’s contribution to photographic history is to demonstrate this movement through images he made himself.

    The Wall After Wolfgang Tillmans—and Ethridge’s Difference

    His wall arrangements of different sizes and genres are often compared with post-Tillmans exhibition practice. In Ethridge’s case, photographs with a uniformly commercial finish make private pictures look like advertisements and advertisements resemble family memories. Le Consortium’s museum exhibition presented selection, placement, and installation as central to the work.*13American Spirit places a model on a break from a fashion shoot, landscapes of the American West, and a digital collage based on the drawer where his mother kept coupons within the same body of work.*18Southern family memory, brands, and references to photographic history share one folder and wall, making private origins inseparable from commercial surfaces.

    Critical Reception—The Risks of Coolness and Advertising Anonymity

    Reviewing MoMA’s 2010 exhibition, The New Yorker acknowledged the work’s intelligence and style while warning that advertising anonymity and calculated cool could become irritating.*16This criticism matters. Challenging genre hierarchies can look like accommodation to the system if the work merely repeats the appeal of commercial imagery. In Ethridge’s strongest pictures, decay, family history, Southern class culture, and the artificiality of digital processing interrupt the pleasure of the polished surface and resist frictionless circulation.

    Making Image Circulation, Commercial Labor, and Private Memory One Production Process

    Lectures and conversations at ICA Miami show that his movement between commercial and fine-art photography has been understood as a coherent methodology.*1American Polychronic edits advertisements, family photographs, landscapes, and gallery works by the same photographer in a nonlinear sequence, showing how meaning changes as images pass through several institutions.*17Postmodern appropriation cited finished advertisements from the outside, while professional institutions evaluated commercial and fine-art photography separately. Ethridge made the commodity-image producer and the family member photographing Southern suburbia the same person at the center of the work. Because family albums and product photographs share the same clear surface, the premise that personal memory remains outside consumer culture also collapses. Image circulation itself becomes a production process through which labor, class, and family history shape photographic meaning.

    Between Paul Outerbridge and Richard Prince—Critiquing the Commodity Image from the Labor That Produces It

    Ethridge has identified Paul Outerbridge, who pursued commercial photography alongside experimental still lifes and nudes, and Richard Prince, who repurposed advertising images as art, as important reference points. The New Yorker’s review of American Polychronic describes how Ethridge moved into the work of making the kinds of commercial images Prince had appropriated.*17In an Aperture conversation, Ethridge says he drew on the Düsseldorf School to add structure to humid, decaying, romanticized images of the South.*15At the same time, middle-class suburbia, a Methodist household, catalogs, and stock photography remained his “script.”*20The clarity of product photography is a visual language formed through Ethridge’s own labor and his memories of home and suburbia. When he reuses a commissioned image in an art setting, the different claims and values assigned by client, publication, model, editor, market, and museum become visible within a single photograph.
